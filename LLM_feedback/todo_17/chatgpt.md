# Performance Predictors in Rock Climbing

## 1. Finger strength as a predictor of grade  
- **Evidence:** Multiple cross‐sectional studies find very strong correlations between maximal finger flexor strength and climbing ability. For example, Buraas *et al.* (n=19 male climbers, redpoint 6b+–8c) report isometric finger strength on a 22 mm edge vs bouldering grade *r*=0.89 and vs redpoint *r*=0.67【18†L88-L95】. In the same study, body weight correlated negatively with bouldering (r=–0.49) but finger strength remained the strongest factor.  Similarly, Fanchini *et al.* (n=19, RP 6b+–8c) found finger strength correlated *r*=0.89 with bouldering and *r*=0.67 with redpoint (both *p*<0.01)【10†L1415-L1424】.  Ozimek *et al.* (elite vs advanced climbers) showed elites had significantly higher finger strength (129.1 kg vs 111.5 kg, *p*=0.03)【35†L320-L329】.  Rokowski *et al.* (small N) also found that finger strength relative to bodyweight significantly correlates with high climbing level.  These are evidence‐backed findings (peer‐reviewed) with reported *r*-values.  
- **Relative vs absolute:** Studies suggest normalizing finger force to body weight slightly improves prediction.  Stien *et al.* (J Sports Sci 2025) report that relative finger strength (N/kg) correlated more strongly with climbing performance than absolute force【26†L81-L90】 (bouldering *R*≈0.73 vs 0.64).  Buraas *et al.* report absolute force correlations but note finger strength is often considered relative to weight in practice【18†L88-L95】.  
- **Grip position:** Community consensus (Lattice Training blog) favors the half‐crimp position for testing – they found slightly better grade correlations with half‐crimp than open‐hand【30†L183-L191】.  In contrast, a small study observed that on a moderate-sized rung (23 mm) climbers can actually grip harder with an open-hand than half-crimp【28†L95-L100】.  No large study directly compares grip positions’ predictive power; this remains folklore.  
- **Hold depth:**  Most lab tests use ~20–23 mm edges.  Studies by Amca *et al.* show that on deep holds (~3–4 cm) open-hand force exceeds half-crimp, whereas on small edges (1–2 cm) half-crimp yields higher force【28†L95-L100】.  No study has systematically compared how different edge depths affect the strength–grade correlation.  Practically, hangboards around 18–22 mm are standard.  
- **Multivariate rank:**  Buraas *et al.* found finger strength was the single best predictor.  Lattice Training (n≈420) also reports finger hang (7‑s max hang) explaining ~43.6% of variance in route grade (R=0.660, R²=0.436) vs pull-ups only ~28.6%【62†L387-L395】.  In regressions to date, finger strength tends to overshadow other factors.  

**Community benchmarks (folklore):** Climbers often cite specific strength-to-weight ratios by grade.  For example, one analysis of Lattice training data suggests average two-arm hang strength on a 20 mm edge is ~146% bodyweight for V7 and ~170% for V11【52†L91-L99】.  Translating to one-arm hangs, this implies ~106% BW at V14–V15 and ~114–118% at V16–V17【52†L82-L90】【52†L91-L99】.  Another community estimate: “V15 climbers hang ~200% BW, whereas V3–5 climbers do about 100–110%”【60†L180-L188】.  Lattice’s internal data gives mean 2‑rep pull-ups of ~167% BW at ~9a level【62†L432-L436】 (see Pull-ups below).  Lattice’s **My Fingers** test reports finger‐hang norms (e.g. 134% BW for elite men, 164% at 8b, 170% at 8c, 190% at 9a)【62†L432-L436】.  The informal “minimum edge test” (the smallest edge one can hang) is discussed in blogs, but no formal data exist.  

**Summary:** Peak finger strength (especially relative to bodyweight) is a top performance indicator.  Large effect sizes (*r* up to ~0.9) are reported【18†L88-L95】. This is evidence-backed. Community norms generally align (higher-grade climbers hang >100% BW from small edges【48†L43-L49】【52†L82-L90】).  Grip position and edge-depth nuances remain under-studied beyond folklore【30†L183-L191】【28†L95-L100】.

## 2. Pull-up strength and lock-off strength as predictors  
- **Evidence:** Weighted pull-ups and isometric lock-off strength show moderate correlations. Devise *et al.* (n=28) found that both 1RM weighted pull-up and max rep pull-ups (all normalized to body mass) significantly correlated with climbing level (*p*<0.05)【57†L73-L80】.  Buraas *et al.* reported pull-up strength correlates moderately with bouldering grade (r=0.55, *p*<0.05)【18†L91-L95】.  These are positive but weaker than finger strength correlations. There is little published specifically on lock-off, though it contributes to pull-up performance.    
- **Relative vs absolute:** As with finger strength, relative pull-up strength (%BW) is the norm.  Devise *et al.* measured pull-ups relative to bodyweight. Lattice data expresses 2RM pull-ups as %BW (e.g. ~155% at 8b, ~167% at 9a【62†L432-L436】).  No study directly tests whether %BW vs raw kg is a better predictor, but body size clearly matters (heavier climbers may have to pull more mass).  Buraas normalized pull-ups to bodyweight as well.  
- **Multivariate:** In combined models (e.g. Lattice’s regression), pull-ups add some independent power: Lattice found pull-ups (R=0.535, R²=0.286 with grade【62†L387-L395】) are weaker than finger hangs (R=0.660), yet still significant.  No published study has given a full multivariate regression with standardized betas including both factors, but finger strength routinely dominates and pull-up strength adds modest independent variance.  
- **Community norms:** Advice varies. Some climbers cite high numbers: e.g. *“I can do +15kg at 70kg BW”* equated to ~85% BW; others claim elites can do double bodyweight. One redditor noted Lattice’s stance that >60% BW added is “diminishing returns”【60†L130-L134】. A Climbing magazine survey (n=287) found no clear pull-up thresholds by grade: climbers with the hardest grades (5.14) spanned from ~10 to 60+ pull-ups【93†L62-L71】.  Lattice’s internal data: mean 2RM pull-up is ~127% BW for 7a–7b climbers, rising to ~167% for ~9a climbers【62†L432-L436】.  In practice, some recommend ~+20–40% BW for intermediate climbers, >+60% for elites, but these are folk estimates.  
- **Relative importance:** Consensus is that pull-up strength helps but is secondary to finger strength.  Experts note finger strength limits high-end; pull-ups matter more at moderate levels or for long routes. The Climbing.com analysis concludes “there is little indication that doing more pull-ups will directly result in greater success”【93†L62-L71】【93†L117-L125】 – echoing research showing weak correlations.

## 3. The “9C Test” (Magnus Midtbø) and similar protocols  
- **Protocol:** The “9C Test” (Midtbø et al.) consists of four exercises:  (1) max-weight 5s hang on a 20 mm edge (two arms, half-crimp)【67†L18-L26】; (2) 1-rep max weighted pull-up【67†L23-L31】; (3) core strength (holding a bent-knee L-sit, full L-sit, or front lever as long as possible)【67†L23-L31】; (4) static dead-hang time on a pull-up bar (maximum seconds without grip change)【67†L27-L31】.  It outputs a composite score mapping to an estimated redpoint grade. OpenClimbing’s calculator describes it and shows example norms (e.g. 20+ s L-sit ~7a, 1 min hang ~6c)【67†L18-L31】. The test is popularized by a YouTube video but has no published validity studies.  
- **Empirical validation:** We found no peer-reviewed study validating the 9C battery against actual climbing grades.  It is entirely **community-derived**. Each component has partial support: finger and pull-up strength (Sections 1–2) correlate with grade, and forearm endurance (hang time) relates to route endurance. Core strength’s role is less documented.  Some climbers have informally “tracked” their 9C scores vs grade on forums, but no formal data.  Anecdotally, high-level climbers easily max the finger and hang components (e.g. many V10s can hang 200% BW; one user noted V10s doing 200% BW on 9C hang, arguing the test would not discriminate at that level【66†L159-L168】). Overall, the 9C test is **folklore** – a plausibility-based toolkit but unvalidated.  
- **Comparison to Lattice battery:**  The 9C’s first two exercises (finger hang, pull-up) mirror Lattice’s key tests (Max Hang, My Lift).  Lattice emphasizes relative finger/pull strength and also includes a critical-force endurance measure.  9C’s core and fixed-hang are unique – Lattice does not include an L-sit or pure hang endurance test in its standard profiling.  Conversely, Lattice uses a forearm “repeaters” or CF test to gauge anaerobic endurance, which 9C omits.  Both approaches agree finger/pull strength are primary; 9C adds core and static endurance “for fun”【64†L163-L170】【67†L18-L31】.  
- **Limitations:** 9C ignores nearly all climbing-specific factors: technique, footwork, movement economy, route-reading, flexibility, psychological composure, and dynamic power.  Hooper (Hooper’s Beta) notes that many strong climbers (able to pass 9C scores) still fail technical crimpy problems【64†L163-L170】【66†L202-L206】.  The endurance hang (#4) mostly tests finger pump tolerance and willpower, not route pacing.  Thus, 9C is “maxed out” by relatively modest ability: even intermediate climbers might satisfy its raw strength parts.  It likely has **no predictive validity** at lower grades (where technique matters) and saturates at higher grades (ceiling effect). 

## 4. Aerobic capacity, forearm endurance, and lactate threshold  
- **Forearm endurance:**  Sustained finger-endurance tests are relevant to route climbing.  Critical Force (CF) — the asymptote of repeated-hang work—has been proposed as an endurance marker. Baláš *et al.* (2024) used a 4-min intermittent hang test: they found the “minimum force” from the end of the test (CF_min) correlated with climbing level (r≈0.704) slightly better than the traditional CF metric (r≈0.661)【82†L82-L89】. However, Lattice’s analysis found CF (from repeaters) had very low predictive power (R=0.401, R²≈0.16) with route grade【62†L409-L417】.  Overall evidence is mixed: climbers certainly need more local muscular endurance than non-climbers, but few cross-sectional studies isolate endurance’s unique effect.  MacLeod *et al.* (2007) showed climbers outperform non-climbers on intermittent hang endurance.   
- **Aerobic capacity (VO₂, LT):**  Direct links to climbing grade are unclear. Climbs involve brief bouts of high intensity, with VO₂ generally low-to-moderate (Watts *et al.* 2000 found climbers rarely hit VO₂max, instead showing rising lactate ~7–10 mmol/L during routes).  Saul *et al.* (2019) emphasize “aerobic forearm capacity” as important【78†L1-L4】, but no large study reports, say, VO₂max vs grade.  We found **no evidence** of significant correlations between whole-body VO₂max or systemic lactate threshold and climbing performance.  Those are **biologically plausible** predictors (higher LT could delay pump), but empirical support is scant.  One likely reason: climbing performance is more limited by local factors.  
- **Community views:** Climbers typically say endurance matters more on long routes, while raw strength rules for bouldering.  For example, some coaches use a “glass of strength” analogy: once you have ‘enough’ strength, endurance becomes limiting.  However, concrete values are seldom given.  Lattice’s data (Table above) suggest that grip endurance (e.g. repeat hangs) adds little extra prediction once max strength is accounted for【62†L387-L395】.  In training lore, “power endurance” is often prescribed (<10%-of-training) except when preparing for endurance-specific projects.

## 5. Anthropometrics and body composition  
- **Evidence:**  Low body mass and high lean fraction are believed advantageous, but studies paint a nuanced picture.  Ozimek *et al.* found no significant differences between elite and advanced climbers in body fat% (11.9 vs 10.3%, *p*=0.48) or lean mass (46.7 vs 48.5 kg, *p*=0.39)【35†L320-L329】.  Ape index (arm span/height) also did not differ significantly (1.03 vs 1.00, *p*=0.38)【35†L320-L329】.  Fanchini *et al.* reported body weight negatively correlated with bouldering (r=–0.49)【10†L1420-L1424】, consistent with heavier climbers doing slightly worse when strength isn’t proportionally higher.  Lattice’s big-data analysis (8a.nu logs) shows virtually **no correlation** between BMI and best grade across thousands of climbers【97†L169-L175】.  In regressions (e.g. Laffaye *et al.* 2019 review), anthropometric factors (body mass, BMI) often enter modestly but explain only part of variance (Laffaye et al. reported ~64% of variance by combining strength + body).   
- **Ape index:**  Although often touted (long reach=advantage), published data do not show a clear effect when controlling for strength.  The Ozimek study (above) found no ape-index difference between groups.  We found no regression isolating ape index vs grade.  Likely, any benefit is small or masked by training.  
- **Height:**  Taller height can help reach but also adds mass.  In the 8a.nu analysis, taller climbers tended to grade slightly lower on average【105†L300-L307】; this may reflect the weight penalty of height.  No controlled study of height vs performance was found.  
- **Body mass:**  The role of weight is complex. Short-term weight loss can help when above strength plateau, but systematic low weight for its own sake is not strongly supported.  Lattice found that most top climbers still fall in “healthy” BMI range【97†L169-L175】.  Community debate (“weight cutting”) remains unsettled.  
- **Finger morphology:**  Few studies.  One recent MRI study of pulleys found climbers have thicker A2 pulleys than non-climbers (to handle strain), but that was a **result** of climbing, not a predictive trait.  No evidence links finger tendon size to innate performance.  
- **Community beliefs:** Climbers often claim an ideal body: very lean (6–10% body fat), low weight, and ape index ≥1.05. Anecdotes on forums suggest “sub-8% fat for men” or “+10 cm ape index is awesome,” but these are folklore.  As Lattice’s large-n analysis shows, BMI varies widely even at top grades【97†L169-L175】, so extreme body shaming or weight obsession is not evidence-based.

## 6. Other physical qualities studied as predictors  
- **Shoulder and upper-body strength:** Mixed evidence. Some small studies (Wong *et al.*) show climbers have stronger shoulder rotators than non-climbers, but correlations with grade are unclear.  Mermier *et al.* (2000s) reported advanced climbers have greater shoulder endurance and hip mobility than intermediates【75†L411-L415】.  The systematic review notes a high correlation (r≈0.77) in one report between shoulder concentric strength and climbing grade【98†L31-L39】, but details are sparse.  In practice, shoulder/back strength is necessary for many moves but seems to contribute less to max grade once finger strength is available.  
- **Hip flexibility/strength:** Grant *et al.* found advanced climbers had significantly better hip flexibility than recreational climbers【75†L411-L415】.  Limited data suggest core/hip strength can help high steps and compression moves, but no effect sizes are reported.  
- **Core strength:** Surprisingly little controlled evidence.  The 9C test assumes core (L-sit) matters, but we found no journal study correlating core endurance/tests to grade.  It’s plausible (core stabilizes body on steep routes), but absent direct data, any claim is **bioplausible** only. Some coaches rank core third after fingers/pull-ups for advanced climbing, but this is anecdotal.  
- **Lower-body strength:** Generally considered non-limiting for climbing; studies focus more on flexibility.  We found no study linking leg strength (e.g. squat) to climb performance. Lattice’s analysis and reviews do not list any effect of leg strength.  Climbers rarely train heavy legs, suggesting little payoff at climbing task.  
- **Training experience:** Years climbing or climbing-specific training volume often correlate with grade, but this is mostly biographical. One large study (Magiera *et al.* review) found years of climbing was a strong predictor in regressions【75†L399-L407】, but again this is intertwined with accumulated skill/strength.  We lack pure tests of “years” in prospective design.  
- **Balance/proprioception:** Almost no research. It is assumed good balance helps, but studies of static balance (standing on one leg, etc.) show at best weak links to climbing ability. The systematic review cites balance under tactical-cognitive factors, but no effect sizes are given. Likely bioplausible (balance matters on slabs) but minor.

## 7. Multivariate models: best predictor combos  
- **Regression studies:** A few reports combine variables.  Laffaye *et al.* (reviewing R² from older studies) note up to ~64% of variance explained by combining strength and body metrics【75†L407-L410】.  Magiera *et al.* (review) report models explaining ~58–77% variance with mixed factors (strength, endurance, anthropometry, mental)【75†L401-L409】.  However, these often pool very diverse factors.  No single paper provides a transparent multivariate model with all betas.  Lattice’s own multivariate model (presentation) suggests finger strength is the top beta, with pull-ups and BMI smaller independent effects.  
- **Machine learning/clustering:** No formal ML on large climber datasets was found.  There are amateur efforts (e.g. a Reddit user modeling combined lead/boulder comps via Elo【100†L11-L19】, but not peer-reviewed).  The domain is ripe for ML, but not yet realized in literature.  
- **Explained variance:**  Even the best combinations leave much unaccounted: ~20–40% of ability remains unexplained by physical tests in these studies. Likely missing are technical skill, problem-solving, psychological factors (fear, persistence), and training quality.  
- **Unmeasured factors:**  Most authors note “technique/skill and psychological factors” as key gaps. For example, Hooper’s Beta emphasizes that strength tests cannot capture movement efficiency or mental composure.  We concur: differences in experience, movement skill, fear response, visual-spatial problem solving, and injury history likely account for much of the unexplained variance.  

## 8. Statistical estimators and probabilistic models of grades  
- **Elo/IRT/Rasch in climbing:**  There is scant formal academic work applying these to climbing. One preprint (Villatoro-Paz 2025) proposes an *adapted Elo* for elite bouldering【100†L0-L3】, but it’s not peer-reviewed.  Reddit posts mention Elo-like scoring for routes (treating holds as opponents)【100†L10-L17】, but these are hobby projects.  We found no published use of Item Response Theory or Rasch models to estimate climber ability vs route difficulty.  
- **Grade estimation:**  Some data-driven approaches exist.  For example, Vertical-Life’s news blog discusses grade adjustments based on large logs (8a.nu improving grading consistency)【101†L1-L9】.  “Sendage” (a training site) has community analytics but no accessible models.  One can say probabilistic models are virtually non-existent in the literature; climbing grades remain largely empirical/consensus-based rather than statistically derived.  
- **Big-data studies:**  The 8a.nu data analyses (Mark Dodd on Medium, Lattice BMI study) illustrate trends (e.g. BMI vs grade【105†L270-L278】【97†L169-L175】) but aren’t predictive models per se.  No paper we saw tries to predict a climber’s future best from past logbook data.  The Terrain (8a) data confirms known trends (e.g. distribution skewed to 5.11c–5.13d【102†L163-L172】) but not “performance prediction”.  
- **Community data science:** A number of blogs have regressed personal metrics vs grade (e.g. BMI, hang times)【105†L270-L278】.  Lattice’s blog is essentially a community data analysis citing R² for predictors【62†L387-L395】.  Reddit threads also share regression lines for, e.g., max hang vs grade, but these are anecdotal.  All such efforts provide intuition, not robust models.  
- **Summary:** There is **no established probabilistic rating system** for climbing grades akin to chess Elo or item-response.  Most “data science” in climbing is blogs and forums using 8a.nu/Sendage logs; it confirms many intuitive correlations (leaner climbers, stronger hangs at higher grades) but remains largely exploratory.  No peer-reviewed predictive model of performance has been validated.

## 9. Test batteries in research and practice  
- **Research batteries:**  Giles *et al.* (2006) proposed a broad test battery (strength, power, endurance, flexibility) but did not publish performance norms.  Recently, Vollans *et al.* (2023 systematic review) catalog 63 different tests, noting no standard battery【75†L384-L393】【75†L469-L477】.  Some studies use composite scores (e.g. combining hang time, pull-ups, bench), but no single validated “climbing fitness test” is agreed upon.  Most lab studies simply pick 1–3 tests per study (e.g. finger hang, pull-ups, lactate threshold, specific strength tests).   
- **Lattice athlete profiling:**  Lattice Training offers “assessment” tools (My Fingers, My Lift, My Pull, etc.) with proprietary norms. Their public blog shares some norms (see §1–2).  The core components are finger hangs on 20 mm, weighted finger hangs (MXEdge Lift), pull-up strength, pinch strength, and some flexibility tests【71†L205-L214】【71†L221-L230】. Lattice’s data (n~thousands) is cited in blogs but the raw battery isn’t formally published in a journal.  
- **9C test:**  Already described above.  It’s purely community.  No official norms or validation exist beyond enthusiasts’ anecdotes【64†L163-L170】【67†L18-L31】.  
- **Giles et al. (2006) protocol:**  This old JSCR paper tested finger strength (23 mm hang), grip endurance (AFBR), arm strength (pull-ups), push strength (bench), and VO₂max on treadmills.  It reported that lead climbers had better endurance and finger strength, while boulderers had more power, but sample was small.  Values: e.g. elite boulderers could do ~15 pull-ups and bench ~100 kg, whereas lead climbers less.  (We did not find their effect sizes; it’s often cited qualitatively.)  
- **Other community batteries:**  There are spreadsheets/apps online where climbers self-record hangs, pull-ups, etc.  Many gyms offer “climbing fitness tests” mixing hangs, laps, pump boards.  However, none have published validity.  In sum: practice uses a mix of finger hang, weighted hangs, pull-ups, hangs-to-failure, and core strength; research lacks a standardized, validated battery.

## 10. Causation caveats  
- **Correlation ≠ causation:**  All studies cited above are cross-sectional observational.  They show association, not that improving the test will raise grade.  For example, climbers who climb harder have strong fingers, but we cannot assume adding finger strength *causes* grade jumps. This issue is often noted (our systematic review even covers it【69†L1-L10】).  
- **Reverse causation:**  Experienced climbers train more and thus become stronger.  Their high test scores may be **result of** climbing harder, not pre-condition.  Only a few interventions exist (e.g. hangboard training studies) showing strength gain, but even then grade improvement is rarely measured.  
- **Training targets:**  Very few prospective studies link training-induced gains to actual climbing improvements.  Hangboard training can increase hang strength (e.g. Munn et al. RCT), but often climbing grade was not measured.  Thus, we cannot assert that focusing on any one predictor will necessarily produce harder sends, though it is bioplausible for finger strength.  
- **Grade measurement:**  Another caveat: climbing grade is a coarse ordinal scale with much noise.  Self-reported max grades (as in many studies) can vary by 1–2 grades, so even a “perfectly predicted” model might get swamped by grading variability.  

## 11. Open questions  
Current literature leaves many questions unresolved. Examples:  

- **Does the 9C test predict improvement?**  No data exist on whether climbers who achieve higher 9C scores subsequently improve more (prospective prediction).  
- **Predictor importance over time:**  As climbers advance, do limiting factors shift (e.g. does endurance become relatively more important once finger strength is sufficient)? Longitudinal studies could clarify.  
- **Causal training effects:**  Which of these predictors, when trained, actually boost climbing ability? Hangboard RCTs improve hang strength, but do they improve climbing performance? Few controlled trials have measured actual grade outcomes.  
- **Non-physical factors:**  How much do mental skills, technique, and problem-solving ability contribute, and can they be quantified?  
- **Optimal testing protocols:**  Among various finger tests (different grips/holds), which most reliably reflect climbing-specific capacity? Existing work hints at differences but no consensus test.  

*(This review collates all available evidence we could find; gaps and “lack of data” statements above indicate where the literature is silent.)*
