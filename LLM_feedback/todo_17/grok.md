**1. Finger strength as a predictor of climbing grade**

Maximal finger strength (MFS), typically measured as isometric force or hang time on small edges (e.g., 15–22 mm, half-crimp or open grip), shows the strongest and most consistent association with climbing grade among physical metrics. In a 2025 study of 19 male climbers (redpoint 6b–8c), climbing-specific distal finger strength (hanging from a 22 mm edge) correlated very strongly with bouldering performance (r = 0.89, explaining ~79% variance) and strongly with redpoint grade (r = 0.67, ~45% variance); handgrip strength was only moderate for redpoint (r = 0.54) and unrelated to bouldering. A 2025 camp4humanperformance analysis found maximal strength (no arm fixation) the best predictor: r² = 0.48 (sport climbing) and r² = 0.66 (bouldering), outperforming endurance tests.

Langer et al. (2023) systematic review of performance testing reported middle-to-high correlations (r = 0.58–0.84 overall; up to r = 0.90–0.93 for max hang time in some studies) between isometric finger maximum strength/endurance and climbing ability across intermediate-to-elite samples; force-on-hold tests explained 56–73% variance in some multivariate contexts. Relative MFS (force or % bodyweight) consistently outperforms absolute MFS because climbing is bodyweight-dependent; body mass showed moderate negative associations in the Buraas study (r = −0.49 bouldering).

Half-crimp generally shows stronger predictive validity than open-hand for most grades, though both are used; Lattice profiling standardizes on 20 mm edges in half-crimp or open. Edge-depth specificity favors 18–22 mm edges in profiling; shallower edges (e.g., 15 mm) discriminate elites better in some force-on-hold tests. In multivariate models (see section 7), MFS often emerges as the top independent predictor, outperforming pull-ups or anthropometrics.

**Community benchmarks (folklore):** Lattice Training data associate higher %BW on 7–10 s max hangs (20 mm edge) with grade bands (e.g., advanced V4–V8 ~123% BW; elite V9–V12 ~151% BW; higher-elite V13+ ~183% BW; overall R = 0.704, R² = 0.496 for bouldering). r/climbharder and coaching blogs frequently cite “~1.5–2× BW on 20 mm” for V8+ or “100% BW one-arm hang” for elite; these align with Lattice norms but remain cross-sectional. Lattice “minimum edge” tests (smallest edge for 7–10 s hang at bodyweight) are used for profiling and training prescription; no universal published norms exist beyond their internal models.

All data are **Evidence-backed** (peer-reviewed cross-sectional; sample sizes 19–900+; self-reported or verified grades) except community thresholds (**FOLKLORE_VERIFIED** via ≥3 sources: Lattice, r/climbharder, coaching blogs).

**2. Pull-up strength and lock-off strength as predictors**

Weighted pull-up 1RM or 2RM (%BW) shows moderate correlations with grade but weaker than finger strength. Lattice sport-climbing data (N=420): 2RM pull-up %BW R = 0.535, R² = 0.286 overall; pull-ups strongest at advanced levels (7a–7b+, R = 0.295) but drop behind finger strength at elite/higher-elite. Bouldering data (N=901): pulling strength R = 0.582, R² = 0.337 overall, significant at advanced/elite but not higher-elite. Devise et al. (2023) found 1RM pull-up r = 0.45 with IRCRA redpoint level (force-dominant parameters correlated; velocity not).

Relative (%BW) pull-up strength predicts better than absolute due to bodyweight demands. In multivariate models, pull-ups add modest independent variance beyond finger strength (e.g., Power Company heatmaps: max hang + weighted pull-up combo strongest overall). Isometric lock-offs (e.g., bent-arm hang) correlate moderately (r = 0.23–>0.80).

**Community benchmarks (folklore):** Lattice norms: advanced ~128% BW 2RM; elite ~150% BW; higher-elite ~166% BW. r/climbharder and Power Company data suggest +30–50% BW 1RM for V7–V8, with diminishing returns past V8–V10 (“finger strength matters more past V7”). Practitioner consensus: pull-ups secondary to fingers at high grades (**FOLKLORE_VERIFIED**; ≥3 sources including Lattice, Mountain Project, UKC forums).

**Evidence-backed** (cross-sectional regressions); few interventions test causation directly.

**3. The 9c test (Magnus Midtbø) and Community Protocols**

**Protocol (folklore):** Developed by Stian Christophersen & Martin Mobråten, popularized by Magnus Midtbø. Four components scored 1–10 points each (total 4–40 maps to approximate sport grade):
- Unweighted bar dead-hang (time-to-failure; e.g., 6 pts = 3 min, 10 pts = 6 min).
- 1RM weighted pull-up (%BW or added load).
- 5-second weighted dead-hang on 20 mm edge (added load %BW).
- Core test (knee raises / L-sit / bar plank scaled by level).
Total score predicts grade (e.g., 40 = 9c; 30 = 8c; 20 = 7c+).

**Evidence evaluation:** No peer-reviewed validation study exists linking the full battery to actual climbing grade (searches returned zero controlled trials). Individual components are supported (finger strength, pull-ups; see sections 1–2), but the composite formula is untested. Community attempts (r/climbharder data projects) show finger strength as the strongest single predictor within the battery, with pull-ups second; core/endurance lag.

**Comparison with Lattice:** 9c overlaps on finger strength (20 mm weighted hang) and pull-ups; Lattice adds power endurance (60% load intermittent hangs), flexibility, and critical force. Lattice uses regression models for profiling (R² up to 0.50 for fingers); 9c is a simple additive score without validated weighting. Lattice is broader and data-driven; 9c is narrower and folklore-based.

**Limitations and critique:** Ignores technique, footwork, movement efficiency, route-reading, fear management, flexibility, and aerobic forearm capacity. Predictive validity likely weakens below ~7c where technique dominates and at elite levels where specificity matters more. All **Folklore** (no peer-reviewed validation; community calculators and videos only); components individually **Evidence-backed**.

**4. Aerobic capacity, forearm endurance, and lactate threshold as predictors**

Forearm endurance (time-to-failure at fixed %MFS or intermittent hangs) and critical force (CF = highest sustainable intermittent force without fatigue) correlate moderately-to-strongly with route (endurance) performance but weakly with bouldering. Giles/Fryer all-out tests: CF %BW explained ~60% variance in sport grade (R² ≈ 0.60); ff-CF %BW R² = 0.61 sport vs. 0.26 bouldering. Baláš 2024 validation: CF min (end-force) R = 0.704 with ability. Langer review: intermittent finger endurance r = 0.51–0.78; climbing-specific cardiorespiratory endurance high validity.

VO₂max and lactate threshold show weaker or inconsistent links (arm-crank/row ergometry r = 0.20–0.85; cycle ergometer less specific). Blood lactate accumulation during hangs predicts endurance better than gross CF in some NIRS studies.

**Community discussion:** Endurance becomes limiting above ~7c–8a on routes (“strength for boulders, endurance for routes”); Lattice CF data show 70% increase from 5.11– to 5.13+. **Evidence-backed** for CF/forearm endurance in routes (cross-sectional + some validation); VO₂max/lactate **Bioplausible** (mechanism via aerobic forearm capacity, but climbing-specific data limited).

**5. Anthropometrics and body composition**

Anthropometrics explain little variance once strength/endurance are controlled. Langer review and multiple studies: height, weight, BMI, ape index show weak/non-significant correlations after adjustment (e.g., <1% variance in Mermier et al.; ape index r ≈ 0–0.40, often non-significant). Lower body mass/lean mass favors elites (negative r with grade in some datasets), but causation unclear. Finger morphology (e.g., A2 pulley) unstudied as predictor.

**Community/folklore:** Widespread belief in positive ape index (+ advantage) and “lighter = better”; weight-cutting common but evidence shows no benefit for non-elites (trainable strength/endurance dominate). **Evidence-backed** (weak/null correlations); folklore beliefs contradicted by data (**TO_VERIFY**).

**6. Other physical qualities studied as predictors**

- **Shoulder strength (push/pull ratio):** Moderate correlations (pull-dominant); no strong independent predictor in multivariate.
- **Hip flexor strength/flexibility:** Foot-raise tests r = 0.53–0.95; climbing-specific flexibility contributes in some models.
- **Core strength:** Weak/mixed (e.g., fishing kicks r = −0.12 to −0.42); not retained in most regressions.
- **Lower-body strength:** Low correlation (CMJ r = 0.23–0.33); negligible vs. upper-body metrics.
- **Years climbing/technique proxies:** Strong predictor in experience-adjusted models (technique accounts for much unexplained variance).
- **Balance/proprioception:** Limited data; postural stability noted in some reviews but unquantified.

All **Evidence-backed** via cross-sectional studies (Langer review synthesis); sample sizes vary, grades intermediate–elite.

**7. Multivariate models: what combination of variables best predicts grade?**

Trainable variables (finger/upper-limb strength + endurance + flexibility/power) explain 58.9–77% variance:
- Mermier et al.: 58.9% (strength/endurance + lower-limb power + flexibility).
- Laffaye et al.: 64% (upper-limb/finger strength + anthropometrics).
- Magiera et al.: 77% (including mental endurance + anthropometrics).
Lattice regressions: fingers + pull-ups explain ~43–50% in sport/bouldering; adding CF/endurance improves models modestly. No large ML/clustering studies published; citizen-science ML on self-reported data shows strength-to-weight hangs + pull-ups top for bouldering, endurance metrics for sport.

**Gap:** 23–41% unexplained variance attributed to technique, movement efficiency, fear management, tactics, route-reading, and psychological factors (unmeasured in physical batteries).

**8. Statistical Estimators and Probabilistic Models of Grades**

Academic work uses Item Response Theory (IRT, aka “Route Response Theory”) and Bayesian models on 8a.nu/Sendage logs to estimate latent climber ability and route difficulty probabilistically. Drummond (2021) Bayesian model: each grade increment ≈2.1× difficulty (French/Ewbank/UIAA) or 3.17× (V-scale) based on success probability. Rosenthal (2022) IRT model on 8a.nu flash/redpoint data ranks climbers/routes via latent ability vs. difficulty.

Big-data 8a.nu analyses (400k+ ascents) model peak performance trends, age-grade curves, and grade distributions; grades redefined via success-rate statistics (Monte Carlo-like). No widespread Elo system; citizen-science regressions on scraped data correlate metrics with self-reported max grade (e.g., BMI weak).

**Blog-level:** Lattice regressions (R² 0.29–0.50); r/climbharder/StrengthClimbing calculators use multivariate formulas. **Evidence-backed** for IRT/Bayesian (peer-reviewed arXiv + blogs); community regressions **Folklore** or citizen-science.

**9. Test batteries used in research and practice**

- **Lattice athlete profiling:** Components include 7–10 s max hang (20 mm %BW), 2RM pull-up %BW, intermittent power endurance, flexibility; population: 400–900+ climbers; strong validity (R² up to 0.50); internal norms available via My Fingers tool.
- **9c test:** As above; no published validity; community norms via calculators.
- **Giles/IRCRA-related (Draper 2021 / Fryer 2020):** 10-item battery (flexibility, strength, power, endurance); sport-specific; high reliability/validity for key parameters.
- **Community spreadsheets/apps:** StrengthClimbing analyzer, open-climbing 9c calculator; self-reported.

**10. Causation caveats**

All correlations in sections 1–6 are overwhelmingly **cross-sectional** (cannot imply causation; elite climbers train more). Few intervention studies: 5–10 weeks hangboard/dynamic finger training increases MFS but does not always improve bouldering grade (e.g., Saeterbakken 2024: strength ↑ but performance unchanged). Reverse causation likely: higher-grade climbers develop higher test scores via climbing volume. Prospective/intervention evidence strongest for finger strength and CF as training targets, but grade gains require technique integration.

**Open questions the literature cannot yet answer**
1. Does the 9c test (or any battery) prospectively predict *grade improvement over time* (longitudinal training response) rather than current cross-sectional grade?
2. How does relative importance of predictors (finger vs. pull-up vs. CF) shift across the full grade spectrum (e.g., V4–V17 or 5.10–9c)?
3. What percentage of unexplained variance is attributable to technique/movement efficiency vs. psychological factors, and can these be quantified in field tests?
4. Do intervention studies improving CF/forearm endurance causally raise redpoint grade on routes (beyond correlation)?
5. Are there sex- or discipline-specific (bouldering vs. sport) multivariate models that retain different predictors at elite levels?
6. Can machine-learning models on large 8a.nu + test-battery datasets achieve >70% predictive accuracy when including technique proxies?
7. Do structural factors (e.g., tendon insertion, pulley cross-section) set hard ceilings on MFS that training cannot overcome?
