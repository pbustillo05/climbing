Below is an **evidence-tiered, source-dense synthesis** of (i) **statistical/probabilistic estimators of climbing performance** and (ii) **empirical predictors** (finger strength, pulling strength, endurance, anthropometrics, etc.) including **peer‑reviewed literature** and **community “citizen science”**. I’m treating “performance” as: **reported redpoint/onsight grade**, **competition-style hold count**, or **laboratory circuits**—and I flag where **self‑report** vs **objective** outcomes matter.

Important constraint: you required **effect sizes (r/R²/β, 95% CI)** for *every claim* when available. Many climbing papers (especially older ones) report **group differences** and/or **p-values** without r/β/CIs; in those cases I (a) explicitly label the missing statistics and (b) cite the paper anyway.

I. Evidence tier rubric used here
- **EVIDENCE‑BACKED** = peer‑reviewed quantitative study with appropriate methods; includes cross-sectional correlation, prospective prediction, or intervention. (Cross-sectional ≠ causation.)
- **BIOPLAUSIBLE** = mechanistic plausibility + indirect evidence; no direct controlled evidence linking metric→grade.
- **FOLKLORE** = coach/community protocols or blog regressions; may be “verified” by multiple independent community sources but still not peer‑reviewed.

1) Finger strength as a predictor of climbing grade
------------------------------------------------------------

1.1 Cross-sectional validity: maximal isometric finger strength (MIFS/MIFS%) vs grade

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (large N, direct grade correlation)
- In a large criterion-validity dataset (n = 229 intermediate→higher-elite), **relative** maximal isometric finger strength (pulley add/remove load, ~20 mm edge) correlated with climbing performance at **r ≈ 0.42–0.50** (self‑reported performance; authors report higher agreement when strength is expressed as % body mass). [10.1519/JSC.0000000000003548 | Torr et al. 2022 | Reliability and Validity of a Method for the Assessment of Sport Rock Climbers’ Isometric Finger Strength]

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (peer‑reviewed field + synthesis)
- A 2025 systematic review of finger-strength assessment reliability concludes that fixed edges **~20–23 mm** show consistently high reliability and should be prioritized for standardized monitoring; it also states that MIFS is a key determinant of climbing performance and is often stronger when expressed relative to body mass (this is largely a **synthesis claim**, not a single new correlation). [URL | Pérez‑Cordero et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing: a systematic review] (PubMed entry: [URL | Pérez‑Cordero et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing: a systematic review])

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (very high explained variance—but note outcome definition)
- A 2025 European Journal of Applied Physiology paper reports that climbing‑specific forearm/finger strength can explain **up to ~80%** of performance variation under a specific design: climbers given time to practice standardized boulders/routes (reducing tactical/route-reading noise). Exact regression coefficients/CIs need extraction from full text; the abstract/conclusion emphasizes the “up to 80%” variance explained and the need for climbing‑specific edge testing. [DOI unverified | (Authors not captured in the snippet) 2025 | Climbing performance in males: the importance of climbing-specific finger strength] (Springer landing page) [URL | (Authors not captured in snippet) 2025 | Climbing performance in males: the importance of climbing-specific finger strength] (PMC full text)

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (older review-level summaries)
- Reviews consistently identify **finger flexor strength** and **strength-to-body-mass** as central to harder climbing, while emphasizing multi-factor performance (technique, tactics, psychology). [10.1007/s00421-003-1036-7 | Watts 2004 | Physiology of difficult rock climbing]
- Another foundational review similarly highlights high relative strength and repeated isometric demands. [10.2165/00007256-200636060-00006 | Giles et al. 2006 | The physiology of rock climbing]

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (between-group differences consistent with “predictor” framing but not correlation)
- Elite vs advanced climbers differ on finger strength and arm endurance in a small sample (n ≈ 20 total). This supports discrimination but does not provide an r/β. [URL | (Authors not shown in snippet) 2017 | The role of physique, strength and endurance in the achievements of elite climbers] (PubMed/PMC pages: [URL | (Authors not shown in snippet) 2017 | The role of physique, strength and endurance in the achievements of elite climbers])

1.2 Relative MIFS (%BW) vs absolute MIFS: which predicts better?

EVIDENCE‑BACKED
- Torr et al. explicitly report better agreement when finger strength is expressed relative to body mass; correlations reported as r = 0.421–0.503 for the “% body mass” expressions (exact mapping to performance sub-measures requires full text, but direction is clear). [10.1519/JSC.0000000000003548 | Torr et al. 2022 | Reliability and Validity of a Method for the Assessment of Sport Rock Climbers’ Isometric Finger Strength]

BIOPLAUSIBLE mechanism (why relative beats absolute)
- Climbing is essentially repeated bouts of producing force against gravity; **net usable force margin** on small holds depends on (finger force capacity)/(system weight). Thus relative finger strength should map more directly to difficulty, especially on steep terrain where hand loading is high. This is a mechanistic argument consistent with review framing but not a controlled causal proof. [10.1007/s00421-003-1036-7 | Watts 2004 | Physiology of difficult rock climbing] + [10.2165/00007256-200636060-00006 | Giles et al. 2006 | The physiology of rock climbing]

1.3 Grip positions (half‑crimp vs open hand) and predictive validity

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (discriminative physiology)
- Boulder vs lead climbers differ in finger MVC and especially RFD when tested in “crimp” and “open-crimp” positions; RFD showed the largest between-discipline difference (34–38%). This is discipline discrimination rather than grade correlation. [10.1519/JSC.0b013e3182577026 | Fanchini et al. 2013 | Differences in Climbing-Specific Strength Between Boulder and Lead Rock Climbers]
- The 2025 finger-strength reliability systematic review notes half‑crimp as an important performance‑relevant grip position and summarizes reliability of multiple grips; however, “predictive validity” across grips is not consistently quantified with r/β across all included papers. [URL | Pérez‑Cordero et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing: a systematic review]

1.4 Edge depth specificity (18 mm vs 20 mm vs etc.)

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (reliability strongest around 20–23 mm; not necessarily “highest correlation”)
- A systematic review indicates fixed edges of approximately **20–23 mm** show high reliability and should be prioritized, implying a pragmatic standard for monitoring and between-study comparability. [URL | Pérez‑Cordero et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing: a systematic review]
- Lattice’s public testing ecosystem also standardizes around **20 mm** as a global benchmark for comparison (not peer‑reviewed). [https://latticetraining.com/product/my-fingers/ | Lattice Training | My Fingers – Free Finger Strength Assessment]

TO_VERIFY (correlation vs edge depth)
- Evidence that a *specific* depth (e.g., 18 mm) yields *higher r with grade* is not yet consolidated in the sources retrieved here. Many studies use 20–25 mm; some test smaller (10–15 mm). A dedicated extraction from each correlation study is required to answer “best correlation depth” definitively.

1.5 Multivariate models: does finger strength outperform other variables?

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (large explained variance but model details depend on full text)
- Baláš et al. (2012) structural equation model (SEM) used a latent “hand–arm strength and endurance” factor (grip strength, bent-arm hang, finger hang) plus body fat, climbing volume, and experience; the proposed model explained **97%** of variance in climbing performance (UIAA grade). This is very high and raises typical SEM cautions (model fit/overfitting, measurement model choices). [10.1080/17461391.2010.546431 | Baláš et al. 2012 | Hand–arm strength and endurance as predictors of climbing performance]

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (multi-factor performance structure)
- A performance-structure paper reports a multivariate model/root explaining **~77%** variance in “overall performance capacity” including **relative finger strength** (loading 0.490), technique (0.370), mental endurance (−0.410), finger endurance, reaction-time errors, ape index, and VO2 at anaerobic threshold during arm work. (This is more “latent performance capacity” than pure grade.) [URL | (Authors not shown in snippet) 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber]

2) Pull-up strength and lock-off strength as predictors
------------------------------------------------------------

2.1 Max pull-ups / bent-arm hang / pulling strength vs grade

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (test battery + prediction model)
- In Baláš et al. (2012), bent-arm hang and related tests are part of the hand–arm strength/endurance latent variable predicting grade (UIAA). It’s not isolated as a single r with grade in the abstract, but it is embedded in the SEM explaining 97% variance. [10.1080/17461391.2010.546431 | Baláš et al. 2012 | Hand–arm strength and endurance as predictors of climbing performance]
- Classic anthropometric/test-battery work shows elite climbers outperform recreational/non-climbers on pull-ups and bent-arm hang; again, this is discrimination not r/β. [10.1080/02640419608727715 | Grant et al. 1996 | Anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of elite and recreational climbers]

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (RFD / maximal force in climbing-specific pull)
- Isometric pull-up maximal force and RFD on a climbing-specific hold distinguish elite from advanced/intermediate; effect sizes reported (ES ~1.77–1.78 for peak force; ES ~1.02–1.58 for RFD depending on time window). This is group discrimination, not a continuous grade regression. [10.1371/journal.pone.0249353 | Stien et al. 2021 | Upper body rate of force development and maximal strength discriminates performance levels in sport climbing]

2.2 Relative vs absolute pulling strength

BIOPLAUSIBLE
- As with finger force, climbing is weight-sensitive; thus relative pulling strength should often map better to performance than absolute, particularly on steep terrain and on powerful sequences. Mechanism: higher force margin reduces time-to-move and increases capacity to hold positions. Supported conceptually by physiology reviews, but not definitively proven via a controlled causal design. [10.1007/s00421-003-1036-7 | Watts 2004 | Physiology of difficult rock climbing] + [10.2165/00007256-200636060-00006 | Giles et al. 2006 | The physiology of rock climbing]

TO_VERIFY (direct r comparison)
- I did not retrieve a paper in this pass that directly reports “1RM weighted pull-up/BW vs grade” alongside “absolute 1RM vs grade” with r/β and CI. This likely exists in niche strength testing papers, but needs targeted searching by “weighted pull-up” + “climbing grade” + “correlation”.

2.3 Does pulling add independent variance beyond finger strength?

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (indirect)
- The “hand–arm strength and endurance” latent factor in Baláš et al. includes grip/finger hang plus bent-arm hang; this suggests combined upper-body + grip endurance predicts grade well, but it doesn’t isolate incremental R² of pull strength over finger strength alone in the abstract. [10.1080/17461391.2010.546431 | Baláš et al. 2012 | Hand–arm strength and endurance as predictors of climbing performance]

FOLKLORE (highly documented in community; origin is media/YouTube ecosystem)

3.3 Are the four components individually supported as predictors?

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (component support)
- **Finger strength** has moderate-to-strong correlations with performance in large datasets (e.g., r ~0.42–0.50). [10.1519/JSC.0000000000003548 | Torr et al. 2022 | Reliability and Validity of a Method for the Assessment of Sport Rock Climbers’ Isometric Finger Strength]
- **Upper-body pulling force/RFD** differentiates higher levels (elite vs advanced/intermediate). [10.1371/journal.pone.0249353 | Stien et al. 2021 | Upper body rate of force development and maximal strength discriminates performance levels in sport climbing]
- **Endurance / economy**: better climbers show markedly better movement economy (lower VO2, HR at given circuit), with strong correlations (r ≈ −0.82 to −0.84 for VO2 vs ability in a submax test). [10.1155/2014/678387 | Baláš et al. 2014 | The relationship between climbing ability and physiological responses to rock climbing]
- **Core/body tension**: evidence is mixed; some profiles find no large group differences in “core endurance” tests in boulderers vs controls, though shoulder endurance differs. [10.1016/j.wem.2010.11.012 | Macdonald & Callender 2011 | Athletic Profile of Highly Accomplished Boulderers]

4) Aerobic capacity, forearm endurance, lactate threshold, Critical Force
------------------------------------------------------------

4.1 Forearm endurance / oxygenation measures

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (finger endurance physiology)
- A classic climbing-specific finger endurance study compares climbers vs non-climbers and uses NIRS measures; climbers have higher MVC (485 N vs 375 N; p = 0.009), but endurance times at 40% MVC were similar between groups in that specific protocol, highlighting that “endurance” measurement is protocol-sensitive. [10.1080/02640410600944550 | MacLeod et al. 2007 | Physiological determinants of climbing-specific finger endurance and sport rock climbing performance]
- Elite climbers show distinct finger flexor performance and forearm oxygenation characteristics compared to non-climbers (details require full-text extraction for r/β). [10.1007/s00421-011-2260-1 | Philippe et al. 2012 | Climbing-specific finger flexor performance and forearm muscle oxygenation in elite male and female sport climbers]

EVIDENCE‑BACKED (movement economy as “endurance proxy”)
- In a controlled circuit test, self-reported ability correlated strongly and negatively with VO2 and HR during submax climbing (r up to ~−0.84), suggesting higher ability = better economy (which effectively improves endurance at a given absolute task). [10.1155/2014/678387 | Baláš et al. 2014 | The relationship between climbing ability and physiological responses to rock climbing]

8.1 Bradley–Terry / probabilistic ascent modeling in the wild

FOLKLORE / BLOG‑DATA‑SCIENCE (but mathematically sound)

BIOPLAUSIBLE (why Bradley–Terry makes sense for climbing)

10) Causation caveats (applies across sections 1–6)
------------------------------------------------------------

10.1 Cross-sectional correlations ≠ causation
EVIDENCE‑BACKED (design fact)
- Most grade–metric relationships in climbing are **cross-sectional** (measure strength/endurance now; correlate with current grade). These cannot determine whether strength causes grade or whether higher-grade climbers simply trained more (reverse causation) and/or self-select into testing. This limitation is inherent across the cited correlational/discriminative studies. [10.1519/JSC.0000000000003548 | Torr et al. 2022 | Reliability and Validity of a Method for the Assessment of Sport Rock Climbers’ Isometric Finger Strength] + [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]

10.2 Interventions: improving predictors → improving grade
EVIDENCE‑BACKED (training literature exists but is sparse for grade outcomes)
- Hangboard/finger training interventions commonly improve finger strength and endurance metrics; whether they reliably improve outdoor grade is harder because grade is noisy and context-dependent. A systematic review on strength training in climbing exists but extracting “grade change effect sizes” requires full-text review and may show limited direct grade endpoints. [10.1519/JSC.0000000000004286 | Langer et al. 2023 | Strength Training in Climbing: A Systematic Review]
