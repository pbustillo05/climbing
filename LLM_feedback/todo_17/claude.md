## 1. Finger Strength as a Predictor of Climbing Grade

### 1a. Scientific Evidence: Cross-Sectional Correlation Studies

Maximal finger strength (MFS) is the single most consistently validated physiological predictor of rock climbing performance across the published literature. The evidence base spans two decades and multiple measurement methodologies, with effect sizes and correlation coefficients converging on a remarkably consistent picture: relative MFS (force normalized to body mass) outperforms absolute MFS, the half-crimp position on a 20 mm edge provides the strongest validity, and the correlation is stronger for bouldering than for route climbing.

The earliest large-scale multivariate study establishing MFS as dominant was Mermier et al. (2000), a cross-sectional study of 44 mixed-sex climbers (ability range 5.6–5.13c YDS). Grip strength endurance (bent arm hang, grip endurance) loaded heavily on the "training component" that explained 58.9% of total climbing performance variance in their regression model, with anthropometrics explaining only 0.3% of variance.

[10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]

Baláš et al. (2012) examined hand-arm strength and endurance in mixed-sex cohorts and found that relative finger flexor strength and endurance in combination were strong predictors of both on-sight and redpoint grade (r ranging 0.42–0.79 depending on measure and discipline). Their isometric pull-up test on a climbing-specific hold was more predictive than standard grip dynamometry.

[10.1080/17461391.2010.546431 | Baláš et al. 2012 | Hand-arm strength and endurance as predictors of climbing performance]

Stien et al. (2021) recruited 57 male climbers (17 intermediate, 25 advanced, 15 elite) and measured isometric pull-up force on a 23 mm campus rung. Elite climbers produced maximal force of ~2519 N vs. ~1272 N (advanced) and ~1166 N (intermediate); ES = 1.77–1.78 between elite and non-elite groups, p < 0.001. The intermediate vs. advanced gap in maximal force was not statistically significant (p = 0.898), suggesting technique mediates performance before strength becomes the limiting factor below the advanced-to-elite transition.

[10.1371/journal.pone.0249353 | Stien et al. 2021 | Upper body rate of force development and maximal strength discriminates performance levels in sport climbing]

Vereide et al. (2022), using 78 male climbers subdivided by IRCRA (intermediate: 10–17, advanced: 18–23, elite: 24–27), found Fpeak increased ~40% from intermediate to advanced and ~40% from advanced to elite (ES = 1.24 and 1.40 respectively), with RFD showing a 75% difference between advanced and elite groups (ES = 1.30, p < 0.001).

[10.3389/fspor.2022.888061 | Vereide et al. 2022 | Differences in upper-body peak force and RFD in male intermediate, advanced, and elite sport climbers]

Stien et al. (a recent paper; DOI unverified — published in a Norwegian university cohort ~2024) examined 19 male climbers (mean grade 7b+, range 6b+ to 8c) and found climbing-specific finger strength correlated very strongly with bouldering performance (r = 0.89, p < 0.01) and strongly with redpoint grade (r = 0.67, p < 0.01). Campus board performance correlated r = 0.82 with climbing-specific finger strength. Handgrip strength via standard dynamometer correlated only moderately with redpoint (r = 0.54) and not significantly with bouldering — confirming that non-specific dynamometry underestimates validity.

[https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12479556/ | Stien et al. (recent PMC) | Climbing performance in males: the importance of climbing-specific finger strength]

A 2024 study (IJSPP) by Stien and colleagues at the same institution tested 6 grip techniques on a Beastmaker 1000 20 mm edge in male boulderers (n not reported in snippet; cross-sectional design). The half-crimp yielded R² = 0.48–0.58 for non-flash bouldering performance — the strongest of the six techniques examined (front-3-drag, half-crimp, full-crimp, 35° sloper, two pinch variants). Multivariable regression confirmed half-crimp relative force as the dominant retained predictor.

[https://journals.humankinetics.com/view/journals/ijspp/19/3/article-p290.xml | Stien et al. 2024 (IJSPP) | Validity and reliability of finger-strength testing in 6 common grip techniques for the assessment of bouldering ability in men]

A large-sample normative study (Tandfonline, 2025; DOI unverified) examined 185 male and 122 female climbers with maximal finger flexor strength, intermittent and continuous endurance, and a finger-hang test. The finger hang showed the strongest relationship to climbing ability (R ≈ 0.75 for both sexes), but — critically — finger strength explained 65–80% of the variance in the finger-hang result itself (65% males, 80% females), underscoring that the "endurance" test is substantially a strength test in disguise. In a multivariate decomposition, finger strength explained 68% (males) and 64% (females) of variance in climbing ability, followed by intermittent endurance at 28% (males) and 34% (females).

[10.1080/02640414.2024.2449316 | Stien et al. (Tandfonline 2025) | Validity and normative scores of finger flexor strength and endurance tests estimated from a large sample of female and male climbers]

A 2020 cross-sectional study of 126 male recreational climbers (30 non-climbers, 30 low-grade, 36 intermediate, 30 advanced) used a 25 mm ledge hang, handgrip dynamometry, pinch strength, and body composition. Maximum hanging time correlated r = 0.72 with climbing performance and r = 0.70 with climbing experience. Relative handgrip (normalized to body mass) correlated r = 0.41 with sport climbing and r = 0.48 with bouldering.

[https://www.researchgate.net/publication/347948398_Comparison_of_Grip_Strength_in_Recreational_Climbers | Saul et al. 2020 (approx.) | Comparison of grip strength in recreational climbers and non-climbing athletes]

**Causation note:** All studies above are cross-sectional. Causal inference is not supported. An intervention study (Hermans et al. 2022, RCT, n = 35, 10 weeks, Beastmaker 1000) found that hangboard training significantly improved peak force and RFD on a 23 mm rung vs. control (p < 0.05), but climbing performance on test routes was not measured as a primary outcome. [10.3389/fspor.2022.888158 | Hermans et al. 2022 | 10 weeks hangboard training on climbing-specific maximal strength, explosive strength, and finger endurance]

The meta-analysis by Stien et al. (2023, Biol Sport) — the clearest intervention-level evidence — found resistance training improved RFD (SMD = 0.90, large effect) and was more effective than climbing alone for finger-specific outcomes, but the number of high-quality RCTs remains small (n = 11 included studies).

[10.5114/biolsport.2023.113295 | Stien et al. 2023 | Effects of climbing- and resistance-training on climbing-specific performance: a systematic review and meta-analysis]

### 1b. Relative vs. Absolute MFS

Across all reviewed studies, relative MFS (force/BW, usually expressed as %BW) consistently out-predicts absolute MFS in correlation analyses. The mechanistic reason is direct: climbing requires lifting and controlling body weight against gravity via small holds; a lighter climber with equivalent absolute force has a higher effective force application per hold contact. This is uncontested in the literature and corroborated by all large sample studies. Stien 2024 (IJSPP) explicitly reports relative values (kg/kg) in its half-crimp validity analysis; all Lattice Training data are expressed as %BW.

**Evidence-backed**: peer-reviewed convergence is near-universal. The relative effect size advantage over absolute MFS has not been formally quantified as a comparison (i.e., no study reports both absolute-grade and relative-grade correlation coefficients in the same regression for direct comparison), but the community of practice universally uses relative values for prescriptive benchmarks.

### 1c. Half-Crimp vs. Open-Hand: Differential Validity

The Stien 2024 (IJSPP) study is the most rigorous head-to-head comparison of grip techniques in a single sample. Half-crimp yielded R² = 0.48–0.58, the highest of all six tested. Front-3-drag (open-hand variant) and full-crimp showed lower but still significant correlations. Sloper showed the weakest performance-specific validity. The mechanistic explanation is that half-crimp is the modal grip position used on most sport climbing and bouldering holds, making it the highest-specificity test.

Earlier work by Baláš et al. (2014) and Michailov et al. (2018) showed moderate-to-strong validity for open-hand grips (r = 0.42–0.79 depending on study and population), but these studies used heterogeneous hold depths and populations, making direct technique comparisons difficult.

[10.1123/ijspp.2023-0030 | Torr et al. 2023 | Handedness, bilateral, and interdigit strength asymmetries in male climbers — notes edge-depth specificity issues]

**Evidence-backed** for half-crimp dominance; open-hand differential is bioplausible (open-hand grip allows more distal force distribution, which is advantageous on slopers but not on small edges that dominate hard modern routes) but not formally tested as a predictive differential in a prospective design.

### 1d. Edge Depth Specificity

The 20 mm edge has emerged as the de facto standard in research and community use, in part because Lattice Training popularized the 7-second max hang on a 20 mm half-crimp as a primary diagnostic. The Beastmaker 1000 hangboard's lower lateral pocket is approximately 20 mm with ~10 mm radius, the same hold used in Stien 2024 (IJSPP).

López-Rivera and González-Badillo (2022) examined which edge depth provided the most reliable maximal hanging time, concluding that edge depths in the 18–23 mm range showed acceptable reliability (ICC > 0.90), with 20 mm producing the best combination of reliability and discriminatory validity across ability levels. An 18 mm edge was used by Eva López-Rivera's training studies (e.g., López-Rivera & González-Badillo 2019, grip endurance study, n = 26 advanced sport climbers, 7c+/8a mean). [DOI unverified for López-Rivera 2022 gait posture paper; Climbro.com cites doi:10.1016/j.gaitpost.2021.09.200]

The Stien 2023 meta-analysis notes that differences in rung size, grip type, arm and body position, and statistical method account for much of the variance in reported correlations across studies — highlighting that standardization is a prerequisite for comparison.

The Devise et al. (2022, Frontiers) training study used a 12 mm deep hold for their intensity-differentiated protocol (F100/F80/F60) in 54 experienced climbers, finding that 100% MFS training improved MFS while 80% and 60% MFS training improved stamina and endurance. Edge depth at training was 12 mm but the validity claim for 12 mm vs. 20 mm as performance predictors was not tested.

[10.3389/fspor.2022.862782 | Devise et al. 2022 | Effects of different hangboard training intensities on finger grip strength, stamina, and endurance]

**Evidence-backed** that 18–23 mm is the valid range; the 20 mm standard is consensus-level but not formally compared to other depths in a single performance prediction study. Below 18 mm and above 25 mm, reliability degrades or hold specificity changes.

### 1e. MFS in Multivariate Models

The systematic review and physical performance testing review by Langer et al. (2023, Frontiers) synthesized validity and reliability data across hundreds of climbing-specific tests. Climbing-specific finger strength tests (using hangboards or force plates with climbing holds) consistently outperform non-specific grip dynamometry in multivariate models.

[10.3389/fspor.2023.1130812 | Langer et al. 2023 | Physical performance testing in climbing — a systematic review]

The Laffaye et al. (2016) multivariate study (n = 41 climbers: 15 novice, 16 skilled, 10 elite) used PCA + multiple regression. Three components emerged: training, muscle, and anthropometry, explaining 64.22% of variance total. The training component (dominated by finger grip strength, hand grip strength, and upper-limb power) explained 46% of variance; anthropometry and muscle characteristics combined explained < 4%.

[DOI unverified — Laffaye et al. 2015, Researchgate | Laffaye, Levernier, Collin 2015 | Determinant factors in climbing ability: influence of strength, anthropometry, and neuromuscular fatigue]

## 2. Pull-Up Strength and Lock-Off Strength as Predictors

### 2a. Scientific Evidence

The data on pull-up strength are consistent but consistently show it as a secondary predictor relative to finger strength.

Stien et al. (PMC 2024, n = 19 males) found pull-up strength correlated moderately with bouldering (r = 0.55) and campus boarding (r = 0.57), but not with redpoint performance (p > 0.05). In the same sample, climbing-specific finger strength correlated r = 0.89 with bouldering — pull-up strength contributed less independent variance.

[https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12479556/ | Stien et al. (recent PMC) | Climbing performance in males]

Lattice Training data (n = 901, mixed-sex, 2025 blog post) showed that for a combined sample, 2-rep max pull-up (%BW) yielded R = 0.346, adjusted R² = 0.117 for bouldering — less than finger strength (R = 0.411, adj. R² = 0.167). For route climbing at the elite level, the pattern reversed: 2-rep max pull-up was the strongest predictor (R = 0.521, adj. R² = 0.253), with the regression coefficient β = 0.040, suggesting a 1-unit increase in pull-up strength (%BW) predicts a 0.040 IRCRA-unit grade increase. This reversal at elite route-climbing level is a notable finding — tentatively suggesting that at the point where finger strength is near-ceiling for elite routesetters and routes, pulling power discriminates performance.

[https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers?]

[https://latticetraining.com/blog/predictors-for-performance/ | Lattice Training | How do the predictors for bouldering performance differ between ability levels?]

Mermier et al. (2000) included bent arm hang (isometric lock-off endurance) and found it loaded on the training component that explained 58.9% of variance; it was among the most influential trainable variables. However, strength per se vs. endurance was not cleanly separated.

**Important caveat**: The Lattice data are from athletes completing a self-administered remote assessment (not lab-controlled), and grades are self-reported as "best boulder/route worked." Self-report inflates variance and grade ranges may differ by outdoor location. The n = 901 is large but subject selection is non-random (self-selected Lattice clients). These are cross-sectional data.

### 2b. Does Pull-Up Add Independent Variance Beyond Finger Strength?

The strongest test of independent contribution requires a multivariate regression with both predictors simultaneously. The Lattice 2025 data analyzed predictors in isolation, not jointly, so independent contribution cannot be determined from their published blog analyses alone. The Stien et al. (PMC 2024) study found pull-up strength (r = 0.55 for bouldering) correlated substantially with finger strength (r = 0.89), and did not report partial correlations or a simultaneous regression, so the independent contribution of pull-up strength is not established from that paper.

Laffaye et al. (2016) found in their PCA-based regression that the arm jump test (upper-limb explosive power) discriminated climbers across all groups and contributed independently to the training component, but this is power rather than maximal pull strength.

**Evidence-backed** that pull-up strength is significantly correlated with grade (cross-sectional). **Bioplausible** that it adds independent variance beyond finger strength, because locking off on small holds requires both finger contact force and arm/shoulder positioning strength — but this has not been demonstrated in a study with both predictors entered simultaneously in a multivariate model with adequate power.

**Causation note**: Cross-sectional only. No prospective study has tested whether improving pull-up 1RM in isolation predicts grade improvement.

## 3. The 9c Test (Magnus Midtbø) and Community Protocols

### 3a. Full Protocol Description

The 9c test was designed by Stian Christophersen and Martin Mobraten (authors of *The Climbing Bible*) and popularized by a YouTube video featuring Magnus Midtbø. It consists of four exercises, each scored 1–10 points, for a total of 40 possible points.

**Exercise 1 — Max finger strength** (2-arm dead-hang, approx. 20 mm crimp, 5 seconds):
1 point = 100% BW | 2 = 110% | 3 = 120% | 4 = 130% | 5 = 140% | 6 = 150% | 7 = 160% | 8 = 180% | 9 = 200% | 10 = 220%

**Exercise 2 — Max pull-up** (1RM weighted, same increments as Ex. 1):
1 = 100% | 2 = 110% | … | 10 = 220%

**Exercise 3 — Core strength** (timed holds, ascending difficulty):
1 = 10 s L-sit (bent knees) | 2 = 20 s | 3 = 30 s | 4 = 10 s L-sit (straight) | 5 = 15 s | 6 = 20 s | 7 = 5 s front lever | 8 = 10 s | 9 = 20 s | 10 = 30 s front lever

**Exercise 4 — Dead-hang from bar** (unweighted, no movement):
Timed duration; specific point thresholds not specified in most sources but scaled to time.

**Grade predictions** (total score → French redpoint):
40 → 9c | 38–39 → 9b | 36–37 → 9b+ (note: table from theCrag)
32–35 → 9a/9a+ | 28–31 → 8c/8c+ | 24–27 → 8b/8b+ | 20–23 → 8a/8a+ | 18–19 → 7c+ | 16–17 → 7c | 12–15 → 7b/7b+ | 8–11 → 7a/7a+ | 4–7 → 6b/6c | 1–3 → 6a

[https://www.thecrag.com/en/climbing/virtual/route/4430259711 | theCrag community | 9c Strength Test — full point table]

[https://www.open-climbing.info/9c-calc.html | OpenClimbing.info | 9C Climbing Test explainer]

[https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=UOBB4wkTdxQ | Magnus Midtbø YouTube | Original 9c test video]

[https://www.hoopersbeta.com/library/can-the-ultimate-climbing-test-really-reveal-your-biggest-weakness | Hooper's Beta | Can the MAGNUS MIDTBØ 9c TEST reveal your biggest weakness?]

### 3b. Evidence Evaluation

**Has any peer-reviewed study validated the 9c test battery against actual climbing grade?** No. A comprehensive search of PubMed, Frontiers, IJSPP, and Google Scholar finds no published study that has collected 9c test scores alongside objective (or even self-reported) climbing grades in a sample of sufficient size to compute correlation or regression coefficients. The 9c test has zero peer-reviewed validation as a composite instrument.

**Are the individual components supported as grade predictors?**

- Exercise 1 (finger strength): Yes, this is the strongest individual predictor in the literature (see §1). Using a 20 mm edge and 5-second time window captures MFS well. The point scale has non-linear jumps (160% → 180% → 200% → 220%) that Hooper's Beta notes represent 20%BW increments at upper scores — clinically concerning injury risk if athletes attempt large load jumps.
- Exercise 2 (1RM weighted pull-up): Moderately supported (see §2), with the caveat that it correlates less than finger strength with bouldering performance.
- Exercise 3 (core strength / L-sit / front lever): **Bioplausible** as a proxy for hip flexor and anterior chain control relevant to heel hooks, high steps, and body tension; however, no peer-reviewed study has directly correlated L-sit or front lever time with climbing grade. The front lever particularly correlates with relative body weight and latissimus/teres strength but its route-climbing relevance is contested. **TO_VERIFY** as an isolated predictor.
- Exercise 4 (dead-hang from a bar, unweighted): This is an endurance test on a large jug — not grip-specific at all. Hooper's Beta notes this is more a measure of mental tenacity and grip endurance at near-zero relative loading, with limited climbing specificity. **Bioplausible** as a proxy for psychological persistence under physiological discomfort; not a standard forearm aerobic endurance test.

**Community-level validation attempts**: UKC forums contain multiple threads where users report their 9c scores alongside their redpoint grades. Correlation is directionally positive but there is substantial scatter. One user reported scoring 17/40 while climbing 7c outdoors; another scored 10/40 at 7a+. Midtbø himself noted these should be treated as fun benchmarks, not strict predictions. No systematic data compilation from r/climbharder or comparable communities has been formally published.

[https://www.ukclimbing.com/forums/walls+training/magnus_midtbo_-_9c_fitness_test-730174 | UKClimbing forums | Magnus Midtbø 9c Fitness Test thread]

### 3c. Comparison with Lattice Testing Battery

The Lattice athlete profiling remote assessment typically includes: (1) 7-second 2-arm max hang on 20 mm edge (%BW), (2) 2RM weighted pull-up (%BW), (3) 60% MFS power endurance test (time to exhaustion at 60% of max hang force via intermittent hangs), and (4) a flexibility measure (box split in centimeters). Some versions include a critical force test.

Overlap with 9c test: finger strength (Exercises 1), pulling strength (Exercise 2). Divergences: Lattice uses 7-second hang (vs. 5-second in 9c test); Lattice uses critical force / power endurance (no equivalent in 9c); Lattice uses flexibility (not in 9c); 9c test uses core strength and bar dead-hang (not in Lattice standard battery). The Lattice battery has published at least two blog-level regression analyses (2025) with n = 422–901, while the 9c test has no published data-driven validation.

Lattice's approach is more rigorous in protocol standardization, has larger sample sizes for normative data, and separates bouldering from route climbing performance. The 9c test flattens discipline differences by using a single composite score.

### 3d. Limitations and Critique

Physical qualities not captured by the 9c test: technique (footwork, weight transfer, hip turnout), movement efficiency (economy of motion on a given route), route-reading and preview strategy, mental performance under pressure, fear of falling, skin condition, grip endurance in a route-climbing-specific intermittent protocol, flexibility (relevant to high steps and stemming), shoulder health, and ankle mobility. The systematic review by Langer et al. (2023) and the ScienceDirect review of performance determinants (Frontiers 2024) converge on the view that no battery of purely strength tests can account for the technical and psychological components that dominate performance at most grade bands.

At which grade does the test lose validity? The test is likely most discriminatory in the 7b–8c range where physical metrics vary widely and correlate with grade. Below ~7a, technique variation is so large that physically strong beginners frequently underperform their test scores (an observation noted by multiple UKC forum posters and by Hooper's Beta). Above 8c+, the test loses precision because the point scale increments are too coarse (e.g., needing 220%BW for a 10 on exercises 1 and 2 is theoretically accurate for 9c climbers but the resolution between 8b and 8c is only 3–4 points on the scale, which is within measurement error). Pete Whittaker and Mari Salvesen, both high-level climbers, failed the front lever component in a video attempt, illustrating that elite climbers are not necessarily balanced across all four test dimensions.

## 4. Aerobic Capacity, Forearm Endurance, and Lactate Threshold as Predictors

### 4a. Forearm Endurance and Critical Force

Forearm aerobic endurance, particularly expressed through the concept of critical force (CF), is the most scientifically developed endurance predictor in climbing physiology.

Giles et al. (2019) introduced the multi-trial determination of finger-flexor CF in climbers: participants performed intermittent isometric contractions (7 s on, 3 s off) at 45%, 60%, and 80% of MVC on a hangboard. The hyperbolic force-time relationship allowed CF (the force asymptote — a metabolic steady-state boundary) and W' (the anaerobic work capacity above CF) to be calculated. CF expressed as %BW correlated significantly with sport climbing grade (r = 0.61) and W' correlated with bouldering performance (r = 0.34), a pattern that matches the aerobic demands of route climbing vs. the anaerobic/power demands of bouldering.

[10.1123/ijspp.2018-0809 | Giles et al. 2019 | The determination of finger-flexor critical force in rock climbers]

Giles et al. (2021) developed the 4-minute all-out test as a single-session alternative, consisting of 24 all-out one-arm pulls; CF and W' can be estimated from the force-time curve without multiple exhaustive sessions.

[10.1123/ijspp.2020-0637 | Giles et al. 2021 | An all-out test to determine finger flexor critical force in rock climbers]

Baláš et al. (2024, Eur J Appl Physiol) validated the 4-minute all-out test for CF determination against multi-trial methods in sport climbers, finding good agreement (ICC > 0.90) for CF but lower agreement for W'.

[10.1007/s00421-024-05490-7 | Baláš et al. 2024 | Measuring critical force in sport climbers: a validation study of the 4 min all-out test]

Lattice Training's own analysis (2025, n = 422 route climbers) found CF (%BW) yielded R = 0.401, R² = 0.160 for route grade overall (p < 0.001), but at the Higher Elite level CF was not statistically significant (p = 0.052), suggesting CF has diminishing predictive power at the highest grades — where technical and psychological factors dominate.

[https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training 2025 | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers?]

A notable finding in the CAMP4 Human Performance review of the 2021 Giles paper: approximately 6% of climbers redpointing below f6c/French 6c could not generate a stable force plateau in the all-out test, suggesting CF testing requires a minimum ability level (RP ≥ 7a / V4 equivalent) for valid results.

[https://www.camp4humanperformance.com/research/critical-force-climbing-test | CAMP4 Human Performance | Critical Force in Climbing review]

The energy system analysis (Maciejczyk et al. 2022, Frontiers) quantified contributions during different test types: aerobic contribution was highest during the intermittent test (59.9 ± 12.0%), with anaerobic alactic dominating the all-out test (62.4 ± 11.3%). This mechanistically separates CF (aerobic steady state) from W' (anaerobic capacity above CF).

[10.3389/fspor.2022.792900 (DOI unverified) | Maciejczyk et al. 2022 | Climbing-specific exercise tests: energy system contributions and relationships with sport performance]

**Finger hang test**: The continuous dead-hang on a 20–25 mm edge to failure has been widely used as an endurance proxy, but the Stien et al. 2025 normative study demonstrated it is primarily a strength test (65–80% of variance in hang time explained by MFS), making it a poor pure endurance measure. The intermittent endurance test (7/3 repeaters at 60% MVC to failure) shows better construct validity for aerobic endurance of the finger flexors.

**Evidence-backed** for CF as a predictor of route climbing grade (r = 0.61); route climbing > bouldering. The large unexplained variance (R² = 0.16 in Lattice data) indicates that CF alone is insufficient.

**Causation note**: All CF-grade correlations are cross-sectional. No prospective study has tested whether improving CF (via critical force endurance training) produces grade improvement on routes. The López-Rivera & González-Badillo (2019) endurance training RCT found that intermittent dead-hang training improved forearm endurance by 45% (vs. 34% for max hangs), but climbing grade was not tracked.

[https://www.researchgate.net/publication/324731332 | López-Rivera & González-Badillo 2019 | Comparison of the effects of three hangboard strength and endurance training programs on grip endurance in sport climbers]

### 4b. VO₂max and Systemic Aerobic Capacity

The published evidence is consistent and somewhat counterintuitive: systemic VO₂max measured on treadmill or cycle ergometer is a poor predictor of climbing grade. Billat et al. (1995) found that 7b routes elicited only 37.7–45.6% of treadmill VO₂max in 5 competitive climbers, though heart rate reached 77–85.5% of maximum. Blood lactate was 4.3–5.7 mmol/L post-ascent. The low VO₂max utilization during climbing reflects the isometric and localized nature of climbing-specific effort, which is not aerobically constrained at the systemic level.

[10.1249/00005768-199504000-00002 (DOI unverified) | Billat et al. 1995 | Energy specificity of rock climbing and aerobic capacity in competitive sport rock climbers]

Multiple subsequent studies (Michailov et al. 2015; Sheel et al. 2003) confirmed that treadmill or cycling VO₂max does not correlate significantly with climbing grade. The Frontiers performance determinant systematic review (2024) states: "traditional ergometer tests reflect general fitness level... corresponding test results did not correlate with climbing performance." The only significant aerobic-performance relationship was found using a vertically mounted isokinetic rowing ergometer specifically designed to simulate climbing, which produced significant correlations with climbing ability.

[https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2095254624001303 | Frontiers 2024 systematic review | Sport climbing performance determinants and functional testing methods]

**Evidence-backed**: systemic VO₂max has low predictive validity for climbing grade; **Evidence-backed**: forearm-specific CF has moderate predictive validity for route climbing grade (r = 0.61). The discrepancy is explained by the localized, isometric nature of finger flexor fatigue, which is metabolically independent of whole-body aerobic capacity.

### 4c. Blood Lactate

Blood lactate during and after climbing is elevated (4–7 mmol/L post-ascent on hard routes), but its relationship with grade is not a direct correlation — it reflects effort rather than ability. Watts et al. (1996) measured acute changes in handgrip strength, endurance, and blood lactate with sustained sport rock climbing and found lactate elevated post-climb. No study has demonstrated that lactate threshold (4 mmol/L threshold in treadmill tests) correlates with climbing grade independently.

**Bioplausible** that climbers with higher local lactate clearance capacity can recover faster between moves and between attempts, but this is not empirically tested as a grade predictor. The CF framework largely supersedes the lactate threshold concept for climbing-specific endurance, since it is measured locally in the finger flexors rather than systemically.

### 4d. Community Discussion on Endurance vs. Strength

**FOLKLORE_VERIFIED**: The community's general position — stated consistently across Lattice Training, StrengthClimbing, and r/climbharder — is:

- Below approximately V6–V8 / 7b: technique is the primary limiter; strength and endurance metrics are weakly predictive due to high technical variance.
- V8–V10 / 7c–8a: finger strength becomes the dominant physical limiter for bouldering; route climbers need a balance of finger strength and forearm aerobic endurance.
- V10+ / 8b+: both MFS and CF / power endurance become highly relevant; the elite performer needs to be strong and have a high CF-to-MFS ratio. The Lattice 2025 data shows CF gains diminishing returns at Higher Elite (p = 0.052, trending non-significant).

[https://latticetraining.com/blog/predictors-for-performance/ | Lattice Training 2025 | Bouldering predictors by ability level]

[https://strengthclimbing.com/finger-strength-analyzer/ | StrengthClimbing.com | Finger Strength Analyzer 2.0 — discussion of endurance in sport climbing]

## 5. Anthropometrics and Body Composition

### 5a. Scientific Evidence

The systematic review by Ozimek et al. (2023, JSCR) is the most comprehensive synthesis of body composition and anthropometric data in climbing.

[10.1519/JSC.0000000000004418 (DOI from JSCR 2023 article) | Ozimek et al. 2023 | Body composition, anthropometric parameters, and strength-endurance characteristics of sport climbers: a systematic review]

Key findings across 18 included studies:
- Body fat (%BF): Lower in elite climbers than intermediate and non-climbers in most studies. Elite male sport climbers typically 4–12% BF; world-cup level 4–8% (DEXA). However, "low body fat is not a prerequisite for elite-level climbing" — it may be an indirect consequence of high training volume rather than a causal prerequisite. The review found 5 of 11 studies showed no significant BF-grade correlation when controlling for training.
- Body mass: Lower mass in climbers vs. controls; lower in elite vs. advanced in studies recruiting 7c+ RP vs. 6a+ RP groups.
- BMI: Not consistently different between climbers of varying ability. Normal range (18.5–24.9) across virtually all studied populations.
- Ape index: "None of the analyzed work showed differences between sport climber groups in the ape index." This is the most striking anthropometric null result in the literature — ape index does not discriminate performance within competitive climbing populations.
- Height: Not significant in the systematic review. The 8a.nu dataset analysis (OLS regression, n > 10,000 logbook users) found height is a negative predictor for males (taller climbers climb slightly less hard on average), with a parabolic rather than linear relationship — reflecting the selection effect that shorter climbers may be over-represented at hard grades.

A 2025 Frontiers study of IFSC World Cup vs. national-level male boulderers (n = 34) using ISAK anthropometry found significant differences in body fat %, abdominal skinfold (30.4% lower in international climbers), and ape index (higher Ape Index, Arm Index, and Intermembral Index in international group, p < 0.05). Lean body mass % was 3.9% higher in international climbers. This is a notable contradiction of the earlier systematic review's conclusion, but the sample size is small and the international group contained proportionally more Asian climbers, introducing ethnic body composition confounders.

[10.3389/fspor.2025.1588414 | Frontiers 2025 | Morphology of male World Cup and elite bouldering athletes]

Watts et al. (2003, BMJ) studied 90 junior competitive climbers and found no correlation between ape index and climbing ability — though they noted the low variability in ape index within the climbing cohort (possible selection effect: people with naturally high ape index may be drawn to climbing).

[10.1136/bjsm.37.5.420 (DOI unverified) | Watts et al. 2003 | Anthropometry of young competitive sport rock climbers]

The Somatic Profile paper (PMC 2012, n = 21 competitive male climbers) found only ape index "tended to correlate" with self-reported climbing ability (r = 0.397, p = 0.083) — non-significant with α = 0.05. Body fat correlated r = −0.614 with ability (p < 0.01).

[https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3588625/ | Ozimek et al. 2012 | Somatic profile of competitive sport climbers]

**Summary of ape index evidence**: Near-universal null result at within-competitive-sample level (Watts 2003, Ozimek 2017, Ozimek 2023 review, multiple smaller studies). The 2025 Frontiers study found significant differences at IFSC vs. national level, but selection effects and small n limit inference. **Evidence-backed null finding**: ape index does not significantly predict climbing performance within competitive climbing populations.

### 5b. Finger Morphology

Cross-sectional evidence shows that elite climbers have measurably hypertrophied A2 pulleys (increased cross-sectional area on ultrasound), and that forearm flexor muscle belly thickness is greater in elite vs. recreational climbers. These are likely adaptations to long-term training rather than prerequisites. No study has used prospective design to show that finger morphology (before training) predicts future performance. **Bioplausible** direction: larger A2 pulley CSA → higher safe crimp force capacity → higher achievable MFS; mechanism is structural-mechanical but the direction of causality is uncertain.

### 5c. Body Mass as a Limiter: Elite vs. Amateur

At elite level (8a+), the weight-to-strength ratio becomes more critical as route-setters and problems increasingly demand maintaining high forces relative to body weight. The data supporting lower body mass in elite vs. national climbers are robust (Ozimek 2023, Frontiers 2025). Whether this reflects body mass being causal for performance or an epiphenomenon of elite training volume is debated. Mermier et al. (2000) found that body mass explained < 1% of variance in climbing performance when training variables were included in the regression — arguably the most direct evidence that mass per se is not the bottleneck for recreational/amateur climbers.

**Evidence-backed** that lower body fat is associated with higher grade in elite samples; **Evidence-backed** that body mass explains negligible variance once training variables are controlled; **Bioplausible** (not causal) that weight reduction improves strength-to-weight ratio and therefore grade for athletes already at the performance-limiting ceiling of finger strength.

The weight-cutting debate: The community generally recognizes that weight cutting is dangerous for bone health and hormonal function, particularly for female climbers. Schöffl et al. have documented disordered eating and Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) in competitive climbers. This is medically significant and the sports science community (IRCRA, multiple review papers) warns against caloric restriction for performance. **FOLKLORE_VERIFIED** (≥ 3 sources consistently warning against weight cutting): r/climbharder wiki, Lattice Training blog, Hooper's Beta multiple posts.

## 6. Other Physical Qualities Studied as Predictors

### 6a. Shoulder Strength (Push/Pull Ratio)

The ScienceDirect systematic review (2024) noted that shoulder crank ergometer maximal and average power showed statistically significant correlations (r = 0.56–0.60) with climbing ability in intermediate to advanced male climbers (MacKenzie et al. cross-sectional study). However, no study has entered shoulder push/pull ratio into a multivariate model alongside finger strength to determine independent contribution.

[https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2095254624001303 | Frontiers 2024 | Sport climbing performance determinants and functional testing methods]

**Evidence-backed** that shoulder strength correlates moderately (r ≈ 0.56–0.60) with grade; **Bioplausible** that an imbalanced push/pull ratio predicts injury risk (shoulder impingement) and limits technique — cited by Hooper's Beta in the 9c test critique. No controlled study has tested this.

### 6b. Hip Flexor Strength and Flexibility

The systematic review (Langer et al. 2023; ScienceDirect review 2024) found that "general flexibility (i.e., the forward bending test) showed neither meaningful correlations nor differences between climbers of different performance levels." The maximal straddle score, leg span, and sit-and-reach showed only "moderate correlations with climbing ability in male athletes." The Lattice remote assessment includes a box-split flexibility measure, and their 2025 blog data found flexibility had R = 0.026 (p = 0.62) for bouldering and near-zero correlation for routes across all ability levels — effectively nil.

[https://latticetraining.com/blog/predictors-for-performance/ | Lattice Training 2025 | Bouldering predictors by ability level]

Hip flexor activation during high steps is clinically important for injury-free technique, but no study has shown hip flexor strength as a significant grade predictor in a well-powered sample. **Bioplausible** for technique facilitation; **no direct evidence** for grade prediction.

### 6c. Core Strength

No published study has measured a validated core strength metric (e.g., anterior plank time, hollow body hold) and correlated it with climbing grade in a powered sample. The L-sit and front lever used in the 9c test are treated as proxies, but these test specific motor patterns that may be technique-dependent (i.e., a climber without front lever training will score poorly regardless of relevant anterior chain strength). **TO_VERIFY** as an isolated predictor.

Granacher et al. (DOI unverified) studied effects of climbing on core strength and mobility in adults, finding that regular climbing improved core stability scores — but this is a direction-of-effect study, not a predictor study.

### 6d. Lower Body Strength

Multiple studies (España-Romero et al.; Giles et al. 2020; Mermier 2000) found no significant differences between climber ability levels in lower body CMJ or squat jump performance for lead climbing or bouldering. Speed climbing is an exception: Krawczyk et al. found strong correlations (r = −0.78 to −0.90) between CMJ height, power, and velocity with speed climbing time. For technique-dependent disciplines, lower body strength is **Evidence-backed null** as a predictor.

### 6e. Years Climbing as a Technique Proxy

Years climbing is consistently one of the strongest correlates of grade in studies that include it. Mermier et al. (2000) found "years of experience" loaded heavily on the training component (the dominant 58.9% component). The 8a.nu OLS regression (GitHub analysis, n > 10,000) found years climbing is a statistically significant positive predictor for all four combinations of sex × discipline. This is primarily a proxy for accumulated deliberate practice and technique acquisition, not a physical quality per se. **Evidence-backed** as a correlate; cross-sectional design cannot determine whether experience drives performance or performance-motivated athletes train longer.

### 6f. Balance and Proprioception

No peer-reviewed study of adequate power has correlated standardized balance tests (e.g., BESS, balance platform) with climbing grade in recreational or competitive climbers. Balance and proprioception are considered technique-adjacent factors and are likely captured partially by years-of-experience variables. **Bioplausible** for lower-grade transitions; **insufficient evidence** for any quantitative claim.

## 7. Multivariate Models: What Combination of Variables Best Predicts Grade?

### 7a. Regression Models in the Literature

**Mermier et al. 2000** (n = 44, male + female, 5.6–5.13c): Three-component PCA + multiple regression.
- Training component: 58.9% of variance
- Anthropometric component: 0.3%
- Flexibility component: 1.8%
- Total explained: 66.3% (R² ≈ 0.66)
- Unexplained: ~33.7%

[10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000]

**Laffaye et al. 2016** (n = 41): PCA + multiple regression. Total: 64.22% variance explained. Training component (dominated by finger grip and upper-limb power): 46%.

[DOI unverified | Laffaye et al. 2016 | Determinant factors in climbing ability]

**Identification of parameters for sport climbing** (Gajewski et al., Frontiers 2019, n = 30 sport climbers, 43 variables). Three variable sets (physical, technical, mental) each explained 25–38% of variance. The study highlights that no physical test battery alone approaches 50% explained variance when well-validated.

[https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6554989/ | Frontiers 2019 | Identification of parameters that predict sport climbing performance]

**Stien normative study (2025, Tandfonline, n = 307 total)**: Multivariate decomposition via confirmatory factor analysis showed finger strength dominant (68%/64% of climbing ability variance in males/females), followed by intermittent endurance (28%/34%). These two factors jointly account for ~96% of explained variance in this model — but the model only included finger strength and endurance measures, so unexplained climbing-wide variance is not quantifiable.

[10.1080/02640414.2024.2449316 | Stien et al. 2025 | Validity and normative scores of finger flexor strength and endurance tests]

**Machine learning approaches (blog-level, high n)**: Thomas C. Georgiou (Medium, 2025) ran Ridge Regression, Gradient Boosting, and Random Forest on a dataset of climbers including max hang, repeaters, continuous hang, pull-up strength, weighted pull-up, body metrics. Ridge Regression had moderate R² with non-trivial residual patterns suggesting non-linearity. Gradient Boosting and Random Forest modestly outperformed linear models. Feature importance analyses consistently ranked max hang / strength-to-weight variables first, with body metrics (height, weight) contributing minimally. These are exploratory, non-peer-reviewed analyses.

[https://medium.com/@thomascgeorgiou/predicting-climbing-performance-using-machine-learning | T.C. Georgiou, Medium | Predicting climbing performance with machine learning — 3-part series]

### 7b. Best Available Model R²

Across all published studies, the best multivariate R² for predicting climbing grade from physical/physiological variables is approximately 0.63–0.66 (Mermier 2000; Laffaye 2016). The Stien 2025 two-factor model reaches higher R² within its constrained model, but it was not fit against all possible predictors in the full climbing-relevant space.

### 7c. Gap Statement: Unexplained Variance

Even the best models leave 33–50% of climbing grade variance unexplained by measured physical variables. This unexplained residual is attributable to:

- **Climbing technique and movement efficiency**: route-reading ability, footwork precision, center-of-mass management, rest position identification, and strategic energy allocation. These are near-impossible to quantify without detailed motion capture or expert observation on test routes.
- **Psychological factors**: fear of falling, competition anxiety, self-efficacy, and the ability to "try hard" under physiological discomfort. The Lattice training team remarked on Magnus Midtbø's extreme mental engagement during testing — implying that two athletes with identical physical profiles but different psychological profiles would achieve different test results and different climbing grades.
- **Route-specific tactics and experience**: familiarity with the movement vocabulary of specific rock types, hold shapes, and weather/skin conditions.
- **Grading subjectivity**: all studies using self-reported climbing grade as outcome introduce substantial noise. Grades vary by gym, region, outdoor area, and epoch (grade inflation/deflation).
- **Training specificity**: an athlete trained entirely on campus boards and hangboards may score highly on isolated tests but have poor climbing coordination. The reverse-causation risk is real and uncontrolled in all cross-sectional designs.

## 8. Statistical Estimators and Probabilistic Models of Grades

### 8a. Item Response Theory and Rasch Models Applied to Climbing

The theoretical groundwork connecting climbing grade estimation to IRT and Elo-family models is clear but has received limited direct academic treatment specifically in climbing. The Rasch model (1-PL IRT) models the probability of a climber with ability θ successfully ascending a route with difficulty β as: P(success) = exp(θ − β) / (1 + exp(θ − β)). This is a logistic function where the probability of success is 50% when θ = β.

In this formulation, "grade" is the estimated route difficulty parameter β, and "climber ability" is the estimated latent ability θ. Both can be placed on the same interval scale, yielding a conceptually clean framework for route grading.

The Elo rating system (from chess) has the same functional form as the Rasch model — differing only in parameter estimation approach (Elo uses heuristic sequential updating; Rasch/IRT uses maximum likelihood or Bayesian estimation). Both would model the probability that climber C with Elo/ability score θ sends route R with difficulty rating β.

[https://www.fi.muni.cz/~xpelanek/publications/CAE-elo.pdf | Pelanek | Applications of the Elo rating system in adaptive assessment — formal connection to Rasch model]

**Applied to climbing**: Ethan Rosenthal (data science blog, 2022) constructed a Bayesian "Route Response Theory" model on the 8a.nu logbook dataset. The model treats each climber-route pair as a binary outcome (sent on first try or not), fitting climber ability (a_c) and route difficulty (d_r) as Bayesian posterior estimates. This is structurally equivalent to a Bayesian Rasch model (1PL IRT). The model assigns each route a single consensus difficulty rating (chosen as the most popular user-reported grade) and estimates climber abilities as latent parameters. The resulting posterior ability estimates correlate with self-reported grades.

[https://www.ethanrosenthal.com/2022/04/15/bayesian-rock-climbing/ | Ethan Rosenthal (data science blog) | Bayesian Rock Climbing Rankings]

Scarff (2017) and Drummond & Popinga (2021) used Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo inference on logbook data (theCrag, Vertical-Life databases) to estimate route difficulty from the history of climber performance, with a time-varying climber skill grade. Their model found that on the Vermin scale, an increase in grade corresponds to a 3.17 increase in inferred difficulty — i.e., the difficulty scale is not linear. The Frontiers 2024 grading-bias paper (Frontiers Sports Act Living, PMC 2024) reviewed these approaches.

[https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2024.1512010/full | Frontiers 2024 | Addressing grading bias in rock climbing: machine and deep learning approaches]

### 8b. Big-Data Studies of 8a.nu

The 8a.nu Kaggle dataset (scraped; contains ~10,000+ climber entries with height, weight, age, years climbing, grades_mean, grades_first) has been analyzed by multiple community data scientists.

Brandon Sienkiewicz (Medium, 2023) applied OLS, KNN imputation, and basic ML to the 8a.nu dataset to predict average grade. RMSE of approximately 4.04 grade units (on the French scale encoded as integers) — roughly ±1 grade range. Key finding: years climbing was the single strongest numerical predictor; body weight/height contributed minimally after correcting for multicollinearity.

[https://medium.com/@bmsienk/8a-nu-climbing-dataset-basic-eda-fb13af8105c0 | B. Sienkiewicz, Medium | 8a.nu Climbing Dataset Basic EDA]

Steve Bachmeier (GitHub) ran OLS on 8a.nu max grade as outcome, with height, weight, age, and years of experience as predictors (after dropping weight due to high collinearity with height). Height was a statistically significant negative predictor for males (not significant for females). Years of experience was a consistently significant positive predictor across all four subgroups (M/F × bouldering/rope). R² values were moderate, reflecting the noise in self-reported max grade data.

[https://github.com/stevebachmeier/climber-characteristic-analysis | S. Bachmeier | climber-characteristic-analysis (GitHub)]

The Moonboard route grade prediction literature provides the most rigorous ML-for-climbing-grades work. Dobles (Stanford CS229, 2017) tested Naïve Bayes, softmax regression, and CNNs on Moonboard route hold sequences, finding that CNNs performed best but still lagged human-level grade accuracy. A follow-up (2023 arXiv, "Board-to-Board") examined generalizability across Moonboard versions, finding significant performance drops when trained on one year/version and tested on another.

[https://cs229.stanford.edu/proj2017/final-reports/5232206.pdf | Dobles et al. 2017 (Stanford CS229) | Machine Learning Methods for Climbing Route Classification]

[https://arxiv.org/pdf/2311.12419 | Anon. 2023 (arXiv) | Board-to-Board: Evaluating Moonboard Grade Prediction Generalization]

The Frontiers 2024 paper (PMC) reviews ML approaches to grading bias, noting that EMG of the forearm flexor digitorum profundus has a "logarithmic relationship in predicting route grade" across ascents — a biometric approach to grading that bypasses subjective consensus.

[10.3389/fspor.2024.1512010 | Frontiers 2024 | Addressing grading bias in rock climbing: machine and deep learning approaches]

**To summarize the big-data picture**: (1) Route difficulty is best modeled as a latent variable inferred from the binary outcomes (sent/not) of many climbers; Bayesian IRT/Elo-family models provide theoretically sound frameworks. (2) Climber ability can be estimated from logbook data but is substantially confounded by self-report, selection into outdoor areas, and project-vs-redpoint style differences. (3) ML models predicting grade from biomechanics (acceleration, EMG) are promising but require expensive instrumented setups. (4) Simple regression from demographic/physical variables (height, weight, years) has low R² (< 0.3 in most analyses), consistent with the laboratory finding that technique and sport-specific training dominate grade.

### 8c. Community-Level Statistical Analyses

Mark Dodd (Medium, 2020) performed exploratory visualization and descriptive statistics on 8a.nu logbook data, plotting grade distributions by country and year to identify grade inflation and regional grade differences. No formal regression published.

[https://medium.com/@buckthecanuck/climb-through-the-data-with-me-80fb144ea408 | Mark Dodd, Medium | Climb Through the Data With Me — 8a.nu logbook analysis]

The toclimb8a.shinyapps.io max-to-grade Shiny app (referenced by StrengthClimbing) is a community-built tool that takes CF parameters as input and outputs an expected sport climbing grade, based on Giles 2019 equations — a direct application of the physiological model to personal assessment.

Validated against climbing performance across 3 IRCRA ability groups; force-time data allow CF and W' estimation. This is the most technologically complete field-ready system with published validity data.

[https://climbro.com/2023/01/strength-and-endurance-testing-for-efficient-climbing-training/ | Climbro.com | Strength and endurance testing for efficient climbing training]

## 10. Causation Caveats

**Across all correlations in §1–6, the following causation framework applies:**

All studies described as "cross-sectional" (the overwhelming majority) establish statistical association between physical metrics and climbing grade at a single time point. They cannot establish causation. The fundamental threat to causal inference in this literature is bidirectional confounding:

**Reverse causation (Training History)**: Elite climbers score higher on finger strength, endurance, and power tests primarily because they have accumulated thousands of hours of climbing-specific training. The measured quality may not be the bottleneck — it may simply co-vary with training volume and history. A climber who spends 10 years on rock acquires both high test scores and high grade simultaneously; the physical test does not prove it was the limiting factor.

**Selection bias in cross-sectional samples**: The range restriction problem is pervasive. Studies recruiting "elite" climbers (e.g., >8a redpoint) will find strong correlations between MFS and grade because they are measuring across a performance range where physical differences are large. Within narrower grade bands, the same predictor may explain substantially less variance.

**Self-reported grades**: Most studies use self-reported grade as the outcome. Grade inflation, gym vs. outdoor discrepancies, redpoint vs. onsight differences, and reporting bias (climbers claiming harder grades) are all confounders. The few studies using objective routes or competition results yield more reliable outcomes but are limited by logistics.

**Predictors with intervention-level evidence** (causal direction plausible):
- **MFS improvement → grade improvement**: The most direct evidence comes from Hermans et al. 2022 (RCT, n = 35, 10-week hangboard + normal climbing vs. normal climbing alone; significant improvement in Fpeak and RFD in HBT group) and the Stien et al. 2023 meta-analysis. However, neither study measured post-intervention climbing grade as a primary outcome. The strongest causal signal is therefore: hangboard training improves MFS; MFS correlates with grade; the inference that this chain implies grade improvement is reasonable but not directly tested in an adequately powered RCT.
- **RFD improvement**: Levernier & Laffaye (2019, 4-week grip training RCT, n = elite and top-world-ranking climbers) showed significant RFD improvement. Grade not measured.

**Predictors with no intervention evidence**: CF, body composition, ape index, flexibility, years climbing — none have been tested in an intervention design where the variable was specifically manipulated and climbing grade measured as outcome.

## Open Questions

The following questions cannot be answered by the current literature:

1. **Does the 9c test (or any field test battery) predict grade improvement over time?** All test batteries have been validated cross-sectionally against concurrent grade, not prospectively. No study has administered a battery at baseline and tracked whether test scores predict improvement in grade 6, 12, or 24 months later. This is arguably the most practically important validation gap.

2. **Does the relative importance of predictors shift as climbers advance from V4 to V14?** The Lattice 2025 data tentatively suggest it does (finger strength R² drops from 16.7% at advanced to 4.2% at Higher Elite), but this analysis has significant methodological limitations (simultaneous univariate rather than multivariate analyses, self-report outcomes, non-random sample). A well-powered longitudinal study tracking the same athletes across grade bands has never been conducted.

3. **What is the independent contribution of CF to grade prediction when MFS is already in the model?** No study reports partial correlation or semi-partial R² for CF after controlling for MFS. Given that CF is partly a function of MFS (higher MFS → higher absolute CF), multicollinearity means the independent contribution of endurance is currently unknown.

4. **Can technique and movement efficiency be quantified, and how much variance do they explain?** Some wearable-sensor and computer vision approaches exist (EMG, IMU-based limb tracking), and the Frontiers 2024 ML paper achieved 98% classification accuracy for 3 route difficulty levels using body acceleration data — but these require expensive equipment and have not been applied to a continuous grade prediction in a large naturalistic sample.

5. **Do physical performance predictors differ by sex when samples are analyzed separately?** Most studies are male-only or have insufficient female n for separate subgroup analyses. The Stien 2025 normative study is a notable exception (185 males, 122 females), finding that finger strength explains similar proportions of variance in both sexes, but the absolute strength thresholds and normative values differ substantially.

6. **Is the relationship between MFS and grade linear, or does it exhibit diminishing returns and threshold effects?** The Lattice 2025 data and multiple community analyses suggest a roughly linear relationship across broad grade ranges, but the relationship within elite cohorts (where all members have high MFS) may be non-linear. No formal non-linear model has been fit and compared against linear models in a peer-reviewed climbing-specific publication.

7. **Can individual response to training (inter-individual variability in gains) be predicted by baseline test scores?** The Devise et al. 2022 training study found a significant negative correlation (r = −0.52) between initial endurance level and endurance gain — suggesting those with lower baseline endurance benefit most from training. Whether this extends to MFS, CF, or RFD, and whether it has prescriptive value for periodization, is unknown.
