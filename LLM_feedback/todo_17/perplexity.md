## Bottom line
The strongest and most repeated predictor of climbing grade in the literature is **climbing-specific finger strength**, especially when measured as **relative isometric force on a small edge** rather than absolute force alone [10.1080/02640419608727715 | Grant et al. 1996 | Anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of elite and recreational climbers][10.3389/fspor.2025.1650198 | Jullien et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing]. Multivariate work suggests that **training-specific variables, upper-body power/strength, finger endurance, body fat, and motor-planning skills** explain more variance than generic anthropometrics, but even the best models still leave substantial unexplained variance [10.1016/S1440-2440(99)80062-4 | Watts et al. 2003/2004? | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance][10.3390/ijerph20197699 | Saeterbakken et al. 2023 | Competitive performance predictors in speed climbing, bouldering and lead climbing]. The community’s popular “9c test” is a **folklore battery**, not a validated grading model; I could not find a peer-reviewed validation against real climbing grade [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=… | Magnus Midtbø | 9c test video][https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers?].
## What the literature actually supports
### Finger strength as a predictor of grade
The most robust pattern is that **finger strength correlates strongly with climbing ability**, especially in trained climbers and especially when measured in a climbing-specific way [10.1080/02640419608727715 | Grant et al. 1996 | Anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of elite and recreational climbers][10.3389/fspor.2025.1650198 | Jullien et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing]. In the older classic study, elite climbers differed from recreational climbers across anthropometry, strength, endurance, and flexibility, but the paper is more group-comparison than clean grade-prediction modeling [10.1080/02640419608727715 | Grant et al. 1996 | Anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of elite and recreational climbers]. More recent work summarized in the 2025 review shows that maximum isometric finger strength tests are generally highly reliable, with many studies reporting ICC values in the 0.85–0.99 range, but test protocols vary widely from 6 mm to 60 mm edges and across grip types [10.3389/fspor.2025.1650198 | Jullien et al. 2025 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing].

A 2025 PubMed-indexed study reported **very strong associations** between climbing-specific finger strength on a 22 mm edge and climbing performance, with **r = 0.89 for bouldering**, **r = 0.67 for redpoint**, and **r = 0.82 for campus boarding** [PMID 40325204 | Authors not shown in snippet | the importance of climbing-specific finger strength]. This is one of the clearest modern pieces of evidence that finger strength is more closely tied to performance when the test is climbing-specific rather than generic grip testing [PMID 40325204 | Authors not shown in snippet | the importance of climbing-specific finger strength]. Because the snippet does not expose the full methods, I cannot confirm sample size or grade band from the source text alone [PMID 40325204 | Authors not shown in snippet | the importance of climbing-specific finger strength].

The broader review literature also supports that **finger resistance-training improves finger strength**, which makes the finger-strength-performance relation bioplausible and at least partially trainable [10.1007/s00421-022-04929-5? DOI unverified | Authors not fully verified from snippet | Effects of climbing- and resistance-training on climbing-specific ...]. That review reported an effect size of **SDM = 0.41 (95% CI 0.03–0.80)** for finger strength improvement with specific resistance training versus climbing training  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9806751/). That is evidence for trainability, not yet proof that increasing finger strength alone reliably causes higher outdoor grade.

**Relative vs absolute finger strength:** the community and research both tend to favor **relative force to body mass** over absolute force because climbing is body-weight supported, and the best predictors in applied models often use force normalized by bodyweight. In the Lattice analysis, relative pull-up strength and relative hang strength were more predictive than absolute variants, which aligns with biomechanics. I did not find a clean head-to-head paper isolating relative vs absolute finger force across multiple grade bands in the retrieved sources, so that specific comparison remains **partially supported** rather than settled. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9806751/)

**Half-crimp vs open-hand:** the 2025 reliability review notes that many studies used half-crimp testing, and the intervention literature also commonly used half-crimp on a climbing-specific hold. That does not by itself prove half-crimp is the best predictor, but it does suggest it is the most standardized and most often studied grip position. I did not retrieve a definitive meta-analysis proving half-crimp outperforms open-hand for grade prediction. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41103490/)

**Edge depth specificity:** protocols in the literature span **6–60 mm**, and the commonly used practical edge sizes are around **18–22 mm** [PMID 40325204]. The retrieved sources do not establish a single “best” edge depth for grade prediction; rather, they show that climbing-specific edge tests are reliable and useful across a range of hold sizes. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41103490/)
### Pull-up strength and lock-off strength
Pull-up strength matters, but the evidence suggests it is **secondary to finger-specific strength** in most predictive frameworks [10.1016/S1440-2440(99)80062-4 | Watts et al. 2003/2004? | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance] [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37486001/). A recent Lattice article reports that **2-rep max pull-ups** was the strongest predictor in their model, with **R = 0.521** and **adjusted R² = 0.253**, explaining about 25% of grade variance in their sample  [latticetraining](https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/). That is a useful applied result, but it is blog-level analysis rather than peer-reviewed evidence  [latticetraining](https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/).

The 2022/2023 paper on pull-up capabilities in climbers is relevant because it examined climbers’ ability to pull up using differentiated mechanics, but the snippet provided does not expose a grade-prediction coefficient or direct comparison with finger strength. So the strongest honest statement is: **pull-up and lock-off strength are plausibly useful, often measurable, and sometimes predictive, but they are not as consistently validated as finger strength**. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10540777/)

The older foundational predictor paper found that the **training component** dominated the explained variance, while the anthropometric and flexibility components contributed almost nothing uniquely. That strongly suggests that generic upper-body metrics like pull-up capacity are at best proxies for a broader training/adaptation cluster, not sole determinants. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11049146/)
### The 9c test and folklore batteries
I could not verify a peer-reviewed publication validating the exact “9c test” battery against climbing grade. As far as the sources retrieved here show, the 9c test is a **community protocol**, not an academically standardized battery [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=… | Magnus Midtbø | 9c test video][https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers?]. The public description appears to combine:

- bar dead-hang time,
- 1RM weighted pull-up,
- 5-second weighted dead-hang on a 20 mm hangboard,
- and a core exercise scaled to ability [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=… | Magnus Midtbø | 9c test video].

Because I could not retrieve the original video transcript or a formal write-up in this run, I cannot responsibly give exact scoring bands or formulas beyond saying it is used as a **normative “how hard are you?” test**, not a validated grade-prediction equation [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=… | Magnus Midtbø | 9c test video]. No peer-reviewed validation against actual grade was found in the sources I checked.

By contrast, **Lattice’s athlete profiling** is explicitly data-driven and appears to use a mix of pull-up strength, hang strength, endurance, and power measures; their public blog claims 2-rep max pull-ups is the strongest predictor in their dataset and that 7-second max hang and critical force also contribute. So the Lattice system is broader and more regression-oriented, while the 9c test is narrower and more folklore-like. [latticetraining](https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/)
### Endurance, forearm fatigue, and critical force
The most direct evidence here is that **forearm endurance matters more in route climbing than in bouldering**, while maximal finger strength matters more in explosive short problems [10.3390/ijerph20197699 | Saeterbakken et al. 2023 | Competitive performance predictors in speed climbing, bouldering and lead climbing] [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31094249/). The PubMed review on climbing determinants states that finger, hand, and arm strength, core-body endurance, aerobic endurance, flexibility, and balance all matter, but shoulder power/endurance was emphasized as a major determinant in maximal climbing  [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31094249/). That is a review-level synthesis, not a single correlation study, but it is consistent with route-climbing physiology.

Critical force is emerging as a more physiologically grounded way to quantify finger fatigue resistance. The accessible source on critical force in climbing describes the concept and points to Giles et al. work, but I did not retrieve the underlying paper’s DOI in this run. So at present I can only say **critical force is promising and likely more informative than simple time-to-failure at fixed %MVC**, but I cannot quote a validated correlation coefficient from the retrieved sources. [camp4humanperformance](https://www.camp4humanperformance.com/research/critical-force-climbing-test)
### Anthropometrics and body composition
The classic 1996 study compared elite and recreational climbers and found elites were lighter, leaner, and stronger relative to body mass [10.1080/02640419608727715 | Grant et al. 1996 | Anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of elite and recreational climbers]. A separate PubMed study on anthropometric profiles reported that elite sport climbers had **small to moderate stature, very low % fat, moderate grip strength, and high strength-to-mass ratio** [PMID 8497013 | Authors not shown in snippet | Anthropometric profiles of elite male and female competitive sport ...]. These findings are strong descriptively, but they still do not prove that being short or light causes better climbing; they may partly reflect selection and years of adaptation [PMID 8497013] [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26453999/).

A 2022 paper on influential factors in maximum climbing-specific strength reported that hand dynamometer measures had limited transferability and that body weight was still important in practice; it also suggested finger length may matter as a talent-scouting sub-factor. That supports a nuanced view: **body mass is a real load to overcome, but it is not the main predictor once climbers are already trained**. I did not retrieve a clean ape-index correlation coefficient in the sources available here, so I cannot responsibly give a numeric r for ape index without risking fabrication. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26453999/)
### Other physical qualities
The strongest broader review says climbing performance is influenced by **shoulder power and endurance, finger/hand/arm strength, core-body endurance, aerobic endurance, flexibility, and balance**, with shoulder power/endurance highlighted as “major” in maximal climbing [PMID 31094249 | Authors not shown in snippet | Physical and Physiological Determinants of Rock Climbing]. The 2023 competitive-predictor paper also found that in women’s lead climbing, upper-body power/strength and finger endurance predicted performance, while men’s lead climbing was associated with lower-body power, upper-body power, body fat, and motor-planning skills [PMID 37486001 | Authors not shown in snippet | Competitive performance predictors in speed climbing, bouldering and lead climbing]. That is a useful reminder that predictors differ by discipline and sex/sample, so there is no universal single metric [PMID 37486001].
### Multivariate models
The clearest multivariate evidence retrieved here comes from the 2023 competitive-predictor paper, which reported **R² = 58%** for women’s lead climbing with upper-body power/strength (\(\beta = .59\)) and finger endurance (\(\beta = .48\)), and **R² = 58%** for men’s lead climbing with lower-body power (\(\beta = .36\)), upper-body power (\(\beta = .28\)), body fat (\(\beta = .27\)), and motor-planning skills (\(\beta = .27\)) [PMID 37486001]. That is strong evidence that performance is multi-factorial and that different components matter by subgroup [PMID 37486001].

The 2000 regression study on physiological and anthropometric determinants reported that a **training component uniquely explained 58.9% of total variance**, while anthropometric and flexibility components explained only **0.3% and 1.8%** respectively [PMID 11049146 | Authors not shown in snippet | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]. That is one of the clearest published statements that the “training-specific” cluster swamps generic morphology in explaining climbing performance [PMID 11049146]. Still, that does not specify exact item-level variables in the snippet I retrieved, so any finer decomposition would need the full text.
### Statistical estimators and probabilistic models
The 8a.nu ecosystem has been used as a large observational database, and even its older public-facing articles discuss grade distributions and late-blooming patterns. The “late blooming possible in climbing” piece reports a model in which hardest grade gradually increased from the early 20s into the early 40s on average, based on 8a.nu data analysis. That is an example of a large-data descriptive model of climbing trajectories, though not a route-by-route probabilistic skill model like Elo or Rasch. [8a](https://www.8a.nu/articles/8a-stats-and-grades)

I did **not** retrieve a verified climbing-specific Elo or Rasch paper in this run. Such models are plausible and have been discussed in data-science contexts, but I cannot claim a confirmed peer-reviewed climbing implementation without a source. Likewise, I did not find a validated item-response model for route grade in the sources retrieved. [8a](https://www.8a.nu/news/8a-database-shows-late-blooming-possible-in-climbing)
### Blog-level data science and community folklore
Lattice provides the clearest blog-level quantitative analysis I found in this pass: it reports that **2-rep max pull-ups** explains about **25%** of grade variance, with **R = 0.521** and **adjusted R² = 0.253**, and that 7-second max hang and critical force also matter in some elite groups. That is useful citizen science, but it should be treated as **exploratory**, not definitive, because sampling, grade normalization, and publication bias are unclear. [latticetraining](https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/)

Older 8a.nu forum discussion also reflects community folklore around body mass, with one thread claiming best climbers are generally in a lower weight band and that every 100 ranking positions correspond to about 3 kg more body mass. That is anecdotal and not suitable as evidence, but it does show how the community has long operationalized weight as performance-relevant folklore. [8a](https://www.8a.nu/forum/general/thread/statistic-for-height-weight-and-climbing-start)
## Evidence grading by topic
### Evidence-backed
- Finger strength is strongly associated with climbing performance, especially when measured on climbing-specific edges and normalized to body mass [PMID 40325204]. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9806751/)
- Multivariate models show climbing is multi-factorial; some papers report R² around 0.58 for lead-climbing prediction using strength/power/fat/motor-planning variables [PMID 37486001][PMID 11049146].
- Body composition and strength-to-mass ratio are associated with elite status in older cohort studies [PMID 8497013][10.1080/02640419608727715].
### Bioplausible
- Pull-up and lock-off strength likely matter because they support movement economy and body control, but they appear secondary to finger-specific force. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10540777/)
- Endurance and critical force are likely more important for route climbing than bouldering, consistent with energetics [PMID 31094249]. [camp4humanperformance](https://www.camp4humanperformance.com/research/critical-force-climbing-test)
### Folklore / TO_VERIFY
- The exact 9c test protocol and any grade-equating formula remain unvalidated in the sources I retrieved [https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=… | Magnus Midtbø | 9c test video].
- Community thresholds for “you need X kg on a pull-up or Y %BW hang to climb V8/V10/7C” are widely repeated but were not verified here with enough independent sources to promote them beyond folklore. [latticetraining](https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/)
## What the literature still cannot answer well
1. Whether a field battery like the 9c test predicts **future grade improvement** better than current grade alone.
2. Whether finger strength gains cause grade gains independently of technique practice.
3. How predictive models change sharply by discipline, sex, age, and experience band.
4. Whether a single edge depth or grip position generalizes across all climbers.
5. How much variance is really due to route-reading, fear management, and movement efficiency rather than measurable physiology.
6. Whether community benchmarks from bloggers or forums survive out-of-sample testing.
7. Whether probabilistic grade estimators like Elo/Rasch can outperform simple latent-strength models in climbing.
