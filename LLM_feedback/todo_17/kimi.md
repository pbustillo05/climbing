## 1. Finger Strength as a Predictor of Climbing Grade

**Scientific evidence — cross-sectional correlations.** Maximal isometric finger strength (MIFS) is the most robust single predictor of self-reported and measured climbing grade across dozens of studies. In a 2025 systematic review of reliability studies, MIFS assessed on a 23-mm edge in a seated bent-arm (90°) position correlated with bouldering grade at r = 0.73 and with sport-climbing grade at r = 0.64 (n = 26, intermediate-to-elite, self-reported grade) [10.1080/02640414.2025.2477863 | Vigouroux et al. 2025 | Assessing climbing-specific strength: The impact of body position]. A 2024 study of 19 male climbers (moderate-to-high level, measured bouldering and redpoint performance on steep terrain) reported climbing-specific finger strength on a 22-mm edge explained ~80 % of bouldering variance (r² ≈ 0.80) and ~45 % of redpoint variance (r² ≈ 0.45), with Pearson r values stronger than those for generic handgrip or pull-up strength [PMC12479556 | Stien et al. 2024 | Climbing performance in males: the importance of climbing-specific finger strength]. Another systematic review noted correlations as high as r = 0.89 for bouldering and r = 0.77 for redpoint when finger strength is expressed relative to body mass [S2095254624001303 | Sport climbing performance determinants and functional testing methods: A systematic review]. In a canonical analysis of 30 experienced sport climbers, maximal relative finger strength carried the highest canonical weight (0.490) among all physical, technical and mental predictors [PMC3661882 | Magiera et al. 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber].

**Relative vs. absolute MIFS.** Relative force (N·kg⁻¹ or % bodyweight) consistently outperforms absolute force. A 2025 study reported relative finger strength correlations of r = 0.425–0.767 with climbing performance versus r = 0.422–0.741 for absolute strength [10.1080/02640414.2025.2477863 | Vigouroux et al. 2025 | Assessing climbing-specific strength]. This aligns with the strength-to-weight ratio principle: in gravity-dependent sports, scaling force to body mass captures functional relevance better than raw force.

**Grip position and edge depth.** The half-crimp (HC) position is repeatedly identified as a major performance discriminator, explaining much of the difference between advanced and elite climbers [PMC12521219 | Reliability of finger strength assessment methods in climbing]. The 20-mm edge has become the de facto research and community benchmark (Lattice Training standard), though studies have used 18–23 mm edges with little systematic comparison of which depth yields the *best* correlation. One study used a 22-mm edge at 30° decline [PMC12479556 | Stien et al. 2024 | Climbing performance in males], while another found a 23-mm edge reliable (ICC > 0.95) across four body positions [10.1080/02640414.2025.2477863 | Vigouroux et al. 2025 | Assessing climbing-specific strength].

**Multivariate context.** In canonical and regression models that include endurance, technique, and anthropometric variables, finger strength retains the strongest independent weight. In Magiera et al.’s canonical model it was the dominant predictor (weight 0.490), followed by mental endurance (−0.410) and technique (0.370) [PMC3661882 | Magiera et al. 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber].

**Evidence tier:** Evidence-backed. All data are cross-sectional; no randomized trial has shown that improving finger strength *causes* grade improvement (see Section 10).

## 2. Pull-Up Strength and Lock-Off Strength as Predictors

**Scientific evidence.** Correlations between pull-up performance and climbing grade are weaker and more heterogeneous than for finger strength. A systematic review reported construct validity correlations for pull-up tests ranging from r = 0.08 to 0.72 with self-reported climbing ability [PMC10203485 | Langer et al. 2023 | Physical performance testing in climbing—A systematic review]. In a 2024 study of 28 advanced male climbers (IRCRA 23 ± 2.43), maximal pull-up reps correlated with climbing level at r = 0.61 (p < 0.05), comparable to finger-hang endurance (r = 0.54–0.57) [10.3389/fspor.2024.1410636 | Rokowski et al. 2024 | Importance of shoulder girdle and finger flexor muscle endurance in advanced male climbers]. A separate study found that weighted pull-up 1RM did not add independent predictive variance once finger strength was entered into the model; finger strength remained the principal determinant [PMC12479556 | Stien et al. 2024 | Climbing performance in males].

**Relative vs. absolute pull-up strength.** No study was identified that directly compared relative vs. absolute pull-up 1RM as predictors in a head-to-head regression. However, because climbing involves moving body mass against gravity, relative upper-body strength is bioplausibly more relevant than absolute strength.

**Lock-off / bent-arm hang.** Bent-arm hang time (90° elbow flexion) is a stronger predictor than maximal pull-ups. Mermier et al. (2000) reported bent-arm hang time correlated at r = 0.68 (p < 0.001) with route performance, making it one of the strongest individual predictors after grip strength endurance [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance].

**Community benchmarks (folklore).** Community norms for weighted pull-up 1RM by grade band circulate on coaching blogs and Reddit, but no peer-reviewed normative table was identified. The claim that "finger strength matters more than pull-up strength past V7" is widely repeated in r/climbharder and Lattice Training content but lacks controlled empirical support; it is **TO_VERIFY** (fewer than three independent peer-reviewed sources).

**Evidence tier:** Evidence-backed for correlation, but effect sizes are smaller than for finger strength. Cross-sectional only.

## 3. The 9c Test (Magnus Midtbø) and Community Protocols

**Description (folklore).** The "9c Strength Test" is a four-exercise field battery popularized by Norwegian climber Magnus Midtbø. The protocol, as documented on thecrag.com, awards up to 10 points per exercise (40 points total = 9c) [https://www.thecrag.com/en/climbing/virtual/route/4430259711 | thecrag.com community | ★★★9c Strength Test]:

- **Exercise 1 / Max finger strength:** 5-second dead-hang on ~20 mm crimp. Scoring: 1 pt = 100 % bodyweight (BW), 2 pt = 110 %, 3 pt = 120 %, 4 pt = 130 %, 5 pt = 140 %, 6 pt = 150 %, 7 pt = 160 %, 8 pt = 180 %, 9 pt = 200 %, 10 pt = 220 %.
- **Exercise 2 / Max pull-up:** 1RM weighted pull-up. Same percentage-of-BW scale as Exercise 1.
- **Exercise 3 / Core strength:** L-sit (bent knee → straight → front lever progression). 1 pt = 10 s bent-knee L-sit; 4 pt = 10 s straight L-sit; 7 pt = 5 s front lever; 10 pt = 30 s front lever.
- **Exercise 4 / Bar dead-hang:** Unweighted bar hang for time (details truncated in available sources).

Grade mapping: 40 pt = 9c, 39 = 9b+, 38 = 9b, 36 = 9a+, 34 = 9a, 30 = 8c, 25 = 8b, 20 = 7c+, 15 = 7b+, 10 = 7a, 5 = 6c, 1 = 6a.

**Evidence evaluation.** No peer-reviewed study validating the 9c test battery against objectively measured climbing grade was identified in PubMed, Scopus, or Web of Science. The four components are *individually* supported as grade predictors (finger strength, pull-up strength, core endurance, and hanging endurance each correlate with grade in isolation), but no study has tested whether their sum or point scale improves prediction beyond a single strongest predictor. Community-level validation attempts (e.g., r/climbharder threads compiling scores) were searched but no published compilation with regression statistics was found. **Evidence tier: Folklore / TO_VERIFY.**

**Comparison with Lattice athlete profiling.** Lattice Training’s publicly documented battery focuses on a 20-mm edge maximal hang (7–10 s, one-arm or two-arm, expressed as % BW), critical force/endurance testing, and pull-up strength [https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers]. The 9c test and Lattice battery overlap on finger strength and pull-up strength, but Lattice adds explicit endurance metrics (critical force, T80/T60/T50 time-to-failure tests) and does not use a front-lever progression. Lattice presents data as regression-predicted grade probabilities rather than a fixed point scale [https://strengthclimbing.com/finger-strength-analyzer/ | StrengthClimbing / Jędrzej S | Climbing Finger Strength Analyzer].

**Limitations and critique.** The 9c test does not capture technique, footwork, movement efficiency, route-reading, fear management, flexibility, or hip mobility. Its discriminant validity is likely poor at lower grades (roughly < 7a / V5) where technique dominates over raw strength. At elite grades it may suffer from ceiling effects if world-class climbers cluster near the 40-point maximum. The arbitrary 10-point step sizes (e.g., 160 % to 180 % BW) have no published psychometric justification.

## 4. Aerobic Capacity, Forearm Endurance, and Lactate Threshold

**Critical Force (CF).** A 2025 study (n = 132, 7 countries) tested a free-weight critical force protocol (ff-CF) on a 20-mm edge. After adjusting for sex, ff-CF expressed as % BW explained 61 % of variance in sport-climbing grade and 26 % in bouldering grade; W′ (anaerobic work capacity above CF) explained 7 % (sport) and 34 % (bouldering). Combined, CF + W′ explained 66 % of sport performance and 44 % of bouldering performance [https://www.camp4humanperformance.com/research/critical-force-climbing-test | Camp4 Human Performance / Fryer et al. 2025 | Critical Force in Climbing]. By contrast, a Lattice Training regression analysis reported CF had an adjusted R² of only 0.020 (2 %) in their dataset, though the p-value was significant (0.035) [https://latticetraining.com/blog/what-is-the-number-1-measure-of-performance-for-sport-climbers/ | Lattice Training | What is the number 1 measure of performance for sport climbers]. The discrepancy likely reflects differences in sample heterogeneity, grade range, and whether CF is tested in isolation or alongside maximal strength.

**Forearm endurance (time-to-failure).** Intermittent finger endurance tests (e.g., 10:10 or 7:3 work:rest ratios) discriminate between ability levels. A study of women found the highest correlations (R = 0.429) when 9 % force deviation and 1 s pulling-time deviation were tolerated [10.3389/fspor.2022.902521 | Frontiers in Sports and Active Living 2022 | Optimization of an Intermittent Finger Endurance Test]. In Mermier et al.’s canonical model, isometric finger endurance (E70%z10/10) carried a weight of 0.340, the fourth-strongest predictor [PMC3661882 | Magiera et al. 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber].

**VO₂max and blood lactate.** VO₂max measured on a cycle ergometer did not differentiate climbers of varying ability in at least one study [PMC3661882 | Magiera et al. 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber]. Blood lactate accumulates with climbing difficulty, but lactate threshold has not emerged as an independent grade predictor in multivariate models; it is more commonly discussed as a mechanistic correlate of forearm pump rather than a statistical predictor of grade [https://thesportjournal.org/article/analysis-of-contemporary-anaerobic-sport-specific-training-techniques-for-rock-climbing/ | Mabe & Butler 2016 | Analysis of Contemporary Anaerobic Sport Specific Training Techniques for Rock Climbing].

**Community discussion.** The community dichotomy—"endurance limits route climbers, strength limits boulderers"—is broadly supported by the CF data (CF predicts sport better than bouldering, W′ predicts bouldering better) [https://www.camp4humanperformance.com/research/critical-force-climbing-test | Camp4 Human Performance / Fryer et al. 2025 | Critical Force in Climbing]. Grade thresholds (e.g., "endurance becomes the bottleneck at 5.12+") are folklore without consensus cutoffs.

**Evidence tier:** Evidence-backed for CF correlations; Bioplausible for lactate threshold as a direct grade predictor.

## 5. Anthropometrics and Body Composition

**Ape index.** Elite international boulderers show a higher ape index (1.06 ± 0.02) than national-level competitors (1.03 ± 0.03) [10.3389/fspor.2025.1588414 | Frontiers in Sports and Active Living 2025 | Morphology of male world cup and elite bouldering athletes]. However, when training variables are statistically controlled, ape index explains negligible variance. Mermier et al. (2000) found the anthropometric component (height, weight, ape index, % body fat) explained only 0.3 % of total performance variance in a multiple regression model (R² total = 66.3 %) [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]. Magiera et al.’s canonical analysis gave ape index a weight of −0.319, but the authors noted its role was "not fully explained" and may reflect collinearity with shoulder width rather than reach advantage [PMC3661882 | Magiera et al. 2013 | The Structure of Performance of a Sport Rock Climber].

**Body mass and body fat.** Elite male lead climbers at the >8c redpoint level show very low body fat (7.95 %), and male boulderers >8B average 7.43 % [https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10212580/ | Ginszt et al. 2023 | Body Composition, Anthropometric Parameters, and Strength-Endurance Characteristics of Sport Climbers: A Systematic Review]. Lower body mass distinguishes elite lead climbers from speed climbers and less advanced climbers. However, within trained climbing populations, the *variance* in body fat explains almost none of the *variance* in grade once strength and endurance are accounted for [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance].

**Finger morphology.** One ultrasound study of 65 recreational climbers found that higher-ability climbers exhibited greater tendon-to-bone distance (TBD) differences between relaxed and flexed states, suggesting adaptive laxity or thickening of the A2 pulley system with training [PMC12138156 | Gender Differences in the Morphology of the Finger Flexor Pulley System]. No study was identified linking A2 pulley cross-sectional area or finger length to climbing grade in a predictive regression.

**Community folklore.** The belief that a high ape index (>1.0) is a structural prerequisite for elite climbing is widespread on r/climbharder and coaching blogs. The empirical evidence contradicts this: ape index is not a significant predictor when training is controlled. The "lighter is always better" meme is also contested; evidence shows elite climbers are lean, but deliberate weight cutting in non-elite climbers carries injury risk and no evidence-based performance payoff. **Evidence tier for folklore claims: TO_VERIFY.**

## 6. Other Physical Qualities Studied as Predictors

**Shoulder strength and endurance.** Elite climbers demonstrate greater shoulder girdle endurance than recreational climbers and non-climbers [10.1080/026404101750238953 | Grant et al. 2001 | A comparison of the anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of female elite and recreational climbers and non-climbers]. In advanced male climbers, shoulder girdle endurance (Edlinger test, pull-up test) correlates with grade at r = 0.60–0.61, comparable to finger endurance [10.3389/fspor.2024.1410636 | Rokowski et al. 2024 | Importance of shoulder girdle and finger flexor muscle endurance]. Michailov and Baláš found finger strength correlates more strongly with performance than shoulder girdle strength [10.3389/fspor.2024.1410636 | Rokowski et al. 2024 | Importance of shoulder girdle and finger flexor muscle endurance].

**Hip flexibility.** Grant et al. (2001) found elite female climbers had greater hip flexibility than recreational climbers and non-climbers [10.1080/026404101750238953 | Grant et al. 2001 | A comparison of the anthropometric, strength, endurance and flexibility characteristics of female elite and recreational climbers and non-climbers]. However, Mermier et al. (2000) found the flexibility component (hip and shoulder ROM) explained only 1.8 % of performance variance [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]. Climbing-specific flexibility tests (e.g., foot raise from a climbing position) correlate more strongly with grade than generic sit-and-reach tests [https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1130812/full | Langer et al. 2023 | Physical performance testing in climbing—A systematic review].

**Core strength.** Core tests (plank, Superman, leg raise, fishing kicks) show low to moderate correlations with climbing ability. "Momentum absorption" tests correlated at r = −0.01 to −0.31, and leg raises at r = 0.30–0.45 [https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2023.1130812/full | Langer et al. 2023 | Physical performance testing in climbing—A systematic review]. No core test emerged as an independent predictor in multivariate models.

**Lower-body strength.** No significant correlation between lower-body strength (knee extension/flexion, leg press) and climbing grade was identified in the reviewed literature. Lower-body power is considered important for movement efficiency but not a statistical discriminator of grade in cross-sectional samples [https://medcraveonline.com/MOJSM/MOJSM-08-00183.pdf | MedCrave 2025 | Specific strength training strategies across body regions].

**Years climbing / experience.** Mermier et al. reported years of climbing experience correlated with performance at r = 0.55 (p < 0.01) [10.1136/bjsm.34.5.359 | Mermier et al. 2000 | Physiological and anthropometric determinants of sport climbing performance]. Self-rated climbing ability (a proxy for technique and experience) correlated at r = 0.70 (p < 0.001).
