### 2. Narrative synthesis

#### Sub-section 1: Sleep

**General athletic performance**

- **Sleep extension** of ≈+110 min/night for 5–7 weeks in collegiate basketball players improved sprint time by ~4.3% and shooting accuracy by ~9%, and reduced PVT reaction time and sleepiness. [Evidence-backed, Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3119836/)  
  These are whole-body, skill and neuromotor outcomes, not finger-specific, but they indicate that extending sleep from <7 h to ≈8.5 h meaningfully improves high-intensity repeated efforts and reaction time—both relevant to route-reading and decision-making in climbing.

- **Sleep restriction** consistently impairs maximal strength, power, and reaction time in broader literature (e.g., partial sleep deprivation reducing 1RM and isokinetic torque), though effect sizes vary (often d≈0.3–0.8). [Evidence-backed, Functional] (inferred from multiple strength-sport studies; not climbing-specific).

**Climbing-specific data**

- No controlled studies directly link sleep duration/quality to climbing performance, grip strength, or finger endurance. [Bioplausible, Functional]  
  Extrapolation from grip and isometric strength data in other sports suggests similar vulnerability of finger flexor force to sleep loss, but this remains untested in climbers.

**Connective tissue and collagen synthesis**

- Slow-wave sleep is a major window for growth hormone secretion, which supports collagen synthesis; chronic sleep restriction reduces nocturnal GH pulses and IGF-1. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]  
  However, there are **no direct human trials** linking sleep manipulation to tendon collagen synthesis or injury rates. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

- Given that pulley and flexor tendon remodeling occurs over days–weeks, chronic partial sleep loss is mechanistically likely to slow matrix turnover and repair, but this is extrapolated from endocrine data, not tendon biopsies. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

**Practical recommendations for climbers**

- **Target duration:** 8–9 h/night for high training loads, mirroring sleep-extension protocols that improved performance. [Bioplausible→Evidence-backed extrapolation, Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3119836/)  
- **Timing:** Consistent bed/wake times, minimizing late-evening blue light and heavy meals to preserve slow-wave sleep. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]  
- **Sleep hygiene:** Dark, cool room; pre-sleep wind-down; limiting caffeine after early afternoon. These are standard clinical recommendations, not tested in climbers. [Folklore: general sports sleep practice, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3119836/)  

For finger/forearm/connective tissue, sleep should be treated as a **global rate-limiter**: it does not selectively accelerate tendon vs muscle recovery, but inadequate sleep likely constrains both neuromuscular performance and collagen remodeling timelines. [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]

#### Sub-section 2: Heart rate variability (HRV) monitoring

**What HRV measures**

- HRV (typically RMSSD or high-frequency power) reflects beat-to-beat variability driven largely by parasympathetic activity; higher resting HRV generally indicates greater autonomic recovery and lower acute stress load. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [Europe PMC](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/17849143)  

**HRV-guided vs fixed-schedule training**

- In Kiviniemi et al. (2007), HRV-guided endurance training over 4 weeks produced greater gains in maximal running velocity than predefined training (ΔLoadmax 0.9±0.2 vs 0.5±0.4 km·h⁻¹; p=0.048; d≈0.7). VO₂peak improved significantly only in the HRV group. [Evidence-backed, Functional]   [Europe PMC](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/17849143)  

- A meta-analysis of HRV-guided training (Granero-Gallegos et al., 2020) reported a small but significant advantage for VO₂max (ES≈0.40; HRV-guided vs control ES difference ≈0.19). [Evidence-backed, Functional]   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/17/21/7999)  

These data show that HRV-guided load adjustment can modestly improve endurance adaptations, presumably by avoiding training on days of autonomic “under-recovery.” [Evidence-backed, Surrogate→Functional]

**Thresholds and decision rules**

- Common field rules (e.g., train hard if morning RMSSD within ±1 SD of 7–10 d rolling mean; reduce load if below) are derived from Kiviniemi’s protocol and subsequent coaching practice, not systematically validated across sports. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]   [Europe PMC](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/17849143)  

- No RCT has compared **different HRV thresholds** or algorithms for “ready to train” decisions. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

**Device algorithms**

- Commercial wearables (Whoop, Garmin, Oura, HRV4Training) generally validate resting HRV (RMSSD) against ECG with small biases, but their **readiness scores** and proprietary recovery indices have not been validated against performance or injury outcomes in climbers. [Evidence-backed for raw HRV, Surrogate; Bioplausible for readiness scores, Functional]  

**Relevance to tendon/pulley readiness**

- HRV primarily reflects autonomic and cardiovascular status; it is sensitive to global load, illness, and psychological stress. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]  

- There is **no evidence** that HRV tracks local tendon or pulley fatigue or microdamage. Tendon pathology often progresses with minimal systemic stress response. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]  

- For climbers, HRV can help avoid **systemic overreaching** (which indirectly protects connective tissue by limiting total load), but it cannot indicate whether A2 pulleys or flexor tendons have recovered from high strain hangboard sessions. [Bioplausible, Functional]

#### Sub-section 3: Contrast therapy (cold/hot alternation)

**Mechanism**

- Alternating cold and hot water induces cycles of vasoconstriction/vasodilation, theoretically enhancing convective removal of metabolites and promoting reperfusion. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2026.1738075/full)  [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01800-1)  

**Evidence vs passive rest**

- Most RCTs and meta-analyses pool contrast therapy with CWI and other hydrotherapies; specific contrast-only data are limited. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01800-1)  

- In Moore et al. (2023), CWI was **more effective than contrast water therapy** and warm water for soreness and CK reduction after strenuous exercise; contrast was not clearly superior to passive rest for performance recovery. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01800-1)  

- Dupuy et al. (2018) similarly found small-to-moderate benefits of hydrotherapies on DOMS and CK, but contrast therapy’s effects were modest and often not superior to CWI. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40279-022-01800-1)  

**Connective tissue–specific evidence**

- No RCTs have examined contrast therapy on tendon or ligament recovery (e.g., collagen turnover, tendon stiffness, or injury rates). [Bioplausible, Surrogate]  

**Cold-water immersion (CWI) and hypertrophy**

- Acute and chronic post-strength CWI (10°C, 10–20 min) attenuates anabolic signaling and hypertrophy: Roberts et al. (2015) showed greater increases in type II fiber CSA (+17%), isokinetic work (+19%), and satellite cell responses in active recovery vs CWI over 12 weeks. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [QUT ePrints](https://eprints.qut.edu.au/86123/)  

- A recent meta-analysis (“Throwing cold water on muscle growth”) reported that RT alone produced SMD 0.36 (95% CrI 0.10–0.61) in hypertrophy vs CWI+RT SMD 0.14 (95% CrI −0.08–0.36); comparative cSMD −0.22 (95% CrI −0.47 to 0.04), suggesting small attenuation of hypertrophy. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11235606/?utm)  

For climbers doing fingerboard and heavy pulling, frequent post-session CWI may blunt muscle hypertrophy of forearm flexors—potentially undesirable if strength is a limiting factor. [Bioplausible→Evidence-backed extrapolation, Functional]

**Practical protocol (if used)**

- Meta-analyses suggest CWI at 10–15°C for 10–15 min, whole or partial limb immersion, reduces DOMS and CK at 24–48 h without clear performance benefit. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9896520/)  [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2026.1738075/full)  

- For climbers, **occasional** CWI/contrast (e.g., during competition blocks or dense training weeks) may be justified to manage soreness, but **routine use after every strength/hangboard session** is likely counterproductive for hypertrophic adaptation. [Bioplausible, Functional]

#### Sub-section 4: Soft-tissue work (massage, foam rolling)

**Foam rolling and massage on recovery**

- Wiewelhove et al. (2019) meta-analysis: post-exercise foam rolling produced small improvements in sprint (+3.1%, g=0.34), strength (+3.9%, g=0.21), and moderate reductions in muscle pain (g=0.47). [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2019.00376/full)  

- Pearcey et al. (2015) showed that 20 min of foam rolling immediately, 24, and 48 h after heavy squats reduced DOMS (d=0.59–0.84) and attenuated decrements in sprint, jump, and strength-endurance (d=0.48–0.87). [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4299735/)  

- Recent RCTs show foam rolling reduces muscle stiffness and improves viscoelastic properties, with modest effects on lactate and CK. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-66577-x)  

**Forearm/finger-specific data**

- There are **no RCTs** specifically on forearm massage or finger flexor soft-tissue work after climbing or isometric finger tasks. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]  

- Mechanistically, soft-tissue work may increase local blood flow, reduce perceived stiffness, and modulate nociception, which could help forearm recovery after pumpy sessions but does not directly affect tendon collagen synthesis. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

**Timing**

- Most studies apply soft-tissue work **immediately post-exercise** and/or on subsequent days; benefits on DOMS and performance are seen across 24–72 h. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4299735/)  [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2019.00376/full)  

- There is no clear evidence that next-day vs immediate timing materially changes outcomes, as long as total dose (e.g., ≥120 s per muscle group) is sufficient. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]   [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-66577-x)  

**Climbing-specific tools**

- Finger/forearm massage tools (e.g., forearm rollers, finger flexor release devices) are widely promoted in climbing communities and by coaches, but lack controlled data. [Folklore: climbing/coaching practice, Surrogate]  

For climbers, soft-tissue work is best viewed as a **symptom-management tool** (DOMS, stiffness, perceived readiness) rather than a driver of tendon healing. [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]

#### Sub-section 5: Active recovery sessions

**Active vs passive recovery**

- In swimmers, 10 min of self-paced active recovery (~69% of trial velocity) after a 200 m maximal effort increased lactate removal (4.30±1.74 vs 1.76±1.70 mmol·L⁻¹; p<0.001) and removal rate (0.43 vs 0.18 mmol·L⁻¹·min⁻¹), without affecting subsequent performance in that session. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5499938/)  

- Other studies comparing active vs passive recovery show faster lactate clearance and sometimes better perceived recovery, but **little consistent benefit** on performance 24 h later. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]   [ijrep.org](https://ijrep.org/effects-of-various-recovery-modalities-on-lactate-clearance-and-subsequent-exercise-performance/)  [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2022.819588/full)  

**What is “active recovery” for climbers?**

- **Bioplausible options**:  
  - Very easy traversing or jug laps at RPE 3–4/10, avoiding high finger force. [Bioplausible, Functional]  
  - Low-load antagonist work (scapular stability, external rotation) and mobility/yoga focusing on shoulders/hips. [Bioplausible, Functional]  
  - Light aerobic work (walking, cycling) to enhance systemic blood flow. [Evidence-backed in other sports, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5499938/)  

**Forearm blood flow and lactate**

- Isometric finger flexor work produces high local occlusion; light dynamic movement of the forearm and hand increases local blood flow and accelerates metabolite clearance, as shown in forearm exercise studies (not climbing-specific). [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]  

- For climbers, low-intensity movement on big holds or off-the-wall forearm mobility likely aids **muscle** recovery (pump, soreness) but does not materially change **tendon** recovery timelines (days–weeks). [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]

#### Sub-section 6: Nutrition timing for connective tissue

**Muscle-focused protein timing**

- Post-exercise protein intake of ~20–40 g high-quality protein (whey, mixed) within ~2 h of exercise maximizes muscle protein synthesis; repeated doses across the day (e.g., 4×20 g) support hypertrophy. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]  

- These data are robust but almost entirely muscle-focused; they do not directly address tendon collagen synthesis. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]

**Connective tissue–specific timing**

- Shaw et al. (2017) showed that 15 g vitamin C–enriched gelatin taken 1 h before 6 min of rope skipping (3×/day for 3 d) doubled serum PINP (collagen I propeptide) vs placebo and improved collagen content and mechanics in engineered ligaments treated with post-supplement serum. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  

- This suggests that **pre-exercise** ingestion of collagen/gelatin plus vitamin C, timed to peak amino acid availability (~1 h), can acutely augment collagen synthesis in response to loading. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate]

- No trials have yet tested this protocol on **clinical tendon outcomes** (pain, stiffness, injury rates) or in climbers. [Bioplausible, Functional]

**Carbohydrate and energy availability**

- Low energy availability is associated with impaired bone and tendon health (e.g., higher stress fracture risk, poorer healing) in endurance and aesthetic sports; mechanisms include reduced IGF-1 and sex hormones. [Evidence-backed, Surrogate & Functional]  

- While no climbing-specific data exist, underfueling likely compromises both muscle and tendon adaptation to high finger loads, increasing injury risk. [Bioplausible→Evidence-backed extrapolation, Functional]

For climbers, **global protein and energy sufficiency** plus **targeted collagen+vitamin C before high-strain tendon loading** is the most defensible connective tissue–oriented nutrition strategy at present. [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]

### 3. Practical recovery protocol (4–5 d·wk⁻¹ climber)

**Assumptions:** Intermediate–advanced climber, 4–5 climbing/hangboard days per week, mixed bouldering and route work, with significant fingerboard or limit bouldering 2–3×/wk.

#### Sleep

- **Target:** 8–9 h/night, with consistent schedule.  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible→Evidence-backed extrapolation, Functional] (based on Mah et al. and broader athlete data).   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3119836/)  

- **Implementation:**  
  - Fixed bedtime/waketime; limit screens and caffeine in the 2–3 h pre-bed; cool, dark room.  
  **Label:** [Folklore: sports sleep hygiene, Surrogate]

#### HRV use

- **Use HRV as a global load gauge, not a tendon gauge.**  
  - Morning RMSSD (or device “recovery score”) tracked over 7–10 d rolling window.  
  - If HRV is within normal range and subjective readiness is good, proceed with planned high-intensity session.  
  - If HRV is markedly depressed for ≥2 days and the climber feels fatigued, downgrade to technique/volume or rest.  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]   [Europe PMC](https://europepmc.org/article/MED/17849143)  [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/17/21/7999)  

- **Do not** rely on HRV to decide whether pulleys/tendons are ready for another max hang; base that on local symptoms (pain, stiffness) and conservative loading progressions.  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible, Functional]

#### Contrast therapy / CWI

- **Routine use after strength/hangboard:**  
  - Avoid regular post-session CWI (10–15°C, 10–15 min) after heavy fingerboard or strength sessions, to minimize risk of blunting hypertrophic adaptations in forearm flexors.  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed extrapolation, Surrogate & Functional]   [QUT ePrints](https://eprints.qut.edu.au/86123/)  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11235606/?utm)  

- **Occasional use:**  
  - In competition blocks or dense training weeks, use partial forearm/hand CWI or contrast 1–2×/wk to manage soreness (e.g., 10–12°C for 10 min, or 1–2 min cold / 2–3 min warm ×3–4 cycles).  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9896520/)  [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/sports-and-active-living/articles/10.3389/fspor.2026.1738075/full)  

#### Soft-tissue work

- **Frequency:** 3–5×/wk, 5–10 min per session.  
  - 60–120 s of rolling/massage per forearm flexor group, plus gentle finger flexor/extensor soft-tissue work.  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed for general foam rolling, Surrogate & Functional; Bioplausible for forearm-specific]   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2019.00376/full)  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4299735/)  

- **Timing:** Immediately post-session or later the same day; repeat on high-DOMS days.  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

- **Climbing-specific tools:** Use if subjectively helpful, but treat as optional.  
  **Label:** [Folklore: climbing/coaching practice, Surrogate]

#### Active recovery

- **1–2 dedicated active recovery days per week** (especially after very hard finger days):  
  - 20–30 min easy aerobic work (walk, bike) at RPE 3–4/10.  
  - 15–20 min easy jug laps or traversing with strict rule: no small holds, no near-failure pump.  
  - 15–20 min antagonist and mobility work (scapular control, rotator cuff, hip mobility).  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed for lactate clearance in other sports, Surrogate; Bioplausible for climbing, Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5499938/)  [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/physiology/articles/10.3389/fphys.2022.819588/full)  

- Goal is to improve **muscle** recovery and maintain movement quality, not to load tendons further.  
  **Label:** [Bioplausible, Functional]

#### Nutrition timing

- **Daily protein:** ~1.6–2.2 g·kg⁻¹·d⁻¹, distributed in 3–4 doses of 20–40 g.  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed (muscle), Surrogate]  

- **Collagen/gelatin protocol for high-strain tendon sessions (e.g., max hangs, campus):**  
  - 15 g gelatin or collagen hydrolysate + 50 mg vitamin C (e.g., orange juice) taken ~60 min before the session, 1–2×/day on those days.  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed (PINP, engineered ligaments), Surrogate; Bioplausible for tendon outcomes, Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  

- **Carbohydrate and energy:**  
  - Ensure adequate carbohydrate around sessions (e.g., 0.5–1 g·kg⁻¹ pre and post) and avoid chronic low energy availability, especially in lean climbers.  
  **Label:** [Evidence-backed in other sports, Surrogate & Functional]

### 4. Open questions

1. **Does sleep extension improve climbing-specific performance and finger flexor strength/endurance?**  
   - No RCTs have tested sleep manipulation on climbing grades, time-to-failure on small edges, or injury rates. [Bioplausible, Functional]

2. **Can HRV-guided periodization reduce finger/pulley injury incidence in climbers?**  
   - Existing HRV trials focus on endurance performance; injury outcomes and local tendon health are untested. [Bioplausible, Functional]

3. **What is the net effect of regular post-session CWI/contrast on finger flexor hypertrophy and tendon stiffness in climbers?**  
   - Muscle data suggest blunted hypertrophy, but tendon stiffness and injury risk under climbing-specific loading remain unknown. [Bioplausible, Surrogate & Functional]

4. **Do forearm/finger-specific soft-tissue interventions meaningfully alter tendon gliding, pulley stress, or injury risk?**  
   - Current evidence is limited to muscle DOMS and performance; tendon-level imaging and biomechanics in climbers are lacking. [Bioplausible, Surrogate]

5. **Does pre-exercise collagen+vitamin C supplementation reduce time to return from pulley/tendon injuries or lower injury incidence in high-volume climbers?**  
   - Shaw et al. provide strong mechanistic support (PINP, engineered ligaments), but no clinical or sport-specific outcome trials exist. [Evidence-backed (mechanistic), Surrogate; Bioplausible (clinical), Functional]   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)
