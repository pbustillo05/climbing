# 2. Narrative Synthesis

# Sub-section 1: Sleep

### Performance outcomes

**[Evidence-backed]** Sleep loss impairs physical performance. A 2022 meta-analysis found overall strength decrements of **−2.85%** (95% CI −4.47 to −1.23) under acute sleep-loss conditions. PM testing showed larger deficits than AM testing. **Functional outcome** (strength tasks). ([PMC][4])

**[Evidence-backed]** A 2025 meta-analysis reported reductions in explosive power, maximum force, speed, and motor control under sleep deprivation. **Functional outcomes**. ([PMC][5])

**[Evidence-backed]** Reaction time slows substantially after one night sleep deprivation (e.g., +15% in college athletes). **Functional cognitive-motor outcome**. ([PMC][8])

### Climbing-specific relevance

**[Bioplausible]** No strong RCTs in climbers. However, climbing depends on reaction time, motor precision, and repeated maximal grip recruitment, domains consistently harmed by sleep loss.

### Connective tissue

**[Bioplausible]** Slow-wave sleep is associated with pulsatile growth hormone secretion, relevant to collagen turnover. Direct RCT evidence linking sleep extension to pulley/tendon healing is absent. Mechanistically, chronic short sleep may impair tendon remodeling through endocrine/inflammatory pathways.

### Practical recommendation

**[Evidence-backed]** Target **7–9 h/night**; many athletes may benefit from **8–10 h opportunity in bed** during heavy blocks.
**[Evidence-backed]** Maintain fixed wake time, dark/cool room, reduce evening alcohol.
**[Folklore: elite coaching practice]** Add 20–30 min nap after poor sleep before evening climbing.

### Bottom line for climbers

Sleep is likely the highest-yield recovery tool for finger force expression and skill consistency, though tendon-specific data are indirect.

# Sub-section 2: Heart Rate Variability (HRV) Monitoring

### What it is

**[Evidence-backed]** HRV quantifies beat-to-beat RR interval variation, reflecting autonomic balance (especially vagal tone). Lower-than-normal HRV often tracks stress, illness, insufficient recovery, or heavy training.

### HRV-guided training evidence

**[Evidence-backed]** Endurance RCTs show small improvements when training intensity is adjusted using HRV versus fixed schedules. Meta-analysis found **ES 0.187** favoring HRV-guided training for VO₂max. ([PubMed][3])

### Ready-to-train thresholds

**[Folklore: endurance coaching]** “Green HRV = train hard, red HRV = rest” is oversimplified.

**[Evidence-backed]** Better methods use rolling baselines (e.g., 7-day LnRMSSD average) and smallest worthwhile change rather than single-day values. ([MDPI][9])

### Device validity

**[Evidence-backed]** Chest-strap ECG remains criterion standard. Smartphone photoplethysmography apps (e.g., HRV4Training) show acceptable agreement in resting conditions. Wearables vary by firmware and sleep artifact handling.

### Tendon/pulley relevance

**[Evidence-backed]** HRV does **not** directly measure tendon readiness.

**[Bioplausible]** HRV may reflect systemic fatigue and readiness to tolerate load, but a normal HRV can coexist with irritated A2 pulley or flexor tendinopathy.

### Practical use in climbers

Use HRV only as a secondary signal; pair with:

* morning finger pain/stiffness
* max hang readiness
* skin status
* elbow pulley palpation

# Sub-section 3: Contrast Therapy (Cold/Hot Alternation)

### Mechanism

**[Bioplausible]** Alternating cold/hot may modulate vascular tone, pain perception, edema, and subjective freshness.

### Evidence

**[Evidence-backed]** Meta-analysis supports small-to-moderate benefits of contrast water therapy for soreness and perceived fatigue versus passive rest after exercise. Primarily **surrogate outcomes** (DOMS, CK, fatigue), some performance retention. ([PMC][6])

**[Evidence-backed]** Dupuy et al. found water immersion modalities beneficial for soreness/fatigue; not a magic bullet. ([PubMed][7])

### Connective tissue

**[Evidence-backed]** No direct evidence for pulley/tendon healing acceleration.

### Cold-water immersion (CWI)

**[Evidence-backed]** Useful for soreness and repeated competition days.

**[Evidence-backed]** Frequent post-strength CWI may blunt hypertrophic signaling and strength adaptation—relevant after hangboard/max-strength sessions.

### Practical protocol

**[Bioplausible]**

* Contrast: 1 min cold (10–15°C) / 2–3 min warm (36–40°C) × 4–6 rounds
* Use after comps or volume days, not after max finger-strength sessions

# Sub-section 4: Soft-Tissue Work (Massage, Foam Rolling)

### Evidence

**[Evidence-backed]** Massage has among the strongest effects for DOMS reduction in meta-analysis; performance effects are smaller/inconsistent. ([PubMed][7])

**[Evidence-backed]** Foam rolling improves ROM and perceived recovery, little consistent direct performance enhancement.

### Forearm-specific evidence

**[Bioplausible]** Very limited direct RCTs on climber forearm flexors after isometric pump sessions.

### Timing

**[Evidence-backed]** Immediate post-session reduces soreness perception; next-day may restore ROM. Effects mostly subjective/surrogate.

### Climbing tools

**[Folklore: climbing community]**

* lacrosse-ball forearm stripping
* finger flexor release with thumb pressure
* massage gun to forearm bellies

Useful if symptom-modulating; avoid aggressive pressure on irritated pulleys.

---

# Sub-section 5: Active Recovery Sessions

### Evidence

**[Evidence-backed]** Low-intensity active recovery often improves lactate clearance faster than passive rest. Useful mainly when next effort occurs same day or <24 h later.

### For climbers

**[Bioplausible]**

* easy jug mileage
* traversing below pump threshold
* zone-2 cardio
* scapular antagonist work
* mobility/yoga

### Forearm blood flow

**[Bioplausible]** Light contractions enhance muscle pump and reperfusion to finger flexors, potentially reducing residual stiffness after hard sessions.

### Caveat

Too much “recovery climbing” still loads pulleys. Intensity matters more than intention.

---

# Sub-section 6: Nutrition Timing for Connective Tissue

### Muscle protein synthesis

**[Evidence-backed]** Post-training protein: **0.25–0.4 g/kg** high-quality protein within ~2 h supports MPS; daily total intake more important than exact timing.

### Connective tissue specific

**[Evidence-backed]** Shaw et al. (2017): **15 g gelatin + vitamin C 1 h pre-exercise** increased collagen synthesis markers during intermittent loading. **Surrogate outcome**, not injury reduction. DOI:10.3945/ajcn.116.138594

**[Bioplausible]** 15–20 g collagen/gelatin + 50 mg vitamin C 30–60 min before finger rehab/loading may enhance tendon remodeling stimulus.

### Energy availability

**[Evidence-backed]** Low energy availability impairs recovery, endocrine function, bone health; likely increases tendon risk.

### Carbohydrate

**[Evidence-backed]** Carbohydrate supports glycogen restoration for repeated sessions and reduces excessive stress signaling.

# 3. Practical Recovery Protocol (Climber training 4–5 d/week)

## Daily

* **Sleep: 8 h target, 9 h opportunity in bed during heavy blocks** — **[Evidence-backed]**
* Fixed wake time ±30 min — **[Evidence-backed]**
* 30–40 kcal/kg FFM/day adequate fueling baseline — **[Evidence-backed]**

## Morning readiness

* HRV 3–5 min seated upon waking; compare to 7-day baseline — **[Evidence-backed]**
* If HRV suppressed + sore fingers + poor sleep: downgrade session — **[Bioplausible]**

## Before finger-strength / rehab sessions

* 15–20 g collagen or gelatin + vitamin C 30–60 min pre-session — **[Bioplausible → supported by surrogate evidence]**

## After hard climbing / hangboard

* 25–40 g protein meal within 2 h — **[Evidence-backed]**
* Carbohydrate if next session <24 h — **[Evidence-backed]**

## Recovery days (1–2×/week)

* 20–30 min easy aerobic + scapular antagonist + mobility — **[Evidence-backed/Bioplausible]**
* Optional easy jug climbing only if pain-free — **[Bioplausible]**

## Contrast therapy

* 1–2×/week after comps or high-volume pump sessions only — **[Evidence-backed for soreness]**
* Avoid routine post-max-strength immersion — **[Evidence-backed]**

## Soft tissue

* 5–10 min forearm massage/foam roll as tolerated — **[Evidence-backed for soreness, not performance]**
