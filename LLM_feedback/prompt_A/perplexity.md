### Fatigue interference between modalities

**[Bioplausible]** High-intensity finger loading from limit bouldering can plausibly blunt hangboard quality if the two sessions are placed too close together because both depend on the same local tissues, grip-force output, and motor-unit recruitment, and the climber will usually fail the second session at lower absolute force before the intended stimulus is reached. The available material does not provide controlled evidence for an exact minimum separation in climbers, so any 24 h, 48 h, or 72 h rule is a heuristic rather than an evidence-backed threshold. [tensionclimbing](https://tensionclimbing.com/blogs/blog/hangboarding-a-way)

**[Evidence-backed]** The climbing-specific review snippet available here states that systematic resistance training, including fingerboard and campusboard work, can improve climbing-specific fitness more than climbing training alone, which supports the idea that supplemental finger training matters, but it does not answer how to schedule it within a week. That means the concurrent-training question still needs extrapolation from general strength-endurance interference literature rather than a climbing-specific scheduling RCT. ### Session sequencing rules

**[Bioplausible]** If the goal of the day is finger strength, hangboard work should generally precede any fatiguing projecting session only when the hangboard stimulus is the priority and the climber can still execute maximal hangs with high force output. If the goal is limit bouldering quality, the bouldering should usually come first because it is more skill- and coordination-dependent, and fatigue would reduce movement quality and attempt quality.

**[Folklore: James Clucas / Climbing magazine]** The MoonBoard-oriented advice in the retrieved source recommends keeping limit-bouldering sessions to 2 d·wk⁻¹ max and separating them by at least 1 rest day and/or regular climbing day. That is a coaching heuristic, but it is consistent with a high-fatigue, high-quality-session model. [jamesclucas](https://www.jamesclucas.com/training/moonboardformaxpower-wxmd6)

**[Bioplausible]** MoonBoard sessions belong close to limit bouldering days because they are both high-intensity, finger-intensive, and power-dominant; the practical difference is that MoonBoard work is more standardized and usually more repeatable, while outdoor-style projecting is more variable and more coordination-heavy. ### Example weekly structures

**[Folklore: James Clucas / Tension Climbing]**
- Mon: Hangboard max hangs, 20–30 min warm-up + 30 min work, 85–95% MFS, 5–6 sets, full recovery.
- Tue: Rest or mobility.
- Wed: Limit bouldering or MoonBoard, 90–120 min, 90–100% effort, low volume, high attempt quality.
- Thu: Top-rope / lead endurance, 90–120 min, submaximal volume, continuous or interval routes.
- Fri: Rest or easy technique mileage.
- Sat: Second high-intensity finger session, either hangboard or limit bouldering, not both if quality drops.
- Sun: Rest or easy movement.

This structure matches the heuristic of 2 hard finger days per week with at least 1 recovery day between them. [jamesclucas](https://www.jamesclucas.com/training/moonboardformaxpower-wxmd6)

### Deload triggers

**[Bioplausible]** A climber should reduce volume and/or intensity across all modalities when performance drops persist for more than 1–2 sessions despite normal sleep and fueling, when warm-up feels abnormally poor, when finger-tendon soreness is present at session start, or when maximal hangs / limit grades regress at the same workload. Those are classic overreaching signals, but the climbing literature snippet available here does not provide a validated predictive model for overreaching or overtraining.
