## 2. Narrative synthesis

### a) Fatigue interference between modalities

**Interference between limit bouldering and hangboard adaptation**

Limit bouldering at 90–100% effort imposes near-maximal isometric finger-flexor loading plus high central nervous system (CNS) demand, producing substantial peripheral fatigue and central fatigue within a single session. **[Bioplausible]**  
Hangboard max-hang protocols at ≥80% MFS similarly stress the same motor units and tendon structures, with high local metabolic and mechanical load but lower skill and cognitive demands than limit bouldering. **[Evidence-backed]** (López-Rivera & González-Badillo 2019)  
Performing a high-volume limit bouldering session immediately before hangboard training likely reduces the capacity to reach true ≥90% MFS on the board, thereby lowering effective training intensity and potentially blunting strength adaptation. **[Bioplausible]**  
This inference is supported indirectly by Devise et al., who show that intensity-specific gains depend on actually training at the target relative MVC (F60/F80/F100), implying that any prior fatigue that reduces achievable MVC will shift the effective stimulus away from the intended zone. **[Evidence-backed]** (Devise et al. 2022)  
Stien et al. report that climbing-specific resistance interventions produce meaningful gains in finger strength when intensity is high and volume is sufficient, but they do not systematically analyze within-day sequencing of climbing vs. hangboard, leaving a gap regarding direct interference between these two modalities. **[Evidence-backed]** (Stien et al. 2023)  

**Concurrent-training literature and applicability to climbing**

Concurrent training meta-analyses show that endurance performed before strength in the same session or on the same day can reduce strength gains, especially when endurance is high volume, high intensity, or involves the same muscle groups. **[Evidence-backed]** (e.g., Wilson et al. 2012; Murlasits et al. 2018)  
These analyses report small-to-moderate negative effects (often SDM around −0.3 to −0.5) on strength outcomes when endurance is prioritized or when recovery between modalities is short (<6–8 h), suggesting that residual fatigue and molecular signaling competition (AMPK vs. mTOR) contribute to interference. **[Evidence-backed]**  
Climbing’s finger-loading profile differs from typical concurrent paradigms because both “strength” (hangboard, limit bouldering) and “endurance” (route climbing) rely heavily on isometric finger flexor actions, so the same local tissues are taxed across modalities, increasing the likelihood of local interference even when systemic load seems moderate. **[Bioplausible]**  
Therefore, applying concurrent-training findings to climbing suggests that high-volume route endurance sessions performed immediately before or after maximal finger-strength work may impair strength adaptation more than in sports where strength and endurance use different muscle groups. **[Bioplausible]**  

**Minimum recovery time between high-intensity finger sessions**

Most climbing-specific intervention studies (e.g., López-Rivera & González-Badillo; Devise et al.; studies in Stien’s meta-analysis) schedule finger-strength sessions at 2–3 sessions·week⁻¹, implicitly providing ≥48 h between high-intensity finger-loading days. **[Evidence-backed]**  
No RCT directly compares 24 h vs. 48 h vs. 72 h spacing of maximal finger-strength sessions in climbers, so any specific hourly recommendation remains untested. **[Evidence-backed]**  
Given tendon and pulley tissue remodeling timelines (collagen synthesis peaking around 24–48 h after loading and remaining elevated up to 72 h) and the high strain on A2/A4 pulleys during maximal crimping, a minimum of 48 h between true ≥85–90% MFS sessions targeting the same grips is mechanistically justified. **[Bioplausible]**  
Practitioner consensus from sources such as the Anderson brothers’ “The Rock Climber’s Training Manual” (2nd ed.), Hörst’s “Training for Climbing” (3rd ed.), and Lattice Training blog articles typically recommends 48–72 h between maximal hangboard or limit bouldering sessions for most climbers. **[Folklore: Anderson & Anderson 2014; Hörst 2016; Lattice Training blog]**  
Online communities such as r/climbharder frequently endorse a heuristic of “no more than 2–3 hard finger days·week⁻¹ with ≥48 h between them,” but these recommendations are not supported by controlled trials. **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki]**  

### b) Session sequencing rules

**Hangboard before or after projecting/limit bouldering**

López-Rivera & González-Badillo demonstrate that high-intensity max-hang protocols at ~80–90% MFS improve finger strength and endurance when performed as the primary strength stimulus, but they do not embed these sessions within a same-day climbing practice, so direct sequencing evidence is absent. **[Evidence-backed]**  
Given that maximal strength gains depend on recruiting the highest-threshold motor units and achieving near-maximal force, performing hangboard max-hangs before limit bouldering in the same session risks pre-fatiguing the finger flexors and reducing the ability to attempt true limit problems. **[Bioplausible]**  
Conversely, performing limit bouldering first may compromise subsequent hangboard intensity, as the climber may no longer be able to pull at ≥90% MFS, effectively turning the hangboard work into submaximal endurance rather than strength training. **[Bioplausible]**  
Coaching literature often recommends either (1) separating hangboard and limit bouldering onto different days or (2) placing a short, low-volume max-hang block before bouldering as a “primer,” with strict volume caps to avoid fatigue. **[Folklore: Anderson & Anderson 2014; Hörst 2016; Lattice Training blog]**  
No RCT compares “hangboard-first vs. bouldering-first” within the same day in climbers, so any rule here is extrapolated from general strength-training principles and practitioner experience. **[Evidence-backed]**  

**Placement of Moonboard sessions relative to hangboard days**

Moonboard sessions at 70–100% effort involve repeated near-maximal moves on small edges and often produce high local finger fatigue and skin wear, similar in loading profile to limit bouldering but with more standardized holds and angles. **[Bioplausible]**  
Because Moonboard problems often cluster around the same grip types and board angle, they can function as high-intensity power–endurance or strength–power sessions, depending on problem selection, and thus should be treated as finger-intensive strength/power days rather than “easy technique” days. **[Bioplausible]**  
Given the overlapping tissue demands, Moonboard sessions should generally not be scheduled on consecutive days with hangboard max-hangs targeting the same grips, to avoid excessive cumulative strain on pulleys and flexor tendons. **[Bioplausible]**  
Practitioner templates from Moonboard training guides and commercial coaching programs often place Moonboard sessions either (1) on the same day as limit bouldering (as the main bouldering modality) or (2) on a separate day from hangboard, with at least 48 h between heavy finger sessions. **[Folklore: Moonboard training guides; Lattice Training blog]**  

**Placement and fatigue profile of top-rope/lead endurance sessions**

Route endurance sessions at 60–75% of maximal effort (e.g., continuous climbing, 4×4s, ARCing) impose substantial local metabolic stress (forearm occlusion, high time under tension) but lower peak finger force than limit bouldering or max-hang training. **[Bioplausible]**  
Stien et al. include some interventions that combine strength and endurance elements, but they do not isolate the acute fatigue profile of endurance sessions relative to strength sessions, so direct comparative data are lacking. **[Evidence-backed]**  
From general exercise physiology, submaximal endurance work can be performed more frequently (3–5 days·week⁻¹) than maximal strength work without impairing recovery, provided that local tissue strain remains below injury thresholds. **[Evidence-backed]**  
Therefore, top-rope/lead endurance sessions can often be placed on days adjacent to, or even on the same day as, maximal finger-strength work, provided intensity is kept at 60–75% effort and volume is controlled, but this remains untested in climbing-specific RCTs. **[Bioplausible]**  

**Same-day hangboard and limit bouldering: feasibility and ordering**

No published RCT has directly tested same-day hangboard + limit bouldering vs. separated days in climbers, so any prescription is based on mechanistic reasoning and coaching practice. **[Evidence-backed]**  
If both are performed on the same day, a common heuristic is to perform the modality that is most sensitive to neural freshness (limit bouldering) first, followed by a reduced-volume hangboard block that maintains intensity but limits total sets (e.g., 2–3 sets of 3–5 max-hangs at ≥85% MFS). **[Folklore: Anderson & Anderson 2014; r/climbharder wiki]**  
Mechanistically, this ordering prioritizes skill and problem-solving under maximal recruitment while still allowing a small number of high-intensity isometric efforts, accepting some reduction in absolute force but preserving specificity to climbing. **[Bioplausible]**  
Alternatively, some coaches advocate a brief max-hang “primer” (e.g., 1–2 sets at ~80–85% MFS) before limit bouldering to enhance neural drive, with strict limits to avoid fatigue; this approach is widely used but not empirically validated. **[Folklore: Lattice Training blog; Hörst 2016]**  
Inter-session gaps of ≥3–4 h within the same day (e.g., morning hangboard, evening bouldering) may reduce acute interference, extrapolating from concurrent-training data showing less interference when modalities are separated by ≥6 h, but this has not been tested in climbers. **[Bioplausible]**  

### c) Example weekly structures

Below, all templates assume an intermediate-to-advanced climber (V6–V9 / 5.12–5.13c redpoint), 4–5 training days·week⁻¹, with ≥48 h between true maximal finger-strength sessions targeting the same grips.

#### Template 1: Strength-priority microcycle (4 days·week⁻¹)

- **Mon: Hangboard max-hangs (primary strength stimulus)**  
  6–8 warm-up sets; then 3–5 sets of 10 s hangs at 85–90% MFS on half-crimp edge; 2–3 min rest between sets; total session duration ~30–40 min; no additional hard climbing. **[Evidence-backed]** (Structure aligned with López-Rivera & González-Badillo 2019; intensity rationale from Devise et al. 2022; exact schedule extrapolated)  
- **Tue: Rest or low-intensity technique/top-rope (optional)**  
  If climbing, keep intensity ≤60% perceived effort; focus on movement drills, large holds, and low pump; total duration ≤60 min. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Wed: Limit bouldering / Moonboard session**  
  20–30 min progressive warm-up; then 60–90 min of limit attempts at 90–100% effort on V-hard for the climber (e.g., V8–V10 for a V7 climber), with long rests (3–5 min) between attempts; total session duration 2–2.5 h including warm-up and cool-down. **[Bioplausible]** (Limit bouldering as primary strength–power stimulus; structure consistent with coaching practice) **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki; Moonboard guides]**  
- **Thu: Rest or easy aerobic/antagonist work**  
  Non-climbing conditioning (e.g., 30–40 min easy cardio, scapular and rotator cuff work) at low intensity; no finger loading beyond daily living. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Fri: Hangboard max-hangs (secondary strength session)**  
  Similar to Monday but with slightly reduced volume (e.g., 3–4 work sets instead of 5); maintain 85–90% MFS; avoid additional hard climbing. **[Evidence-backed]** (Frequency and intensity consistent with intervention studies using 2 sessions·week⁻¹)  
- **Sat: Top-rope/lead endurance**  
  90–120 min of routes at 60–75% of max effort (e.g., 4–6 routes 2–4 grades below redpoint; short rests); aim to accumulate time under tension without failure on every route; can include 4×4-style intervals. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Sun: Rest**  
  No climbing; optional light mobility and general physical activity only. **[Bioplausible]**  

This template is **evidence-derived** for the hangboard frequency and intensity (2 sessions·week⁻¹ at ≥80% MFS) but **folklore-derived** for the exact weekly arrangement and placement of limit bouldering and endurance days. **[Evidence-backed + Folklore: mixed]**  

#### Template 2: Performance-priority microcycle (projecting focus, 5 days·week⁻¹)

- **Mon: Limit bouldering / Moonboard projecting**  
  2–3 h total; 20–30 min warm-up; then 90–120 min of attempts on 1–3 hard problems at 90–100% effort; long rests; minimal volume on easier problems. **[Bioplausible]** **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki; Moonboard guides]**  
- **Tue: Hangboard max-hangs (reduced volume)**  
  4–6 warm-up sets; then 2–3 sets of 10 s at 85–90% MFS; 3 min rest; total session ≤30 min; no additional hard climbing. **[Evidence-backed]** (Intensity and basic structure from López-Rivera & González-Badillo; reduced volume is extrapolated to accommodate higher overall climbing load) **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Wed: Active recovery / technique laps**  
  60–90 min of easy climbing at ≤60% effort; large holds; focus on footwork and efficiency; avoid pump and finger pain. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Thu: Projecting day (outdoor or indoor)**  
  2–3 h of attempts on primary route or boulder project at 85–100% effort; volume controlled by limiting total hard attempts (e.g., 10–20 high-quality tries); no hangboard. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Fri: Rest or very easy movement**  
  No structured training; optional walking or mobility; no finger loading beyond easy warm-up-level gripping. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Sat: Top-rope/lead endurance**  
  90–120 min; routes 2–3 grades below project; focus on continuous climbing and pacing; can include 3–5 sets of linked routes with 2–3 min rest. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Sun: Rest**  
  Full rest from climbing; optional general physical activity. **[Bioplausible]**  

This template is primarily **folklore-derived** in its weekly structure, with **evidence-backed** elements for hangboard intensity and frequency; it prioritizes performance freshness on projecting days by limiting heavy finger work immediately beforehand. **[Evidence-backed + Folklore: mixed]**  

#### Template 3: Mixed strength–endurance microcycle (4 days·week⁻¹, higher endurance emphasis)

- **Mon: Hangboard + short bouldering**  
  20 min warm-up; 3–4 sets of 10 s max-hangs at 80–85% MFS (slightly submaximal to reduce injury risk); then 45–60 min of submaximal bouldering at 70–85% effort (no true limit attempts); total session ~90 min. **[Bioplausible]** (Combines evidence-based hangboard intensity with folklore-based same-day sequencing) **[Evidence-backed + Folklore: mixed]**  
- **Tue: Top-rope/lead endurance**  
  90–120 min; focus on continuous climbing and pump management; intensity 60–75% effort; avoid maximal crimping. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Wed: Rest**  
  Full rest from climbing; optional low-intensity cross-training. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Thu: Limit bouldering / Moonboard**  
  2–2.5 h; 20–30 min warm-up; then 60–90 min of high-intensity problems at 90–100% effort; long rests; no hangboard. **[Bioplausible]** **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki; Moonboard guides]**  
- **Fri: Rest or easy technique**  
  ≤60 min of very easy climbing at ≤60% effort if desired; otherwise rest. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Sat: Top-rope/lead endurance or performance day**  
  Either repeat endurance session (if in base phase) or use as performance day (onsighting or redpointing at 85–95% effort) with volume controlled; avoid additional hangboard. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Sun: Rest**  
  No climbing. **[Bioplausible]**  

This template is largely **folklore-derived**, with **bioplausible** spacing (≥48 h between heavy finger sessions) and **evidence-backed** intensity targets for hangboard work. **[Evidence-backed + Bioplausible + Folklore: mixed]**  

### d) Deload triggers

**When to reduce volume/intensity across modalities**

No climbing-specific RCT has tested planned deload weeks vs. continuous loading in intermediate-to-advanced climbers, so deload prescriptions are extrapolated from strength and endurance training literature. **[Evidence-backed]**  
General strength-training research suggests that periodic reductions in volume (e.g., 30–50% for 5–7 days every 3–6 weeks) can maintain or even enhance strength by allowing recovery and supercompensation, but these data are not finger-specific. **[Evidence-backed]**  
Given the high injury incidence in finger pulleys among climbers and the slow remodeling of tendon tissue, implementing a deload week every 3–5 weeks of progressive loading, with reductions in both intensity (e.g., cap at ≤80% MFS) and volume (e.g., 50% fewer sets and hard attempts), is mechanistically justified. **[Bioplausible]**  
Coaching sources (Anderson & Anderson; Hörst; Lattice Training) commonly recommend deloads when subjective fatigue accumulates, performance plateaus for ≥2 weeks, or early signs of overuse (persistent finger soreness, reduced ROM, increased stiffness) appear. **[Folklore: Anderson & Anderson 2014; Hörst 2016; Lattice Training blog]**  

**Performance markers and subjective readiness signals**

Stien et al. highlight that improvements in finger strength and climbing performance are detectable over 4–10 weeks of structured training, but they do not report specific early-warning markers of overreaching. **[Evidence-backed]**  
In general strength and endurance sports, markers such as decreased performance in standardized tests (e.g., drop of ≥5% in MVC or time-to-failure), elevated perceived fatigue, sleep disturbances, and mood changes are associated with functional overreaching, but these associations are not yet validated in climbers. **[Evidence-backed]**  
A practical, bioplausible rule is to track a simple finger-strength test (e.g., 10 s hang at a fixed load or edge) once per week; a consistent drop of ≥5–10% in achievable load or hang time over 1–2 weeks, combined with increased soreness and reduced motivation, can be treated as a trigger for a deload week. **[Bioplausible]**  
Practitioner communities (r/climbharder, coaching blogs) often use heuristics such as “two bad weeks in a row” or “persistent finger soreness >48 h after sessions” as signals to reduce load, but these thresholds are not empirically validated. **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki; Lattice Training blog]**

## 3. Open questions

1. **Optimal spacing of high-intensity finger sessions**  
   No study has systematically compared 24 h vs. 48 h vs. 72 h spacing of ≥85–90% MFS hangboard or limit bouldering sessions in climbers, leaving the minimum effective recovery interval for maximizing strength gains and minimizing injury risk unknown. **[Evidence-backed]**  

2. **Same-day sequencing of hangboard and climbing**  
   There is no RCT or controlled trial directly comparing performance and adaptation outcomes for “hangboard-before-climbing,” “climbing-before-hangboard,” and “separate-days” conditions in intermediate-to-advanced climbers, so all current sequencing rules are based on extrapolation and folklore. **[Evidence-backed]**  

3. **Interaction between Moonboard-style training and hangboard work**  
   The specific interference or synergy between system-board (Moonboard/Tension board) sessions and hangboard max-hangs, especially when performed within the same microcycle or mesocycle, has not been quantified in terms of finger strength, power, or injury incidence. **[Evidence-backed]**  

4. **Dose–response for combined strength–endurance climbing programs**  
   Stien et al. synthesize hangboard and resistance interventions, but there is no comprehensive dose–response model for how many weekly sessions of hangboard, limit bouldering, Moonboard, and endurance climbing can be combined before interference or overuse outweighs benefits in advanced climbers. **[Evidence-backed]**  

5. **Objective readiness and monitoring tools specific to climbing**  
   No validated, climbing-specific monitoring system (e.g., weekly finger MVC tests, HRV, subjective scales) has been shown to predict overreaching or injury risk reliably enough to guide deload timing and weekly integration decisions in V6–V9 / 5.12–5.13c climbers. **[Evidence-backed]**
