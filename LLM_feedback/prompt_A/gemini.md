### 2. Narrative synthesis

**a) Fatigue interference between modalities**

**[Bioplausible]** Limit bouldering requires near-maximal isometric finger loading combined with high central nervous system (CNS) demand, inducing peripheral neuromuscular fatigue and localized structural degradation in the forearm flexors. Placing a high-intensity hangboard session (≥80% MFS) within `<48 h` of limit bouldering risks suppressing motor unit recruitment during the hangboard session, blunting the mechanical tension stimulus required for tendon and muscle adaptation. The gap: Direct climbing-specific RCTs measuring CNS fatigue duration post-limit bouldering are absent.

**[Evidence-backed]** Concurrent training literature demonstrates a robust interference effect when endurance protocols precede or coincide with maximum strength training, driven by AMPK-mediated inhibition of mTORC1 (Wilson et al. 2012, DOI: 10.1519/JSC.0b013e31823a3e2d, ES = -0.32 to -0.55 for concurrent vs. pure strength). Applying this to climbing, high-volume top-rope or lead sessions `<24 h` prior to hangboarding will systematically reduce MFS output. 

**[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki / Lattice Training]** The community consensus dictates a minimum `48 h` recovery window between any two "high-intensity finger sessions" (defined as limit bouldering, Moonboard, or max-hangs). 

**[Evidence-backed]** Physiologically, muscle force-generating capacity returns to baseline `48–72 h` post-heavy isometric loading, supporting the practitioner heuristic, though specific tendon collagen synthesis peaks at `24 h` and remains elevated for `72 h` post-loading (Baar 2017, DOI: 10.1007/s40279-017-0693-4).

**b) Session sequencing rules**

**[Bioplausible]** When performed on the same day, hangboard training should strictly precede projecting or limit bouldering. Mechanism: Isometric max hangs require peak CNS freshness to execute at ≥85% MFS. Executing hangs post-bouldering guarantees submaximal mechanical loading due to peripheral fatigue, whereas a low-volume max-hang protocol (e.g., 3×10 s) prior to bouldering serves as a post-activation potentiation (PAP) stimulus without severely depleting local ATP/PCr stores.

**[Folklore: Moonboard app guides / Training for Climbing (Hörst 2016)]** Moonboard sessions are categorized identically to limit bouldering due to their highly stereotyped, dynamic, and finger-intensive movement profile. They must replace, not add to, a max-hang or limit bouldering day within a microcycle.

**[Bioplausible]** Top-rope and lead endurance sessions represent high-volume, low-intensity continuous loading (<60% MFS), resulting in metabolic fatigue (H+ accumulation) rather than high mechanical tension. They must be placed at the end of a microcycle or strictly after high-intensity modalities to prevent the aforementioned AMPK/mTORC1 interference.

**[Folklore: Lattice Training]** Hangboard and limit bouldering can be combined in a single session only if hangboard volume is reduced by `50%` and executed immediately post-warmup, separated from bouldering by a `15–20 min` passive rest interval.

**c) Example weekly structures**

**Template 1: Strength & Power Focus (4 days/week)**
- Mon: Hangboard max-hangs (85% MFS, 3×5 sets, 20 min) followed by Limit bouldering projecting (90–100% effort, 90 min). **[Bioplausible: PAP effect + sequenced for CNS freshness]**
- Tue: Rest. **[Evidence-backed: 48 h structural recovery requirement]**
- Wed: Moonboard session (benchmark problems, 85–95% effort, 90 min). **[Folklore: Moonboard training guides]**
- Thu: Rest.
- Fri: Hangboard max-hangs (85% MFS, 3×5 sets, 20 min) + accessory weight training (60 min). **[Evidence-backed: López-Rivera 2019]**
- Sat: Top-rope/lead endurance (routes at 60–75% max effort, 2 h). **[Bioplausible: placed post-strength to avoid interference]**
- Sun: Rest.

**Template 2: Route-Climber Strength Maintenance (5 days/week)**
- Mon: Rest.
- Tue: Hangboard submaximal repeaters (70% MFS, 7s on / 3s off, 20 min) + limit bouldering (60 min). **[Folklore: r/climbharder wiki / Anderson & Anderson 2014]**
- Wed: Top-rope/lead redpoint burns (80–90% effort, 2 h). **[Bioplausible: high-load skill practice]**
- Thu: Rest.
- Fri: Limit bouldering projecting (90–100% effort, 2 h). **[Folklore: Lattice Training]**
- Sat: Rest.
- Sun: Top-rope/lead high-volume endurance (50–60% MFS, 4×4 intervals, 2 h). **[Bioplausible: localized aerobic capacity loading]**

**d) Deload triggers**

**[Folklore: Training for Climbing (Hörst 2016)]** A scheduled deload must occur every 4th week, reducing global training volume by `50%` and intensity by `20%` across all modalities.

**[Bioplausible]** Performance markers such as a `>10%` reduction in objective MFS via a standardized hangboard warmup test, or a sustained drop in morning HRV baseline over `3+ days`, serve as early indicators of non-functional overreaching. Mechanism: Chronic localized forearm fatigue masks strength adaptations; a 7–10 day reduction in high-load isometric tension allows localized tissue remodeling (collagen cross-linking) to complete and CNS fatigue to dissipate. Gap: No validated climbing-specific readiness metric exists in peer-reviewed literature.

### 3. Open questions

1. What is the precise dose-response relationship and interference magnitude of executing high-volume aerobic climbing (top-rope/lead) immediately following a max-hang protocol (≥85% MFS) within the same 120-minute session?
2. Do the highly dynamic and shock-loading profiles of Moonboard climbing induce a different rate of flexor tendon structural degradation or recovery compared to strictly static hangboard isometric loading at matched perceived exertion?
3. What is the minimum effective dose (frequency and total time-under-tension) of high-intensity hangboarding required to maintain MFS during a 6-to-8 week sport climbing performance/peaking phase?
4. Can localized forearm muscle oxygenation ($SmO_2$) measured via NIRS during submaximal screening serve as a reliable, non-invasive predictor of recovery status and readiness for maximal isometric finger loading?
