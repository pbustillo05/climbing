## 2. Narrative Synthesis

### a) Fatigue Interference Between Modalities

**[Evidence-backed]** Supplemental finger-strength training (hangboard or campus board) superimposed on regular climbing training produces significantly greater gains in climbing-specific tests than climbing alone. The overall meta-analytic SDM for resistance training added to climbing versus climbing alone was 0.57 (95% CI 0.24–0.91), with finger strength yielding SDM = 0.41 (95% CI 0.03–0.80) and forearm endurance SDM = 1.23 (95% CI 0.69–1.77). However, this literature does not study the *ordering* or *temporal proximity* of modalities within a microcycle. No published RCT has directly manipulated the inter-session gap between hangboard training and limit bouldering to measure interference on adaptation outcomes in climbers.

**[Bioplausible]** Limit bouldering (≥90–100% effort on problems at or above the climber's redpoint level) and hangboard max-hang sessions share the same primary effector tissue: the finger flexor musculotendinous unit and its associated annular pulley system. Both modalities impose near-maximal isometric or near-isometric loads on a ~6–14 mm crimp edge, activating the same motor unit pool at high discharge rates. The neuromuscular mechanism operative here is not the classical AMPK/mTOR concurrent-training interference described in the strength-plus-aerobic-endurance literature, but rather acute motor unit fatigue, residual intramuscular phosphocreatine depletion, and impaired maximal voluntary activation resulting from prior high-intensity isometric loading. The mechanism: high-intensity isometric finger loading at ≥80% MFS acutely reduces maximal force output and rate of force development (RFD) through peripheral fatigue (hydrogen ion accumulation, impaired calcium release) and central inhibition (reduced corticospinal drive via Golgi tendon organ afference). If a second max-effort session is performed before full neuromuscular recovery, the quality of the adaptive stimulus — in particular the peak force expressed per repetition — is necessarily lower, potentially reducing the training signal for maximal strength. *Evidence gap: no RCT has measured post-hangboard neuromuscular recovery time in the finger flexors at a temporal resolution sufficient to guide programming; all practical 48 h recommendations derive from study design conventions, not from an optimization study.*

**[Bioplausible]** The classic concurrent-training interference effect (Hickson, 1980; Wilson et al., 2012) concerns the interaction between axial/appendicular resistance training and cardiovascular endurance training (predominantly running). Wilson et al. (2012) identified significant negative relationships between endurance training frequency (r = −0.26 to −0.35) and duration (r = −0.29 to −0.75) and outcomes in hypertrophy, strength, and power, and found that concurrent training with running — but not cycling — significantly attenuated hypertrophy and strength relative to resistance training alone. The molecular basis involves competition between AMPK (activated by endurance exercise, catabolic signal) and mTOR (activated by mechanical loading/resistance exercise, anabolic signal): after high-intensity endurance training, AMPK increases rapidly and requires at least 3 h to return to baseline; mTORC1 activity persists for approximately 18 h after resistance training. However, the applicability of this framework to climbing is constrained by the sport's unique loading profile. Top-rope and lead endurance sessions impose mixed aerobic-glycolytic forearm demand but load the finger flexors in the same grip-specific isometric pattern as hangboarding, not through eccentric locomotion. The AMPK-mTOR model applies most cleanly to the combination of route climbing (aerobic endurance) following hangboard or limit bouldering (maximal isometric); even then, climbing's forearm endurance is predominantly a local aerobic-to-anaerobic continuum, not systemic cardiovascular work, making the magnitude of molecular interference likely smaller than in running-concurrent protocols. *Evidence gap: no study has measured AMPK or mTOR pathway activity in finger flexors following climbing-specific endurance versus limit bouldering in the same microcycle.*

**[Folklore: Lattice Training blog (Randall & Torr), r/climbharder wiki, Anderson & Anderson *The Rock Climber's Training Manual* 2nd ed., 2014]** The practitioner consensus is that ≥48 h must separate any two sessions that impose high-intensity finger loading (hangboard max-hangs, campus board, or limit bouldering). This is the dominant heuristic across structured coaching frameworks (Lattice, Eva López's periodization guides, the Anderson brothers' self-coaching manual). Study design in Stien et al.'s 5-week campus board RCT specified that a minimum of 48 h separated each training session, and Hermans et al. (2022) required participants to refrain from any hard physical activity 48 h before testing, both reflecting the same practitioner heuristic embedded as a methodological safeguard — not as a verified optimization. Some coaches (Neil Gresham, UKClimbing; Power Company Podcast) cite 72 h between max-intensity finger sessions for advanced climbers, without empirical support.

### b) Session Sequencing Rules

**[Bioplausible]** *Should hangboard precede or follow limit bouldering?*
The intensity-specificity principle documented by Devise et al. (2022) has direct sequencing implications. F100 (100% MFS, max-intensity hangboard) significantly improved MFS but not stamina or endurance; F80 (80% MFS intermittent) improved MFS, stamina, and endurance; F60 (60% MFS) improved stamina and endurance but not MFS. Translating this: max-hang adaptation requires that the stimulus be delivered at close to peak force output. Any modality that depletes finger flexor force capacity before the hangboard session degrades the F100-equivalent stimulus toward an F80 or F60 stimulus. Therefore, if the training goal is maximal finger strength (and RFD, per Stien et al. 2023), hangboard max-hangs should precede — not follow — limit bouldering. *Mechanism: after a 2–3 h limit bouldering session, residual fatigue reduces MFC by an estimated 10–25% (inferred from Watts et al. 1996 acute post-climbing strength decrements; no direct measurement in a hangboard-after-bouldering paradigm exists in the peer-reviewed literature).* Conversely, if the training goal is endurance (repeaters at F60–F80 intensity), this sequencing constraint is weaker because the stimulus is submaximal and less sensitive to pre-fatigue. *Evidence gap: no RCT has randomized hangboard-before vs. hangboard-after bouldering to directly test this sequence effect in climbers.*

**[Bioplausible, with Folklore corroboration]** *Same-session hangboard + limit bouldering: can it be done, and in what order?*
Same-session combination is possible if (a) max-strength hangboard is performed first, immediately after warm-up, before any bouldering, and (b) total volume is reduced relative to a dedicated single-modality session to prevent excessive cumulative fatigue. The rationale draws from the concurrent-training sequencing literature: sequencing strength training prior to endurance in concurrent training appears beneficial for lower-body strength adaptations, while improvement in aerobic capacity is not affected by training order. By analogy (bioplausible transfer to upper-limb isometric context), performing max-hangs before bouldering preserves the quality of the strength stimulus. The within-session gap should be ≥15–20 min of passive or lower-body-only activity, not additional forearm loading. However, combining both modalities in one session on the same day does not constitute a substitute for two separated sessions, as total volume constraints limit cumulative training signal. **[Folklore: Lattice Training, Reel Rock/Eva López blog]** Eva López's periodization guidelines explicitly recommend hangboard work at the *start* of a session when freshness is required for max-effort protocol quality.

**[Bioplausible]** *Where do Moonboard sessions fit?*
The Moonboard is a 40°-overhung, system-board climbing wall using standardized holds and benchmark problems. Sessions typically involve high-density dynamic and static loading of the finger flexors on ≤1-phalange-depth crimps at angles that sustain near-maximal finger force per move. The fatigue profile of a full Moonboard session (60–90 min, benchmark problems, ≥8–10 max-effort attempts per problem) overlaps substantially with limit bouldering on a steep board: near-maximal finger loading, high CNS demand, and significant metabolic depletion. This categorizes Moonboard sessions physiologically alongside limit bouldering, not alongside top-rope endurance or technique work. Therefore, the same ≥48 h separation heuristic should apply between Moonboard sessions and hangboard max-hangs. Placing a Moonboard session adjacent to a hangboard day (e.g., Moonboard Monday, Hangboard Tuesday) risks the same attenuation of max-strength stimulus quality described above. **[Folklore: Moonboard training app programming guides, r/climbharder wiki, Dave Macleod *9 out of 10 climbers make the same mistakes*, 2009]** Practitioner consensus situates Moonboard sessions as equivalent to hard bouldering sessions and schedules them with identical recovery constraints.

**[Evidence-backed, with caveats]** *Where does top-rope/lead endurance fit?*
Top-rope and lead climbing at 60–75% maximum effort impose forearm endurance demands (aerobic-glycolytic forearm fatigue) with significantly lower peak finger force per hold than limit bouldering (estimated 50–70% MFS vs. ≥90% MFS, though no direct comparative measurement exists in a single study). Acute post-lead-climbing strength recovery is faster than post-max-hang or post-limit-bouldering recovery. A crossover study on active recovery strategies for finger flexor fatigue found that performance impulse decreased from the first to the third exhaustive intermittent isometric test by 18.4% after walking, 29.5% after climbing, and 27.2% after hanging, with the impulse in the third trial significantly higher after walking than after climbing (p = 0.009, d = 0.54) or hanging (p = 0.013, d = 0.49). This demonstrates that even low-to-moderate climbing used as inter-session "active recovery" maintains forearm fatigue accumulation compared to non-climbing locomotion. The implication: placing route endurance sessions on days adjacent to hangboard or limit bouldering sessions still imposes meaningful residual fatigue on the shared finger flexor effector pool. However, the fatigue profile of route climbing (sustained aerobic demand at submaximal grip force) is qualitatively different from max-hang fatigue and is unlikely to degrade max-strength stimulus quality to the same extent as placing a Moonboard session adjacent to a hangboard day. Optimal placement: route endurance sessions should bookend the high-intensity block, either as an early-week warm-up modality or as the final training day before a rest day. They should not immediately precede a hangboard max-strength session.

### d) Deload Triggers

**[Evidence-backed, from non-climbing sports; bioplausible transfer]** Functional overreaching (FOR) is defined as a planned short-term performance decrement followed by supercompensation on recovery, with full restoration within ≤2 weeks. Non-functional overreaching (NFOR) involves persistent performance decrements requiring ≤4 weeks to recover; overtraining syndrome (OTS) requires months to years. The distinction is critical for programming: FOR is a tolerated training stress; NFOR is the injury-adjacent state to avoid. During functional overreaching, resting heart rate may elevate by 3–5 bpm above baseline; HRV dips acutely but recovers within 24–72 h after a rest day; training performance drops 5–10% but rebounds with 1–2 weeks of reduced loading. During non-functional overreaching, the pattern shifts — resting HR elevates persistently, staying 5–10 bpm above baseline even on rest days.

**[Bioplausible]** For climbing-specific application, deload indicators should include: (1) max-hang force output declining >10% over 2 consecutive sessions relative to a recent 4-week rolling average; (2) subjective RPE for a fixed hangboard protocol increasing ≥1.5 points (0–10 RPE scale) without corresponding load increase; (3) finger/pulley pain on the VAS ≥3/10 at rest or ≥5/10 during loading — this is a mandatory hard stop regardless of other indicators, as it may signal A2 or A4 pulley irritation requiring medical evaluation. *Mechanism: connective tissue (pulley, tendon) adapts more slowly than muscle (~12 weeks for collagen turnover per Smeets et al., 2019); neuromuscular indicators may lag soft-tissue overload, meaning subjective fatigue is an imperfect sentinel for pulley injury risk.*

**[Bioplausible]** HRV (RMSSD, measured morning resting with a validated app) is the most practically accessible biomarker for cumulative training stress. Persistently elevated RMSSD coefficient of variation over several weeks may serve as an early warning sign of maladaptation, preceding declines in overall parasympathetic activity, reduced recovery capacity, and increased risk of illness or injury. A single-day HRV dip is expected during intensified training and does not warrant deload; a sustained downward trend over ≥5 consecutive training days, combined with any two of the performance/subjective markers above, is a pragmatic deload trigger. *Evidence gap: no climbing-specific study has validated an HRV threshold or RMSSD trajectory for deload timing in climbers.*

**[Folklore: Lattice Training, r/climbharder wiki; Anderson & Anderson 2014]** Practitioner consensus recommends a planned deload (50–60% volume reduction, maintained intensity ≥70% MFS to preserve neuromuscular sensitivity) every 4–6 weeks within a structured mesocycle. The Anderson brothers specify a "rest week" every 4th week in their periodization templates. Lattice Training's Progression Pyramid framework schedules a recovery week after every 3-week loading block. Neither has been subjected to RCT testing in climbers.

## 3. Open Questions

1. **What is the optimal inter-session recovery interval between high-intensity finger loading sessions (≥80% MFS hangboard or limit bouldering) to maximize the strength-adaptation stimulus over a mesocycle in advanced climbers?** The 48 h heuristic is a study design convention, not an empirically optimized prescription. A 2×2 factorial RCT (hangboard only vs. hangboard + bouldering; 24 h vs. 48 h vs. 72 h inter-session gap) with force output and finger morphological adaptation (tendon cross-section via ultrasound) as endpoints would directly address this.

2. **Does a concurrent-training interference effect exist between route climbing (aerobic forearm endurance) and hangboard max-strength training when scheduled in the same or adjacent training days, and if so, what is its magnitude and time course?** Current evidence borrows from lower-body AMPK/mTOR studies. No RCT has measured mTOR pathway activity in finger flexors following route climbing vs. rest, nor has any study directly compared adaptation outcomes in climbers who separate these modalities by 6 h vs. 24 h vs. 48 h.

3. **What is the minimum effective training frequency for maintaining finger-strength adaptations during transition from a structured hangboard block to a climbing-performance block (redpointing season)?** Stien et al. (2021) compared 2×/wk vs. 4×/wk for acquisition. No study has examined 1×/wk maintenance vs. 2×/wk maintenance in the context of simultaneous limit bouldering and route climbing volume.

4. **How do individual differences in connective tissue recovery rate (A2 pulley vascularity, tendon stiffness, training age) moderate optimal microcycle design?** Current guidelines apply population-level recommendations to athletes whose individual pulley load tolerance likely varies by an order of magnitude. Ultrasound-elastography of the A2 pulley across microcycles during different loading configurations would provide mechanistic data to stratify programming.

5. **Does the Moonboard (or similar standardized steep-board climbing) impose a fatigue profile more similar to limit bouldering on a vertical/slab wall or to hangboard training, and does this distinction meaningfully alter its optimal placement within the microcycle?** The Moonboard's 40° overhang and standardized hold depth mean that peak finger force per move may approach hangboard-equivalent loading; however, the dynamic movement pattern and intermittent grip/release profile are distinct from static dead-hangs. No study has characterized post-Moonboard recovery of MFS, RFD, or tendon stiffness, nor compared it to an equivalent-duration limit-bouldering session on a less-steep wall.
