## 1.2 **Collateral Ligament Sprains (PIP/DIP/MCP)**  
Common in dynamic moves, pockets, and sideways loading.  
Management differs from pulley injuries:  
- Early controlled ROM  
- Buddy taping  
- Avoiding varus/valgus stress  
- Return-to-load often **2–6 weeks**, depending on grade.

**Gap:**  
No mention of collateral ligament injuries, despite being a top‑3 finger injury in recreational climbers.

## 1.3 **Volar Plate Injuries**  
Frequently occur during hyperextension (e.g., slipping off a hold).  
Management includes:  
- Early ROM  
- Extension‑block splinting  
- Avoiding hyperextension for 4–6 weeks.

**Gap:**  
Completely absent from the section.

## 1.4 **Lumbrical Strains (“Lumbrical Shift Syndrome”)**  
Occurs when two adjacent fingers load different hold depths (e.g., mono + two‑finger pocket).  
Symptoms: pain in palm, pain with FDP glide.  
Management:  
- Avoid differential loading  
- Gradual reintroduction of pockets  
- 4–8 week recovery typical.

**Gap:**  
Not mentioned, despite being a well‑recognized climbing‑specific injury.

You correctly emphasize US as first‑line, but two updates are missing:

## 3.2 **No mention of NSAIDs and their risks**  
NSAIDs are widely used by climbers, but:

- They may help short‑term pain in capsulitis/synovitis.

**Gap:**  
You should include a section on NSAIDs, their risks, and when they are appropriate (e.g., capsulitis, not acute pulley rupture).

## 3.3 **No discussion of corticosteroid injections**

They are **contraindicated** for pulley injuries.

This distinction is clinically important and missing.
