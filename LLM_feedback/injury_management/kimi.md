## 1. CRITICAL GAPS

### A. Finger Flexor Tenosynovitis / Synovitis / Capsulitis
Your section mentions tenosynovitis only in passing (seven patients in Schöffl 2003 with persistent pain). This is a major omission. Tenosynovitis is one of the most common overuse injuries in climbers and is the primary differential diagnosis for pulley injury. You need a dedicated subsection covering:

- **Diagnosis**: Differentiation from pulley injury (gradual onset, pain radiating into palm/forearm, US showing fluid around tendon and thickened pulley). Mohn et al. (2022) found A2 pulley affected in 84.6% and A4 in 23.1% of 65 climbers, with mean symptom duration of 30.5 weeks and an undulating course .
- **Natural history**: Favorable. Mohn et al. found all patients resumed climbing, ~75% regained or exceeded prior level, and outcomes were largely independent of specific therapy content .
- **Corticosteroid injections**: Schöffl et al. (2019) reported 84.2% pain reduction in 42 climbers with chronic tenosynovitis; 73.8% pain-free after second injection at mean 20.9 days, with no complications . This is controversial but must be presented as an evidence-based option after conservative management fails.
- **Conservative management**: Modelling clay exercises, compression fingerlings (nighttime), load reduction, and grip modification (avoid full/half crimp) were commonly used but did not significantly predict outcome in regression analysis .

### B. Lumbrical Injuries
You mention lumbrical involvement only as part of Schöffl Grade IV. Lumbrical tears are a distinct climbing-specific injury. Schweizer (2003) described three cases caused by one-finger pocket holds (ring or little finger), where quadriga effect shifts FDP tendons and increases distance between lumbrical origins . US can show organized hematoma. This needs its own paragraph with mechanism, diagnosis, and management (relative rest, avoid pockets, gradual return).

### C. Collateral Ligament Injuries (PIP/DIP)
Also mentioned only in Schöffl Grade IV. Collateral ligament injuries occur from sidepulls, gastons, and dynamic lateral loading . They present with focal joint-line tenderness, swelling, and pain with lateral stress testing. Management is typically 3 weeks rigid protective splinting if stable. This is a common injury in climbing and deserves coverage.

### D. Pulley Protection Splint / Unloader Ring / Thermoplastic Ring
You mention "thermoplastic ring" once in the Schöffl protocol, but you miss the two key papers on this:
- **Schneeberger & Schweizer (2016)**: Prospective series of 47 climbers. The Pulley Protection Splint (PPS) reduced tendon-phalanx distance from 4.4±1.0 mm to 2.3±0.6 mm after A2 rupture, and 38/43 regained prior climbing level at ~9 months .
- **Kadar et al. (2021)**: Biomechanical cadaver study showing thermoplastic rings restore ROM and tendon loads to near-native levels after pulley venting .

This is one of the few conservative interventions with both clinical and biomechanical evidence. It warrants its own evidence box.

### E. NSAIDs and Tissue Healing
You do not address NSAIDs anywhere. Given that climbers routinely use ibuprofen/diclofenac, this is a significant gap. The evidence is nuanced:
- **General tendon-to-bone healing**: Duchman et al. (2019) systematic review found insufficient evidence for or against NSAIDs following acute injury or surgical repair; one RCT showed higher rotator cuff repair failure with celecoxib vs. ibuprofen/control .
- **Flexor tendon repair**: Rouhani et al. (2013) RCT found high-dose ibuprofen (2400 mg/day) significantly improved ROM and DASH scores after zone II flexor tendon repair with no re-ruptures .
- **Bone healing**: A 2024 meta-analysis found no significant increase in non-union with NSAIDs (pooled OR 1.11, 95% CI 0.99–1.23) .

**Recommendation**: You should include a bioplausible or evidence-backed box stating that short-term NSAID use for pain control in pulley injuries is not clearly contraindicated by human evidence, but prolonged use during early healing phases is mechanistically concerning due to COX inhibition and prostaglandin suppression.

### F. Re-injury Epidemiology
You state "re-injury rates are approximately 15--25\% in case series follow-up" without citation. A 2016 secondary analysis of British climbers found the average probability of sustaining at least one re-injury was 35.6% (95% CI 34.7–36.8%), with fingers being the most common site (26% of re-injuries from repetitive overuse) . The relative risk of re-injury after previous injury was 1.55 (95% CI 1.34–1.80).

## 2. OUTDATED OR QUESTIONABLE CLAIMS

### A. H-Taping Effectiveness
Your section states H-taping is "near-universal in climbing rehabilitation practice" and cites Larsson 2022 for "low-to-moderate certainty for bowstringing reduction (15--22\%)." However, you **miss the Salas et al. (2023) cadaver study**, which is the highest-quality biomechanical evidence to date:

> "We found that H-taping is not effective as prophylaxis against A2 pulley ruptures or as a stabilizing treatment method for partially ruptured pulleys." 

This study used 56 paired comparisons in fresh-frozen cadaver hands and found no significant difference in failure force or fingertip force with H-taping. You need to add this as a contradictory evidence source. H-taping may still have a role for pain modulation or proprioception (hence "folklore"), but its biomechanical protective effect is now directly challenged.

### B. Surgical Management: Graft-Based Reconstruction as Default
Your section presents graft-based reconstruction (palmaris longus, extensor retinaculum) as the standard for Grade III–IV. You completely miss **Balandier et al. (2025)**, who reported direct suture repair (without graft) in 6 elite climbers with isolated ruptures and 2 with multiple ruptures :

- Mean return to pre-injury climbing level: **5.8 months** (range 3–8 months).
- All achieved excellent Buck-Gramcko scores.
- US at 3 years confirmed pulley continuity.

This is faster than typical graft-based return-to-sport timelines (6–12 months for Grade IV). You should add direct suture as an emerging alternative with potentially lower morbidity.

### C. MRI Sensitivity for Partial Tears
You state: "MRI sensitivity for complete pulley tears is approximately 90--95\% and for partial tears 65--80\% in the general hand surgery literature." The 2022 diagnostic imaging review by Berrigan et al. (which you cite) actually reports MRI sensitivities ranging from **0.79 to 1.00** and specificities from **0.86 to 1.00** across 3 studies, with the lowest values occurring in neutral finger positioning and higher values in crimp grip positioning . Your 65--80% figure for partial tears is not well-supported in the climbing-specific literature and appears to be extrapolated from non-climbing hand surgery. The Berrigan review emphasizes that dynamic US with forced flexion remains superior for partial tears.

### D. Tyler Nelson Protocols
You correctly classify these as Folklore. However, you can now strengthen the bioplausible framework by citing **Gilmore et al. (2024)** (already in your bibliography as GKAO24), which retrospectively validated that submaximal daily hangboarding ("Abrahangs," ~40% max) produced equivalent strength gains to Max Hangs, with additive effects when combined . This does not validate Nelson's specific rehab thresholds, but it provides the first empirical support for low-intensity, high-frequency isometric loading in climbers—mechanistically consistent with the Cook & Purdam reactive-phase model.

## 3. EVIDENCE UPDATES (New Literature to Integrate)

### A. Systematic Review of Pulley Injuries (2023)
A 2023 systematic review (PMC10495204) synthesized 4 clinical studies (189 patients, 208 pulleys) and confirmed that Grade III is a "gray zone" where both conservative and surgical treatment yield favorable results . However, the review noted that surgical results were more positive than conservative in Grades 3 and 4 for functional recovery and return to sport. This supports your existing text but provides a more recent synthesis than citing individual papers.

### B. Eccentric Loading and Pulley Stress
Schöffl's biomechanical study (cited in a 2023 blog summary) found that **eccentric loading imposes higher stress on the pulley system than concentric loading** . This has direct rehab implications: climbers rehabilitating from pulley injuries should be cautious with exercises involving controlled lengthening under load (e.g., slowly opening out of a crimp) and foot-pop scenarios.

### C. Finger Extensor / Antagonist Training
Vigouroux et al. (2023/2024) found that higher-grade climbers have increasingly imbalanced flexor-to-extensor ratios, and extensor-only training reduced this imbalance . While not yet linked to injury reduction in a prospective trial, this should be mentioned in the prevention section as a bioplausible intervention.

### D. PIP Joint Synovitis / Capsulitis Case Study
A 2023 case study detailed a 6-week rehab protocol for PIP capsulitis in a climber using ice, compression wrapping, finger trap oscillations, IASTM, tendon glides, and rubber-band extensor work . The climber returned to unrestricted climbing at 6 weeks. This provides a structured protocol you can reference for joint-specific inflammatory conditions.

## 4. MISSING FOLKLORE & COMMUNITY PRACTICES

### A. "No-Hangs" / Regenerative Hangs for Synovitis
Community practice (e.g., Crimpd app, Hawker Place Physio) advocates "no-hangs" or "regenerative hangs" for chronic synovitis: feet on ground, 10-second holds at ~30% max, every 30 seconds, rotating grip types (open, pinch, pocket) to "pump" synovial fluid without joint compression . No RCT exists, but it is widely practiced.

### B. Compression Fingerlings / Floss Bands
Mohn et al. (2022) documented 35.4% of tenosynovitis patients used compression fingerlings at night . Community sources also recommend Coban/self-adherent wrap or floss bands for daytime compression during flare-ups .

### C. Modelling Clay / Putty Exercises
Used in 75.4% of tenosynovitis cases in the Mohn cohort as a warm-up and strengthening tool . This is a specific community/clinical practice not mentioned in your section.

### D. Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training
Hooper's Beta and some PT clinics now recommend BFR for maintaining strength during finger injury rehab when load must be kept low . No climbing-specific studies exist, but it is emerging folklore.
