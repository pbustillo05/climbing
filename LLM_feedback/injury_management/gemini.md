### 1. Major Pathology Gaps (The "Big Misses")
Your current draft relies heavily on the A2 pulley, flexor tendons, and skin. However, it completely ignores several pathologies that make up a massive percentage of climbing clinic visits:

* **Lumbrical Muscle Tears:** This is a classic climbing injury caused by the "dropped finger" position in pocket climbing (typically when the middle and ring fingers are loaded while the pinky is dropped into the palm). The shear force tears the lumbrical muscle. 
* **Collateral Ligament & Joint Capsule Injuries:** Collateral ligament sprains (at the PIP and MCP joints) and capsulitis (often mimicking pulley injuries with swollen, stiff joints) are highly prevalent. They are driven by torsional forces during dynamic moves, sloper grips, or crack climbing, rather than direct bowstringing forces. 
* **Youth-Specific Injuries (PPSI):** This is a critical omission. With the explosion of youth climbing, **Primary Periphyseal Stress Injuries (PPSI)**—or epiphyseal growth plate stress fractures—are the most common sport-specific injury in adolescents. Treating a youth climber's finger pain as a standard soft-tissue pulley strain is a dangerous oversight that can lead to permanent joint deformity. 

### 2. Mistakes and Questionable Claims
* **The Biomechanical Mismatch of the Achilles Extrapolation:** You rightly note that utilizing the Alfredson Achilles protocol (heavy-load eccentrics) is an extrapolation. However, you should emphasize that it is mechanically questionable. The Achilles is a tensile structure; the A2 pulley is a retinacular structure loaded via shear and compression. Heavy slow eccentrics do not map functionally to pulley tissue. Tyler Nelson’s isometric hangboard protocols are actually much closer to the tissue's specific function and should be framed as mechanically sound, rather than just "folklore."
* **Conflating Taping and Pulley Protection Splints (PPS):** In Section 1.4.3, you lump H-Taping and ring splinting together. This misrepresents the clinical consensus. Tape stretches under load and fails to provide rigid structural unloading. Custom thermoplastic Pulley Protection Splints (unloader rings), pioneered by Andreas Schweizer at Balgrist, are the actual gold standard for conservative management of Grade II–III tears. They physically approximate the tendon to the bone continuously, whereas tape is primarily used for psychological support, skin protection, or post-splint transitioning. 

### 3. Missing Clinical & Pharmacological Context
* **The NSAID Debate:** The draft lacks discussion on pharmacological management. Current sports medicine consensus limits NSAID use to the first 48–72 hours of an acute pulley injury to control excessive swelling. Prolonged NSAID use is actively contraindicated, as it impairs collagen synthesis and prostaglandin E2 (PGE2)-mediated early tendon healing. 
* **Rest vs. Load:** While you reference the Cook & Purdam continuum, you should explicitly state the modern clinical shift: absolute rest (beyond the initial acute 5–14 day inflammatory phase) is detrimental. Early mobilization and symptom-guided progressive loading are strictly preferred to prevent scar tissue adhesion in the flexor sheath.

### 4. Folklore & Community Practices to Add
* **The Lumbrical Stress Test:** A standard community diagnostic tool. If a climber feels pain when extending one finger while the adjacent fingers are held in tight flexion, it indicates a lumbrical tear, not a pulley injury.
* **Buddy Taping (Double X Method):** While H-taping is covered for pulleys, "buddy taping" the injured finger to a healthy adjacent finger is the universal folklore standard for collateral ligament sprains and lumbrical tear prevention. 
* **The 24-Hour Symptom Rule:** A vital heuristic in community hangboard rehab protocols. Rehab hangs should induce no pain greater than a 3/10, and any residual stiffness or soreness must resolve within 24 hours. If symptoms linger longer, the mechanical load was too high.
