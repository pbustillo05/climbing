**Major issues with existing claims and citations (accuracy, evidence tiering, and representation of sources):**

- **Schöffl et al. (2003) grading system, immobilization durations, and return-to-climbing timelines (Gr I–III conservative management)**: These remain the foundational reference and are appropriately labeled Bioplausible / case-series level throughout. No major errors in presentation. However, newer systematic reviews (e.g., Artiaco et al. 2023) explicitly affirm that conservative management yields excellent functional outcomes (Buck-Gramcko scores) for Gr I–III in both climbers and non-climbers, with no new RCTs contradicting the timelines. Minor note: the DOI for this paper is now resolvable (10.1580/1080-6032(2003)014[0094:piirc]2.0.co;2), resolving the “unresolved via CrossRef” footnote.

- **H-taping (biomechanical claims and clinical practice)**: This is the most significant outdated/weakened claim. The section cites Schweizer (2000) and Schöffl et al. (2007) for bowstringing reduction (2.8–22%) and +13% crimp MVC. Both papers exist and are correctly summarized (Schöffl 2007 DOI: 10.1123/jab.23.1.52). However, a 2023 cadaveric biomechanical study (Salas et al.) found **H-taping ineffective** as prophylaxis against A2 ruptures *or* as stabilization for *partial* tears; it provided no meaningful reduction in bowstringing or increase in fingertip force post-injury. A 2022 systematic review (Larsson et al.) rates evidence for bowstringing reduction as low-to-moderate certainty only, with very low certainty for pain, return-to-sport, or re-injury prevention. The section’s “Bioplausible” tiering and folklore box are still accurate, but the evidence has weakened since the draft; this should be flagged as “conflicting recent biomechanical data” to avoid over-stating clinical utility.

- **Immobilization and thermoplastic ring for Gr III**: Correctly noted (10–14 days). Recent PT literature heavily emphasizes **custom pulley-protection splints (PPS / thermoplastic ring orthoses)** as the *gold standard* for Gr II–III (superior mechanical unloading vs. tape alone). The section mentions this briefly but under-emphasizes it relative to current clinical practice (see below).

- **Tyler Nelson / Camp 4 Human Performance protocols**: Correctly and appropriately labeled Folklore (mechanistic framework Bioplausible via Cook & Purdam, but no pulley-specific validation). Community and podcast sources confirm this is practitioner consensus, not trial data.

- **Collagen supplementation (Shaw et al. 2017 and non-climbing meta-analyses)**: Evidence tiering is accurate (Evidence-backed for surrogate markers/skin endpoints in non-climbers; Bioplausible for climbing pulley/skin healing). No new climber-specific RCTs or pulley-outcome studies have appeared.

- **Diagnostic imaging (US vs. MRI, Klauser, Berrigan, Iruretagoiena)**: Excellent and up-to-date. Dynamic US remains first-line with 94–100% sens/spec; TBD cutoff heterogeneity is correctly highlighted. No changes needed.

- **Sjöman et al. (2023) experience-dependent risk**: Correctly presented as Evidence-backed (observational association only); mechanistic interpretation appropriately Bioplausible.

**Gaps in literature coverage (prevalence-weighted finger injuries and management elements missing or under-represented):**

The section is already narrowly focused on the highest-prevalence pathologies (pulley injuries >> flexor strains >> skin), which is appropriate. However, several clinically common, high-recurrence soft-tissue/joint issues are absent and should be considered for a truly comprehensive review:

- **Flexor tenosynovitis** (often co-occurs with or mimics pulley injury; high recurrence if loading is mismanaged). Distinct US findings (tendon sheath fluid, thickening) vs. pulley bowstringing. Management parallels pulley (load reduction, progressive isometrics) but may include short-term NSAIDs/ice for flare-ups.

- **PIP/DIP capsulitis / synovitis** (very common overuse; swollen “puffy” joints). Caused by repetitive crimp compression. Recent case series and protocols (e.g., Vagy 2023) emphasize early deload, joint mobilizations, contrast baths, and avoidance of full crimp until resolved. High recurrence without load management.

- **Collateral ligament sprains** (PIP/DIP; less common than pulleys but recurrent in crimping/twisting). Differentiated by lateral stability testing and US.

- **Acute-phase management (ice, NSAIDs, cryotherapy)**: Completely absent. Climbing-specific sources and general tendinopathy reviews recommend short-term (first 48–72 h) ice for pain control and modest swelling reduction; NSAIDs only if pain/swelling is excessive (caution: may blunt healing in tendinopathy). No climbing RCTs, but consensus in PT protocols.

- **Pulley-protection splints (PPS / ring orthoses)**: Mentioned once for Gr III but vastly under-emphasized. Schneeberger & Schweizer (2016) prospective series (n=47) showed excellent outcomes with continuous PPS wear (2 months) + functional therapy; reduces tendon–phalanx distance more effectively than tape. Multiple PT resources (Climbing Doctor, Rock Rehab) now call this the preferred conservative tool for Gr II–III. Fabrication details and return-to-climbing guidelines are standardized in clinical practice.

- **“Rest vs. load” debate**: The tendinopathy extrapolation (Cook & Purdam continuum, Alfredson eccentrics, Rio isometrics) is solid but could explicitly note that *early controlled loading* (isometrics at ≤3/10 pain) is now preferred over prolonged rest in modern climber protocols—consistent with your Bioplausible framing but worth reinforcing with recent expert consensus.

No major climbing-specific RCTs have emerged since Sjöman 2023; the “no climbing rehab RCTs” warning box remains 100% accurate.

**Folklore / community practices not yet addressed (or only lightly touched):**

- **Pulley-protection splint (PPS) fabrication and commercial kits**: Near-universal recommendation in climbing PT circles (Climbing Doctor, TrainingBeta “Rock Rehab Pyramid”, Hooper’s Beta). Preferred over H-taping for acute Gr II–III.

- **Unloader / circumferential rings** (pre-molded or custom thermoplastic): Frequently discussed as superior to tape for ongoing protection during early return-to-climbing.

- **Differentiation pitfalls**: Community often confuses pulley strain, tenosynovitis, and capsulitis; dynamic US is repeatedly stressed as essential to avoid mis-management.

- **Callus / skin and flapper timelines**: Your to-verify box is spot-on; no new empirical data.
