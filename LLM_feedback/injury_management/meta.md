## Finger Injury Management — exhaustive review

Your draft is unusually careful about evidence levels, which makes the gaps easier to spot. I read the full LaTeX and compared it against the 2022-2025 climbing medicine literature, PubMed/MEDLINE systematic reviews, and current community protocols from Volker Schöffl, The Climbing Doctor, and Hooper's Beta.

### 1. Mistakes and outdated information

**Diagnostic cutoffs are stale**
- You cite Iruretagoiena 2020: TBD cutoffs 1.9–5.1 mm for complete rupture. The 2024 MDPI systematic review (14 studies, PRISMA) now converges on >2 mm for A2/A4 injury on dynamic US, and notes bowstringing clinically correlates with multi-pulley injury (A2+A3+A4). It also reports a 41.4% grip-strength deficit as a functional diagnostic threshold — not mentioned in your table.
- A 2023 cadaver US study (cited in that review) found >3 mm TBD reliably indicates complete A2 tear, with 1.6 mm for partial — tighter than your range.

**Schöffl grading description needs correction**
- Your text: Grade II = "partial tear with mild bowstringing (<2mm)", Grade III = "complete A2 tear (>2mm)".
- Schöffl's original and the 2022 systematic review define: Grade 2 = complete A4 OR partial A2; Grade 3 = complete A2 OR A3; Grade 4 = multiple ruptures OR single rupture + lumbrical/collateral injury. The distinction matters because Grade 3 in the literature includes isolated A3 ruptures, which you omit.

**Immobilisation timelines are case-series folklore from 2003**
- You correctly flag them as bioplausible, but newer data challenges the 10–14 day rigid immobilisation for Grade II–III.
- Hartnett et al. (2023) pulley-ring orthosis (PRO) case series: 12 climbers with Grade I–III A2 injuries used a custom thermoplastic ring and were allowed to climb as tolerated from week 1. All returned to pre-injury level with improved pain and strength, no failures. This directly contradicts "14d immobilisation" as standard.

**Warm-up evidence is mis-weighted**
- You cite FIFA 11+ (~50% lower-limb injury reduction) as mechanistic support. A 2024 systematic review of 7 climbing studies (n≈4000) found *no protective effect* of warm-ups or stretching for finger injuries, and called evidence for preventive measures "inconsistent". Keep the mechanistic rationale, but note the climbing-specific null finding.

**Return-to-sport after surgery**
- You state Grade III conservative 3–5 months, surgical 3–6 months. The 2025 direct-suture series (8 elite climbers) achieved pre-injury level at mean 5.8 months (range 3–8) with *shorter* recovery than traditional graft reconstruction (>6 months). The 2022 systematic review of 189 patients also found satisfactory outcomes for both conservative and surgical management in Grade 3–4, with no clear superiority — undermining a conservative-only recommendation for Grade III.

### 2. Major gaps — injuries you don't cover

Your section is pulley-centric. Prevalence data suggest you need at least three additional entities, weighted by recurrence:

- **Finger joint capsulitis/synovitis** – Schöffl et al. 2025 Frontiers prospective case series (n=50, 2015-2018) developed a stage-specific algorithm (conservative → steroid → radiosynoviorthesis). All patients resumed climbing within 12 months, pain dropped significantly, no OA correlation. This is the first climbing-specific treatment pathway for PIP capsulitis and is absent from your review.

- **Flexor tenosynovitis** – Mohn et al. 2022 retrospective 10-year database (n=65). 91% managed with load reduction + taping, 75% regained or exceeded prior level, no surgery needed. Predictors: initial daily-life pain and climbing >7b. You mention tenosynovitis only in passing under pulley complications.

- **Lumbrical shift syndrome** – Healio clinical review notes lumbrical injuries are "prevalent" alongside pulley and tendonitis, especially in pocket climbing. Hooper's Beta (2025) provides grade I/II rehab protocols: early manual therapy, lumbrical-specific isometrics, and progressive pocket loading — completely missing from your text.

- **Collateral ligament PIP sprains** – not mentioned, yet community case reports suggest they account for 5–10% of finger presentations and mimic pulley pain.

### 3. Questionable claims needing nuance

**NSAIDs** — you omit them entirely, but literature is conflicted:
- Review in J Appl Physiol (2013) shows COX-2 inhibition impairs bone and tendon-to-bone healing in animal models, with human retrospective data linking indomethacin to 29% nonunion vs 7% controls.
- Conversely, a randomized double-blind trial post-flexor tendon repair found ibuprofen improved ROM and reduced adhesions (DASH +11.4%).
Your "rest vs load" debate should include this pharmacologic modifier.

**H-taping clinical benefit** — you correctly label it bioplausible, but the 2022 BMC systematic review (Larsson et al.) concluded low-moderate certainty for 15–22% bowstringing reduction in *injured* fingers only, no effect in healthy fingers, and *zero* RCTs on re-injury. The +13% crimp MVC from Schöffl 2007 (n=12, no CI) remains a single-source claim — flag it as such.

**Eccentric loading transfer** — Alfredson Achilles data are strong, but a 2023 study on eccentric finger movements found they occur frequently in *all* grip types during normal climbing, not just slips. This challenges the assumption that eccentrics are a novel rehab stimulus for pulleys.

### 4. Folklore and community practices not addressed

Your folklore boxes cover flappers and taping, but miss practices now standard in high-level rehab circles:

- **Pulley rings / unloader orthoses** – beyond PRO, Schöffl posted 3D-printed therapeutic rings (lime/pink/teal) in 2025, designed to offload A2 during early loading. Community adoption is growing on Instagram and in German climbing PT clinics.

- **"Climb-through" protocols** – The Climbing Doctor and Tyler Nelson now advocate pain-guided hangboarding from week 1–2 for Grade I (20–40% MVC, open-hand), progressing 5–10% weekly. Your text mentions Nelson but doesn't detail the progression thresholds that coaches actually use.

- **Lumbrical rehab specifics** – Hooper's Beta recommends: (1) passive interossei stretch, (2) resisted lumbrical "table-top" holds, (3) two-finger pocket isometrics with adjacent fingers splinted. No RCT, but widely disseminated.

- **Skin management nuance** – Antihydral (methenamine) use for sweaty skin is folklore with dermatology risk (glassy skin, splits). You mention it briefly; community consensus now favors 2–3x/week application only, with mandatory moisturization — worth noting.

### 5. Suggested additions and corrections

- Update Table 1 to include 2024 diagnostic thresholds: US >2 mm TBD, grip deficit >41.4%, and note heterogeneity.
- Revise Schöffl grading to match 2022 systematic review definitions.
- Add a short subsection on capsulitis with the 3-stage algorithm, and on tenosynovitis with load-reduction data.
- Create a "non-pulley soft-tissue injuries" box covering lumbrical shift (mechanism: single-finger pocket with adjacent fingers extended) and collateral ligament sprain.
- Replace immobilisation dogma with a spectrum: rigid immobilisation (historical) vs early functional bracing with PRO (2023 evidence).
- Include NSAID discussion with balanced animal/human data, recommending acetaminophen for analgesia in first 2 weeks if bone-tendon healing is a concern.
- Acknowledge that no climbing RCT exists as of 2025 — the 2022 systematic review included only 4 clinical studies (all case series, MINORS low-moderate), confirming your caveat but updating the source base.
