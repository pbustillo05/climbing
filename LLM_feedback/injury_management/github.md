## 5) Big missing injury categories (high prevalence in climbers) that your section barely covers or omits

Right now, you go deep on A2 pulley injuries, mention flexor tendon strains, then skin. For “Finger Injury Management” in climbers, the big omissions are:

### A) Flexor tendon tenosynovitis (very common overuse condition)
You mention tenosynovitis only in passing (in the capsulitis paper intro, etc.), but you don’t provide a dedicated subsection or management pathway.

There’s a climbing-specific retrospective descriptive study with outcomes and symptom duration:

- [10.3390/biology11060815 | Mohn et al. | Nonoperative Treatment of Finger Flexor Tenosynovitis in Sport Climbers—A Retrospective Descriptive Study Based on a Clinical 10-Year Database]

This is directly relevant to:
- natural history (“favorable course” claim),
- load reduction as the dominant intervention,
- typical symptom duration (they report long duration; useful for expectation management),
- predictors (daily life pain intensity; higher climbing grade predicting longer duration).

### B) PIP/DIP capsulitis/synovitis (“finger joint capsulitis”) — common in climbers
You currently do not have a section on it, but a climbing-specific case study provides a structured rehab framework:

- [10.3389/fspor.2023.1185653 | Vagy | Clinical management of finger joint capsulitis/synovitis in a rock climber]

Even if it’s only a case report, it matters because:
- climbers commonly mislabel this as “pulley tweak,”
- management often differs (joint unloading, mobility, movement correction),
- return timelines/irritability behave differently than pulley tears.

### C) Lumbrical tear / “lumbrical shift syndrome” (pocket holds)
You don’t cover lumbrical injuries at all. This is a classic climbing-specific entity with a clear mechanism (one-finger pockets, adjacent finger flexion).

Add:
- [10.1016/S0266-7681(02)00250-4 | Schweizer | Lumbrical Tears in Rock Climbers]

### D) Chronic joint adaptations / early OA (important for “management expectations” and “return-to-crimp” decisions)
You mention OA only indirectly (in capsulitis paper intro, etc.). There are climbing-specific imaging studies showing osteophytes/cartilage changes in elite climbers, including longitudinal follow-up:

- [10.1080/17461391.2019.1631389 | Pastor et al. | Cartilage abnormalities and osteophytes in the fingers of elite sport climbers: An ultrasonography-based cross-sectional study]
- [10.1080/17461391.2021.1943716 | Pastor et al. | Long-term evolution of cartilage abnormalities and osteophytes in the fingers of elite sport climbers: A cross-sectional 10-year follow-up study]

These help you justify:
- why chronic PIP/DIP pain might not be “inflammation you can rehab away,”
- why load management and grip-style modification can be long-term necessities.

## 8) Community / folklore practices you can add (and label)

These show up repeatedly in climbing hubs and forums; you don’t need to endorse them, but acknowledging them (as folklore) improves completeness:

1) **Rigorous use of rigid pulley orthoses** (SPOrt/unloader rings) especially for *continuing to climb while rehabbing* chronic pulley insufficiency.  
   - [https://www.rocknsport.com/climbers-finger | The Climbing Doctor / RocknSport | Climber’s Finger]

2) **“No-hang” loading** (lifting weights off the ground via edge) as an early-stage alternative to hanging bodyweight (perceived safer, easier to dose). Common in r/climbharder and coaching circles.

3) **Contrast baths / heat-first protocols** for chronic synovitis/tenosynovitis; ice for acute flare-ups (very common, evidence mixed).

4) **Rice bucket / finger extensor work** for tenosynovitis/capsulitis management (often used to manage irritability while maintaining circulation and tendon gliding).

If you want to cite a mainstream climbing-mag protocol summary (lower evidence, but influential), you can optionally include:
