## I. CRITICAL FACTUAL ERRORS

### 1. Schöffl (2003) Grade II Definition Is Wrong

**This is the most consequential factual error in the section.** The tex defines:

> Grade II: "Partial tear with mild bowstringing (<2 mm TBD increase under load on US)"

But the actual Schöffl 2003 grading system defines Grade II as:

> **Complete rupture of the A4 pulley, OR partial rupture of the A2 or A3 pulley.**

This is confirmed in multiple secondary sources: the Artiaco et al. (2023) PMC systematic review, Crowley (2012), Bosco et al. (2022), and the Miro et al. (2021) review, all citing Schöffl 2003 verbatim. A complete A4 rupture is Grade II — a clinically meaningful distinction that changes management. Grade III then means complete rupture of A2 **or** A3. The tex collapses these into a "partial vs complete A2" binary that doesn't exist in the original paper. The `<2mm TBD` criterion is actually the boundary for **Grade I** (pulley strain without significant tendon-bone dehiscence), not Grade II.

Additionally, Grade IV has since been refined by Simon, Lutter & Schöffl (2022) into:

- **Grade IVa**: Double-pulley rupture (A2/3 or A3/4)
- **Grade IVb**: Triple-pulley rupture (A2/3/4), or single-pulley rupture with FLIP phenomenon, progressive contracture, or complicating pathology (tenosynovitis, collateral ligament injury, lumbrical involvement)

The tex treats Grade IV monolithically, which is now outdated.

## II. MAJOR STRUCTURAL GAPS — ENTIRE INJURY CATEGORIES MISSING

### Gap 1: Flexor Tenosynovitis — The Second Most Common Climbing Finger Injury

Tenosynovitis of the flexor tendons is entirely absent from the section despite being the **second most common climbing finger injury**: 7% in Schöffl 2003, 7% in Lutter et al. 2020 (MLTJ), and ranking among the top 4 injuries across all three Schöffl longitudinal cohorts (1998–2001, 2009–2012, 2017–2018). This is the injury most confused with pulley injury and most likely to cause prolonged recovery if managed as a pulley tear.

**What the literature says:**

- Mohn et al. (2022) retrospective study of 65 climbers with confirmed flexor tenosynovitis treated conservatively: all returned to climbing, ~75% regained or exceeded prior level, mean symptom duration 30.5 weeks. Load reduction (91%) was the most common and apparently sufficient treatment; no correlation found between specific therapy content and outcome, suggesting a favorable natural course. No randomized trial exists.
- Schöffl et al. (2019, *IJDRR*) uncontrolled case series (n=42): corticosteroid injection (×2 injections 7–10 days apart) was effective for **chronic** tenosynovitis (>6 weeks): 73.8% pain-free after 2nd injection; 84.2% mean pain reduction; no complications. Surgical treatment was not required in any case.
- Ultrasound diagnosis: "halo phenomenon" (peritendinous fluid in the tendon sheath around the flexor tendon) on high-resolution US is pathognomonic.

### Gap 2: PIP/DIP Joint Capsulitis/Synovitis — The Third Most Common

This injury is entirely absent despite being the **third most common** climbing finger injury (6–7% of all injuries; ~18.8% of finger injuries per Lutter 2020). A dedicated subsection is warranted.

**What the literature says:**

- Mechanism: high peak pressure in PIP joint under crimp loading → inflammatory mediators → synovial effusion → chronic capsulitis → potential osteoarthritis if untreated.
- Presentation: diffuse PIP joint swelling, stiffness (especially morning), pain on active flexion/extension, no bowstringing, no tenderness over the pulley course.
- Diagnosis: US shows joint effusion ± periarticular hyperemia; MRI if needed; must exclude pulley injury, collateral ligament injury, and volar plate pathology.
- Management per Schöffl (2025, Frontiers): load reduction + anti-inflammatory (first-line); hyaluronic acid or corticosteroid injection for chronic cases (>6 weeks). Schöffl 2025 algorithm paper reported 72% returned to prior level (28% showed decreased performance, often due to extended forced rest rather than residual symptoms).
- Long-term risk: untreated chronic capsulitis → early osteoarthritis. Enlargement of PIP joints is commonly seen in long-term climbers and represents a spectrum from hypertrophied periarticular tissue to osteophyte formation.

[10.3389/fspor.2025.1497861 | Schöffl, Lutter et al. | Efficacy of a new treatment algorithm for capsulitis of the fingers in rock climbers]
[10.3389/fspor.2023.1185653 | Tanguay et al. | Clinical management of finger joint capsulitis/synovitis in a rock climber]

### Gap 3: Lumbrical Muscle Tears — Fourth Major Climbing-Specific Finger Injury

Lumbrical injuries are absent despite being well-characterized since at least 2003 (Schweizer) and comprehensively studied by Lutter et al. (2018, n=60). These are increasingly common with the rise of fingerboard training and pocket holds.

**What the literature says:**

- Mechanism: "lumbrical shift" / "quadriga effect" — injury when ring or middle finger is loaded in pocket (flexed) while adjacent fingers are extended, creating a shearing force on the bipennate 3rd and 4th lumbrical muscles (unique anatomy: dual FDP origin). Classically: 2-finger pocket with ring and middle fingers, with the index/little fingers extending upward.
- The **lumbrical stress test** is the key diagnostic test: pain provoked by passive extension of the ring finger while the patient attempts to close their fist. Pain disappears when the middle finger is extended simultaneously (quadriga effect is neutralized).
- Classification (Lutter et al. 2018): Grade I = microtrauma, US-negative; Grade II = muscle fiber disruption visible on US; Grade III = musculotendinous disruption (US/MRI).
- In 60 consecutive patients: 57/60 were climbers; all recovered with adapted functional therapy; Grade III healing significantly longer. Return to climbing: 6+ weeks for Grade I/II, 3–6 months for Grade III.
- Key clinical distinction: pain is in the **palm** (at/proximal to MCP joint, radial side of ring finger) vs. a pulley injury (focal pain over proximal phalanx).

[10.1177/1753193418765716 | Lutter, Schweizer, Schöffl, Römer & Bayer | Lumbrical muscle tear: clinical presentation, imaging findings and outcome]
[https://www.hoopersbeta.com/library/how-to-fix-lumbrical-shift-syndrome-in-rock-climbers-2025 | Hooper's Beta | How to Fix Lumbrical Shift Syndrome from Rock Climbing (2025)]

### Gap 4: The Pulley Protection Splint (PPS/Thermoplastic Ring) — The Primary Conservative Management Device

The tex covers H-taping but completely omits the **pulley protection splint (PPS)** — a thermoplastic circumferential ring worn full-time during rehabilitation. This is now the dominant conservative management device for Grade II-III pulley injuries in climbing medicine.

**What the literature says:**

- Schneeberger & Schweizer (2016): 47 complete pulley ruptures in 45 climbers; PPS worn continuously for 2 months. US-measured TBD decreased from mean 4.4±1.0 mm to 2.3±0.6 mm for A2 (a 50% reduction); 4.4→2.3 mm; A4 from 2.9±0.7 to 2.1±0.5 mm (40% reduction). **38/43 climbers (88.4%) regained prior climbing level at mean 8.8 months.**
- The PPS works differently from H-tape: it's a firm ring that physically compresses the tendon back toward the bone continuously (including at rest), not just during dynamic loading.
- The Schöffl 2020 MLTJ review explicitly recommends: "the pulley support ring therapy must be started within a few days after the trauma and be performed strictly for 6 to 8 weeks." If not started promptly or worn inconsistently, contracture risk increases.
- H-tape and PPS serve different roles: H-tape is for activity modification during reloading; PPS is for continuous passive bowstringing reduction during healing. Both may be used together.

[10.1016/j.wem.2015.12.017 | Schneeberger & Schweizer | Pulley ruptures in rock climbers: outcome of conservative treatment with the pulley-protection splint — a series of 47 cases]

### Gap 5: The FLIP Phenomenon — Explains Chronic Tenosynovitis After Pulley Rupture

The tex says (in the surgical indications box) "persistent symptomatic bowstringing with functional deficit" as a surgical indication, but doesn't explain **why** some patients develop chronic tenosynovitis after seemingly minor ruptures. The FLIP (Flap Irritation Phenomenon) is the mechanism.

**What the literature says:**

- Schöffl I. et al. (2011, JAB): cadaver study of 16 fingers loaded to rupture. In one finger, the pulley stump **capsized under the intact tendon sheath**, creating constant mechanical friction (like a saw on the tendon). Two patients with prolonged recovery were diagnosed clinically: US showed oscillating hyperechoic structure; MRI confirmed. Surgery (stump removal ± repair) resolved the condition.
- Clinical implication: a patient with Grade I or II pulley injury who shows no bowstringing but has persistent VAS>3 at rest, palpable irregularity, and US evidence of a "flipping" echoic structure should be referred to a climbing-experienced surgeon for consideration of FLIP diagnosis.

[10.1123/jab.27.4.291 | I. Schöffl, T. Baier & V. Schöffl | Flap Irritation Phenomenon (FLIP): Etiology of Chronic Tenosynovitis After Finger Pulley Rupture]

### Gap 6: Adolescent Physeal/Periphyseal Injuries — Most Common Injury in Young Climbers

The section mentions no consideration for climbers under 18, despite physeal stress injuries being the most common climbing-specific injury in adolescents (vs. pulley rupture in adults). As climbing enters schools and youth competition programs post-Tokyo, this population is increasingly relevant.

**What the literature says:**

- The crimp grip in adolescents (growth plates open until ~16-17 in boys, ~14-15 in girls) preferentially stresses the PIP joint physis rather than the pulley (the physis is the mechanical weak point). Presentation: PIP joint pain and swelling in a young climber; often initially misdiagnosed as pulley injury.
- Schöffl V. et al. (2022, Am J Sports Med): prospective multicenter algorithm study. Management algorithm: stop climbing immediately; XR (may appear normal); MRI if XR negative and high suspicion. Treatment: rest from climbing until pain-free; protected loading; total rest typically 6–12 weeks; no permanent damage if caught early, but risk of physeal damage and growth disturbance if ignored.
- Schöffl V. et al. (2025, BMJ Open Sport Exerc Med): updated prospective analysis of 137 injuries in 95 adolescent patients, finding physeal injuries in 29.9% of all injuries.

[10.1177/03635465211056956 | Schöffl V., Schöffl I., Flohé S., El-Sheikh Y. & Lutter C. | Evaluation of a Diagnostic-Therapeutic Algorithm for Finger Epiphyseal Growth Plate Stress Injuries in Adolescent Climbers]

### Gap 7: NSAIDs in Finger Injury Management — Completely Absent

The section says nothing about NSAIDs despite climbers frequently self-medicating. This is a contested, clinically important topic, especially for managing acute vs. chronic conditions.

**What the literature says:**

- For **tenosynovitis** (primarily inflammatory): NSAIDs likely beneficial short-term (analgesia + anti-inflammatory for acute phase). But sustained use may be counterproductive as tenosynovitis transitions from inflammatory to mechanical.
- For **pulley / fibrocartilaginous tissue healing**: The evidence is conflicted. Multiple in-vitro studies show COX-2 selective inhibitors harm tenocyte proliferation and collagen synthesis. Non-selective NSAIDs show mixed evidence in animal models. One human trial on Achilles tendinopathy (ibuprofen, 1 week) showed no functional benefit vs. placebo. A scoping review (31 human + animal studies) concluded NSAIDs likely have little role in tendinopathy beyond acute pain relief and may impair collagen matrix remodeling.
- For the **A2 pulley specifically** (fibrocartilaginous ligament, not tendon or pure ligament): there is **no data**. Extrapolation from tendons is uncertain.
- Practical gap: Many climbers take ibuprofen chronically while continuing to train through pulley pain. No study has measured the effect on healing outcomes.

[10.1152/japplphysiol.01203.2012 | Lisowski et al. | NSAID therapy effects on healing of bone, tendon, and the enthesis]

### Gap 8: Asymptomatic Bowstringing in Experienced Climbers — Critical for US Interpretation

The tex presents TBD cutoffs (1.9–5.1 mm range for complete rupture) without acknowledging that **experienced high-level climbers have physiologically elevated TBD even without injury history**.

Miro et al. (2021) noted that experienced climbers had significantly longer TBD-25% (0.3 mm longer at A2, 0.4 mm longer at A4) than non-climbers, even without reported injury. This means:
1. The diagnostic cutoffs derived from non-climber populations may be systematically too low for experienced climbers.
2. Structural "bowstringing" visible on US may represent adaptation (pulley hypertrophy/remodeling) rather than current injury.
3. Return-to-climb criteria based solely on TBD normalisation may be over-conservative in experienced athletes.

This directly challenges the "gaps" section item 4 (ultrasound-guided return-to-climb criteria) — but the tex's framing doesn't mention the adaptation confound at all.

### Gap 9: Prophylactic Taping Is Counter-Evidenced — Not Mentioned

The tex only discusses taping for injured fingers. Current evidence suggests **prophylactic/circumferential taping on healthy fingers may be biomechanically counterproductive and does not prevent injury**:

- Warme & Brooks (2000, Am J Sports Med): prophylactic circumferential taping did not protect against A2 rupture in a cadaveric model and increased the sustained load on the pulley.
- Larsson et al. (2022) SR (`LNB22`, already in your bib): "no significant effect in uninjured fingers" — correctly cited in your H-tape box but its prophylaxis-negative finding deserves explicit call-out.
- Schöffl 2020 MLTJ explicitly states: "A general protective pulley-tape around an intact pulley is very unlikely to be effective in healthy fingers and showed evidence of even increasing injury risk."

The community practice of "pre-taping" as injury prevention is thus not supported and potentially harmful. This is high-value folklore to address.

### QC1: "Ring and middle fingers account for ~98% of pulley injuries" — This needs a source

The tex states this as established fact. The Vigouroux et al. (2008) abstract in your bibliography supports ring/middle finger predominance, and the Schöffl (2003) cohort confirms ring finger most common. But "~98%" is a very precise claim — the actual Schöffl data shows ring + middle = the dominant digits but I cannot find "98%" stated in any paper. Your tex bibliography includes `VQPC08` (Vigouroux 2008 conference abstract), which may be the source, but that is flagged as "verify from full proceedings." This claim needs a specific source.

### QC2: "92% conservative Gr. I-III returned to prior level" — Presentation Is Misleading

The tex's study table says Schöffl 2003 achieved "92% conservative Gr. I--III returned to prior level." But the actual text of Schöffl 2003 states: "all patients treated conservatively returned to their previous level of sports activity, **except for seven who needed more time** to return to full recovery because of persistent pain due to tenovaginitis." This is not the same as a 92% success rate — the seven "failures" eventually returned; the full return rate was 100% over a longer timeframe. The "92%" is a derived figure and mischaracterizes the finding.

### QC3: The Schweizer (2000) Taping Data — "distal placement" Claim

The tex says Schweizer (2000) found "more distal placement (toward distal end of proximal phalanx) reduced bowstringing by 22% and absorbed ≈12%." But Miro et al. (2021) specifically states that taping over the **distal end of the proximal phalanx** is recommended — i.e., the more distal position is the clinically preferred one. The tex presents both as options without clearly recommending the more effective position, which is the distal-proximal-phalanx location (not PIP bridge). This may cause practitioners to default to the less effective central or proximal placement.

### QC4: Larsson 2022 SR data on taping effect in injured fingers

The tex cites Larsson 2022 (`LNB22`) as showing "low-to-moderate certainty for bowstringing reduction (15--22%) in injured fingers." This is an accurate representation, but the same SR found **no significant effect on maximal voluntary contraction or muscle activation in uninjured climbers**, and also noted that taping effects are biomechanical only with no clinical outcome RCTs. The distinction between "injured" and "uninjured" effects of taping is important for the prophylaxis vs. rehab use cases and deserves more explicit treatment.
