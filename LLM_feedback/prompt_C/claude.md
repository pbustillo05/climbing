# Finger Injury Management in Rock Climbing: A Literature Review

> **Scope note**: No randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have been conducted specifically on climbing finger injury rehabilitation. All rehabilitation protocols herein are classified by actual evidence tier. Evidence-backed claims from non-climbing RCTs are clearly distinguished from bioplausible extrapolations and community folklore.

## 2. Narrative Synthesis

### 2.1 A2 Pulley Injuries

#### 2.1.1 Anatomy and Mechanism

The flexor tendon sheath of digits II–V contains five annular (A1–A5) and three cruciate (C1–C3) pulleys. The A2 pulley originates from the proximal half of the proximal phalanx and is the largest and biomechanically most critical of the annular pulleys; it prevents bowstringing of the flexor digitorum profundus (FDP) and flexor digitorum superficialis (FDS) during digit flexion. The ring (digit IV) and middle (digit III) fingers are injured at 2–3× the rate of the index and little fingers, attributable to their central anatomical position and their role as primary force-bearing digits in crimp grip configurations. **[Bioplausible: anatomical reasoning; supported by case series data in Schöffl et al. 2003, 2015]**

Grip position critically determines A2 pulley load. In the full-crimp position (DIP joints held in approximately 0–20° flexion or hyperextension by co-contraction of the intrinsics, PIP joints at ~90° flexion, MCP joints at ~45° flexion), the A2 pulley bears the highest mechanical load due to the acute angle of approach of the FDP tendon against the pulley. Schweizer (2001) estimated full-crimp A2 forces in the range of 35–40 N per kg body mass in cadaveric and model studies, compared with substantially lower forces in half-crimp (~30–35 N/kg) and open-hand positions (~20–25 N/kg). **[Evidence-backed for biomechanics: Schweizer 2001 — DOI unverified, verify before submission. Effect size: approximately 30–50% load reduction comparing full-crimp to open-hand; no 95% CI reported in available sources.]** The implication for injury prevention — preferring open-hand grip during training volume accumulation — is widely extrapolated from this biomechanical data but has not been confirmed in prospective injury-prevention trials. **[Bioplausible]**

#### 2.1.2 Grading

The Schöffl grading system (2003) classifies A2 pulley injuries on a four-grade scale and remains the dominant clinical and research classification scheme:

- **Grade I**: Partial tear, no bowstringing on static or dynamic ultrasound. Clinically: tenderness over A2, pain with resisted PIP flexion.
- **Grade II**: Partial tear with mild bowstringing (<2 mm tendon-to-bone distance increase on ultrasound under load). Clinically: palpable swelling, pain with active loading.
- **Grade III**: Complete A2 tear, bowstringing clearly visible on ultrasound (>2 mm tendon-to-bone distance increase). May have bruising and palpable defect.
- **Grade IV**: Complete tear of A2 plus involvement of adjacent pulleys (most commonly A3 or A4) or damage to the FDP/FDS tendon itself. Associated with greater bowstringing and potential for flexor tendon dysfunction.

**[Evidence source for grading: Schöffl et al. 2003 — case series, DOI unverified. No validated inter-rater reliability data for this grading system is available from climbing-specific RCTs.]**

#### 2.1.3 Diagnosis

**Physical examination**: Pain reproduced by resisted PIP flexion and direct palpation over the A2 pulley (proximal volar aspect of proximal phalanx) is the primary clinical indicator. Bowstringing may be palpable or visible in Grade III–IV injuries when the digit is actively flexed against resistance. Sensitivity and specificity of clinical examination alone, without imaging, are not established from controlled diagnostic accuracy studies. **[Bioplausible evidence tier for clinical exam reliability]**

**Imaging**: High-resolution ultrasound (≥15 MHz linear probe, dynamic assessment in both extended and flexed positions) is the preferred first-line modality. Dynamic assessment — comparing tendon-to-bone distance in 20° passive extension vs. full active flexion — is essential; static views alone miss partial tears and underestimate Grade II bowstringing. MRI offers superior soft-tissue contrast and does not require active patient participation (advantageous when swelling limits dynamic examination), but is significantly more expensive, less accessible, and cannot be performed dynamically. **[Bioplausible: consensus from climbing sports medicine literature — Schöffl et al.; formal diagnostic accuracy studies with ROC analysis and sensitivity/specificity data are absent from the climbing-specific literature as of the time of writing.]**

Klauser et al. have published on ultrasound anatomy and pathology of the flexor tendon sheath in context relevant to climbers (exact citation and DOI require verification before submission). In the general hand surgery literature, MRI sensitivity for complete pulley tears is approximately 90–95% and for partial tears approximately 65–80%, though these figures derive from non-climbing populations and cannot be directly applied. **[Bioplausible extrapolation]**

#### 2.1.4 Conservative Management (Grade I–II; Most Grade III)

Conservative management is the standard of care for Grade I–II injuries and the dominant approach for Grade III in non-elite climbers, with surgical intervention reserved for Grade III in elite or professional athletes requiring accelerated return timelines and for most Grade IV injuries. **[Evidence source: Schöffl et al. 2003, 2015 — case series only]**

**Immobilization**: Short-term immobilization (2–5 days) in a neutral or slight flexion splint is typically recommended in the acute phase (first 72 hours) to reduce inflammatory response. Prolonged immobilization (>1 week) is not supported by evidence from pulley-specific studies and is inconsistent with the broader tendon and ligament healing literature, which demonstrates that early controlled mechanical stimulation promotes aligned collagen deposition. **[Bioplausible: extrapolated from tendon biology (Kannus 2000; Józsa & Kannus 1997) — specific DOIs require verification]**

**Return-to-loading timeline**: Proposed timelines from clinical practice and case series are:
- Grade I: Progressive reloading beginning at 1–2 weeks post-injury; return to climbing 4–6 weeks
- Grade II: Progressive reloading beginning at 3–4 weeks; return to climbing 6–10 weeks
- Grade III (conservative): Progressive reloading beginning at 6–8 weeks; return to full climbing 3–5 months

These timelines represent clinical experience reported in Schöffl et al. (2003) and related practitioner literature and have not been validated in controlled trials. **[Folklore → Bioplausible tier: mechanistically consistent with tendon healing biology but no climbing-specific RCT confirmation]**

**H-taping / ring splinting**: H-tape (figure-8 taping around the PIP joint, creating a dorsal bridge) and thermoplastic ring splints (custom-fabricated, limiting full PIP extension and thereby reducing the force arm that loads the A2 pulley in the crimp position) are widely employed during return-to-climbing phases. The biomechanical rationale — reducing pulley stress by restricting crimp depth — is plausible; Schweizer (2001) provides indirect support via force measurements showing grip-position dependence of pulley load. However, no RCT or controlled case series has measured re-injury rates or mechanical outcomes with vs. without taping. **[Folklore: Climbing community universal practice; Bioplausible mechanical basis from Schweizer 2001. Effectiveness for re-injury prevention unproven.]**

**Load-managed rehabilitation via tendinopathy principles**: The Cook and Purdam (2009) continuum model proposes a reactive–dysrepair–degeneration spectrum for tendon pathology, with management principles varying by stage: reactive stage warrants load reduction (not complete rest) and isometric loading; late dysrepair and degenerative stages benefit from progressive isotonic and functional loading. Alfredson et al. (1998) demonstrated that heavy-load eccentric training was superior to continued activity alone for chronic Achilles tendinopathy (100% vs. 0% return to running at 12 weeks; effect size large, 95% CI not reported in original paper — verify). Rio et al. (2015) demonstrated that isometric leg extension produced immediate pain reduction (VAS −2.2 vs. +0.4 for isotonic; 95% CI not available from secondary source — verify original) and reduced cortical inhibition in patellar tendinopathy. **[Evidence-backed for Achilles and patellar tendinopathy; Bioplausible extrapolation to A2 pulley injury only — pulleys are annular ligamentous structures, not tendons, and loading responses may differ.]**

The pulley is anatomically and biologically distinct from the Achilles tendon: it is a condensation of the fibrous flexor sheath composed primarily of type I collagen with fibrocartilaginous areas at regions of high compressive load, whereas the Achilles is a large energy-storage tendon. The cellular and matrix responses to progressive loading likely overlap (fibroblast mechanosensing, integrin-mediated collagen remodeling) but cannot be assumed to be identical. The application of progressive loading protocols to pulley rehabilitation is therefore bioplausible but constitutes an extrapolation from a different tissue type. **[Bioplausible]**

#### 2.1.5 Surgical Management (Grade III–IV)

Surgical reconstruction of the A2 pulley is indicated for: (a) Grade IV injuries with documented tendon involvement; (b) Grade III injuries in elite athletes or professional climbers where conservative management has failed or timeline demands accelerated return; (c) cases with persistent symptomatic bowstringing after adequate conservative management (typically ≥4–6 months). **[Folklore → clinical consensus: Schöffl case series, climbing sports medicine practitioner consensus]**

Surgical techniques include direct suture repair, loop reconstruction using a free palmaris longus graft or local tissue, and various other described reconstructions. Case series by Schöffl and colleagues (multiple publications, 2000–2020) report return-to-sport at 3–6 months post-surgery, with re-injury rates of approximately 15–25% in case series follow-up. Functional outcomes are generally reported as good or excellent in the majority of cases at 1-year follow-up. **[Low-quality evidence: case series only; no controls; heterogeneous surgical techniques; publication bias likely. All outcome data from this domain must be described as case series evidence, not controlled evidence. Specific citation DOIs require verification before submission.]**

#### 2.1.6 Load-Managed Hangboard Rehabilitation During Recovery

Tyler Nelson (Camp 4 Human Performance) has disseminated progressive loading protocols for pulley rehabilitation, primarily through clinical practice, online content, and professional development channels for climbing physiotherapists and coaches. His protocols apply the Cook-Purdam and Alfredson load-management principles to structured hangboard exercises, proposing: (a) isometric loads beginning at 60–70% of perceived maximum tolerable load in the early reactive phase; (b) progressive increases in duration and intensity following symptom-guided progression rules; (c) grip-position progression from open-hand through half-crimp, with full-crimp avoided until late-stage rehabilitation. **[Folklore: Tyler Nelson, C4HP — primary source is practitioner/coaching channels, not peer-reviewed publication. The mechanistic framework is Bioplausible based on Cook & Purdam 2009 and Alfredson et al. 1998.]**

The specific load thresholds proposed (e.g., 60–70% perceived maximum) are not empirically derived from pulley-specific loading studies and represent clinical judgment extrapolated from tendinopathy rehabilitation literature. No peer-reviewed study has measured A2 pulley load during hangboard exercises with validated force-sensing systems and correlated those measurements with tissue healing outcomes. The evidence gap is therefore substantial: the framework is logically consistent with tendon biology but no quantitative validation exists in the target tissue. **[Bioplausible: mechanistic framework; Folklore: specific thresholds and protocols]**

### 2.2 Flexor Tendon Strains

#### 2.2.1 Anatomy and Mechanism

The FDP and FDS tendons traverse the fibrous flexor sheath through the annular and cruciate pulley system. FDP inserts into the distal phalanx; FDS splits at the level of the proximal phalanx to insert bilaterally onto the middle phalanx base. In rock climbing, flexor tendon strains (partial or microtear injuries to the tendon proper, as distinct from pulley rupture) typically occur at the musculotendinous junction (MTJ) or within the substance of the tendon under repeated high-load crimp grip loading. They are less common than annular pulley injuries as primary presenting diagnoses but may co-occur, particularly in Grade IV pulley injuries where FDP/FDS involvement is definitional. **[Bioplausible: anatomical reasoning and clinical report; no climbing-specific epidemiological data distinguishing flexor tendon strain prevalence from pulley injury prevalence in isolation]**

#### 2.2.2 Grading

A parallel three-grade system is used:
- **Grade I**: Minor fiber disruption; no architectural loss on ultrasound; tenderness over tendon course; pain with resisted flexion.
- **Grade II**: Partial tear; visible hypoechoic defect on ultrasound; weakened active flexion; may have focal swelling.
- **Grade III**: Complete or near-complete disruption; functional deficit in active flexion; requires urgent surgical evaluation.

Grade III flexor tendon injuries in climbers are uncommon and represent a different management pathway (surgical primary repair, formal hand surgery protocol) that is outside the scope of conservative rehabilitation management. **[Grading: clinical consensus; no climbing-specific grading validation study]**

#### 2.2.3 Management Principles

Management of Grade I–II flexor tendon strains follows principles closely parallel to A2 pulley Grade I–II management: (a) acute load reduction for 5–14 days; (b) early progressive loading beginning in the second or third week, guided by pain response; (c) progressive return to full loading over 6–12 weeks. The mechanistic basis is identical to the Cook-Purdam continuum framework described above. **[Bioplausible: same extrapolation from tendinopathy literature; no climbing-specific RCT or case series specifically distinguishing flexor tendon strain from pulley injury outcomes]**

Differentiating pulley injury from flexor tendon strain clinically is important because the anatomical location of maximal tenderness, the ultrasound findings, and the prognostic implications differ. Tenderness localised to the volar surface of the proximal phalanx over the A2 pulley region, with bowstringing visible on dynamic ultrasound, is more consistent with a pulley injury. Tenderness along the tendon course proximal or distal to the pulley region, with internal tendon changes on ultrasound but no bowstringing, is more consistent with a primary tendon strain. In clinical practice, these lesions co-occur, and precise anatomical attribution requires high-quality dynamic ultrasound. **[Bioplausible]**

### 2.3 Skin Management

#### 2.3.1 Flappers (Traumatic Skin Tears)

A "flapper" is a traumatic partial-thickness skin tear, typically occurring at the base of a callus where the transition between thickened and normal skin creates a stress riser under tangential shear load on rock hold edges. They are most common on the second and third digit palmar skin and the thenar eminence. **[Folklore: climbing community phenomenology; no biomechanical study of flapper mechanism exists]**

**Acute management**: Standard wound care principles apply — trim any detached skin flap with sterile scissors to prevent bacterial trapping and maceration; irrigate; apply a thin moisturizer or skin balm (e.g., Joshua Tree Salve, Climb On, or neutral white petrolatum) to maintain epithelial moisture and prevent desiccation-induced delayed healing. Taping (pre-wrap + athletic tape or climbing-specific finger tape over the healed or partially-healed wound) allows earlier return to climbing. **[Folklore: climbing community universal practice. General wound healing evidence supports moist wound environment for epidermal regeneration — this extrapolation is Bioplausible, but no climbing-specific trial exists.]**

**Return to climbing**: Anecdotal community consensus suggests 3–7 days for mild flappers, 7–14 days for deeper or larger wounds before return to rock texture loading. No empirical data on healing timelines for climbing-specific flappers exist. **[Folklore: community consensus, no data]**

**Prevention — callus filing**: Filing calluses to reduce height and smooth the callus-to-normal-skin transition is near-universal in elite climbing practice, with the mechanistic goal of reducing the stress riser. This is logical based on basic biomechanical principles (stress concentration at thickness transitions) but has not been evaluated in any study measuring flapper incidence as a function of callus management practices. **[Folklore: universal climbing community practice, sourced primarily from practitioner consensus (e.g., Eric Horst, *Training for Climbing*; Will Anglin, Lattice Training) and athlete social media; no empirical study]**

#### 2.3.2 Chronic Skin Thinning

Chronic skin thinning from repeated friction over small-radius holds (particularly granite slabs and small crimps) results in reduction of the stratum corneum thickness below the functional baseline, presenting as hypersensitivity, split skin, and reduced friction coefficient. Contributing factors include: training volume, hold substrate roughness, ambient humidity (very low humidity accelerates transepidermal water loss and skin cracking), and individual skin phenotype. **[Bioplausible for mechanism; no climbing-specific study measures stratum corneum thickness longitudinally as a function of training load]**

**Interventions**: Climbing-specific skin care products (e.g., Rhino Skin Solutions, Friction Labs Chalk products) are marketed for skin conditioning, recovery, and friction optimization. No peer-reviewed study has evaluated any climbing skin product for outcomes of skin health, injury rate, or return-to-climbing time. **[Folklore: manufacturer claims; Bioplausible for emollient/humectant mechanisms based on general dermatology literature on moisturizer efficacy for skin barrier repair]**

Humidity management during training (moderate indoor humidity of 40–60% is anecdotally preferred by high-volume climbers over very low or very high extremes) has no direct evidence base in climbing. **[Folklore: practitioner consensus]**

#### 2.3.3 Collagen Supplementation for Skin Healing

Shaw et al. (2017) demonstrated in an RCT (n=8 crossover) that 15 g gelatin supplemented with 48 mg vitamin C, consumed 1 hour before intermittent exercise, doubled circulating collagen synthesis markers (P1NP) compared with placebo (effect size large; exact 95% CI requires verification from the original publication) and improved mechanical properties of in vitro–engineered ligament constructs. **[Evidence-backed for surrogate markers of collagen synthesis: Shaw et al. 2017, DOI: 10.3945/ajcn.116.138594 — verify before submission. Effect size: approximately 2× elevation of P1NP synthesis markers. Note: n=8 is very small; surrogate endpoint; no injury or skin-healing outcome data.]**

The dermatological literature on oral collagen peptides (hydrolyzed collagen, 5–10 g/day for 8–12 weeks) demonstrates modest improvements in skin elasticity and hydration as measured by corneometry and cutometry in double-blind RCTs (e.g., Proksch et al. 2014, *Skin Pharmacology and Physiology* — DOI unverified, verify before submission; Inoue et al. 2016 — DOI unverified). Effect sizes for skin elasticity improvements are typically 5–15% over control, statistically significant in trials of adequate duration. However, these studies were conducted in populations with age-related collagen loss, not in athletes with mechanically induced skin thinning, and the relevant endpoint (skin barrier competence under mechanical abrasion) has not been measured. **[Bioplausible for some benefit to skin collagen content; no direct evidence for climbing-specific skin healing or skin thinning prevention. Do not overstate.]**

### 2.4 General Injury Prevention Principles

#### 2.4.1 Warm-Up Efficacy

The general sports medicine literature supports warm-up for injury prevention via several proposed mechanisms: increased muscle and connective tissue temperature (improving viscoelastic properties), increased neuromuscular activation speed, and enhanced motor pattern recruitment. However, RCT evidence for warm-up reducing acute soft tissue injury rates in climbing specifically does not exist. The most methodologically robust warm-up RCT in a proximate domain is the FIFA 11+ protocol in football (soccer), which demonstrated ~50% reduction in lower-limb injury rates in a large cluster-RCT (Soligard et al. 2008, *BMJ* — DOI unverified, verify before submission). Extrapolation to finger pulley injuries in climbing is weak: the tissue type, injury mechanism, and loading characteristics differ substantially. **[Bioplausible: mechanistic basis for warm-up exists; evidence-backed only for lower-limb injuries in other sports. Climbing-specific warm-up RCT evidence: absent.]**

#### 2.4.2 Load Management — The 10% Rule

The "10% rule" (weekly training load increases should not exceed 10% of the prior week's load) is widely cited in running medicine and climbing coaching. Its evidence base is weaker than commonly assumed. In running, the rule derives primarily from epidemiological observation and retrospective analysis rather than prospective RCT. Gabbett (2016, *British Journal of Sports Medicine* — DOI: 10.1136/bjsports-2015-095788 — unverified, verify before submission) and subsequent work on the acute:chronic workload ratio provide a more quantitative framework, but even this evidence is primarily observational, contested in terms of thresholds, and derived from team sports not climbing. **[Folklore elevated to guideline status: the specific 10% threshold is unsupported by direct RCT evidence. Load management as a general principle is Bioplausible based on tissue adaptation biology; the specific 10% figure is arbitrary.]**

#### 2.4.3 Experience-Dependent Injury Risk

Sjöman, Grønhaug & Julin (2023) is the only peer-reviewed quantitative injury-risk survey conducted in a climbing-specific population. In their cross-sectional study of n=434 Finnish climbers, the association between route/bouldering-specific finger strength training (RFT) and sport/performance-impairing finger injury (SPIIF) was experience-dependent and directionally opposite in the two experience groups examined:

- In climbers with ≥6 years of experience, RFT was **negatively associated** with SPIIF (χ²=4.57, p=0.03), suggesting that structured finger strength training may be protective in experienced climbers.
- In climbers with <6 years of experience who had reached performance grade ≥7a (French sport), RFT was **positively associated** with SPIIF (χ²=4.61, p=0.03), suggesting that finger strength training in less experienced, rapidly progressing climbers may increase injury risk.

**[Evidence-backed for this specific association: Sjöman et al. 2023, DOI: 10.1016/j.wem.2023.06.004. Quality note: cross-sectional design prohibits causal inference; self-reported injury and training data; Finnish climbing population may not generalize; no dose-response data; no adjustment for confounders such as training volume, hold type, or warm-up practices.]**

This bidirectional pattern is consistent with theoretical models of training load injury risk in developing athletes (rapid capacity acquisition outpacing structural tissue adaptation) and the protective adaptation hypothesis in experienced athletes (accumulated structural adaptation to finger loading). The mechanism for protection in experienced climbers is bioplausible — structural adaptation of the fibrous flexor sheath, pulley hypertrophy, and neuromuscular co-contraction patterns — but has not been directly measured. **[Bioplausible for mechanism; Evidence-backed for the association in this specific dataset.]**

## 3. Open Questions

1. **What are the load-to-failure properties and healing kinetics of the human A2 pulley under progressive loading protocols?** No in vivo or ex vivo study has measured how incremental hangboard-based loading affects pulley tissue mechanics during the healing process. Without this data, progressive loading protocols remain mechanistically motivated but empirically unvalidated. A cadaveric or ultrasound-based tendon-to-bone distance tracking study during standardized progressive loads would substantially advance this field.

2. **Does grip position restriction during rehabilitation (enforced open-hand loading) improve healing outcomes relative to symptom-guided restriction?** H-taping and ring splinting are mechanistically motivated by Schweizer's (2001) force data, but no controlled study has compared re-injury rates, healing time, or functional outcomes between taped/splinted and untaped/unsplinted groups during rehabilitation.

3. **What distinguishes the injury phenotype of rapidly progressing novice climbers from that of experienced climbers, and what structural or neuromuscular factors mediate the protective association observed in Sjöman et al. (2023)?** A prospective cohort study tracking climbers from onset of structured finger training through their first 2–5 years, with objective load monitoring, ultrasound-based pulley morphology assessment, and grip-force measurement, would provide the first mechanistic evidence for the experience-dependent risk pattern.

4. **Do collagen synthesis supplementation protocols (gelatin + vitamin C, 15 g pre-training, per Shaw et al. 2017) improve clinically meaningful pulley or flexor tendon healing outcomes?** Shaw et al. demonstrated elevated surrogate markers; no study has measured actual injury recovery time, re-injury rate, or imaging-based healing in athletes using this protocol during rehabilitation. A moderate-sized RCT (n=60–80, Grade II–III A2 pulley injury) with ultrasound-confirmed outcomes is feasible.

5. **What is the incidence and natural history of subclinical pulley injury in high-volume climbers, and what proportion of complete (Grade III) A2 tears are preceded by detectable partial-tear prodromal stages?** Cross-sectional ultrasound surveys of asymptomatic high-volume climbers could determine subclinical injury prevalence; longitudinal follow-up could determine what proportion progress to symptomatic injury and whether early intervention alters natural history. This would directly inform whether routine ultrasound screening during training load intensification phases is justified.
