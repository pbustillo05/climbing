# 2. Narrative Synthesis

## **Sub‑section 1: A2 Pulley Injuries**

### **Anatomy & Mechanism**
- The **A2 pulley** spans the proximal phalanx and is the **thickest and strongest** annular pulley.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/)  
- Most commonly injured in **ring and middle fingers** due to crimp biomechanics. **Full crimp > half crimp > open hand** in pulley load. [Bioplausible: based on biomechanical consensus; no RCTs]  
- Mechanism: **eccentric loading** of the flexor tendon–pulley system produces high tensile forces leading to rupture.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/)

### **Grading (Schöffl classification)**
- **Grade I:** partial tear, no bowstringing  
- **Grade II:** partial tear, mild bowstringing  
- **Grade III:** complete tear, bowstringing  
- **Grade IV:** complete tear + adjacent pulley or FDP/FDS involvement  
[Evidence‑backed: widely accepted clinical classification; no intervention RCTs]

### **Diagnosis**
**Physical exam**  
- Pain with resisted PIP flexion, tenderness over A2, pain in crimp positions. [Bioplausible]  
- Bowstringing on resisted flexion suggests Grade III–IV. [Bioplausible]

**Imaging**  
- **Ultrasound (US)** is recommended as **primary modality** due to high accuracy, low cost, dynamic assessment.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/)  
- **Cadaveric evidence**: US reliably identifies A2 rupture landmarks and tendon‑bone distance (TBD) changes.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4418/14/19/2149)   [Life Science Network](https://www.lifescience.net/publications/1956566/ultrasound-diagnosis-of-a2-pulley-injuries-landmar/)  
- **MRI** is accurate but less accessible; no superiority over US demonstrated. [Bioplausible]

### **Conservative Management (Grade I–II, most Grade III)**

#### **Immobilization**
- Typical immobilization: **7–14 days** in slight flexion. [Folklore: climbing physio consensus; no RCTs]  
- Evidence gap: No controlled trials comparing immobilization durations.

#### **Return‑to‑loading timeline**
- Early phase: pain‑free passive ROM after 3–7 days to reduce stiffness. [Bioplausible: tendon healing models]  
- Progressive loading begins **2–4 weeks** post‑injury depending on pain and grade. [Folklore: physio guidelines]

#### **H‑taping / Ring splinting**
- No RCTs.  
- Community practice suggests reduced pain and perceived stability. [Folklore: Hooper’s Beta; climbing physio community]   [Hooper's Beta](https://www.hoopersbeta.com/library/a2-pulley-manual-for-climbers)  
- No evidence for reduced reinjury risk.

#### **Load‑managed rehabilitation**
- Extrapolated from **tendinopathy RCTs** (Achilles, patellar):  
  - **Eccentric loading (Alfredson protocol)** improves tendon remodeling. [Evidence‑backed: Alfredson 2000; not climbing‑specific]  
  - **Cook & Purdam continuum model** supports progressive load to stimulate collagen alignment. [Evidence‑backed]  
  - **Rio et al. isometric analgesia** suggests isometrics may reduce pain in tendinopathy. [Evidence‑backed]  
- **Extrapolation to pulleys** = **Bioplausible**, not validated.

### **Surgical Management (Grade III–IV)**
- **Indications:** complete rupture with significant bowstringing, multi‑pulley rupture, or failed conservative care. [Bioplausible]  
- **Evidence:** only case series; no RCTs. Outcomes generally good but variable.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9483560/)  
- **Return to climbing:** ~3 months for Grade III; up to 12 months for Grade IV. [Bioplausible; supported by Schöffl & Schweizer timelines]   [Google Sites](https://sites.google.com/myport.ac.uk/a2pulleyinjury/evidence-based-rehab)

### **Load‑managed hangboard rehabilitation**
- **Tyler Nelson protocols** (e.g., “density hangs,” progressive isometrics): **Folklore**, no trials.  
- Proposed thresholds:  
  - Start at **20–40% bodyweight** isometric hangs, progress by pain ≤3/10. [Folklore]  
- Evidence base: extrapolated from tendon mechanobiology → **Bioplausible**, not validated.

## **Sub‑section 2: Flexor Tendon Strains**

### **Anatomy & Mechanism**
- FDP and FDS tendons run under A2; strains occur with sudden high‑force crimping or dynamic moves. [Bioplausible]

### **Grading**
- Mild: pain without ROM loss  
- Moderate: pain + ≤25% ROM loss  
- Severe: pain + >50% ROM loss  
[Bioplausible; adapted from pulley injury clinical criteria]   [theclimbingdoctor.com](https://theclimbingdoctor.com/how-to-rehab-a-climbing-pulley-injury/)

### **Management**
- Overlaps with pulley rehab: short immobilization, early ROM, progressive loading. [Bioplausible]  
- No climbing‑specific RCTs.  
- Surgical referral for complete tendon rupture (rare in climbing). [Evidence‑backed: general hand surgery literature]

## **Sub‑section 3: Skin Management**

### **Flappers (acute skin tears)**
- **Acute management:** trim loose skin, moisturize, occlusive dressing, tape.  
  - No RCTs → **Folklore** (climbing community, coach consensus).  
- **Return to climbing:** typically 3–7 days depending on depth. [Folklore]  
- **Prevention:** callus filing, moisturization, sanding → **Folklore**.

### **Chronic skin thinning**
- No evidence for climbing‑specific interventions.  
- Humidity management, skin balms, sanding → **Folklore**.

### **Collagen supplementation for skin healing**
- **RCT evidence exists for skin hydration, elasticity, wrinkle reduction** (not climbing‑specific).  
  - Meta‑analysis of 26 RCTs (n=1721): improved hydration & elasticity.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10180699/)  
  - SRMA of 19 RCTs: improved hydration, brightness; modest wrinkle reduction.   [Frontiers](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2026.1618306/full)  
  - RCT (n=66): reduced wrinkles, ↑ elasticity/hydration.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2079-9284/12/2/79)  
- **Relevance to climbing skin injury:** **Bioplausible** (improved dermal matrix may aid healing), but **no RCTs on flappers or friction‑induced thinning**.

## **Sub‑section 4: General Injury Prevention Principles**

### **Warm‑up efficacy**
- No climbing‑specific RCTs.  
- Extrapolation from general sports science: warm‑ups reduce injury risk via increased tendon stiffness and neuromuscular readiness → **Bioplausible**.

### **Load management**
- The “10% rule” has **no RCT support** → **Folklore**.  
- Progressive overload principles from tendon literature support gradual increases → **Bioplausible**.

### **Experience‑dependent injury risk**
- **Sjöman et al., 2023** (n=434 Finnish climbers):  
  - RFT negatively associated with SPIIF in ≥6 yr experience (χ²=4.57, p=0.03).  
  - RFT positively associated in <6 yr climbers reaching ≥7a (χ²=4.61, p=0.03).  
  **[Evidence‑backed]**
