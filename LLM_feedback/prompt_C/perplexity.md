## Narrative synthesis

### A2 pulley injuries

The A2 pulley is the dominant symptomatic pulley injury in climbers and is classically most common in the ring and middle fingers, especially under high-load flexion in the full crimp position. [Evidence-backed] Full crimp loads the flexor system and pulley complex more than half crimp, which loads more than open-hand; the mechanical order is consistently described as full crimp > half crimp > open hand. [Evidence-backed] The clinical relevance is that high pulley force and increased tendon–bone distance drive bowstringing and pain, particularly during abrupt high-force gripping. [Evidence-backed]

#### Anatomy and mechanism

[Evidence-backed] The A2 pulley is located over the proximal phalanx and helps prevent bowstringing of the flexor tendons; the ring finger is often most affected, with the middle finger also highly vulnerable. [Evidence-backed] Biomechanical studies in climbers show very large flexor and pulley forces during crimping, but exact estimates vary by study and climbing task, so force values should be presented cautiously rather than as a single universal number. [Evidence-backed] If you want a concise statement for the review, use: “Crimping generates sufficiently high forces to exceed pulley tissue tolerance, particularly in loaded edge positions.” [Evidence-backed]

#### Grading

[Evidence-backed] Grade I is a partial tear without bowstringing, Grade II is a partial tear with mild bowstringing on ultrasound, Grade III is a complete tear with bowstringing, and Grade IV is a complete tear with adjacent pulley or FDP/FDS involvement. [Evidence-backed] This grading is clinically useful because it maps structural severity to conservative versus surgical consideration. [Evidence-backed]

#### Diagnosis

[Evidence-backed] Typical exam findings include localized volar finger pain, pain with resisted PIP flexion, pain during crimp loading, and sometimes visible or palpable bowstringing in more severe tears. [Evidence-backed] Ultrasound is generally preferred first-line imaging because it is dynamic, inexpensive, accessible, and can show bowstringing during resisted flexion; a 2022 review concluded that US should be the primary imaging modality for A2 pulley injuries. [Evidence-backed] MRI can be helpful when ultrasound is equivocal or when adjacent soft-tissue injury is suspected, but it is usually not the preferred first test for isolated pulley tears. [Evidence-backed] The literature supports US strongly, but the field still lacks large, high-quality diagnostic accuracy studies with uniform reference standards. [Evidence-backed] [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34342037/)

#### Conservative management

[Bioplausible] For Grade I–II injuries and many Grade III injuries, the most defensible approach is early protection followed by progressive reloading, but the exact immobilization duration is not established by climbing-specific RCTs. [Bioplausible] Common protocols use a short period of rest or relative immobilization, then pain-limited motion and delayed loading, with return to controlled loading often beginning around 2–6 weeks depending on symptoms and grade; however, these timelines are based on expert practice, not controlled trials. [Folklore: climbing physio practice] H-taping and ring splinting are widely used to reduce symptomatic load, but their effectiveness is largely inferred from biomechanics and community practice rather than comparative clinical trials. [Folklore: climbing community consensus]

[Bioplausible] Progressive loading is biologically plausible because tendon and enthesis tissues adapt to graded mechanical stress, and the tendinopathy literature supports load-based rehabilitation frameworks such as eccentric loading and the Cook & Purdam continuum model. [Bioplausible] However, those data come from Achilles, patellar, and rotator cuff-type conditions, not finger pulleys, so extrapolation should be stated explicitly as indirect. [Bioplausible] In a literature review, it is accurate to say that load-managed rehab for pulleys is “mechanistically supported but not climbing-RCT validated.” [Bioplausible]

[Bioplausible] A practical synthesis is that hangboard-based rehabilitation can begin only after pain is settling, swelling is minimal, and daily activities are comfortable, with very low-load isometrics or short-duration submaximal hangs progressing gradually. #### Surgical management

[Evidence-backed] Surgery is generally reserved for Grade IV injuries, multi-pulley injuries, or complete tears with major bowstringing, functional deficit, or failure of conservative care. [Evidence-backed] Published outcomes are mostly case series, so the evidence level is low for comparative effectiveness even when results appear favorable. [Evidence-backed] Return-to-climbing after surgery is commonly reported in the range of about 3–6 months, but this depends on reconstruction method, digit involved, and whether the climber returns to easy versus full-performance climbing. [Evidence-backed]

#### Load-managed hangboard rehab

[Folklore: Tyler Nelson and similar physio protocols] Progressive hangboard rehabilitation protocols associated with Tyler Nelson and similar climbing clinicians should be labeled as practitioner-derived protocols, not trial-proven therapies. [Folklore: practitioner protocol] Their core claims are that carefully dosed hangs restore tolerance while minimizing re-injury risk, often using submaximal edge positions, short durations, and gradual load increases; these ideas are biologically plausible but unsupported by climbing-specific RCTs. [Bioplausible] The evidence base is therefore indirect, drawing mainly from tendon adaptation theory and non-climbing loading studies rather than direct pulley trials. [Bioplausible]

### Flexor tendon strains

[Evidence-backed] Flexor tendon strains in climbers are less cleanly defined than pulley injuries because symptoms can overlap with pulley pain, lumbrical strain, and collateral soft-tissue injury. [Evidence-backed] Mechanistically, these involve overload of the FDP/FDS muscle-tendon unit rather than isolated pulley failure, so pain may be more diffuse along the volar finger or into the palm. [Evidence-backed] Management principles overlap with pulley rehab: relative rest, symptom-guided reloading, and a gradual return to gripping loads, but the evidence base is mostly extrapolated from general tendon rehabilitation rather than climbing-specific studies. [Bioplausible]

[Bioplausible] Mild strains are typically treated conservatively with load reduction, early controlled motion, and progressive strengthening once pain is controlled. [Bioplausible] For more severe partial tears, immobilization and delayed return are often recommended, but again this is based on sports medicine convention, not controlled climbing trials. [Folklore: climbing/hand therapy practice] Because the line between flexor strain and A2 injury is often blurry clinically, many rehab protocols are intentionally conservative and essentially identical in the early phases. [Bioplausible]

### Skin management

#### Flappers

[Folklore: climbing community consensus] Acute flapper management is usually to trim only loose nonviable skin, clean the wound, protect it with tape or a dressing, and avoid further shear until the wound seals. [Folklore: practitioner consensus] Moisturizing is commonly recommended for prevention of fissuring and chronic dryness, but immediate heavy moisturizing right before climbing is generally avoided in practice because it may reduce friction. [Folklore: practitioner consensus] There are no controlled climbing trials showing that trimming, taping, or moisturizing speeds return-to-climbing, so any statement about benefit should be clearly labeled as practice-based rather than evidence-backed. [Folklore: practitioner consensus]

[Folklore: climbing community consensus] Return to climbing after a flapper is usually when the wound is closed, non-bleeding, and can tolerate low-shear contact; in practice this is often days to 1–2 weeks depending on depth and size. [Folklore: practitioner consensus] There are no good data defining an exact timeline. [Folklore: practitioner consensus]

#### Chronic skin thinning

[Folklore: climbing community consensus] Chronic skin thinning from repeated friction on small holds is managed with callus filing, volume control, moisturization, and humidity-aware skin care, but these strategies are supported primarily by coach and climber consensus rather than trials. [Folklore: practitioner consensus] The mechanistic rationale is sensible: reducing sharp callus edges may lower shear concentration and reduce flap formation, while moisturization may reduce fissuring, but direct experimental evidence in climbers is lacking. ### General prevention

#### Warm-up

[Bioplausible] Warm-up likely improves tissue temperature, joint lubrication, motor control, and force tolerance, but climbing-specific randomized evidence for injury prevention is limited or absent. [Bioplausible] So warm-up should be described as sensible and likely beneficial, but not proven to prevent finger injury in climbers. [Bioplausible]

#### Load management

[Folklore: sports coaching rule] The “10% rule” is best treated as a rough coaching heuristic, not an evidence-backed climbing-specific prevention law. [Folklore: sports coaching rule] More defensible is symptom- and tolerance-based progression, because the load history, tissue response, and prior injury status vary widely across climbers. [Bioplausible] Progressive overload is biologically grounded, but the exact safe rate of progression for climbing fingers is not established by RCTs. [Bioplausible]
