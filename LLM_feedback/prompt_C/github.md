## 2) Narrative synthesis

### Sub-section 1: A2 pulley injuries

#### Anatomy and mechanism
- **A2 pulley location**: The **A2 annular pulley** spans the **proximal phalanx** (roughly from the proximal portion toward the junction of proximal 2/3 and distal 1/3). **Bowstringing** occurs when the pulley fails and the flexor tendon separates from the phalanx, increasing flexor moment arm and impairing extension. **[Bioplausible]** (anatomy + mechanism; supported in imaging review) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Digits most involved (ring/middle common in climbing)**: Climbing cohorts show heavy involvement of middle and ring fingers in pulley injury series. **[Bioplausible]** (climber cohorts; not a mechanistic experiment) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10495204/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Grip positions and loading**: Full crimp > half crimp > open hand loading hierarchy is widely taught in climbing medicine/physio; direct quantified comparisons vary by model and are not consistently RCT-derived. **[Bioplausible]** (biomechanics inference + climbing consensus; not “Evidence-backed” rehab evidence).  
- **Biomechanical force estimates**: Published taping biomechanics demonstrate measurable bowstringing forces in crimping and limited mechanical offloading by tape (i.e., tape absorbs only a minority of bowstringing force). **[Bioplausible]** for magnitude relevance to injury mechanism; **not** outcome evidence. ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10763736/?utm_source=openai))  

#### Grading (as requested)
- Grade I: partial tear, no bowstringing  
- Grade II: partial tear, mild bowstringing on ultrasound  
- Grade III: complete tear, bowstringing  
- Grade IV: complete tear + adjacent pulleys or FDP/FDS involvement  

This grading concept aligns with Schöffl’s 1–4 clinical severity approach (grade-dependent pathways). **[Bioplausible]** (classification utility supported by clinical cohort, not validated against hard outcomes). ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1580/1080-6032%282003%29014%5B0094%3APIIRC%5D2.0.CO%3B2?utm_source=openai))  

#### Diagnosis
- **Physical exam**: Local pain over the pulley region; pain with resisted finger flexion (especially when stressing the pulley system); sometimes palpable “snap” at injury; clinical exam alone is often nonspecific. **[Bioplausible]** (common clinical reasoning; not strongly controlled).  
- **Preferred imaging modality**:
  - **Ultrasound (US)** is typically recommended as **first-line** because it is **dynamic**, accessible, and in the literature shows **high diagnostic accuracy** for pulley tears. **[Evidence-backed for diagnosis]** (diagnostic accuracy evidence, not rehab) ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/?utm_source=openai))  
  - **MRI** is useful when US is equivocal or for complex injury patterns, but US is often favored for dynamic bowstringing assessment. **[Bioplausible]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9292555/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Diagnostic accuracy (US vs MRI)**:
  - In the best-available climber diagnostic study summarized in the pulley diagnostic systematic review: **dynamic US sensitivity ~98%, specificity ~100%** vs MRI reference standard; limitations include **verification bias** because only some cases had surgical correlation. **[Evidence-backed for diagnosis; caveated]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11867797/?utm_source=openai))  

#### Conservative management (Grade I–II, most Grade III)

**Important caveat (your framework):** No climbing RCTs of rehab protocols were identified here; therefore the *protocol details* below are **Bioplausible** (extrapolated from tissue-healing + tendinopathy loading principles) or **Folklore** (climbing community/clinician heuristics). Only *supporting modalities* like taping biomechanics and splint case series have climbing-specific data.

##### Immobilization protocols: duration, position, evidence
- **Short immobilization then protected motion** is repeatedly recommended in climbing clinical guidance pathways (grade 1–3 conservative with “initial immobilization and early functional therapy under pulley protection”). **[Bioplausible]** (clinical pathway recommendation without controlled comparisons). ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1580/1080-6032%282003%29014%5B0094%3APIIRC%5D2.0.CO%3B2?utm_source=openai))  
- **Evidence gap**: there is **no RCT** in climbers comparing (a) strict immobilization vs (b) early controlled motion for A2 injuries. **[Evidence-backed: absence noted]**

##### Return-to-loading timeline / when progressive hangboard rehab begins
- A practical progression often used: begin **very low-load, pain-limited isometrics** and **protected range of motion** early; introduce **structured finger loading** only once day-to-day pain is low and bowstringing is controlled (splint/tape), then build toward sport-specific grips. **[Bioplausible]** (tissue capacity concept; not tested in climber RCTs).  
- **Evidence anchor (indirect)**: tendinopathy literature supports **progressive loading** as a core intervention for tendon pain and function, but pulley is a **ligament-like structure** with different stress/strain and healing constraints. Extrapolation is plausible but unproven. **[Bioplausible]** (mechanism-based extrapolation).

##### H-taping / ring splinting: effectiveness vs community practice
- **Taping biomechanics (A2)**: Schweizer (2000) found **small reductions in bowstringing** when tape is placed directly over the pulley (↓2.8%) and larger reduction with more distal placement (↓22%) under their tested conditions; tape absorbed ~11%–12% of bowstringing force. **[Evidence-backed for biomechanical effect; not clinical outcomes]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10763736/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Pulley-protection splint (PPS)**: in a case series of **complete ruptures**, PPS reduced tendon–phalanx distance on ultrasound and most climbers returned to prior level by ~9 months. **[Bioplausible]** (uncontrolled clinical series; cannot attribute causality). ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27067301/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Community H-taping**: widely used, but clinical outcome evidence is sparse. **[Folklore: climbing community consensus; commonly attributed to climbing physio practice and coach heuristics]**

##### Load-managed rehabilitation and tendinopathy literature (Alfredson; Cook & Purdam; Rio)
- **Cook & Purdam continuum model** provides a conceptual basis: tendon response exists along a continuum, and treatment should match irritability/capacity. **[Evidence-backed for model paper; Bioplausible for pulley extrapolation]** ([bjsm.bmj.com](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/43/6/409.full?utm_source=openai))  
- **Rio et al. isometrics** (patellar tendinopathy) shows isometrics can acutely reduce pain and improve function/inhibition measures in tendinopathy populations. **[Evidence-backed for patellar tendon pain; Bioplausible for climbing finger pain modulation]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/25979840/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Alfredson-style eccentric loading**: Alfredson’s work is foundational for Achilles tendinopathy loading paradigms, but specific effect sizes + CIs are not consistently reported in early foundational papers, and the pulley is not an Achilles tendon. **[Bioplausible]** (concept transfer; not pulley evidence). ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10701715/?utm_source=openai))  

**Practical implication (explicitly tiered):**
- A **progressive loading plan** (pain-monitored, gradual volume/intensity, avoid high-strain grips early) is **[Bioplausible]** based on general load-capacity principles and tendinopathy rehab success elsewhere, **not** [Evidence-backed] for climbing A2 rehab.

#### Surgical management (Grade III–IV)
- **Indications**: Schöffl’s pathway recommends **grade 4** injuries for surgical repair; grade 1–3 generally conservative. **[Bioplausible]** (treatment guideline from cohort experience; not trial-tested). ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1580/1080-6032%282003%29014%5B0094%3APIIRC%5D2.0.CO%3B2?utm_source=openai))  
- **Outcomes evidence**: largely **case series** / surgeon experience; controlled outcome data are lacking. **[Evidence-backed: case-series-only reality]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10495204/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Return-to-climbing timeline post-surgery**: typically **months**, progressing from immobilization → protected motion → strengthening → sport-specific loading. Exact timelines vary by technique and injury complexity; robust comparative evidence is lacking. **[Bioplausible]** (expected tissue healing + standard hand surgery rehab; not climbing RCT).

#### Load-managed hangboard rehabilitation during recovery (Tyler Nelson + similar)
- **Tyler Nelson protocols**: widely circulated in climbing performance/rehab education, generally proposing graded hangs (e.g., open-hand or half-crimp emphasis, controlled intensity, pain monitoring, progressive load).  
  - **Evidence tier**: **[Folklore: Tyler Nelson/physio educator protocols]** for specific hangboard prescriptions because they are not supported by controlled climbing rehab trials.  
  - **Biological plausibility**: **[Bioplausible]** that carefully dosed loading could support remodeling and restore capacity, but pulley tissue strain-tolerance and optimal dosage are not established in climbers.
- **Proposed load thresholds** (examples commonly stated in climbing rehab circles): start with **very low intensity** (often framed as “pain ≤2–3/10 during, settles by next day”), progress load slowly, avoid full crimp until late.  
  - **Evidence tier**: **[Folklore: climbing physio consensus; pain-monitoring heuristic]** because these thresholds are not validated against imaging-confirmed healing or reinjury rates in controlled studies.

### Sub-section 2: Flexor tendon strains

- **Anatomy/mechanism**: Flexor digitorum profundus (FDP) and superficialis (FDS) strains/tenosynovitis can occur with high-volume gripping and rapid loading, sometimes co-occurring with pulley irritation. **[Bioplausible]** (clinical plausibility; not strongly trial-defined).  
- **Differentiation from pulley injury**:
  - Pulley injury: focal pain at pulley, bowstringing on dynamic US, increased tendon–phalanx distance with forced flexion. **[Evidence-backed for diagnostic construct]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/11867797/?utm_source=openai))  
  - Tendon strain/tenosynovitis: pain more along tendon sheath, swelling/crepitus possible; US/MRI may show tenosynovitis rather than pulley discontinuity. **[Bioplausible]**
- **Grading**: No universally adopted climbing-specific tendon strain grading system found here; use general muscle-tendon strain grading cautiously. **[Evidence-backed: absence noted]**
- **Management principles**:
  - Relative rest from provoking grips; maintain pain-free ROM; gradual reload with protected grips; address contributing volume spikes. **[Bioplausible]**
  - Overlap with pulley protocols: many clinicians will still use **load management**, temporary taping/splinting if pulley also tender, and progressive return to crimping late. **[Bioplausible]**

#### Flappers (acute skin tears)
- **Acute management (trim, cleanse, moisturize, tape)**:
  - Trimming non-viable skin, protecting with dressing/tape, and moisture balance are standard wound-care-adjacent practices, but climbing-specific trials are absent. **[Bioplausible]** (general wound care logic; not climbing RCT).  
  - **Tape** primarily provides mechanical protection; no controlled trials in climbers found here. **[Folklore: climbing community consensus]**
- **Return to climbing**:
  - Typically depends on tear depth, pain, and ability to protect; no quantitative time-to-return data identified. **[Evidence-backed: absence noted]**
- **Prevention (filing calluses, skin routine)**:
  - Widely recommended by climbers/coaches; evidence is experiential rather than trial-based. **[Folklore: climbing coach + community practice]**

#### Chronic skin thinning (friction on small holds)
- **Interventions (skin products, humidity management)**:
  - No peer-reviewed controlled trials identified here that test “climbing skin products” or humidity manipulations on climbing performance/injury endpoints. **[Evidence-backed: absence noted]**
  - Most recommendations (antihydral use, moisturizers, sanding/filing strategies, session timing vs humidity) are practice-based. **[Folklore: climbing community + practitioner consensus]**

#### Collagen supplementation for skin healing (distinct from tendon claims)
- There **are** RCTs showing oral collagen peptides can improve **skin hydration/elasticity/wrinkle-related measures** in dermatologic/cosmetic contexts. **[Evidence-backed for those endpoints; indirect to climbing wounds]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24131075/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Relevance to climbing flappers/abrasions**: these studies do **not** directly test time-to-epithelialization, tensile strength of healed fingertip skin under shear, or return-to-climbing. Therefore, translation to “faster flapper recovery” remains speculative. **[Bioplausible]**

### Sub-section 4: General injury prevention principles

#### Warm-up efficacy for injury prevention
- For climbing finger injuries specifically, controlled prevention trials are scarce; mechanistically, warm-up may increase tissue temperature, improve viscosity/ROM, and modulate neuromuscular coordination. **[Bioplausible]**  
- **Evidence tier**: without climbing-specific RCTs showing reduced incidence, classify as **[Bioplausible]**, not Evidence-backed.

#### Load management principles (rate of progression; “10% rule”)
- The **10% rule** is widely repeated across sports but is not consistently supported as a universally valid threshold across contexts. For climbing finger tissues, it is best treated as a heuristic. **[Folklore: general S&C heuristic]**  
- A stronger, more defensible principle is **individualized load monitoring** (volume, intensity, grip type distribution, recovery) and avoiding abrupt spikes—conceptually supported across overuse injury frameworks but rarely reducible to one number. **[Bioplausible]**

#### Experience-dependent injury risk: Sjöman et al. (2023)
- **Your provided key findings (to include verbatim as facts with tiering):**  
  - In **≥6 yr** experience climbers, RFT negatively associated with SPIIF (**χ²=4.57, p=0.03**).  
  - In **<6 yr** climbers reaching **≥7a**, RFT positively associated with SPIIF (**χ²=4.61, p=0.03**).  
  **[Evidence-backed: observational association; not causal]** (DOI provided: 10.1016/j.wem.2023.06.004)
