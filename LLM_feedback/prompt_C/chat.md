# 2. Narrative Synthesis

# Sub-section 1: A2 Pulley Injuries

## Anatomy and mechanism

**[Evidence-backed]** The A2 pulley is the thickest annular pulley, spanning the proximal phalanx. It restrains FDP/FDS tendons against the phalanx and prevents bowstringing. In climbers, ring and middle fingers are commonly involved due to higher loading in 2-finger pockets/crimp positions. ([PMC][5])

**[Bioplausible]** Grip loading hierarchy is generally:

**full crimp > half crimp > open hand**

because PIP hyperflexion + DIP extension increase flexor tendon moment arm and tendon-pulley contact pressure. Biomechanical lab work consistently shows higher pulley stress in crimping, though exact Newton values vary by model/cadaver setup.

## Grading

Widely used climbing classification:

* **Grade I**: partial tear, no bowstringing
* **Grade II**: partial tear, mild bowstringing on ultrasound
* **Grade III**: complete tear, bowstringing
* **Grade IV**: complete tear + adjacent pulley/FDP/FDS/collateral involvement

**[Bioplausible]** This grading is clinically useful but not validated by prospective outcome trials.

## Diagnosis

**Clinical findings**

* focal pain over volar proximal phalanx
* pain with resisted PIP flexion / crimping
* swelling
* reduced trust in crimp grip
* audible “pop” sometimes reported in complete tears

**Imaging**

**[Evidence-backed]** Ultrasound is generally preferred first-line over MRI because it is dynamic, cheaper, fast, and can visualize bowstringing under resisted flexion. Review data report US sensitivity/specificity commonly **0.94–1.00** in available small studies. MRI sensitivity **0.79–1.00** depending protocol. ([PMC][5])

**[Evidence-backed]** Diagnostic thresholds remain inconsistent: complete A2 rupture tendon-bone distance cutoffs ranged **1.9–5.1 mm** across studies. ([ScienceDirect][4])

**Bottom line:** use **high-resolution dynamic ultrasound first**, MRI if equivocal or multi-structure injury suspected.

---

## Conservative management (Grade I–II, most isolated Grade III)

## Immobilization

**[Bioplausible]**

* Grade I: relative rest, no rigid immobilization or 0–7 d comfort splinting
* Grade II: 7–14 d protective splint / ring support
* Grade III isolated: 10–21 d protection, then guided motion/loading

Rationale: ligamentous tissue benefits from early controlled loading after acute inflammatory phase; prolonged immobilization risks stiffness and collagen disorganization.

No RCTs in climbers.

## Return-to-loading timeline

**[Bioplausible]**

* Pain-free ADLs first
* Begin low-load isometrics / no-hang finger loading around **1–3 wk** (Grade I), **2–4 wk** (Grade II), **3–6 wk** (Grade III) depending symptoms/imaging
* Return to hard crimping often **6–12+ wk**

## H-taping / ring splinting

**[Folklore: climbing physio/common practice; Schöffl-era recommendations]**

H-tape around proximal phalanx is ubiquitous. Proposed effects:

* reduce bowstringing moment arm
* improve confidence
* cue load reduction

**Evidence gap:** no controlled trials showing faster healing or lower reinjury rates.

## Load-managed rehabilitation

**[Evidence-backed for tendon analogues; Bioplausible for pulley]**

Tendinopathy literature supports progressive mechanical loading:

* heavy slow resistance / eccentrics improve tendon pain/function (Achilles, patellar)
* isometrics may reduce pain acutely (patellar tendon)

Pulley tissue is fibrocartilaginous ligament, not tendon; therefore transfer is indirect.

**Extrapolated rehab logic:**

1. pain-calming isometrics
2. submaximal progressive hangs
3. increasing intensity and specificity
4. reintroduce crimp/dynamic moves

## Surgical management (Grade III–IV)

**Indications**

**[Bioplausible / case-series based]**

* multiple pulley rupture
* marked bowstringing with dysfunction
* failed conservative care
* combined tendon injury
* elite athlete requiring maximal force restoration

**Outcomes**

**[Evidence level: case series only]** Most reports describe return to climbing/work with satisfactory motion; no RCTs, no robust comparative data.

**Return timeline:** commonly **3–6+ months**, severe reconstructions longer.

---

## Load-managed hangboard rehabilitation

**Tyler Nelson / climbing rehab protocols**

**[Folklore → Bioplausible]**

Common protocols use:

* 10 s isometric hangs
* 30–60% perceived max initially
* pain ≤2–3/10
* gradual weekly load increments
* edge depth progression before intensity spikes

Why only Bioplausible:

* sensible mechanotransduction rationale
* mirrors successful tendon loading paradigms
* zero controlled pulley trials

**Evidence base:** expert practice, anecdote, uncontrolled outcomes.

# Sub-section 2: Flexor Tendon Strains

## Anatomy / mechanism

FDP inserts distal phalanx; FDS splits to middle phalanx. Strains arise from sudden overload, pockets, dynamic catches, or excessive volume.

## Distinguishing from pulley injury

**Pulley**: localized proximal phalanx pain, bowstringing possible, grip-position specific.
**Tendon strain**: pain along tendon course, pain with active flexion, sometimes crepitus/swelling.

## Grading

* Grade I: overload/reactive pain, no structural defect
* Grade II: partial fiber injury
* Grade III: major tear/avulsion (urgent)

## Management

**[Bioplausible]**

* unload provoking grips
* early motion
* progressive flexor loading
* ultrasound if uncertain vs pulley tear
* overlap with pulley protocols

If FDP avulsion (“jersey finger”) suspected: urgent surgical referral.

# Sub-section 3: Skin Management

# Flappers (skin tears)

## Acute management

**[Folklore: climbing community / gym staff norms]**

* wash wound
* trim loose flap if nonviable
* occlusive dressing or tape
* moisturize surrounding skin after closure phase

No climbing-specific trials.

## Return to climbing

**[Folklore]**

* small tear: 1–3 d modified climbing
* moderate tear: 3–7 d
* larger raw dermis: 1–2 wk

Dependent on pain, infection risk, hold texture.

## Prevention

**[Folklore: common coaching practice]**

* file calluses flush
* moisturize nightly
* avoid over-dry chalk buildup
* manage session volume on sharp holds
* rotate grip styles

## Chronic skin thinning

**[Folklore]**

Products (balms/salves), humidity management, reduced alcohol sanitizers, session spacing are commonly recommended. No peer-reviewed controlled evidence specific to climbers.

## Collagen supplementation for skin healing

**[Bioplausible]**

Oral collagen peptides have some evidence for skin elasticity/hydration in cosmetic dermatology populations, but **no direct evidence for accelerated fingertip skin repair in climbers**. Therefore cannot claim performance-relevant healing benefit.

# Sub-section 4: General Injury Prevention Principles

## Warm-up

**[Bioplausible]**

No strong climbing RCTs. General sports evidence suggests warm-up improves tissue temperature, force readiness, coordination, and may reduce injury risk.

Practical climbing warm-up:

* 5–10 min aerobic movement
* easy traversing
* progressive finger recruitment
* submaximal hangs before limit bouldering

## Load management

## “10% rule”

**[Folklore]**

Popular heuristic; not strongly evidence-backed across sports. Too simplistic for climbing because intensity, grip type, board angle, session density matter more than raw volume alone.

## Better model

**[Bioplausible]**

Use monotony + spikes + symptom response:

* avoid sudden increase in max crimp attempts
* avoid simultaneous increases in board angle + volume + frequency
* monitor 24 h soreness response

## Experience-dependent injury risk

**[Evidence-backed observational]** Sjöman et al. (2023):

* In climbers with **≥6 y experience**, regular finger strength training associated with **lower** injury onset (χ²=4.57, p=0.03)
* In climbers with **<6 y experience** reaching **≥7a**, regular finger training associated with **higher** injury onset (χ²=4.61, p=0.03)

Interpretation: novices may outpace tissue capacity; experienced climbers may program loading better.

# 3. Open Questions

1. **Does progressive no-hang/hangboard rehab outperform rest-only management for Grade I–II A2 pulley injuries?** (RCT needed)

2. **What objective load thresholds (kg, %MVC, tendon-bone distance recovery) best predict safe return to crimping?**

3. **Does H-taping measurably reduce bowstringing, pain, reinjury, or time-to-return compared with sham taping?**

4. **Can ultrasound-guided staging predict prognosis better than symptom severity alone?**

5. **Do collagen peptides / nutrition interventions accelerate pulley or fingertip skin healing in climbers specifically?**

---

## Bottom-line synthesis

For climbing finger injuries, the strongest evidence currently supports **diagnostic ultrasound** and **general progressive loading principles extrapolated from tendon rehab**. Most pulley-specific rehabilitation timelines, taping methods, and hangboard protocols remain **Bioplausible** or **Folklore**, not evidence-backed. The field needs prospective controlled trials more than new anecdotes.
