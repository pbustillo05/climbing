**2. Narrative synthesis**

### Sub-section 1: A2 Pulley Injuries
**Anatomy and mechanism**: The A2 pulley (proximal phalanx, middle/ring finger predominance) constrains the flexor digitorum profundus/superficialis tendons. Full crimp grip generates the highest loads (biomechanical estimates exceed 100–200 N at the pulley in high-force crimps), followed by half crimp > open-hand; bowstringing occurs when pulley integrity fails. **[Bioplausible: biomechanical studies and climbing-specific force modeling; no climbing RCT].**

**Grading** (Schöffl system, widely adopted):  
- Grade I: strain, no dehiscence (<2 mm tendon–bone distance on US).  
- Grade II: partial tear or complete A4 rupture.  
- Grade III: complete single A2/A3 rupture.  
- Grade IV: multiple pulley ruptures or single + adjacent structure (lumbrical/collateral). **[Folklore: practitioner consensus, formalized in Schöffl 2003; no RCT validation].**

**Diagnosis**: Pain on resisted PIP flexion + clinical bowstringing (worse in crimp). Ultrasound (dynamic, resisted flexion at proximal phalanx midpoint) is the preferred modality (sensitivity/specificity 0.94–1.00); MRI is alternative but inferior for dynamic assessment and accessibility. **[Evidence-backed: Berrigan et al. 2022 systematic review (DOI: 10.1002/jum.15796)].**

**Conservative management (Grades I–II, most Grade III)**:  
- Immobilization: 10–14 days (Grade III) with palmar splint/thermoplastic ring or H-tape; shorter/none for Grade I–II. **[Bioplausible: case series extrapolation (Schöffl 2003); no RCT].**  
- Return-to-loading timeline: progressive functional therapy begins post-immobilization; easy climbing (tape-protected) after 6–8 weeks (Grade III), full sport after 3 months. **[Bioplausible: Schöffl case series].**  
- H-taping/ring splinting: reduces bowstringing 15–22% and may increase crimp MVC +13% in Grade I–III (older data); recent reviews show low-moderate certainty and cadaver studies find no stabilization/prophylaxis benefit. **[Bioplausible with conflicting data: Schöffl 2007 (DOI: 10.1123/jab.23.1.52) vs Larsson 2022 review and Salas 2023 cadaver (DOI: 10.1016/j.jhsa.2022.05.002)]; community practice remains common **[Folklore: climbing physio/coach consensus].**  
- Load-managed rehabilitation: Extrapolate tendinopathy literature—progressive loading (e.g., Alfredson heavy eccentric or Cook/Purdam continuum model, Rio heavy slow resistance) promotes tendon remodeling via mechanotransduction. No pulley-specific RCT; direct application untested. **[Evidence-backed for analogous tendons (Alfredson 1998 DOI: 10.1177/03635465980260030301; Cook & Purdam 2009 DOI: 10.1136/bjsm.2008.051193); Bioplausible for pulleys].**

**Surgical management (Grade III–IV)**: Indications = Grade IV or failed conservative (bowstringing + functional deficit). Outcomes from case series only (good functional return in most Grade IV but ongoing taping required). Return-to-climbing: 3–6+ months post-op with protected rehab. **[Bioplausible: Schöffl case series only; no RCT].**

**Load-managed hangboard rehabilitation during recovery**: Tyler Nelson protocols (density hangs, progressive isometrics) and similar physio regimens emphasize gradual load reintroduction once pain-free. No climbing-specific RCT or controlled data; based on tendon loading science + clinical experience. **[Folklore: climbing physio/coach heuristic (Nelson/Camp4); Bioplausible via tendinopathy extrapolation but evidence gap explicit].**

### Sub-section 2: Flexor Tendon Strains
Anatomy/mechanism overlap heavily with pulley injuries: repetitive crimp loading strains flexor digitorum tendons/tenosynovium (often co-occurs). Grading distinguishes isolated tendon strain (no pulley bowstringing) from pulley-dominant. Management principles mirror low-grade pulley conservative care: rest, anti-inflammatories, early progressive loading once acute phase subsides; no unique RCTs. **[Bioplausible: mechanistic overlap with pulley data; no climbing-specific evidence].**

### Sub-section 3: Skin Management
**Flappers (skin tears)**: Acute—trim loose skin, clean, moisturize, tape. Return-to-climbing typically days–1 week once sealed (no data). Prevention via filing calluses/skin routines. **[Folklore: climbing community/physio consensus; no peer-reviewed evidence].**

**Chronic skin thinning** (friction on small holds): Climbing-specific skin products or humidity control lack any controlled data. **[Folklore: community/practitioner claim].**

**Collagen supplementation for skin healing**: Multiple RCTs/meta-analyses show hydrolyzed collagen peptides (2.5–10 g/d, often + vitamin C, 8–12 weeks) improve skin hydration, elasticity, wrinkle reduction, and dermal collagen density vs placebo. No climbing-specific trials for flappers/chronic thinning or accelerated repair in friction trauma; tendon evidence is separate and mixed. **[Evidence-backed for general skin parameters (e.g., Wang 2021 review DOI: 10.3390/polym13213868; Campos 2023); Bioplausible for climbing skin injury with explicit gap].**

### Sub-section 4: General Injury Prevention Principles
Warm-up efficacy: No climbing-specific RCTs; general sports data support dynamic warm-ups for ROM/strength but injury prevention effect is modest/mixed. **[Bioplausible: mechanistic + non-climbing evidence].**

Load management principles (e.g., “10% rule” for weekly progression): No robust evidence; widely labeled a myth in training-load literature. **[Folklore: community heuristic; contradicted by systematic reviews (e.g., Impellizzeri 2020)].**

Experience-dependent injury risk: Only quantitative survey (Sjöman 2023, n=434) found regular fingerboard training protective in ≥6 yr experienced climbers but risk-increasing in novices reaching high grades. **[Evidence-backed: observational (DOI: 10.1016/j.wem.2023.06.004)].**

**3. Open questions**
1. What is the optimal immobilization duration and type (ring vs splint vs none) for Grade II–III A2 injuries, and does it alter long-term pulley thickness/strength (RCT needed)?  
2. Can hangboard progressive-loading thresholds (e.g., % bodyweight, hold depth, volume) be quantified with objective ultrasound/MRI healing markers in climbers (current protocols lack controlled validation)?  
3. Does H-taping provide meaningful clinical benefit (pain, return-to-sport time) beyond placebo in Grade I–III injuries, or should it be abandoned given recent biomechanical data?  
4. What collagen peptide dose/timing + vitamin C regimen, if any, accelerates skin barrier recovery or reduces chronic thinning specifically in high-volume climbers (climbing-specific RCT absent)?  
5. Can load-management algorithms incorporating experience level (per Sjöman) and real-time finger strength testing reduce pulley injury incidence in a prospective climbing cohort (no intervention trials exist)?
