# Prompt H — Skincare Science and Folklore for Rock Climbers

## Context and purpose

I am writing a scientific literature review on training for rock climbing. I need a section on skin management that critically evaluates both the scientific evidence and the rich folklore tradition surrounding climber skincare. The section must explicitly separate evidence tiers, call out where practitioner schools contradict each other, and flag TO_VERIFY claims without presenting any of them as settled guidance.

**Your task**: produce an exhaustive research and community-consensus synthesis on climber skincare. Use your training data AND active web searches across PubMed, Dermatology and Sports Medicine journals, Lattice Training, Hooper's Beta, r/climbharder, climbing community blogs, YouTube channels, and brand-specific content (Rhino Skin Solutions, Climb On, Black Diamond skin care, Joshua Tree). Be exhaustive. This topic is high-folklore-density — cite every community claim explicitly.

## Citation format

- Scientific papers: `[DOI | Authors | Title]`
  - Example: `[10.1111/j.1365-2133.2006.xxxxx | Author et al. Year | Skin friction and callus in grip sports]`
  - If DOI unverified: `[DOI unverified | Authors | Title | Journal, year]`
- Other resources: `[URL or source description]`
  - Example: `[https://rhinoskinsolutions.com/... | Rhino Skin Solutions blog]` or `[r/climbharder wiki — Skin Care thread, top-voted answer]`
- Every factual claim must cite at least one source. No uncited assertions.

## Evidence classification (apply to every claim)

- **Evidence-backed**: peer-reviewed data with adequate controls; cite studies, report effect size and 95% CI.
- **Bioplausible**: mechanistically plausible, no direct controlled intervention evidence; state mechanism and gap.
- **Folklore**: community consensus or practitioner claim; name source explicitly (channel, post, brand, coach).
  - Folklore requires ≥3 independent sources to be labelled **FOLKLORE_VERIFIED**; fewer sources → label **TO_VERIFY**.

Do not promote Folklore to Bioplausible without a stated mechanism. Flag TO_VERIFY claims as explicitly unresolved.

## Writing style

Dense, precise, information-complete. Numbers over words. Flag contested claims explicitly — especially where the dry-skin and moist-skin schools directly contradict each other, name both sides and their sources rather than picking a winner.

## Output is freeform — focus entirely on content

Plain prose and lists, structured by sub-topic. Maximum information density and citation coverage. No LaTeX formatting.

## Sub-topics to cover (be exhaustive within each)

### 1. Skin biology relevant to climbing

**Stratum corneum and callus formation:**
- How repeated friction loading induces hyperkeratosis
- What distinguishes a "good callus" (resilient, moderate depth) from an over-thickened callus prone to flappers
- Turnover rate of stratum corneum; implications for skin recovery timing
- Role of skin hydration in friction coefficient and tear resistance: any studies on friction biomechanics of dry vs. moist skin on rock surfaces

**Skin types and their implications:**
- Skin type variation (thin/dry vs. thick/oily) and its impact on training frequency constraints
- Any published data on skin type and injury rate or performance in climbers
- Genetic vs. environmental contributions to skin type

### 2. The dry-management school

**Core claims:**
- What is the central argument of the "keep skin dry" approach?
- Recommended practices: drying agents, filing protocols, chalk use, humidity management
- Specific products recommended: drying agents (Antihydral, Rhino Skin Dry), their active ingredients, mechanism of action
- Evidence or rationale: friction physics, skin hardening mechanics

**Sources:**
- Name every community, coach, or brand source that advocates this approach
- Any scientific evidence supporting dry-skin management for friction or durability

**Risks and failure modes:**
- Over-drying: fissuring, cracking, increased flapper risk
- Any data or case reports on Antihydral overuse

### 3. The moist-management school

**Core claims:**
- What is the central argument of the "maintain skin moisture" approach?
- Recommended practices: moisturisers, oil-based treatments, overnight creams
- Specific products recommended: Climb On, Joshua Tree, Burt's Bees, Neutrogena Norwegian Formula, coconut oil
- Evidence or rationale: elasticity, crack resistance, tissue repair

**Sources:**
- Name every community, coach, or brand source that advocates this approach
- Any scientific evidence supporting moisture management for skin durability or repair

**Risks and failure modes:**
- Over-moisturising: reduced friction, soft skin susceptible to flappers mid-session
- Timing issues: moisturising too close to climbing vs. overnight protocols

### 4. The reconciliation model

**Is there a synthesis?**
- Some practitioners propose: moisten for repair (overnight), dry before climbing (session-day). What is the evidence or reasoning behind this?
- Any practitioner or community source that has explicitly described a dual-phase approach?
- What are the open questions about whether dry vs. moist is genuinely context-dependent or simply a matter of individual skin type?

### 5. Chalk

**Types of chalk:**
- Loose chalk vs. block chalk vs. chalk balls vs. liquid chalk: friction performance differences, if any
- Magnesium carbonate: mechanism of action (moisture absorption, friction enhancement)
- Liquid chalk: alcohol as skin drying agent; any evidence on skin effects with repeated use
- Colophony (rosin) in some chalks: sensitisation risk, prevalence

**Evidence:**
- Any friction studies comparing chalk types on rock surfaces
- Any dermatological studies on chalk and skin health in climbers

**Community consensus:**
- r/climbharder and community consensus on chalk selection
- Guideline restrictions on chalk in certain areas (environmental impact): not a skincare question but note as context

### 6. Flappers: prevention, treatment, and return-to-training

**Prevention:**
- Filing protocols: when and how to file; tools used (sanding pads, emery boards)
- Any evidence that regular filing reduces flapper incidence vs. community consensus
- Role of edge condition (rough vs. polished holds) in flapper risk

**Acute management:**
- Field treatment of an active flapper: trim, protect, cover
- Taping for flappers: skin-to-skin contact methods vs. fabric coverage
- Products used: New-Skin, super glue (cyanoacrylate) — mechanism and any evidence on accelerated healing vs. standard wound care
- Return-to-climbing timing after a flapper: community benchmarks vs. any dermatological guidance

### 7. Fissures, splits, and chronic skin damage

**Mechanisms:**
- What causes skin splitting at finger pads vs. palmar skin vs. finger joints
- Role of cold weather, low humidity, chalk in fissure development

**Prevention and treatment:**
- Overnight repair protocols
- Products with evidence of efficacy for skin fissure in non-climbing populations (general dermatology literature)
- Super glue for fissures: evidence vs. folklore

### 8. Environmental factors

- Humidity: how ambient humidity affects skin friction and recovery; any quantitative data
- Temperature: cold vs. warm conditions and their effect on skin performance
- Rock type: granite vs. limestone vs. sandstone friction properties and their implications for skin wear rates
- Session timing: morning vs. evening skin condition differences; any evidence

### 9. Skin recovery and training frequency

**Quantitative guidance:**
- How long does skin fully recover after a high-abrasion session?
- Any studies on skin thickness or friction recovery timelines
- Community benchmarks: how many days off before skin is "fresh"? Multiple sources required

**Volume limits:**
- Is there a maximum useful training frequency from a skin perspective (independent of tendon/muscle constraints)?
- Any data on skin training adaptation (what is the steady-state callus level and how long does it take to reach)?
