# Prompt C — Finger Injury Management for Rock Climbers

## Context and purpose

I am writing a scientific literature review on supplemental training and injury management for rock climbing. I need you to draft content for a section on **finger injury management**, covering the most common injuries in rock climbing: A2 pulley injuries (the dominant injury type), flexor tendon strains, and skin injuries (flappers, chronic skin thinning).

**Important caveat**: No randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have been conducted specifically on climbing finger injury rehabilitation. You must classify all rehabilitation protocols by their actual evidence tier — most will be Bioplausible or Folklore, not Evidence-backed. Do not overstate the evidence.

## Evidence classification framework

- **Evidence-backed**: peer-reviewed intervention evidence with adequate controls. Must cite specific study (author, year, DOI). Report effect size and 95% CI. Note: for this topic, most evidence will come from analogous tendon/ligament injuries in other sports (e.g., Achilles tendinopathy, rotator cuff, patellar tendon), not climbing-specific RCTs.
- **Bioplausible**: mechanistically plausible from tendon biology or biomechanics, extrapolated from non-climbing RCTs, or supported by case series. State the mechanism and the gap.
- **Folklore**: climbing community consensus, physio/coach heuristic, practitioner claim. Name the source.

## Writing style

Dense, precise, information-complete. Numbers over words. Flag every evidence tier explicitly.

## What I need

### Sub-section 1: A2 Pulley Injuries

**Anatomy and mechanism**: Brief description of the A2 pulley location (ring and middle finger predominance), the grip positions that load it most (full crimp > half crimp > open hand), and biomechanical force estimates.

**Grading**: 
- Grade I: partial tear, no bowstringing
- Grade II: partial tear, mild bowstringing on ultrasound
- Grade III: complete tear, bowstringing
- Grade IV: complete tear + involvement of adjacent pulleys or FDP/FDS

**Diagnosis**: Physical exam findings (pain with resisted PIP flexion, bowstringing on ultrasound). Is ultrasound or MRI the preferred imaging modality? Cite any diagnostic accuracy studies if available.

**Conservative management** (for Grade I–II, most Grade III):
- Immobilization protocols: duration, position, evidence
- Return-to-loading timeline: when can progressive hangboard-based rehabilitation begin?
- H-taping / ring splinting: evidence for effectiveness vs. community practice
- Load-managed rehabilitation: what does the tendinopathy literature (e.g., Alfredson, Cook & Purdam, Rio et al.) say about progressive loading for tendon repair? Can this be extrapolated to pulley rehab?

**Surgical management** (Grade III–IV):
- Indications for surgery
- Evidence for outcomes (case series only — note this)
- Return-to-climbing timeline post-surgery

**Load-managed hangboard rehabilitation during recovery**:
- Tyler Nelson's and other physio protocols for progressive loading during pulley rehab — classify as Folklore or Bioplausible and explain why
- What load thresholds are proposed, and what is the evidence base?

### Sub-section 2: Flexor Tendon Strains

Brief coverage: anatomy, mechanism, grading (distinguish from pulley injury), management principles. Note overlap with pulley injury protocols.

### Sub-section 3: Skin Management

**Flappers (skin tears)**:
- Acute management: trim, moisturize, tape — any evidence vs. folklore?
- Return to climbing: how long? Any data?
- Prevention: filing calluses, skin care routine — classify as Folklore and source

**Chronic skin thinning** (from friction on small holds):
- Is there any evidence for interventions (climbing skin products, humidity management)?
- Classify as Folklore with sources

**Collagen supplementation for skin healing**: Does any peer-reviewed evidence support collagen peptides for skin-repair acceleration relevant to climbing? (Separate from the tendon evidence.)

### Sub-section 4: General Injury Prevention Principles

- Warm-up efficacy for injury prevention: any RCT evidence? (likely Bioplausible)
- Load management principles: rate of progression, the 10% rule — Evidence-backed or Folklore?
- Experience-dependent injury risk: cite Sjöman et al. (2023) — the only quantitative injury-risk survey in climbing (n=434 Finnish climbers). Key findings: RFT negatively associated with SPIIF in ≥6 yr experience climbers (χ²=4.57, p=0.03); positively associated in <6 yr climbers reaching ≥7a (χ²=4.61, p=0.03).
