# Prompt B — Supplementation for Rock Climbers (Tendons, Skin, Performance)

## Context and purpose

I am writing a scientific literature review on supplemental training for rock climbing, including a section on **nutritional supplementation** relevant to climbing performance, connective tissue health (tendons, pulleys, skin), and recovery. 

One specific focus: the user takes **15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides in a shake before climbing sessions, without vitamin C co-ingestion**. This protocol needs rigorous scientific analysis.

## Evidence classification framework

Every claim must be labelled with one of three tiers:

- **Evidence-backed**: peer-reviewed intervention evidence with adequate controls. Cite study (author, year, DOI). Report effect size and 95% CI.
- **Bioplausible**: mechanistically plausible, no direct controlled evidence. State the mechanism and the gap.
- **Folklore**: community/coach consensus without empirical support. Name the source.

Do not promote tiers without justification. Flag industry-funded studies explicitly.

## Writing style

Dense, precise, information-complete. Numbers over words. No filler. Flag contested claims explicitly.

## Supplements to analyze

For each supplement below, provide: (1) mechanism of action relevant to climbing, (2) best available RCT or systematic review evidence, (3) evidence tier classification, (4) recommended dose and timing if evidence supports it, (5) limitations and caveats.

### Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides (detailed analysis required)

**User protocol**: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides consumed pre-climbing session. **No vitamin C co-ingested.**

Analyze:

1. **Shaw et al. (2017)** — the primary RCT for collagen + vitamin C + exercise. Design: crossover RCT, n=8 healthy males, 15 g collagen + 48 mg vitamin C vs. placebo, 1 h before rope-skipping exercise. Outcome: collagen synthesis rate (serum amino-terminal propeptide of type I procollagen, P1NP) and functional tendon stiffness in vitro. Report: effect size, p-value, the surrogate vs. functional distinction, and limitations (n=8, male-only, healthy tendons only, short duration, no climbing-specific outcome).

2. **Vitamin C co-factor**: Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is essential for prolyl hydroxylase activity, the enzyme that hydroxylates proline residues in collagen precursors. Without adequate vitamin C, collagen triple-helix formation is impaired. The Shaw protocol used 48 mg vitamin C. **The user takes no vitamin C — this is a protocol gap.** Classify the benefit of 15 g collagen without vitamin C vs. with vitamin C, and state what "adequate" vitamin C intake means (dietary RDA vs. supplemental dose used in Shaw).

3. **Timing window**: The Shaw protocol required collagen intake 1 h before exercise to elevate circulating amino acids during exercise-stimulated blood flow. Analyze whether "before climbing" (user's protocol) matches this window, or whether there is variance in the evidence.

4. **Dose**: Is 15 g the evidence-supported dose? What does Lis et al. (2019) or other reviews say about dose-response?

5. **Claim: collagen helps skin healing** — is there any peer-reviewed evidence for collagen supplementation accelerating climbing-specific skin recovery (flappers, skin tears, chronic thinning)? If not, classify as Folklore and source it.

6. **Claim: collagen helps pulley/tendon healing during active climbing** — classify the evidence tier and state the mechanism (proline/glycine substrate supply for type I collagen synthesis in loaded tendons).
