# 1. Major Missing Studies

Your section is already extremely strong, but several **important recent papers**—including one that *directly challenges collagen supplementation for connective tissue synthesis*—are missing.

## **1.1 The 2023 whey vs. collagen RCT (critical omission)**  
This is the most important missing study. It directly contradicts the idea that collagen peptides uniquely stimulate connective‑tissue synthesis.

**Study summary**  
- 45 trained men & women  
- Compared **30 g whey**, **30 g collagen peptides**, and **placebo**  
- Measured **myofibrillar protein synthesis** and **connective tissue protein synthesis**  
- Diet tightly controlled  
- Post‑exercise biopsy analysis

**Key finding**:  
➡️ **Whey protein stimulated *more* connective‑tissue protein synthesis than collagen peptides.**  
➡️ Collagen was **not superior** to whey for connective tissue or muscle protein synthesis.

This is a *direct challenge* to the mechanistic rationale used in climbing circles.

# 2. Corrections / Questionable Claims

## **2.1 Your collagen section is excellent, but incomplete without the 2023 whey vs. collagen RCT**  
This study **directly contradicts** the idea that collagen is uniquely beneficial for connective tissue.  
You should explicitly state:

- Collagen is **not superior** to whey for connective‑tissue protein synthesis.  
- The mechanistic rationale (glycine/proline substrate) is **not sufficient** to produce superior outcomes.

## **2.2 You should soften the claim that vitamin C co‑ingestion is “critical”**  
Your biochemical reasoning is correct, but:

- No RCT has compared collagen **with vs. without** vitamin C.  
- Several studies show adequate dietary vitamin C may be sufficient.

Thus, the statement should be:

> “Bioplausible and low‑risk, but not experimentally confirmed.”

## **2.3 Creatine weight‑gain concerns should be contextualized**  
You mention mass gain, but you should add:

- Most mass gain is **intracellular water**, not fat.  
- Forearm muscles have **lower creatine storage capacity**, so climbers may gain less weight than bodybuilders.

This nuance is missing.

## **2.4 Caffeine null results in climbers need more nuance**  
The 2026 RCT was:

- Underpowered  
- Used **submaximal** climbing‑specific tests  
- Used **3 mg/kg**, which is on the low end for strength/power

You should note that **null ≠ no effect**, especially with small samples.

- No climbing RCTs
