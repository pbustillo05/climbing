### 1. Major Gaps in the Literature Review (Missing Supplements)
The most significant gap in your current draft is the omission of vasodilators and flow-modifying supplements. [cite_start]Forearm pump is a primary limiting factor in sport climbing[cite: 174], and the climbing community has heavily adopted supplements targeting this mechanism.

* **Dietary Nitrates (Beetroot Juice):** This is arguably the most researched climbing-specific supplement after beta-alanine and caffeine. Nitrates convert to nitric oxide, promoting vasodilation and reducing the oxygen cost of submaximal exercise. There are climbing-specific RCTs demonstrating improved time-to-exhaustion on the wall.
    * `[10.1123/ijspp.2017-0518 | Joubert et al. | Dietary Nitrate Supplementation Improves Climbing Performance in Advanced Sport Climbers]`
* **L-Citrulline / Citrulline Malate:** Highly prevalent in climbing community folklore and pre-workouts for mitigating pump. While evidence is strong in generalized resistance training, climbing-specific data is sparse, but its community prevalence warrants a `Folklore` or `To-Verify` box.
* **CBD (Cannabidiol):** Extremely popular in climbing culture for perceived benefits in pulley inflammation, systemic recovery, and pre-climb anxiety management. It entirely lacks climbing-specific clinical trials but is a dominant community practice.
* **BCAAs / EAAs:** Heavily marketed by climbing-specific nutrition brands (e.g., Gnarly Nutrition, PhysiVantage) for intra-session recovery. The literature generally shows no benefit over adequate total daily protein, making this a prime candidate for a myth-busting `Folklore` box.

### 2. Critique of Current Sections & Questionable Claims

**Collagen & Vitamin C**

A typical climber's warm-up lasts 30–45 minutes. If they ingest collagen 60 minutes before the *start* of the session, the peak amino-acid window occurs during low-intensity warm-ups (jugs, mobility), not during the high-strain, pulley-taxing crux limit boulders. You should note this practical misalignment.

**Creatine Monohydrate**
* [cite_start]**Outdated Absolute Claim:** You state that no climbing-specific RCT has measured creatine effects on performance[cite: 134]. While high-quality, large-n modern RCTs are rare, there is literature examining creatine in rock climbing and upper-body ergometry that you should review to soften this absolute claim.
    * `[10.1123/ijsnem.11.3.382 | Jones et al. | Effects of Creatine Supplementation on Performance in Rock Climbers]` (This study specifically addresses the strength-to-mass ratio penalty you hypothesized).
* **Practical Gap:** Creatine also causes intracellular water retention, which can alter skin biomechanics (hydration levels of the stratum corneum). [cite_start]Some climbers anecdotally report "softer" or more easily torn calluses when fully saturated, which ties into your section on climbing skin[cite: 106].

**Caffeine**
* [cite_start]**Outdated Absolute Claim:** You claim that Ruiz-López (2026) is the *only* published climbing-specific caffeine RCT[cite: 201]. There are actually several older studies testing caffeine on climbing performance metrics (e.g., wall climbing time to exhaustion, grip dynamometry in climbers).
    * `[10.1007/s00421-011-2184-7 | Astley et al. | The effects of caffeine on simulated rock-climbing performance and post-exercise recovery]`
* [cite_start]**Practical Gap (Tactile/Motor Control):** You rightfully mention anxiety and tremor[cite: 208]. Another missing factor is the loss of fine motor control and tactile feedback at higher doses (5-6 mg/kg), which is critical for friction-dependent grips (slopers) and precise footwork.

**Beta-Alanine**
* [cite_start]**Practical Gap (Paresthesia):** You mention paresthesia (tingling) as a side effect[cite: 184]. In rock climbing, sensory feedback from the fingertips dictates how hard a climber grips a hold. The community frequently reports that severe paresthesia disrupts this tactile feedback loop, leading to over-gripping and premature pump.
