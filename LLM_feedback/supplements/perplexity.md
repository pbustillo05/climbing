## Missing literature

### Creatine
You should add recent general evidence that strengthens the case for creatine in strength/power athletes, while still noting the lack of climbing-specific trials. A 2024 meta-analysis found creatine plus resistance training improved upper- and lower-body strength in adults under 50, with stronger apparent effects in males. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39519498/)
The section would also benefit from a short note on practical tradeoffs that matter in climbing: acute body-mass gain, water retention, and the fact that these may matter more in lead climbing and slabs than in bouldering. The current draft already mentions mass gain, but it should be elevated as the central caveat. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31012130/)

### Collagen
The strongest recent additions are not climbing-specific, but they matter for balance. A 2023 clinical bottom-line review concluded that exercise plus collagen can improve Achilles tendon properties and pain, but stiffness effects are inconsistent. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37758259/)
A 2023 RCT on specific bioactive collagen peptides found no enhancement of tendon remodeling in healthy young men during resistance training, which is an important counterweight to the more optimistic collagen narrative. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37436929/)
There is also newer work in 2025–2026 suggesting collagen may alter tendon/muscle stiffness in healthy young males, but that evidence is still not climbing-specific and does not yet answer pulley or skin questions directly. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40623147/)

### Caffeine
The draft now needs the 2026 climbing caffeine trial. It is the most directly relevant new paper and should be cited because it found no significant ergogenic effect at 3 mg/kg on climbing-relevant proxy tests, while increasing subjective alertness. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41599897/)
This does not kill caffeine as a climbing aid; it just means the current section should say that evidence for climbing-specific performance remains inconclusive, even though caffeine clearly helps some general strength/endurance outcomes. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41599897/)

### Beta-alanine
Your beta-alanine coverage is already fairly strong because the 2021 climbing RCT is directly relevant. The main gap is that the section should more clearly distinguish “improved campus/easy-traverse performance” from “proved route performance,” because the hard traverse was negative and no redpoint or lead-climbing endpoint was tested. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34069981/)
You may also want to mention that the observed effect is most compatible with short-to-moderate-duration high-intensity efforts, not all forms of climbing pump. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34069981/)

### Omega-3
The omega-3 section is probably adequate in its current skepticism, but it would benefit from a sharper practical note: this is a recovery/anti-inflammatory hypothesis, not a proven pulley-repair intervention. The available rotator-cuff trial remains a useful “closest analog” null-ish result.
