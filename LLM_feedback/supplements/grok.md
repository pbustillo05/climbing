**Critical Updates / Gaps from Recent Literature (2024–2026)**  
**1. Collagen Peptides – Stronger Structural Evidence but Still Low Certainty and No Climbing Data**  
The draft’s Buchalski et al. (2026) SR (8 RCTs, GRADE A structural) is now superseded by a larger, higher-impact meta-analysis:  
Bischof et al. (2024) pooled 19 RCTs (n=768) of long-term CP (mostly 15 g/day × ≥8 weeks) + training. Significant effects on:  
- Tendon morphology (CSA of patellar/Achilles tendons): SMD 0.67 (p=0.011; 4 studies, n=114).  
- Fat-free mass: SMD 0.48 (moderate certainty).  
- Maximal strength: SMD 0.19.  
- Muscle architecture: SMD 0.39.  
GRADE: moderate for body composition; **very low** for tendon morphology/mechanical properties (risk of bias, heterogeneity, indirectness, imprecision, possible publication bias). No effect on tendon stiffness/Young’s modulus. No climbing or finger-pulley data.

Two specific tendon RCTs (Jerger et al.) underpin the morphology signal and should be added to the table:  
- Jerger 2022: 5 g specific CP + 14 weeks high-load RT → significantly greater Achilles tendon CSA increase vs. placebo.  
- Jerger 2023: same 5 g dose → greater patellar tendon CSA adaptation.

Counterpoint meta (Kirmse et al. 2024, 13 studies): CP did **not** improve musculoskeletal performance outcomes (strength, power, recovery) in healthy adults during exercise (SMD ≈ 0.08 for strength parameters; 48/55 outcomes unaffected).

Another Kirmse 2024 RCT found CP during training **does not** further increase connective-tissue protein synthesis rates (measured via deuterated water tracer) beyond training alone.

**Implication for draft**: The bioplausible box (ESS 2) and “Collagen for Pulley and Tendon Remodeling” section can be upgraded slightly for large-tendon CSA data, but the very-low GRADE certainty and complete absence of finger-specific or climbing-functional data must be retained. The “single-source” footnotes on Nulty (2024) and Buchalski (2026) can be removed—they are now published and corroborated. The vitamin-C co-ingestion gap remains unclosed; no new factorial RCT exists.

**2. Creatine**  
No new climbing-specific RCTs. The ongoing trial (NCT06832423) on elite boulderers is still recruiting. Community self-experiments (Lattice Training, pro climbers) and older upper-body power data align with the draft’s “bioplausible + body-mass caveat.” The to-verify box on weight-gain penalty is accurate; anecdotal reports consistently note 0.5–2 kg intracellular water and potential detriment on small-hold performance, but no controlled climbing metric has quantified it. No change needed except perhaps noting the new trial in the gaps section.

**3. Beta-Alanine & Caffeine**  
Sas-Nowosielski (2021) and Ruiz-López (2026) remain the only climbing-specific RCTs; no newer trials found. Effect sizes and limitations are correctly reported. The “hard traverse: NS” caveat and null caffeine results at 3 mg/kg are still the state of the art.

**4. Omega-3 & Magnesium**  
- Omega-3: Recent metas (2024–2025) confirm anti-inflammatory/recovery benefits in general exercise but do not overturn Sandford (2018) null result for tendinopathy pain/function. No finger-pulley or climbing data. The to-verify box is appropriate.  
- Magnesium: Only surveys of supplement use among climbers and anecdotal claims (cramps, forearm pump, sleep). No new RCTs. Bioplausible box is fine.
