**Collagen (gelatin) supplements:** Recent trials suggest collagen + vitamin C can enhance tendon/muscle properties but evidence in climbers is indirect.  A 16‑wk RCT found 10 g daily collagen peptides (no vit C) increased calf muscle and Achilles tendon stiffness and explosive torque development in young men【7†L53-L61】.  A systematic review (n≈250) concluded that **high-dose collagen (15–30 g) plus ≥50 mg vitamin C** during heavy resistance training increases tendon cross-sectional area and stiffness (strong GRADE A evidence), though it doesn’t boost maximal strength【12†L179-L187】.  These support collagen’s “bioplausible” role in tendon remodeling under load, **especially if vitamin C is co-ingested**.  Current text should cite Miyamoto et al. (2025) and Buchalski et al. (2026) to reflect this; the latter explicitly recommends 15–30 g collagen + C to aid tendon adaptation【12†L179-L187】.  Climbers’ folklore (e.g. “collagen heals flappers faster”) remains unsupported by trials (no RCT on skin injuries).  In sum, collagen may help long-term tendon adaptation (particularly with C), but **claims of acute flapper-healing or rapid pulley recovery have no direct evidence**. 

**Creatine:** The draft correctly notes creatine’s proven strength/power effects but cautions weight gain.  Two new community-leaning sources highlight relevant context.  Lattice Training coaches note that climbers “should experiment” with creatine: it enables greater training gains and endurance (via PCr buffering and faster recovery)【65†L169-L173】【65†L237-L244】.  They stress it’s not magic – one must monitor body mass.  Indeed, creatine’s ~1–3 kg weight gain (mostly water) can slightly reduce strength-to-weight; this is real but often transient.  We should ensure the text still cautions that climbers may offset strength gains by added mass.  Evidence from general meta-analyses (not climbing‑specific) strongly supports creatine for high-intensity efforts, and emerging evidence suggests modest cognitive benefits in stress (not yet studied in climbers).  In short, **no climbing RCT yet** but ample indirect support exists.  Emphasize: effective for short, powerful efforts (e.g. bouldering, campus) but may not benefit tiny finger-endurance climbs; use moderate dosing (3–5 g/d) to saturation, and be aware of minor GI upset or weight effects. 

**Beta-Alanine:** The draft cites older meta-analyses.  Crucially, a 2021 climbing‑specific RCT found 4 weeks of β‑alanine (4 g/d) significantly improved performance on ~1‑min continuous efforts (campus board slaps and an “easy” bouldering traverse), but **not** on shorter climbs【44†L163-L170】.  This aligns with beta‑alanine’s buffering role: benefits appear when sustained anaerobic effort lasts ~60–240 s.  We should add Sas-Nowosielski et al. (2021) to acknowledge climbing‑direct evidence【44†L163-L170】.  As always, note the tingling (“paresthesia”) side-effect and recommend slow-release formulas or split doses.  For completeness, mention that climbers with very brief efforts (e.g. speed climbing or sub-30 s boulders) likely see no benefit. 

**Caffeine:** A new triple-blind RCT (Ruiz-López et al. 2026) in 13 intermediate/advanced climbers found that **3 mg/kg caffeine (60 min pre)** did *not* significantly improve pull-up 1RM, power, dead-hang time, grip strength, or RFD【54†L90-L99】.  Climbers felt more alert/energetic, but objective gains were negligible (≈+3% in pull-ups, p≈0.29)【54†L90-L99】.  This suggests caffeine’s ergogenic effects may be too small or task‑specific to matter for most climbing moves.  We should cite this to temper any strong claims.  (By contrast, general meta‑analyses do show small strength gains with 1–7 mg/kg【supplements text】, but these translate to **trivial improvements** in climbers.)  Note: higher doses (e.g. 5–6 mg/kg) were not tested; side effects like jitters and cramping risk grow at those levels.  In practice, caffeine can still help climbers stay alert/focused on long routes or early morning sessions, but **don’t expect big strength boosts on the wall**【54†L90-L99】. 

**Omega-3 (EPA/DHA):** The review text seems cautious, which is appropriate. We know omega-3s have anti-inflammatory effects, but evidence for climbing is very weak.  A few small RCTs (e.g. rotator cuff or patellar tendinopathy) hint at minor improvements in tendon healing or pain, but results are mixed and clinically marginal.  In climbing context, **no RCT has tested fish oil**.  We should emphasize that any tendon benefit is *unproven* and likely requires high doses (≥2 g combined EPA+DHA) for weeks; it’s no “quick fix” for injuries.  Potential value may lie in general recovery/heart health, but claims of direct climbing performance gains are premature.  Side effects (bleeding risk at very high dose) and alternatives (e.g. anti-inflam medications) could be mentioned if relevant. 

**Magnesium:** Again, evidence is limited.  It’s essential biochemically, and supplementation clearly fixes frank deficiencies (improving cramps or weakness).  However, in well‑fed athletes magnesium is rarely ergogenic.  We should note that most claims of magnesium for muscle cramps or sleep are not strongly supported in normal populations.  (An unusual 2023 RCT even found *slightly worse* cycling performance after short-term high-dose Mg【39†】, suggesting over-supplementation can be counterproductive.)  Bottom line: **do not routinely endorse high-dose Mg for climbing performance**.  Instead, recommend a balanced diet (greens, nuts, whole grains) and only supplement if a deficiency is diagnosed.  If we keep Mg in the section, stress that climbing-specific evidence is nil and benefit likely limited to correcting poor intake.
