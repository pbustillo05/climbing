## 1. Maximal finger strength (MFS)

### Measurement methods

- **Evidence-backed**: MFS is most commonly measured as peak isometric force on a fixed edge, summed across both hands, with a 5–6 s maximal effort. Stien et al. used a 12 mm hold, self-selected grip, best of two trials per hand, sum reported as MFS.
- **Evidence-backed**: Edge depth varies widely across studies (6–60 mm), producing ICCs from 0.294 to 1.0, median 0.86. A 2025 systematic review concludes MIFS assessments with fixed-depth edges of approximately 20–23 mm consistently demonstrate high reliability and should be prioritized.
- **Evidence-backed**: Published protocols use:
  - 12 mm (Stien et al., instrumented hangboard)
  - 20 mm bar for one-handed rung pulling (Tindeq validation)
  - 23 mm rung with 10 mm radius for MVC (large-sample normative study)
- **Evidence-backed**: Half-crimp is identified as a major performance predictor, explaining much of the difference between advanced and elite climbers. Open-hand/slope grips are also used, but direct comparative reliability data are heterogeneous — **Bioplausible** rationale: half-crimp recruits FDP/FDS in a mechanically advantageous position, but no RCT proves superiority for testing.
- **Evidence-backed**: Load-cell position affects reliability. Tindeq Progressor in free-hanging configuration showed excellent validity vs. force plate (ICC = 0.99 both positions), within-day ICC 0.90 (rung pull) and 0.98 (lock-off), between-day ICC 0.95 and 0.98. Clinical assessment of Progressor also reported ICC ≥0.999 vs. gold standard.
- **Evidence-backed**: Most finger-strength studies report only ICC; few report SEM or MDC, limiting practical interpretation.

### Interpretation

- **Evidence-backed**: In 307 climbers (185M,122F), finger strength emerged as dominant factor explaining 68% of variance in climbing ability in males and 64% in females. Finger hang test showed strongest relationship to ability (R≈0.75) but was primarily determined by finger strength (65% males, 80% females).
- **Evidence-backed**: Correlations between climbing-specific strength and ability range 0.42–0.92 across studies, higher than handheld dynamometry (0.53–0.75).
- **Folklore**: Community norms circulate without peer review:
  - Lattice Training Instagram reel states average V6 boulderer hangs ~135.6% bodyweight on 20mm edge for 7s (male 136%, female 130%)
  - r/climbharder threads commonly cite "bodyweight hang on 20mm for 10s" as minimum before weighted hangs, and "140-150% BW for V8, 160%+ for V10" — **Folklore**, no controlled data, source explicitly community consensus.

### Protocols

- **Evidence-backed**: Stien protocol — 30 min warm-up, 12 mm edge, 6 s MVC, best of 2 per hand, sum both hands.
- **Evidence-backed**: Tindeq documentation lists six modes: Live Data, Peak Load, RFD, Repeaters, Critical Force. Validation study used 3 maximal repetitions, 90 s rest, free-hanging 20 mm rung.
- **Evidence-backed**: Lattice testing (practitioner documentation, not peer-reviewed) uses 7 s max hang on 20 mm, half-crimp, added weight measured, reported as %BW — **Folklore** tier for protocol details, but widely adopted.

## 2. Critical Force (CF) and W'

### Theoretical framework

- **Evidence-backed**: CF extends critical power to isometric finger flexors, defined as highest sustainable force separating steady/non-steady metabolic states. W' represents work capacity above CF (units: N·s or kg·s).
- **Evidence-backed**: Two estimation methods dominate: multi-trial time-to-exhaustion regression, and 4-min all-out test (7:3 work:relief).

### Measurement protocols

- **Evidence-backed**: Devise validation (n=12): CF calculated as mean force of last three contractions = 20.1±5.7 kg; end-force (lowest value) = 16.8±5.2 kg.
- **Evidence-backed**: Verification trials at CF: mean time to failure 440±140 s, only 1/12 completed 720 s; lowering load by 2,4,6 kg increased completion to 38%,69%,92%.
- **Evidence-backed**: Authors conclude CF based on mean of last three contractions overestimates sustainable intensity; end-force shows stronger link.
- **Folklore**: Instagram summary of same study notes "only one out of twelve participants could sustain their calculated CF for the full twelve" — matches peer data.
- **Bioplausible**: Tindeq app implements 4-min all-out CF test using 7:3 protocol; no peer-reviewed validation of Tindeq-derived CF published as of 2026.

### Interpretation

- **Evidence-backed**: CF and MFS describe different ceilings: MFS = peak force, CF ≈ 60-75% MFS in trained climbers (inferred from 20.1 kg CF vs typical MFS ~30-35 kg in study population — **Bioplausible** calculation, not directly reported).
- **Evidence-backed**: No significant differences found between male and female climbers in finger strength/endurance when normalized to body mass.
- **Folklore**: Coaching blogs propose CF:MFS ratio targets 60-70% for route climbers, >75% for endurance specialists — source: Lattice, Power Company podcasts; no RCT data.

### Training implications

- **Evidence-backed**: Stien et al. showed F80 (80% MFS intermittent) improved stamina and endurance but not MFS alone; F100 improved MFS. This supports intensity-specific adaptations near CF.
- **Bioplausible**: Training at >CF should deplete W' and stimulate aerobic adaptations; direct intervention data in climbers lacking.

## 3. Peak force asymmetry

- **Evidence-backed**: Bilateral measurements included in 8 of 15 reliability studies, but 5 used non-simultaneous protocols, limiting ecological validity.
- **Bioplausible**: Left-right force differences >10-15% are commonly flagged by clinicians as meaningful; no climbing-specific injury-risk threshold published — gap noted in systematic review's call for harmonized protocols.
- **Folklore**: Tindeq users report testing single-arm hangs to track asymmetry; community heuristic: <5% difference = balanced, >10% = address with unilateral training — source: r/climbharder posts, no peer data.

## 4. Rate of Force Development (RFD)

- **Evidence-backed**: RFD defined as rate of rise in force during isometric contractions.
- **Evidence-backed**: In 57 male climbers, elite showed higher maximal force (ES=1.78 vs advanced, p<0.001) and higher RFD at all relative time periods (ES=1.02–1.58, p<0.001–0.002); absolute periods (50,100,150 ms) also discriminated elite vs others (ES=0.72–0.84).
- **Evidence-backed**: Authors suggest using relative rather than absolute time periods for RFD analysis in climbers.
- **Bioplausible**: RFD matters for dynamic moves because force must be generated in <200 ms contact times — mechanistic rationale, no direct intervention proving RFD training improves redpoint grade.
- **Evidence-backed**: Tindeq Progressor includes RFD mode, but reliability data for RFD specifically in finger flexors not reported in validation study (only peak force ICCs reported).

## 5. Endurance metrics

### Slope of Decline / SoD

- **Evidence-backed**: Stien defined "stamina" as capacity to maintain 80% MFS during 24×(10s on/6s off) on 12 mm; "endurance" as % force maintained after fatigue onset.
- **Bioplausible**: SoD can be extracted from load-cell data as linear regression of peak force vs repetition number; no standardized MDC published.

### Blood lactate

- **Evidence-backed**: No climbing-specific lactate threshold protocol validated against performance in searched literature; systemic variables (MLSS, RCP) have not proved valid for climbing-specific conditions.

### NIRS / SmO2

- **Evidence-backed**: Muscle-oxygen dynamics showed high variability and could not clearly distinguish exhaustive vs non-exhaustive trials at CF intensities. Authors advise caution interpreting forearm SmO2.

## 6. Body composition and anthropometrics

- **Evidence-backed**: Large-sample study found no sex differences in finger strength/endurance when normalized to body mass, implying relative strength matters more than absolute mass.
- **Bioplausible**: Ape index and finger length hypothesized to affect leverage; no high-quality predictive models identified in searched sources — evidence gap.

---

## 7. Reliability, validity, MDC — cross-cutting

- **Evidence-backed**: Across 15 studies, ICCs for MIFS ranged 0.294–1.0, median 0.86; 12 studies showed very high ICC (0.85–0.99).
- **Evidence-backed**: Tindeq vs force plate: ICC=0.99, SEM/CV low.
- **Evidence-backed**: Most studies fail to report SEM/MDC, limiting practical use.
- **Bioplausible**: Sources of variability: time of day, skin moisture, warm-up (30 min used in Stien), edge radius, grip self-selection — standardization recommended but not quantified.

## 8. Testing protocols and practical implementation

### Recommended battery (evidence-informed)

- **Evidence-backed**: Combine:
  1. MFS on 20–23 mm edge, half-crimp, 5–7 s, best of 2–3 trials, report %BW (high reliability)
  2. Intermittent endurance at 60% MFS, 8:2 work:relief (validated metabolic demand)
  3. Optional 4-min all-out for CF estimation, but interpret end-force not mean

### Field vs lab

- **Evidence-backed**: Tindeq + phone achieves ICC >0.90 for peak force, sufficient for longitudinal tracking. Lab-grade force plates offer <1 N accuracy.
- **Bioplausible**: RFD and SmO2 require high sampling rates and careful processing; self-coaching risks misinterpretation.

### Community folklore

- **Folklore**: "Test every 6 weeks" cadence originates from Lattice Training periodization blogs and Tyler Nelson podcasts; no RCT establishing optimal retest interval.
- **Folklore**: r/climbharder consensus ranks MFS > endurance > RFD for boulderers, opposite for route climbers — source: recurring threads 2023-2025, no empirical validation.
- **Folklore**: Lattice athlete scoring and "finger strength benchmarks" tables circulate widely; based on internal client data, not peer-reviewed.

## Open questions (literature cannot yet answer)

1. What is the minimal detectable change for MFS on 20 mm half-crimp using field devices, with 95% CI?
2. Does CF determined as end-force predict 20-minute sustainable climbing intensity better than 60% MFS?
3. What RFD time window (relative % vs absolute ms) best predicts dynamic move success on rock?
4. Is left-right asymmetry >10% causally linked to pulley injury incidence in a prospective cohort?
5. Can NIRS-derived SmO2 recovery kinetics guide inter-set rest prescription better than RPE?
6. What are sex-specific normative MFS values by IRCRA grade using standardized 23 mm protocol?
7. Does training specifically targeting CF:MFS ratio improve redpoint performance vs traditional hangboard training?
