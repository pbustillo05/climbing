## 2. Narrative Synthesis

### 2.1 Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides

#### 2.1.1 Mechanism of Action

Type I collagen (the dominant structural protein in tendons, pulleys, ligaments, and skin dermis) is synthesized from procollagen precursors requiring two hydroxylation steps catalyzed by **prolyl hydroxylase** and **lysyl hydroxylase** — both ascorbic acid (vitamin C)–dependent dioxygenases. Hydroxyproline stabilizes the collagen triple helix via inter-chain hydrogen bonding; without adequate hydroxylation, secreted collagen is thermally unstable and subject to intracellular degradation. **[Evidence-backed]**

Hydrolyzed collagen peptides, particularly those rich in Pro-Hyp and Hyp-Gly dipeptides, are absorbed intact from the gut lumen and accumulate in connective tissues, where they may act as substrates for de novo collagen synthesis and/or as signaling molecules upregulating tenocyte collagen gene expression. **[Bioplausible: mechanism established in vitro (Shigemura et al., 2009, *J Agric Food Chem*); direct in vivo mechanistic confirmation in human tendon tissue is lacking]**

Intermittent mechanical loading of tendons and ligaments during exercise increases local blood flow and tenocyte metabolic activity, creating a narrow anabolic window during which circulating amino acid precursors are preferentially incorporated. The Shaw (2017) protocol exploited this by timing collagen ingestion 1 h pre-exercise. **[Bioplausible: based on Shaw RCT data; mechanism is inferred from P1NP kinetics, not directly measured tissue incorporation]**

---

#### 2.1.2 Shaw et al. (2017) — Critical Appraisal

**Design**: Randomized, double-blind, crossover RCT. n=8 healthy males (age 21±0.5 yr; no tendon pathology). Three conditions: 5 g gelatin+vit C, 15 g gelatin+vit C, placebo. Vitamin C dose: 48 mg. Acute protocol: single dose 1 h before 6 min rope-skipping. Washout: 7 days.

**Primary outcomes**:
- Serum P1NP (amino-terminal propeptide of type I procollagen): surrogate biomarker of collagen synthesis rate
- Mechanical properties of in vitro–engineered ligament constructs exposed to participant serum

**Results**: 15 g condition produced approximately 2× greater P1NP AUC vs. placebo over 4 h post-exercise. Serum from 15 g condition significantly increased engineered ligament stiffness and collagen content in vitro vs. placebo serum. 5 g condition intermediate.

**Effect size**: The paper reports significance (p<0.05) for 15 g vs. placebo; formal Cohen's d or 95% CI were not reported in the primary publication. The 2× P1NP increase is a within-person estimate from n=8; precision is low.

**Critical limitations**:

1. **n=8, male-only**: underpowered; effect estimates are imprecise; female hormonal influences on collagen synthesis (estrogen upregulates collagen turnover) unaddressed
2. **Surrogate endpoint**: P1NP is a systemic circulating marker of whole-body collagen synthesis. It does not confirm preferential incorporation into tendon/pulley tissue specifically. No tendon biopsy data exist
3. **Healthy tendons only**: the study population had no tendon pathology. Extrapolation to injured pulleys (A2/A4 annular pulleys, the most common climbing injury) requires a biological leap, since tendinopathic tissue has altered tenocyte phenotype and matrix remodeling dynamics
4. **Acute single-dose design**: no chronic dosing or functional tendon outcome (MRI, ultrasound, load-to-failure) was assessed
5. **Gelatin ≠ hydrolyzed collagen peptides**: gelatin is thermally denatured collagen with higher molecular weight fragments; hydrolyzed collagen peptides are enzymatically processed to lower MW (typically 2–5 kDa), which may alter bioavailability and peptide profile. Lis & Baar (2019) suggest functional equivalence when vitamin C–enriched, but direct head-to-head dose-matching is limited
6. **No climbing-specific outcome**: rope-skipping is a substitute for intermittent loading; climbing involves high-magnitude isometric pulley loading distinct from jump-rope mechanics

**Evidence tier**: **[Evidence-backed]** for acute elevation of systemic P1NP with 15 g collagen + vitamin C pre-exercise in healthy males. **[Bioplausible]** for extrapolation to actual tendon/pulley structural benefit in climbers.

---

#### 2.1.3 Vitamin C Co-factor: The Protocol Gap

**Biochemistry**: Prolyl hydroxylase requires molecular oxygen, α-ketoglutarate, and **ascorbic acid as electron donor**. In the hydroxylation reaction, ascorbic acid is oxidized and must be regenerated. Vitamin C depletion stalls the hydroxylation cycle; under scurvy conditions, collagen triple-helix formation fails entirely. At suboptimal (non-scorbutic) levels, hydroxylation efficiency is reduced in proportion to ascorbate availability. **[Evidence-backed: established biochemistry; Myllyharju, 2003, *Matrix Biol*]**

**Shaw protocol dose**: 48 mg vitamin C was administered co-ingested with gelatin. Note: the adult US RDA for vitamin C is 75–90 mg/day; 48 mg is below RDA but above the level at which prolyl hydroxylase is substantially impaired in non-scorbutic individuals. The rationale for 48 mg in Shaw (2017) is not explicitly justified in the paper — it was likely chosen as a sub-RDA supplemental dose to ensure local tissue availability without pharmacological confounding.

**The user's protocol gap**: The user takes 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides with **no vitamin C co-ingestion**. This introduces two scenarios:

| Scenario | Vitamin C Status | Expected Collagen Synthesis Response |
|---|---|---|
| Adequate habitual diet (≥5 servings fruit/veg; plasma ascorbate ≥50 µmol/L) | Baseline circulating ascorbate likely sufficient | Response may approximate Shaw protocol; co-ingestion provides marginal additional ascorbate | 
| Suboptimal dietary vitamin C (common in high-training athletes with poor diet quality; plasma ascorbate <23 µmol/L) | Prolyl hydroxylase activity reduced | Substrate supply (Pro, Gly, Hyp) provided, but hydroxylation step rate-limiting; collagen synthesis efficiency impaired |

**Classification of the gap**: Adding 48–150 mg vitamin C to the shake is a **[Bioplausible]** improvement with mechanistic justification and supporting evidence from Shaw/Lis studies. Whether it is strictly necessary depends on habitual dietary ascorbate status, which the user has not measured. At minimum, co-ingestion eliminates the risk of this bottleneck.

**Practical threshold**: Plasma ascorbate ≥50 µmol/L is considered replete; this is typically maintained by dietary intakes ≥100 mg/day. Co-ingesting 50–100 mg vitamin C with the collagen shake costs <$0.01/day and eliminates the bottleneck. **Absence of co-ingestion is a correctable protocol gap with no downside.**

#### 2.1.4 Timing Window

The Shaw protocol administered collagen 1 h before exercise to synchronize peak circulating amino acid elevation with exercise-induced tendon blood flow and tenocyte metabolic activation. Serum P1NP peaks approximately 1–2 h post-ingestion of hydrolyzed collagen; exercise window overlap is mechanistically important. **[Bioplausible: from Shaw design rationale; direct evidence that the 1-h window is superior to, e.g., 45 min or 90 min, does not exist]**

The user's "before climbing session" timing is **consistent with Shaw protocol intent** provided intake is 45–75 min pre-session start. Variance in gastric emptying rate (affected by co-ingested food, hydration, anxiety) could shift peak amino acid availability by 15–30 min, which is unlikely to be clinically meaningful given the broad P1NP elevation window (~2–4 h post-ingestion).

**Evidence tier for timing**: **[Bioplausible]**; 1 h pre-exercise is a reasonable approximation of the Shaw protocol window.

---

#### 2.1.5 Dose

15 g is the higher of the two active doses tested in Shaw (2017); the dose-response comparison (5 g vs. 15 g) showed greater P1NP elevation at 15 g. Lis & Baar (2019) replicated this at 15 g with hydrolyzed collagen derivatives. No RCT has tested doses >15 g; no dose-response curve above 15 g exists in the literature. The Praet et al. (2019) trial used 5 g/day (TENDOFORTE brand) in a chronic tendinopathy context, demonstrating clinical efficacy — suggesting 5 g may be sufficient for chronic loading protocols, while acute pre-exercise priming may benefit from the higher 15 g dose. **[Evidence-backed: 15 g is the best-supported acute pre-exercise dose; no evidence that higher doses provide additional benefit]**

#### 2.1.6 Claim: Collagen Supplementation for Climbing-Specific Skin Recovery

**Claim**: Hydrolyzed collagen peptides accelerate healing of flappers (partial-thickness skin avulsions), skin tears, and chronic fingertip skin thinning in climbers.

**Evidence search**: No peer-reviewed RCT or controlled trial examines collagen supplementation specifically for climbing-related skin injuries. The dermatological literature contains evidence that oral collagen peptides modestly improve skin hydration, elasticity, and dermis thickness in cosmetic aging populations (Proksch et al., 2014, *Skin Pharmacol Physiol*; Asserin et al., 2015, *J Cosmet Dermatol*); however, these outcomes (cosmetic aging) differ mechanistically from acute traumatic skin loss and callus reformation.

**Evidence tier**: **[Folklore: source = climbing community, coaches, and supplement marketing; no climbing-specific or wound-healing–specific peer-reviewed evidence for oral collagen peptides in skin tear recovery. Dermal collagen improvement in aging studies does not validate acute wound healing acceleration.]**

The bioplausible mechanism (collagen provision → dermal fibroblast substrate) exists but lacks clinical validation in acute skin injury contexts.

#### 2.1.7 Claim: Collagen for Pulley and Tendon Repair During Active Climbing

**Claim**: 15 g collagen peptides pre-session supports A2/A4 pulley and finger flexor tendon remodeling under active loading.

**Mechanism** (Bioplausible): Tendons and pulleys are ~70% type I collagen by dry weight. During loading, tenocytes upregulate TGF-β1 and IGF-1 signaling, increasing collagen turnover. Net collagen synthesis exceeds degradation in healthy adaptation; in injured or overloaded tissue, the balance may favor degradation. Provision of proline, glycine, and hydroxyproline-containing peptides (the primary amino acid profile of hydrolyzed collagen) may support anabolic capacity during this window. **[Bioplausible: mechanism plausible; no RCT measures direct pulley or flexor tendon collagen content change in climbers receiving oral collagen supplementation]**

The Praet et al. (2019) data (Achilles tendinopathy + eccentric exercise + 5 g collagen peptides) provide the closest clinical analog: significant functional improvement vs. exercise-only, though the industry funding (GELITA AG) and small n limit confidence. The loading pattern and tissue (Achilles vs. finger pulleys) differ substantially.

**Evidence tier**: **[Bioplausible]** for collagen peptides supporting tendon/pulley connective tissue anabolism under climbing-specific loading. **Not yet Evidence-backed** due to absence of climbing-specific RCTs, no human pulley biopsy data, and lack of functional outcomes (load-to-failure, ultrasound tissue thickness, return-to-climbing time).

### 2.2 Creatine Monohydrate

**Mechanism**: Phosphocreatine resynthesis sustains ATP availability during high-intensity short-duration contractions (≤30 s). Relevant to climbing: explosive boulder moves, contact strength, and power output on campus boards. Secondary: creatine may enhance muscle hypertrophy via cell volumization and mTOR signaling, relevant to antagonist muscle development. **[Evidence-backed: well-replicated mechanism]**

**Best evidence**: Lanhers et al. (2017) systematic review found significant improvements in upper-limb strength with creatine supplementation across 22 RCTs; however, the population was predominantly older or clinical. Rawson & Volek (2003, *J Strength Cond Res*) meta-analysis: creatine improves maximal strength by ~5–15% and power by ~5–26% in young trained males (ES variable; 95% CI not pooled uniformly). **[Evidence-backed for neuromuscular power in resistance-trained athletes]**

**Climbing-specific data**: None identified. No RCT measures creatine effect on boulder problem performance, maximum hang time, or campus board power in trained climbers.

**Relevance to climbing**: High for short contact-strength bouldering; moderate for power endurance on routes; low for aerobic/oxidative endurance performance (sport climbing >3 min duration).

**Dose**: Loading: 20 g/day ÷ 4 doses × 5–7 days; maintenance: 3–5 g/day. No timing effect confirmed for maintenance. Creatine monohydrate is the only evidence-supported form; effervescent/ethyl ester/buffered forms show no superiority. **[Evidence-backed: dose]**

**Caveats**: ~5% non-responders (high baseline muscle creatine). Body mass increase (0.5–2 kg water retention) potentially disadvantageous in weight-dependent climbing disciplines.

### 2.3 Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)

**Mechanism**: EPA and DHA compete with arachidonic acid for COX-2 and LOX enzymatic pathways, shifting prostaglandin and leukotriene profiles toward less pro-inflammatory eicosanoids. Relevant to climbing: modulation of tendon/ligament inflammation in overuse pathology; reduction of training-induced DOMS; potentially reduced synovial inflammation in finger joints. **[Evidence-backed: biochemical mechanism; Calder, 2017, *Eur J Clin Nutr*]**

**Tendon-specific RCT evidence**: Limited. Smith et al. (2011) showed reduced CRP and DOMS pain with 3 g/day EPA+DHA in recreational runners; this is not a tendinopathy trial. Specifically for tendinopathy, Mavrogenis et al. and others have examined dietary pattern effects but without isolated EPA/DHA RCTs of adequate power. No climbing-specific RCT exists. **[Evidence-backed for systemic anti-inflammatory effect; Bioplausible for tendinopathy/pulley injury application specifically]**

**Dose**: 2–3 g/day combined EPA+DHA is the dose used in most anti-inflammatory trials. AHA recommends ≥1 g/day for cardiovascular benefit. No dose-response curve for tendon outcomes. Timing irrelevant (daily supplementation, not acute). **[Bioplausible: dose for tendon/pulley outcomes]**

**Caveats**: High-dose EPA+DHA (>3 g/day) has antiplatelet effect; anticoagulant interaction. Fish oil peroxidation if stored improperly. Algal sources available for vegetarians.

### 2.4 Magnesium

**Mechanism**: Mg²⁺ is cofactor for >300 enzymatic reactions including ATP synthesis (Mg-ATP complex), muscle relaxation (antagonizes Ca²⁺ at sarcoplasmic reticulum), and sleep architecture (GABA-receptor modulation). Relevant to climbing: forearm muscle relaxation between moves; sleep quality for recovery; potentially cramp prevention (though evidence for this specific application is weak). **[Evidence-backed: physiological role; Volpe, 2013, *J Am Coll Nutr*]**

**RCT evidence for performance**: Wienecke & Nolden (2016, *MMW Fortschr Med*) showed improved recovery and reduced creatine kinase elevation with Mg supplementation in athletes, but this is a single small trial. A 2017 systematic review found modest evidence for magnesium improving sleep quality specifically in older adults (Abbasi et al., *J Res Med Sci*). **[Bioplausible for climbing performance benefit in athletes without documented deficiency]**

**Relevance**: Athletes in caloric restriction, heavy sweating, and high training loads are at elevated risk of suboptimal magnesium status (not frank deficiency). Climbers maintaining body weight through dietary restriction represent a risk group. Serum magnesium is an insensitive biomarker; erythrocyte or ionized magnesium testing more informative.

**Dose**: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium/day (as glycinate, malate, or citrate — superior bioavailability vs. oxide). Evening dosing supports sleep benefit. **[Bioplausible: dose and timing for climbing-specific outcomes]**

**Caveats**: Diarrhea at high doses (>400 mg oxide); glycinate/malate forms better tolerated.

### 2.5 Beta-Alanine

**Mechanism**: Beta-alanine is rate-limiting precursor for carnosine synthesis in skeletal muscle. Carnosine (β-alanyl-L-histidine) acts as intramuscular pH buffer (pKa ~6.83), attenuating H⁺ accumulation during high-intensity glycolytic work. Relevant to climbing: forearm pump — the burning sensation associated with intramuscular acidosis from sustained gripping during endurance/power-endurance routes — is mechanistically identical to the acidosis carnosine buffers. **[Bioplausible for climbing; mechanism Evidence-backed in general athletic contexts]**

**RCT evidence**: Hobson et al. (2012, *Amino Acids*, meta-analysis, k=15 RCTs): beta-alanine significantly increased exercise capacity for efforts lasting 60–240 s (ES = 0.374, 95% CI: 0.140–0.607). This duration range corresponds precisely to sustained pump sections on sport climbing routes. No climbing-specific RCT exists. **[Evidence-backed for 60–240 s high-intensity exercise; Bioplausible for climbing pump reduction]**

**Dose**: 3.2–6.4 g/day in divided doses (0.8–1.6 g per dose, every 3–4 h) to minimize paresthesia. Carnosine loading takes 4–12 weeks at therapeutic doses. **[Evidence-backed: dose]**

**Caveats**: Paresthesia (harmless tingling, primarily face/scalp/hands) is near-universal at single doses >800 mg; divided dosing or slow-release formulations mitigate this. The performance benefit is most pronounced in efforts of 60–240 s — longer routes with lower intensity may see less benefit. No benefit for maximal strength or power.

### 2.6 Caffeine

**Mechanism**: Non-selective adenosine receptor antagonist (A1, A2A). Reduces perceived exertion, enhances neuromuscular activation, increases pain tolerance, and potentiates motor unit recruitment. Relevant to climbing: sustained grip force maintenance, delayed onset of perceived forearm pump, acute power output. **[Evidence-backed]**

**Best evidence**: Grgic et al. (2020, *BJSM*) meta-analysis (k=34 studies): significant effect on muscle strength (ES = 0.20, 95% CI: 0.13–0.26) and muscular endurance (ES = 0.41, 95% CI: 0.24–0.58). Isometric grip strength specifically: Lopes-Silva et al. and Bazzucchi et al. (2011, *Muscle Nerve*) demonstrated caffeine (6 mg/kg) significantly increased maximal voluntary isometric contraction and reduced fatigue index in forearm flexors — directly analogous to climbing grip demands. **[Evidence-backed]**

**Climbing-specific data**: No RCT has measured caffeine effect on climbing-specific performance (redpoint rate, hangboard time-to-failure, route completion). Extrapolation from grip strength and endurance data is mechanistically justified but not empirically confirmed in climbing populations.

**Dose**: 3–6 mg/kg body mass, 45–60 min pre-session. Lower doses (3 mg/kg) sufficient for endurance/perceived exertion benefit with reduced side effects; higher doses (6 mg/kg) for maximal strength/power with increased anxiety, heart rate, and sleep disruption risk. **[Evidence-backed]**

**Caveats**: Habitual consumers show tolerance (attenuated effect); 48–96 h abstinence before target sessions may restore response. Caffeine disrupts sleep architecture at doses >3 mg/kg when consumed within 6 h of sleep — important for recovery in high-training-load periods. Interaction with anxiety: climbing involves fear stimulus; caffeine may exacerbate anxiety-driven performance decrements on serious routes.

### 3.2 What Is Missing from the Protocol

**Critical gap**: Vitamin C co-ingestion. Fix: add 50–150 mg ascorbic acid to the shake. Cost: negligible. Risk: none at this dose. Justification: mechanistic necessity for prolyl hydroxylase activity; supported by Shaw (2017) and Lis & Baar (2019). **This is the single highest-priority protocol modification.**

**Secondary gap**: Habituation to chronic collagen protein supplementation is untested. Current evidence is derived from acute single-dose designs; optimal chronic supplementation frequency (daily vs. pre-session only) is unknown. Given that connective tissue collagen turnover is slow (half-life months to years), daily collagen intake (even on rest days) may be more physiologically appropriate than session-only dosing.

**Measurement gap**: The user has no assessment of habitual dietary vitamin C intake, plasma ascorbate level, or habitual protein intake. If total dietary protein is insufficient (<1.6 g/kg/day), the anabolic signal from collagen peptides may be insufficient to overcome overall negative nitrogen balance.

## 4. Open Questions the Literature Cannot Currently Resolve

1. **Does 15 g collagen peptides + vitamin C produce structural change in human finger pulleys (A2/A4) as measured by high-resolution ultrasound, histological biopsy, or functional load-to-failure testing?** The P1NP biomarker used in all human collagen RCTs is a systemic surrogate; no study has directly measured tendon or pulley collagen content change attributable to oral supplementation in any population.

2. **What is the minimum effective vitamin C dose when co-ingested with collagen peptides, and does background dietary ascorbate status in athletes with replete diets render co-ingestion unnecessary?** Shaw used 48 mg; Lis tested enriched preparations. The dose-response curve for vitamin C augmentation of collagen synthesis has not been characterized, nor has a threshold for "sufficient background ascorbate" been established.

3. **Is there a dose-response relationship above 15 g collagen peptides for connective tissue outcomes, and does chronic daily dosing (as opposed to acute pre-session dosing) produce superior structural or functional outcomes in climbers?** No RCT has tested >15 g/day or compared pre-session vs. daily chronic protocols with functional climbing outcomes.

4. **Does beta-alanine–mediated carnosine loading reduce perceived forearm pump and improve climbing-specific endurance (number of moves on a route, redpoint time to failure) in trained climbers?** The 60–240 s effort duration range matches sport climbing, but no climbing-specific RCT exists; the pump sensation may also have vascular (venous occlusion, capillary density) rather than exclusively metabolic (H⁺ accumulation) determinants in experienced climbers, which carnosine would not address.

5. **In the context of climbing-induced connective tissue microtrauma (pulley strains grade I–II, active training with subclinical tendinopathy), does the anabolic window model (collagen + vit C + exercise loading) produce measurable outcomes in terms of return-to-climbing time, re-injury rate, or maximum force-to-failure on affected pulleys?** Praet et al. (2019) approaches this in Achilles tendinopathy; no equivalent trial exists in climbing-specific finger pulley pathology, which is the highest-prevalence connective tissue injury in the sport.
