## 2. Narrative synthesis

### 2.1 Hydrolyzed collagen peptides (priority 1)

#### Mechanism of action relevant to climbing

- **[Bioplausible]** Type I collagen constitutes ≈65–80% of tendon dry weight and a major fraction of pulley and ligament ECM, providing tensile stiffness and load-bearing capacity in finger flexor units.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Hydrolyzed collagen and gelatin deliver high amounts of glycine, proline, hydroxyproline, and hydroxylysine, which are rate-limiting substrates for collagen synthesis in fibroblasts under load.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Vitamin C is an essential cofactor for prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases; inadequate ascorbate impairs hydroxylation, triple-helix stability, and crosslinking, reducing collagen fibril quality and tendon mechanical properties.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** In climbing, repeated high-strain isometric loading of finger flexor tendons and pulleys (crimping, half-crimp) likely increases local collagen turnover; providing substrate and cofactor during periods of elevated tendon blood flow could augment matrix synthesis and remodeling.

#### Best available RCT and mechanistic evidence

**Shaw et al. 2017**

- **[Evidence-backed]** Shaw et al. used a randomized, double-blind, crossover design with 8 healthy males ingesting 0, 5, or 15 g vitamin C–enriched gelatin (≈15 g collagen) containing 48 mg vitamin C, 1 h before 6 min of rope skipping, repeated 3×/day for 3 d.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Serum amino acids (glycine, proline, hydroxyproline, hydroxylysine) peaked ≈1 h after ingestion, confirming a timing window where collagen-derived amino acids are elevated during exercise-induced hyperemia.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Engineered ligaments incubated with serum collected 1 h after 15 g gelatin showed increased collagen content and improved maximal tensile load vs serum from placebo; quantitative ES not reported but p<0.05.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Gregory-Shaw-3/publication/310471896_Vitamin_C-enriched_gelatin_supplementation_before_intermittent_activity_augments_collagen_synthesis/links/5a55a4f3aca272bb6962852c/Vitamin-C-enriched-gelatin-supplementation-before-intermittent-activity-augments-collagen-synthesis.pdf)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** In vivo, 15 g gelatin 1 h pre-exercise doubled serum PINP vs baseline, indicating increased type I collagen synthesis; exact SDM and 95% CI not reported, but authors describe ≈2× increase with statistical significance.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Limitations: n=8, male-only, healthy, short 3 d intervention, surrogate markers (PINP, engineered ligament mechanics), no tendon imaging, no sport performance outcomes, no climbing-specific loading.

**Lis & Baar 2019**

- **[Evidence-backed]** Lis & Baar tested 15 g vitamin C–enriched gelatin, 15 g vitamin C–enriched hydrolyzed collagen, a gelatin+HC gummy, and placebo in 10 recreationally active males using a randomized, double-blind, crossover design with 1 h pre 6 min jump rope.   [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** All collagen conditions increased circulating amino acids similarly; PINP tended to increase ≈20% from baseline in gelatin and HC but not placebo or gummy; variability prevented statistical significance vs placebo.   [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** This supports that 15 g vitamin C–enriched collagen derivatives can modestly increase collagen synthesis markers when timed 1 h pre-loading, but effect magnitude and consistency remain uncertain.

**Praet et al. 2019 and clinical tendon outcomes**

- **[Evidence-backed]** Praet et al. studied 20 patients with chronic mid-portion Achilles tendinopathy in a double-blind, placebo-controlled cross-over RCT using 5 g/day specific collagen peptides (TENDOFORTE®) plus twice-daily calf strengthening for 6 months.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** After 3 months, VISA-A increased by 12.6 (95% CI 9.7–15.5) in the collagen-first group vs 5.3 (95% CI 2.3–8.3) in placebo-first; after cross-over, both groups improved further, suggesting collagen may accelerate but not replace exercise-induced recovery.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Microvascularity decreased similarly in both groups, indicating symptomatic improvement without clear vascular structural differences.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** This trial is industry-funded (TENDOFORTE®/Gelita) and pilot-scale; tendon site (Achilles) differs from finger flexor/pulley tissues in climbers.

**Systematic reviews**

- **[Evidence-backed]** Khatri et al. reviewed 15 RCTs and concluded collagen peptides (typically 5–15 g/day) combined with exercise improve joint pain and function and increase collagen synthesis markers, with limited or no additive effect on muscle protein synthesis vs higher-quality proteins.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** The review highlights that collagen benefits are most consistent for joint/tendon pain and function, not for strength or hypertrophy, and that mechanisms remain incompletely defined.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  

#### Vitamin C co-factor and the user’s lack of co-ingestion

- **[Evidence-backed]** Both Shaw and Lis explicitly used vitamin C–enriched collagen (48 mg ascorbic acid in Shaw; similar enrichment in Lis) to ensure adequate cofactor availability for prolyl hydroxylase during the post-ingestion window.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Vitamin C RDA is 75–90 mg/day for adults; Shaw’s 48 mg dose is below RDA but likely sufficient to saturate local enzymatic needs when combined with dietary intake, assuming no deficiency.  
- **[Bioplausible]** If dietary vitamin C intake is already ≥RDA, additional supplemental vitamin C with collagen may be redundant; however, no RCT has directly compared collagen with vs without added vitamin C under controlled dietary ascorbate.  
- **[Bioplausible]** The user’s protocol (15 g collagen without explicit vitamin C) may still be effective if habitual diet provides ≥75–90 mg/day vitamin C, but this remains untested; the specific benefit of collagen without co-supplemental vitamin C is therefore mechanistically plausible but not directly evidenced.

#### Timing window relative to climbing

- **[Evidence-backed]** Shaw and Lis both timed collagen ingestion 1 h before brief plyometric loading (6 min rope skipping or jump rope), with amino acid peaks at ≈1 h and exercise-induced hyperemia overlapping this peak.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Climbing sessions typically last 1.5–3 h; ingesting collagen 45–60 min pre-warm-up should ensure elevated collagen-derived amino acids during early high-strain tendon loading (e.g., warm-up hangs, first hard boulders).  
- **[Bioplausible]** No RCT has compared pre- vs post-exercise collagen timing for tendon outcomes; extrapolation from muscle protein synthesis literature (where pre/post protein timing differences are small) may not apply due to lower tendon perfusion and slower turnover.  
- **[Bioplausible]** The user’s “before climbing” intake aligns broadly with the 1 h pre-exercise window; precision (e.g., 60±15 min) is likely more important than exact minute-level timing.

#### Dose–response

- **[Evidence-backed]** Shaw showed a dose–response in amino acid levels and PINP between 5 g and 15 g gelatin, with 15 g producing the largest increase and significant PINP doubling.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Lis used 15 g for all collagen conditions and observed ≈20% PINP increases (non-significant), suggesting 15 g is at least sufficient to alter collagen synthesis markers in some individuals.   [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Khatri’s review indicates most tendon/joint RCTs used 5–15 g/day; higher doses (15 g) were associated with increased collagen synthesis markers, but no clear linear dose–response beyond 15 g is documented.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Praet used 5 g/day specific peptides and observed clinically meaningful VISA-A improvements, indicating that lower doses can be effective when combined with structured loading.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** For climbers, 10–15 g collagen/gelatin 1 h pre-loading appears to be the most evidence-aligned dose range; doses <5 g may be suboptimal for tendon collagen synthesis.

#### Collagen and skin healing

- **[Evidence-backed]** An umbrella review (Ravindran et al. 2026) reports that collagen supplementation improves skin elasticity and hydration across multiple RCTs, with clinically meaningful benefits for dermal health; climbing-specific skin outcomes were not studied.   [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/asjopenforum/article/doi/10.1093/asjof/ojag018/8446510)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Improved dermal collagen content and hydration could theoretically enhance resistance to micro-tears and accelerate healing of abrasions, but no RCT has examined flappers, split tips, or chronic fingertip thinning in climbers.  
- **[Folklore: r/climbharder, various coaching blogs]** Claims that collagen supplementation accelerates healing of climbing-specific skin injuries (flappers, splits) are widely repeated in climbing communities but lack direct empirical support.  
- **[Bioplausible]** Mechanistically, increased systemic collagen availability and dermal collagen synthesis could modestly improve wound healing, but the magnitude and relevance for high-friction climbing skin trauma remain unknown.

#### Collagen and pulley/tendon healing during active climbing

- **[Evidence-backed]** Praet’s Achilles tendinopathy trial suggests that specific collagen peptides plus loading can accelerate symptom improvement and functional recovery vs loading alone, at least in a lower-limb tendon.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Khatri’s review indicates collagen peptides can reduce joint pain and improve function in degenerative conditions and after joint injury, supporting a role in connective tissue recovery.   [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Finger flexor tendons and A2/A4 pulleys are collagen-rich structures undergoing high strain in climbing; providing glycine/proline substrate and vitamin C during loading could enhance type I collagen synthesis and crosslinking, potentially supporting remodeling and healing.  
- **[Bioplausible]** Active climbing during tendon healing may benefit from improved collagen synthesis if loading is within a therapeutic range; however, excessive strain could still exceed tissue capacity regardless of supplementation.  
- **[Folklore: Lattice Training blog, various physio podcasts]** The specific claim that “15 g collagen + vitamin C 1 h before rehab or climbing will directly speed pulley healing and allow earlier return to hard climbing” is widely promoted but extrapolates from Shaw and Praet without climbing-specific RCTs.

### 2.2 Additional supplements (priority 2)

#### Creatine monohydrate

- **[Bioplausible]** Creatine increases intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, enhancing ATP resynthesis during short-duration, high-intensity efforts, which are central to powerful moves and repeated attempts in bouldering and hard sport climbing.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8949037/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Wu et al. (2022) reviewed 16 RCTs and concluded creatine supplementation is efficient for muscle growth in healthy young individuals with adequate training, with consistent improvements in strength and fat-free mass vs placebo; ES and CI vary by study.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8949037/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Zhang et al. (2025) meta-analyzed creatine RCTs and found significant strength gains vs control, with greater effects in untrained individuals and with low–moderate doses plus high-intensity training; pooled ES not in snippet but direction is robust.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12665265/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** For climbers, increased upper-body pulling and finger flexor strength could improve performance on powerful boulders and steep routes, but no climbing-specific creatine RCT exists.  
- **[Bioplausible]** Typical evidence-based dosing is 3–5 g/day (after optional 20 g/day loading for 5–7 d), taken daily irrespective of training time; timing relative to climbing is less critical than total daily intake.  
- **[Bioplausible]** Potential downside for climbers is small increases in body mass (≈1–2 kg water and lean mass), which may reduce strength-to-weight ratio; net effect likely positive for power-oriented climbers but uncertain for weight-sensitive onsight performance.  
- **[Folklore: r/climbharder, commercial blogs]** Community debates about creatine “making you too heavy to climb” are largely anecdotal and not supported by controlled climbing performance data.

#### Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA)

- **[Bioplausible]** EPA/DHA exert anti-inflammatory and pro-resolving effects, modulate cell membrane composition, and may influence tendon healing and muscle recovery by reducing pro-inflammatory cytokines and oxidative stress.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11243702/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Fernández-Lázaro et al. (2024) found that omega-3 supplementation reduced CK and LDH vs control in 3/4 RCTs and lowered CRP in 2/13, with mixed effects on DOMS and no consistent performance enhancement.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11243702/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Sandford et al. (2018) reported no significant difference in OSS at 2 months between omega-3 and placebo in rotator cuff–related shoulder pain, but a modest SPADI improvement (−8.3; 95% CI −15.6 to −0.94) at 3 months favoring omega-3.   [BMJ Open Sport & Exercise Medicine](https://bmjopensem.bmj.com/content/4/1/e000414)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Doses in RCTs typically range 1.5–2.5 g/day combined EPA+DHA; timing is not acutely performance-critical and is usually divided with meals.  
- **[Bioplausible]** For climbers, omega-3 may modestly aid recovery from high-volume training and possibly support shoulder or elbow tendinopathy management, but tendon-specific benefits are small and not consistently superior to placebo.  
- **[Folklore: physio blogs, sports nutrition marketing]** Claims that omega-3 “strengthens tendons” or “prevents pulley injuries” in climbers are not supported by current RCTs.

#### Magnesium

- **[Bioplausible]** Magnesium is a cofactor in ATP-dependent reactions, influences neuromuscular excitability, and may affect muscle cramps and sleep quality, which indirectly impact recovery and training quality.  
- **[Evidence-backed]** General RCTs in athletes show that magnesium supplementation can correct deficiency and may improve performance in deficient individuals, but effects in replete athletes are small or absent; no climbing-specific RCTs exist.  
- **[Bioplausible]** Typical supplemental doses are 200–400 mg elemental magnesium/day, taken in the evening to potentially support sleep; excessive doses can cause GI distress.  
- **[Folklore: coaching forums]** Use of magnesium to “prevent forearm pump” or “stop finger cramps on routes” is anecdotal and not supported by targeted trials.

#### Beta-alanine

- **[Bioplausible]** Beta-alanine increases intramuscular carnosine, enhancing buffering of H\(^+\) during high-intensity efforts of 1–4 min, which overlaps with sustained forearm contractions on long crux sequences or power-endurance circuits.  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Multiple RCTs in cycling and high-intensity exercise show beta-alanine (4–6 g/day for ≥4 weeks) improves performance in tasks lasting 60–240 s, with ES typically small–moderate; no climbing-specific RCTs.  
- **[Bioplausible]** For climbers, beta-alanine may be most relevant for pumpy sport routes and power-endurance training; paresthesia is a common side effect at higher single doses.  
- **[Folklore: climbing blogs]** Specific claims that beta-alanine “eliminates pump” in climbing are extrapolated from cycling/rowing data and not empirically tested.

#### Caffeine

- **[Bioplausible]** Caffeine antagonizes adenosine receptors, increases CNS arousal, reduces perceived exertion, and can enhance strength and endurance; in climbing, this could affect grip strength, power, and decision-making.  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Grgic’s meta-analysis found caffeine (1–7 mg/kg) produced a small but significant improvement in isometric handgrip strength, d=0.17 (95% CI 0.10–0.23; p<0.001).   [Clinical Nutrition ESPEN](https://www.clinicalnutritionespen.com/article/S2405-4577%2821%2901153-0/fulltext)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Abbas-Hashemi et al. showed 400 mg/day caffeine for 8 weeks improved handgrip strength by 4.7±3.2 kg vs 0.56±3.1 kg in cirrhotic patients (p=0.006), suggesting chronic caffeine can enhance grip even in clinical populations.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12797547/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Ruiz-López et al. (2026) found that 3 mg/kg caffeine 60 min pre-testing did not significantly improve climbing-specific performance (pull-up 1RM, power, dead-hang time, RFD) in 13 male climbers; ES were trivial–small, with some non-significant trends.   [MDPI](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/18/2/284)  
- **[Bioplausible]** Standard ergogenic dosing is 3–6 mg/kg 30–60 min pre-exercise; individual sensitivity and habituation modulate response.  
- **[Bioplausible]** For climbers, caffeine may provide small benefits in alertness and perceived effort, but climbing-specific performance gains appear minimal at 3 mg/kg in current data.  
- **[Folklore: climbing gyms, blogs]** Heavy caffeine use before redpoints is common practice; expectations of large performance boosts are not supported by current climbing RCTs.

## 3. Practical assessment of the user’s collagen protocol

**User protocol:** 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides consumed before climbing sessions, **without vitamin C co-ingestion**.

### 3.1 Supported benefits by claim

- **Claim: enhanced tendon/pulley collagen synthesis with 15 g collagen pre-climbing**

  - **[Evidence-backed]** 15 g vitamin C–enriched gelatin/collagen 1 h pre-loading increases collagen synthesis markers (PINP) and improves engineered ligament mechanics in healthy males.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
  - **[Bioplausible]** The user’s 15 g hydrolyzed collagen dose matches the evidence-supported amount; if taken ≈1 h pre-climbing, amino acid availability likely overlaps with tendon loading.  
  - **[Bioplausible]** Without explicit vitamin C co-ingestion, effectiveness depends on dietary vitamin C sufficiency; if diet meets RDA, enzymatic cofactor needs may still be met.  
  - **Net classification:** **[Bioplausible]** for tendon/pulley benefits in this exact protocol (15 g collagen without added vitamin C), because no RCT has tested collagen alone in this timing context.

- **Claim: accelerated healing of finger tendinopathy or pulley injuries while continuing to climb**

  - **[Evidence-backed]** Specific collagen peptides (5 g/day) plus calf strengthening improved VISA-A scores more than placebo in Achilles tendinopathy, suggesting accelerated clinical improvement.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
  - **[Bioplausible]** Extrapolation to finger flexor tendons and pulleys is mechanistically plausible but untested; tissue type and loading differ.  
  - **Net classification:** **[Bioplausible]** for climbers with finger tendon/pulley issues; **not** directly evidence-backed for climbing injuries.

- **Claim: improved skin healing (flappers, splits)**

  - **[Evidence-backed]** Collagen supplementation improves skin elasticity and hydration in general populations.   [Oxford Academic](https://academic.oup.com/asjopenforum/article/doi/10.1093/asjof/ojag018/8446510)  
  - **[Bioplausible]** Better dermal collagen and hydration may modestly support wound healing, but climbing-specific skin trauma has not been studied.  
  - **[Folklore: r/climbharder, coaching blogs]** Direct claims about faster flapper healing with collagen are anecdotal.  
  - **Net classification:** **[Bioplausible]** for general skin health; **[Folklore]** for climbing-specific skin recovery.

### 3.2 What is missing from the protocol

- **[Evidence-backed]** All mechanistic RCTs (Shaw, Lis) used vitamin C–enriched collagen, with 48 mg vitamin C per 15 g serving, to ensure cofactor availability.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Bioplausible]** The user’s lack of explicit vitamin C co-ingestion introduces uncertainty: if dietary vitamin C is low, collagen synthesis may be suboptimal; if adequate, additional vitamin C may be unnecessary.  
- **[Bioplausible]** The protocol does not specify timing (e.g., 60 min pre-climbing); taking collagen immediately before or during climbing may misalign amino acid peaks with early tendon loading.  
- **[Bioplausible]** The protocol is not explicitly linked to a structured tendon-loading program (e.g., progressive fingerboard rehab), which appears critical in Praet’s and other tendon trials.

### 3.3 Evidence-backed “ideal” protocol for tendon-focused use

- **[Evidence-backed]** Dose: 15 g collagen/gelatin appears optimal for increasing collagen synthesis markers in mechanistic studies; 5 g/day specific peptides can be effective clinically for Achilles tendinopathy.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6356409/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Vitamin C: ≈48 mg vitamin C co-ingested with collagen, as in Shaw, is sufficient to support collagen synthesis; higher doses have not been shown superior.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Timing: ingestion 60 min before targeted tendon loading (e.g., rope skipping, jump rope) is used in all mechanistic RCTs; this likely generalizes to 60 min pre-climbing or pre-rehab fingerboard work.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/30859848/)  
- **[Evidence-backed]** Frequency: Shaw used 3×/day for 3 d; most clinical trials use once-daily dosing; no clear evidence that multiple daily doses are superior for chronic tendon adaptation.   [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/)  [ResearchGate](https://www.researchgate.net/journal/Amino-Acids-1438-2199/publication/354402382_The_effects_of_collagen_peptide_supplementation_on_body_composition_collagen_synthesis_and_recovery_from_joint_injury_and_exercise_a_systematic_review/links/613844dc637a811d6d584446/The-effects-of-collagen-peptide-supplementation-on-body-composition-collagen-synthesis-and-recovery-from-joint-injury-and-exercise-a-systematic-review.pdf)  
- **[Bioplausible]** For a climber with finger tendon/pulley concerns, an evidence-aligned protocol would be: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen or gelatin + ≈50 mg vitamin C, taken ≈60 min before a structured tendon-loading session (e.g., rehab fingerboard or controlled climbing) on 3–7 d/week, integrated into a progressive loading plan.

## 4. Open questions

1. **[Bioplausible]** Does 15 g hydrolyzed collagen **without** added vitamin C, in individuals with adequate dietary vitamin C, produce the same tendon collagen synthesis and clinical outcomes as vitamin C–enriched collagen in Shaw- and Praet-style protocols?  
2. **[Bioplausible]** In climbers with finger flexor or pulley injuries, does collagen supplementation (with or without vitamin C) plus structured rehab improve time to return to previous grade, re-injury rates, or tendon/pulley imaging markers vs rehab alone?  
3. **[Bioplausible]** Is there a dose–response relationship between collagen intake (5 vs 10 vs 15 g) and tendon adaptation in high-strain, low-perfusion tissues like finger pulleys, and does this differ between injury rehab and prophylactic use?  
4. **[Bioplausible]** How does chronic collagen supplementation interact with other common climbing supplements (creatine, caffeine, omega-3) in terms of tendon adaptation, injury risk, and performance, including potential synergistic or antagonistic effects?  
5. **[Bioplausible]** Can beta-alanine, creatine, and caffeine, alone or in combination, meaningfully improve climbing-specific performance (redpoint grade, bouldering grade, competition results) in controlled RCTs, and how do these effects compare in magnitude to training interventions such as hangboarding or structured periodization?
