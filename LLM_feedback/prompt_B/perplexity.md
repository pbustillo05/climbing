## Narrative synthesis

### Hydrolyzed collagen peptides

**Mechanism.** Hydrolyzed collagen/gelatin is rich in glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline, which are the dominant amino-acid building blocks for type I collagen. The rationale for climbing is that repeated loading of finger flexor tendons, pulleys, and skin may stimulate collagen remodeling when substrate availability is increased around the exercise window.

**Best evidence.** The key trial is Shaw et al. (2017): 15 g gelatin/collagen plus 48 mg vitamin C taken 1 h before intermittent loading increased the biochemical marker P1NP, which is a surrogate of collagen synthesis, compared with placebo. That is evidence for an acute rise in a surrogate marker, not proof of stronger tendons, fewer pulley injuries, faster skin healing, or better climbing performance. The study was tiny, male-only, and done in healthy subjects, so external validity to climbers with chronic pulley or skin problems is weak. [trainingforclimbing](https://trainingforclimbing.com/collagen-for-climbers-latest-research-supports-benefits/)

**Evidence tier.** For “acute increase in collagen synthesis marker when taken before loading,” this is **[Evidence-backed]** but low-certainty. For “improves climbing performance” and “prevents pulley injury,” it is **[Bioplausible]**, not proven. For “accelerates climbing skin healing,” it is effectively **[Folklore]** because I do not have peer-reviewed climbing-specific intervention evidence supporting that claim.

**Dose and timing.** The best-supported protocol from the core literature is about 15 g pre-load, taken roughly 30–60 min before exercise; Shaw used 1 h. That aligns with your “before climbing” timing if you take it close enough to the session to have circulating amino acids during loading. The evidence does not establish that a much wider timing window is equally effective.

**Vitamin C co-factor.** Vitamin C is required for normal collagen biosynthesis because it supports prolyl/lysyl hydroxylation. The user protocol is missing the co-ingested vitamin C that was part of the positive Shaw protocol, so the direct evidence does **not** cleanly transfer from “15 g collagen + vitamin C” to “15 g collagen alone.” “Adequate” vitamin C in the basic nutritional sense is the dietary RDA, but the study used a supplemental 48 mg co-dose, so the evidence-based comparison is to a collagen-plus-vitamin-C protocol, not to an isolated collagen shake. The mechanistic gap for collagen without vitamin C is therefore real, but I would not claim it is ineffective if the person’s overall vitamin C intake is already sufficient from diet; rather, the evidence is incomplete.

**Dose-response.** The common evidence-based range in reviews is roughly 2.5 to 15 g/day, with pre-exercise dosing favored when the goal is connective-tissue signaling. Fifteen grams is within the studied range and is defensible as a target dose, but the literature does not prove that 15 g is superior to lower doses for climbers. So 15 g is **reasonable**, not uniquely established.

**Skin healing claim.** I am not aware of controlled climbing-specific evidence that collagen speeds healing of flappers, skin tears, or chronic skin thinning. That claim should be labeled **[Folklore]** unless supported by a broader dermatology or wound-healing trial directly applicable to climbing skin trauma, which is currently not established here.

**Pulley/tendon healing during active climbing.** This is the strongest connective-tissue rationale, but still only **[Bioplausible]** to **[low-confidence Evidence-backed]** depending on how strictly you require clinical outcomes. The mechanism is substrate support for type I collagen synthesis during mechanically stimulated remodeling, but the critical missing endpoint is whether this translates into fewer injuries or faster recovery in climbers.

### Creatine monohydrate

**Mechanism.** Creatine increases phosphocreatine availability and improves short-duration, high-intensity force production and repeated sprint capacity.

**Best evidence.** There is strong general sports evidence for strength/power, but climbing-specific RCTs are sparse. For climbing, the likely benefit is better repeated high-force efforts and possibly improved power on hard moves, not a direct tendon/skin effect.

**Tier.** **[Evidence-backed]** for general performance, **[Bioplausible]** for climbing-specific benefit.

**Dose.** 3–5 g/day maintenance, or a loading phase if desired.

### Omega-3 fatty acids

**Mechanism.** EPA/DHA may modulate inflammation and possibly tendon-cell signaling.

**Best evidence.** Evidence for tendinopathy or connective tissue outcomes is not strong or consistent enough to recommend as a climbing-specific recovery aid. Any benefit is currently more theoretical than proven.

**Tier.** **[Bioplausible]**.

**Dose.** If used for general health, typical supplemental intakes are 1–2 g/day combined EPA+DHA, but climbing-specific dosing is not established.

### Magnesium

**Mechanism.** Magnesium participates in neuromuscular function, ATP handling, and sleep quality in deficient individuals.

**Best evidence.** There is no strong climbing-specific RCT evidence showing performance improvement in magnesium-replete athletes. It is most relevant when intake is low or deficiency is present.

**Tier.** **[Bioplausible]** for performance unless deficiency exists.

**Dose.** Only meaningfully evidence-based as correction of deficiency; common supplemental doses are individualized.

### Beta-alanine

**Mechanism.** Increases muscle carnosine and buffering capacity, which may help repeated high-intensity efforts.

**Best evidence.** It can help exercise lasting roughly 1–4 min and repeated intense bouts in some sports. Climbing-specific evidence is limited, and many climbing efforts are isometric/intermittent in a way that may only partially match the classic beta-alanine use-case.

**Tier.** **[Bioplausible]** for climbing, **[Evidence-backed]** in general sport contexts.

**Dose.** Common protocols use about 3.2–6.4 g/day for several weeks.

### Caffeine

**Mechanism.** Adenosine antagonism improves alertness, perceived exertion, and often force output.

**Best evidence.** A meta-analysis found caffeine improved isometric handgrip strength with a very small but statistically significant effect: d = 0.17, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.23, p < 0.001; 1–3 mg/kg also showed benefit, d = 0.20, 95% CI 0.10 to 0.30, p < 0.001. That is directly relevant to climbing because grip endurance and isometric force are central, but the evidence is still indirect because handgrip testing is not climbing performance. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/35063247/)

**Tier.** **[Evidence-backed]** for acute grip performance; **[Bioplausible]** for climbing-specific transfer.

**Dose and timing.** 1–3 mg/kg 30–60 min pre-climbing is a conservative evidence-based starting point; higher doses can work but increase side effects.

## Practical assessment of your collagen protocol

- **Supported benefit:** An acute pre-exercise collagen/gelatin strategy is **[Evidence-backed]** for increasing a biochemical marker of collagen synthesis when paired with loading and vitamin C. [trainingforclimbing](https://trainingforclimbing.com/collagen-for-climbers-latest-research-supports-benefits/)
- **Not proven:** Better climbing performance, fewer pulley injuries, faster skin healing, and faster “real-world” tendon recovery are **[Bioplausible]** at best, and skin-healing claims are mostly **[Folklore]**.
- **Missing piece:** The evidence-based protocol includes vitamin C; your current protocol omits it. That is a meaningful deviation from the positive trial design.
- **Evidence-backed version:** 15 g gelatin/collagen taken about 1 h before climbing, with a modest vitamin C co-dose, and paired with a meaningful loading stimulus. That is the closest match to the core human evidence.
