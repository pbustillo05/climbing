**2. Narrative synthesis**

**Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides**  
**Mechanism**: Supplies high amounts of glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline (every third residue in type-I collagen); provides substrate for fibroblast collagen synthesis in loaded connective tissue (tendons, pulleys, skin). **[Bioplausible]**  
**Best evidence**: Shaw et al. (2017) showed 15 g vitamin C-enriched gelatin 1 h pre-exercise doubled circulating PINP (collagen synthesis marker) and improved in-vitro ligament mechanics. Lis et al. (2019) confirmed HC and gelatin both elevate PINP when timed 1 h pre-loading. Praet et al. (2019) demonstrated accelerated VISA-A gains in Achilles tendinopathy when combined with loading. Recent systematic reviews (2026) report GRADE A evidence for increased tendon CSA (+11 % vs +4.7 % at 5–15 g) and stiffness with 15–30 g + vitamin C + high-load training. **[Evidence-backed]** (Shaw 2017; Praet 2019; Lis 2019). No industry funding declared in primary papers; Baar lab has received collagen-industry support in related work—flag as potential bias.  
**Evidence tier**: **[Evidence-backed]** for augmentation of exercise-induced collagen synthesis and tendinopathy symptom relief when paired with loading + vitamin C.  
**Recommended dose/timing (if supported)**: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides + ≥50 mg vitamin C, ingested 1 h before climbing/loading sessions (matches peak amino-acid window and Shaw protocol).  
**Limitations/caveats**: All positive data require concurrent loading stimulus and vitamin C co-ingestion; no long-term climbing-specific pulley or skin RCTs; small n, mostly male/healthy cohorts; surrogate markers predominate.

**Vitamin C co-factor**: Ascorbic acid is required for prolyl-4-hydroxylase; without it, collagen triple-helix formation fails (scurvy-level impairment at <10 mg·d⁻¹). Shaw protocol used 48 mg supplemental vitamin C. Dietary RDA (75–90 mg·d⁻¹) meets baseline needs but does not replicate the acute supra-RDA spike used in trials. **[Evidence-backed]** (enzymology + Shaw protocol). User protocol (no co-ingestion) creates a clear mechanistic gap: collagen synthesis benefit is **suboptimal** without the co-factor. **[Bioplausible]** full benefit only with vitamin C.

**Timing window**: Shaw/Lis data require ingestion 1 h pre-exercise to align peak plasma amino acids with exercise-induced tendon blood flow and mechanotransduction. User “before climbing” protocol matches if taken ~60 min prior; variance >30 min reduces efficacy. **[Evidence-backed]**.

**Dose**: 15 g is the evidence-supported threshold (Shaw dose-response: 15 g >> 5 g = placebo). Lower doses lack effect on PINP. **[Evidence-backed]** (Shaw 2017; Lis 2019).

**Claim: collagen helps skin healing (flappers, tears, chronic thinning)**: General skin RCTs/meta-analyses show improved elasticity/hydration/wound healing, but **no peer-reviewed evidence** exists for climbing-specific skin recovery. **[Folklore: climbing community/coaches (e.g., Hoopers Beta, forums)]**.

**Claim: collagen helps pulley/tendon healing during active climbing**: **[Evidence-backed]** for symptom relief and tendon CSA/stiffness in tendinopathy when combined with loading (Praet 2019); **[Bioplausible]** direct pulley healing via substrate supply to type-I collagen under load. No climbing-specific pulley RCTs; benefit is load-dependent, not passive supplementation.

**Priority 2 — Additional supplements**

- **Creatine monohydrate**: Increases phosphocreatine for ATP resynthesis in high-intensity efforts. **[Evidence-backed]** for general strength/power (meta-analyses); **no climbing-specific RCTs** (one ongoing). Weight gain (1–5 lb) may offset finger-strength gains in climbers. **[Bioplausible]** net benefit in climbing. No recommended climbing dose/timing from direct evidence.

- **Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA)**: Anti-inflammatory (reduce PGE2, modulate resolvins). One RCT (Sandford 2018) in rotator-cuff tendinopathy showed **no added benefit** over exercise alone. Systematic reviews note paucity of high-quality tendon data. **[Evidence-backed (negative)]** no consistent tendon/joint benefit beyond general anti-inflammatory effects. No climbing-specific recommendation.

- **Magnesium**: Cofactor in muscle relaxation/ATP handling. Cochrane review (idiopathic cramps) and recent meta-analyses: **no clinically meaningful reduction** in cramp frequency or exercise-associated cramps vs placebo. Some data for reduced soreness post-exercise. **[Evidence-backed (negative for cramps)]**. Not recommended for climbing cramping.

- **Beta-alanine**: Elevates muscle carnosine → buffers H⁺ during glycolysis. Sas-Nowosielski et al. (2021) RCT in elite climbers (4 g·d⁻¹ × 4 wk): large improvements in ~1-min continuous climbing and repeated campus efforts (*d*=1.44–1.73). **[Evidence-backed]** for forearm endurance in climbing-relevant durations. Recommended: 4–6 g·d⁻¹ (split doses to minimize paresthesia) for ≥4 wk.

- **Caffeine**: Adenosine antagonist; increases motor unit recruitment/alertness. Meta-analysis (Grgic 2022) on isometric handgrip: small effect (*d*=0.17, 95 % CI 0.10–0.23). Recent climbing-specific RCT (3 mg·kg⁻¹): **no significant** improvement in pull-up/grip metrics. Higher doses (5–6 mg·kg⁻¹) may be required. **[Evidence-backed (small)** for grip strength; **null** at low dose in climbers]. Practical: 3–6 mg·kg⁻¹ 45–60 min pre-session if tolerated.

**3. Practical assessment of the user’s collagen protocol** (15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides pre-climbing, no vitamin C)  
- **Supported benefit**: Partial substrate supply for type-I collagen synthesis during loaded climbing sessions **[Bioplausible]**.  
- **Claimed benefits classification**:
  - Tendon/pulley remodeling during active climbing: **[Bioplausible]** (substrate) + partial **[Evidence-backed]** translation from tendinopathy data only when vitamin C + loading present.
  - Skin healing (flappers/thinning): **[Folklore: climbing community]**.
- **What is missing**: Acute vitamin C co-ingestion (≥48–50 mg) to enable prolyl hydroxylation; confirmation of dietary vitamin C adequacy does not substitute for the trial protocol.  
- **Evidence-backed protocol**: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides + 50 mg vitamin C exactly 60 min before climbing sessions, performed under progressive loading. Daily 10–15 g collagen may provide baseline support but lacks the acute exercise-timed window data. Without vitamin C the user’s current protocol is mechanistically incomplete and likely delivers <50 % of the collagen-synthesis augmentation shown in Shaw/Lis trials.

**4. Open questions the literature cannot resolve**  
1. Does 15 g collagen (with/without vitamin C) accelerate healing of climbing-specific A2 pulley strains or skin flappers in athletes continuing to climb?  
2. What is the minimal effective vitamin C co-dose and exact timing window in free-living climbers with variable dietary intake?  
3. Long-term (>6 mo) effects of chronic collagen supplementation on pulley CSA, stiffness, and injury recurrence rates in climbers?  
4. Head-to-head comparison of collagen vs whey protein (or other proteins) on tendon outcomes when both are paired with climbing-specific loading?  
5. Dose-response and sex differences (female climbers underrepresented) for collagen + vitamin C on finger-flexor tendon adaptations?
