## 2. Narrative synthesis

### Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides

**1. Shaw et al. mechanism and findings**
- **Design**: crossover, healthy males, gelatin +48 mg vit C 1 h pre-exercise. **[Evidence-backed]**
- **Outcome**: circulating glycine +376 µmol/L, proline +162 µmol/L, hydroxyproline to 105 µmol/L at 1 h. **[Evidence-backed]**
- **Functional test**: engineered ligaments treated with post-supplement serum showed increased collagen content and improved modulus and ultimate tensile strength (p<0.05). **[Evidence-backed]** — in vitro surrogate, not human tendon.
- **In vivo**: PINP doubled with 15 g vs placebo, maintained 72 h. **[Evidence-backed]**
- **Limitations**: n=8, male-only, no injured tendons, no climbing outcomes, short duration, surrogate marker reflects largely bone metabolism not tendon. **[Evidence-backed]**

**2. Vitamin C co-factor**
- Prolyl and lysyl hydroxylases require ascorbate for hydroxylation of proline/lysine; without it, triple-helix formation fails and collagen is degraded. **** — established biochemistry, scurvy data.
- Shaw used 48 mg vit C, below US RDA (90 mg men, 75 mg women) but sufficient to prevent deficiency in well-nourished subjects. **[Evidence-backed]**
- No RCT tests collagen without vit C in humans. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Classification: benefit of 15 g collagen **without** vit C is **** reduced, because substrate rises but hydroxylation may be rate-limited if systemic ascorbate <30 µmol/L. With typical diet, most athletes exceed RDA, so absolute deficiency unlikely, but co-ingestion ensures maximal enzyme saturation during peak aminoacidemia. ****[Bioplausible]

**3. Timing window**
- Amino acids peak 60 min post-ingestion for 15 g; 30 min for 5 g. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Exercise performed at peak maximizes delivery via exercise-induced hyperemia. ****
- User protocol "before climbing" matches if 45-60 min pre-load. Variability >30 min reduces effect. ****[Bioplausible]

**4. Dose**
- 15 g rescues collagen synthesis in middle-aged men where exercise alone fails. 30 g augments further (+26% AUC vs 15 g). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Systematic review: doses 15-30 g/d + vit C show structural tendon gains; 5 g shows only within-group changes. **[Evidence-backed]**
- No dose-response data for climbers specifically. **[Evidence-backed]**

**5. Skin healing claim**
- General skin: 5 g collagen +80 mg vit C ×16 wk improved dermis density and texture in aging women. **[Evidence-backed]**
- No RCT on collagen for climbing flappers, split tips, or callus repair. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Classification: "collagen accelerates climbing skin healing" = **[Folklore: climbing gyms, social media, coach blogs]** — plausible substrate supply but untested in this context.

**6. Pulley/tendon healing during active climbing**
- Mechanism: loading stimulates tenocyte TGF-β signaling; provision of glycine (33% of collagen), proline, hydroxyproline increases intracellular pools for type I procollagen synthesis. ****
- Human data: collagen + loading increases tendon CSA and stiffness (GRADE A). **[Evidence-backed]**
- No RCT demonstrates reduced pulley rupture rates or faster return to climbing. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Classification: structural adaptation = **[Evidence-backed]**; injury prevention/healing in climbers = **** — mechanistically reasonable, lacks direct clinical trials.[Bioplausible]

### Priority 2 — Additional supplements

**Creatine monohydrate**
- Mechanism: increases muscle phosphocreatine, accelerating ATP resynthesis during 5-15 s maximal efforts. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Best evidence: meta-analysis 34 studies, MVC strength +7% in knee extensors, muscular endurance +18% in open-endpoint tests. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Climbing-specific: none identified; survey of 110 climbers found creatine rarely used and no performance data. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Tier: general strength/power = **[Evidence-backed]**; climbing forearm endurance = **** (short intense moves rely on PCr), but weight gain (~1-2 kg) may impair power-to-weight. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Dose: 3-5 g/d maintenance, no loading required.[Bioplausible]

**Omega-3 EPA/DHA**
- Mechanism: EPA/DHA compete with arachidonic acid, reducing pro-inflammatory eicosanoids. ****
- Best evidence: rotator-cuff RCT 2.57 g/d ×8 wk showed no OSS benefit vs placebo (mean diff -0.1, 95% CI -2.6 to 2.5). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Mendelian randomization suggests genetically higher omega-3 associated with +18% Achilles tendinitis risk. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Tier: tendon health in athletes = **[Evidence-backed]** no benefit, possibly harm at high doses.[Bioplausible]

**Magnesium**
- Mechanism: cofactor for ATPases, neuromuscular excitability. ****
- Best evidence: Cochrane review and 2017 JAMA RCT n=94 found magnesium oxide no better than placebo for nocturnal leg cramps (Δ -0.38 cramps/wk both groups). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Climbing relevance: no RCT on forearm pump or cramping. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Tier: cramp prevention = **[Evidence-backed]** ineffective in general population; sleep quality in deficient individuals = ****. Dose if used: 300-400 mg elemental Mg at night; excess causes diarrhea.[Bioplausible]

**Beta-alanine**
- Mechanism: increases muscle carnosine, intracellular H+ buffer. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Best evidence: meta-analysis shows +2.85% performance, effect largest for 60-240 s efforts (g=0.55). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Climbing: sustained forearm contractions 30-120 s rely heavily on glycolysis and acidosis; no climbing RCT exists. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Tier: forearm endurance = ****; direct evidence = **[Folklore: climbing forums]**. Dose: 4-6 g/d split doses ×4 wk to raise carnosine ~60-80%.[Bioplausible]

**Caffeine**
- Mechanism: adenosine antagonism, reduced RPE, increased calcium release. **[Evidence-backed]**
- Best evidence in climbers: 3 mg/kg showed no significant improvement in pull-up or grip metrics (all p>0.05, trivial effect sizes g<0.17). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Subjective energy/alertness increased (p<0.05). **[Evidence-backed]**
- Tier: acute climbing performance at 3 mg/kg = **[Evidence-backed]** no benefit; higher doses untested, general strength sports show benefit 3-6 mg/kg. Dose if trialed: 3-4 mg/kg 45-60 min pre, avoid evening sessions due to sleep disruption.

## 3. Practical assessment of your collagen protocol

**Your current**: 15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides, pre-climbing, no vitamin C.

- **What is supported**:
  - 15 g provides substrate to raise glycine/proline/hydroxyproline ~1 h post-ingestion. **[Evidence-backed]**
  - When timed 45-60 min before loading, this can augment exercise-induced collagen synthesis (PINP ↑ ~2×). **[Evidence-backed]**
  - Long-term (≥12 wk) with loading may increase tendon CSA/stiffness. **[Evidence-backed]**

- **What is missing**:
  - **Vitamin C co-ingestion**: Shaw protocol used 48 mg; reviews recommend ≥50 mg. Without it, hydroxylation may be submaximal, especially if dietary intake is marginal. ****
  - **Consistency**: benefits seen with daily intake before targeted loading, not sporadic. **[Evidence-backed]**
  - **Loading specificity**: 6 min intermittent high-impact or heavy resistance (≥70% 1RM) drives synthesis; easy climbing may be insufficient stimulus. ****

- **Evidence-backed protocol**:
  1. 15-20 g hydrolyzed collagen or gelatin + 50-80 mg vitamin C (e.g., orange juice, 100 mg tablet) 45-60 min before session.
  2. Follow with 5-10 min of high-intensity tendon loading (hangboard repeaters at 70-80% max, or weighted pull-ups), then rest ≥6 h before next hard loading.
  3. Repeat daily for ≥12 weeks during training blocks. 30 g may offer additional 20-30% synthesis boost if tolerated. **[Evidence-backed]**

- **Skin claim**: no evidence collagen speeds flapper healing in climbers; maintain skin hygiene, moisturize, and allow mechanical adaptation. **[Folklore: climbing community]**[Bioplausible]

## 4. Open questions

1. Does collagen + vit C reduce pulley injury incidence in climbers over a season? No prospective injury RCT exists.
2. What is the minimal effective vitamin C dose when co-ingested with collagen in athletes with adequate baseline status? Current data use 48-80 mg arbitrarily.
3. Does collagen supplementation without structured high-load stimulus improve tendon properties in climbers who primarily do submaximal volume?
4. Can beta-alanine or creatine improve forearm critical force or time-to-failure on standardized climbing tests? No climbing-specific RCTs published.
5. Does long-term omega-3 supplementation (>3 g/d) impair or enhance tendon remodeling under heavy eccentric loading? Conflicting mechanistic vs clinical data.
