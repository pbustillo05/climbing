### 2. Narrative Synthesis

#### Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides (User Protocol: 15 g pre-climbing, no Vitamin C)

**1. Shaw et al. (2017) Analysis**
**[Evidence-backed]** (DOI: 10.3945/ajcn.116.138594). This crossover RCT (n=8 males) showed that 15 g of gelatin with 48 mg of vitamin C consumed 1 hour before 6 minutes of rope skipping doubled collagen synthesis rates (measured via serum P1NP AUC) compared to placebo. 
* **Surrogate vs. Functional distinction:** P1NP is a systemic blood biomarker of type I collagen turnover, not a direct measure of site-specific tendon hypertrophy or strength. The functional claim (increased stiffness) was demonstrated *in vitro* on engineered ligaments treated with the subjects' serum, not in the subjects' own tendons.
* **Limitations:** Extremely small sample (n=8), male-only, healthy subjects only. The exercise stimulus (rope skipping) heavily loads the Achilles/patellar tendons, leaving transferability to the upper-extremity flexor tendons and finger pulleys in rock climbing plausible but empirically unverified. Note: Co-author Baar holds patents related to engineered ligaments and nutrition.

**2. Vitamin C Co-factor & The Protocol Gap**
**[Bioplausible]** Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is an obligatory electron donor for the enzyme prolyl hydroxylase.  This enzyme converts proline to hydroxyproline, a step necessary to form and stabilize the collagen triple helix. 
* **The gap:** The user takes 15 g of collagen *without* vitamin C. While hydrolyzed collagen contains pre-hydroxylated amino acids, the *de novo* synthesis of collagen in the user's tendons stimulated by climbing requires available systemic vitamin C. 
* **Adequate intake:** The Shaw protocol utilized 48 mg of vitamin C, which is well below the US RDA of 90 mg for men/75 mg for women, suggesting that an average balanced diet may provide sufficient baseline vitamin C. However, taking it concurrently ensures peak local availability during the exercise-induced hyperemic window. Supplementing collagen without concurrent vitamin C yields a suboptimal enzymatic environment for crosslinking.

**3. Timing Window**
**[Evidence-backed]** (DOI: 10.1123/ijsnem.2018-0385). Lis and Baar (2019) demonstrated that serum levels of glycine, proline, and hydroxyproline peak 60 minutes post-ingestion. 
* **Matching user protocol:** Tendons are avascular; nutrient delivery relies on the "sponge effect" of mechanical loading (fluid extrusion during tension, fluid uptake during relaxation). Therefore, the user's "pre-climbing" protocol is mechanistically optimal *only if* consumed exactly 45–60 minutes before the active climbing load begins, aligning peak systemic amino acid availability with localized mechanical loading.

**4. Dose Response**
**[Evidence-backed]** In Shaw et al. (2017), 15 g outperformed 5 g and 0 g. Subsequent systematic reviews support 15 g/day as the minimal effective dose for maximizing systemic collagen synthesis rates, confirming the user's 15 g dose is optimal.

**5. Claim: Accelerates skin healing (flappers, splits)**
**[Folklore: Climbing community consensus/Reddit]**. There is no peer-reviewed climbing-specific RCT demonstrating that acute oral collagen ingestion accelerates epidermal or dermal wound healing of traumatic climbing skin injuries. Systemic amino acids supply building blocks for tissue repair, but prioritizing them specifically to climbing calluses via oral intake lacks targeted empirical support.

**6. Claim: Aids pulley/tendon healing during active climbing**
**[Bioplausible]**. Active climbing provides the mechanical mechanotransduction (via integrin signaling) required to upregulate collagen gene expression in loaded finger flexors and pulleys. Providing 15 g of systemic collagen peptides elevates the necessary substrate (glycine, proline) precisely when local blood flow and cellular signaling demand it. Direct *in vivo* climbing pulley RCTs are absent, but the translation from lower-body tendon models is highly sound.

#### Priority 2 — Additional Supplements

* **Creatine Monohydrate**
    * **Mechanism:** Augments intramuscular phosphocreatine stores, accelerating ATP regeneration during brief, intense efforts (e.g., limit bouldering).
    * **Evidence:** **[Evidence-backed]** for general anaerobic power. Climbing-specific data is highly mixed due to the strict strength-to-weight ratio; water retention (typically 1–2 kg) often offsets gross strength gains on the wall.
    * **Dose:** 3–5 g/day (no loading phase to minimize rapid weight gain).

* **Beta-alanine**
    * **Mechanism:** Rate-limiting precursor to carnosine, an intracellular acid buffer buffering $H^+$ accumulation during glycolysis.
    * **Evidence:** **[Bioplausible]** for 1–10 minute sustained climbing (sport routes). Some climbing-specific trials show minor improvements in continuous climbing efforts or campus board endurance, but effects are often marginal.
    * **Dose:** 3.2–6.4 g/day for 4+ weeks.

* **Caffeine**
    * **Mechanism:** Adenosine receptor antagonist; lowers perceived exertion, increases motor unit recruitment.
    * **Evidence:** **[Evidence-backed]** for subjective energy. However, Ruiz-López et al. (2026) found a 3 mg/kg dose yielded trivial/non-significant effects on grip strength and pull-up endurance in trained climbers, despite improving subjective alertness. 
    * **Dose:** 3–6 mg/kg, 45–60 mins pre-session.

* **Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)**
    * **Mechanism:** Incorporation into cell membranes, shifting eicosanoid production toward anti-inflammatory pathways (resolvins, protectins).
    * **Evidence:** **[Bioplausible]** for managing reactive tendinopathy. No climbing-specific RCTs exist, but systemic data suggests utility in generalized joint health.
    * **Dose:** 2–3 g combined EPA/DHA daily.

* **Magnesium**
    * **Mechanism:** Cofactor in ATP utilization and neuromuscular transmission.
    * **Evidence:** **[Folklore: General sports nutrition consensus]** for acute cramp prevention in euvolemic, well-fed athletes. Efficacy is highly dependent on addressing a pre-existing deficiency rather than offering ergogenic over-compensation.

### 3. Practical Assessment of the User's Protocol

**What benefit is supported by evidence?**
* **[Bioplausible]**: Enhanced substrate availability for pulley/tendon remodeling, assuming the climbing session provides sufficient mechanical stimulus.
* **[Folklore]**: Accelerated healing of topical skin tears.

**What is missing from the protocol?**
* **Vitamin C co-ingestion:** The absence of ~50 mg ascorbic acid critically limits the prolyl hydroxylase activity needed to effectively weave the ingested amino acids into new, functional collagen triple-helices.
* **Precise timing:** "Before climbing" is vague. If consumed 10 minutes prior, peak blood levels will occur during the warm-up rather than the peak loading phase. 

**The evidence-backed protocol:**
Consume 15 g of hydrolyzed collagen peptides **co-ingested with ~50 mg of vitamin C**, exactly **45 to 60 minutes before** loading the target connective tissues on the wall or hangboard.
