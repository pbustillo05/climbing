# 2. Narrative synthesis

# Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides

## A. Mechanism relevant to climbing

**[Bioplausible]** Hydrolyzed collagen provides glycine, proline, hydroxyproline-rich peptides and amino acids that may increase substrate availability for **type I collagen synthesis** in tendons, annular pulleys, ligaments, and dermis. Mechanical loading increases tendon fibroblast signaling; concurrent amino acid availability may augment matrix remodeling.

For climbers, target tissues include:

* flexor tendon sheath/pulleys (A2/A4 stress)
* common flexor tendon
* finger skin dermis/ECM
* shoulder/elbow connective tissues

Direct proof in climbers is absent.

---

## B. Shaw et al. (2017): primary RCT

**Citation:** Shaw G et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 2017;105:136–143. DOI: 10.3945/ajcn.116.138594

## Design

* randomized crossover acute study
* **n=8**
* healthy young men only
* 0 g / 5 g / 15 g gelatin + **48 mg vitamin C**
* consumed **1 h pre-exercise**
* exercise = intermittent rope skipping to increase perfusion

## Outcomes

1. **Serum P1NP** (amino-terminal propeptide of type I procollagen)
   Marker of collagen synthesis, **not direct tendon healing**

2. Engineered ligament model exposed to participant serum

## Findings

**[Evidence-backed for surrogate only]**

* 15 g condition increased circulating amino acids
* increased P1NP significantly vs lower doses/placebo
* authors concluded augmented collagen synthesis milieu

## Important distinction

This is **not evidence that climbers heal tendons faster**. It is evidence that an acute nutritional protocol can transiently elevate a **biochemical marker** associated with collagen synthesis.

## Limitations

* n=8
* male-only
* healthy tissue, not injured tendons
* acute study
* rope skipping, not climbing
* no pulley injuries
* no performance outcomes
* no long-term imaging/histology
* several authors linked to collagen-performance research ecosystem (not disqualifying, but note incentive alignment)

## Tier

* **[Evidence-backed]** for raising surrogate marker P1NP acutely
* **[Bioplausible]** for improving tendon remodeling/healing
* **Not evidence-backed** for climbing performance

([PubMed][1])

---

## C. Vitamin C co-factor: your protocol gap

Vitamin C is required for **prolyl and lysyl hydroxylase**, enzymes needed for hydroxylation of procollagen residues, enabling stable triple helix formation and crosslinking.

## What this means

Without sufficient vitamin C:

* severe deficiency impairs collagen formation (scurvy physiology)
* marginal intake may reduce optimal synthesis efficiency

## Your current protocol

15 g collagen **without co-ingested vitamin C**

## Interpretation

### If your diet already contains adequate vitamin C:

**[Bioplausible]** Lack of co-ingestion may matter little if plasma ascorbate is already sufficient.

### If intake is low / inconsistent:

**[Bioplausible]** You may be missing a cofactor used in the only commonly cited protocol.

## Adequate intake

* US RDA: **90 mg/day men**, **75 mg/day women**
* Shaw acute dose: **48 mg** added to gelatin

Thus, Shaw used **less than daily RDA**, suggesting mega-dosing is unnecessary.

## Practical classification

* Benefit of collagen **with adequate vitamin C status**: **[Bioplausible + partial surrogate evidence]**
* Benefit of collagen with poor vitamin C intake: uncertain / potentially blunted

---

## D. Timing window

Shaw/Lis protocols used **1 hour pre-exercise** to align elevated amino acids with exercise-induced tissue perfusion.

## Your protocol: “before climbing”

If “before” = 45–75 min pre-session, this matches evidence best.

If consumed immediately walking into gym:

* likely less aligned with aminoacidemia peak depending on product digestion.

## Tier

**[Bioplausible supported by acute pharmacokinetic logic]**

---

## E. Dose: is 15 g supported?

15 g is the most repeatedly studied acute dose in Baar-associated literature.

Lis 2019 found **15 g hydrolyzed collagen** and gelatin produced similar amino acid rises; P1NP trend but nonsignificant.

No robust human dose-response curve proving 5 g <10 g <15 g <20 g for tendon outcomes.

## Classification

* **15 g = evidence-informed convention**
* not definitively optimal

([ResearchGate][2])

---

## F. Claim: collagen helps climbing skin healing

No RCTs found on:

* flappers
* split tips
* chronic fingertip thinning
* climbing callus recovery

Skin collagen turnover biology makes it plausible, but there is **no climbing-specific evidence**.

## Tier

**[Folklore: climbing community / coaches]**

---

## G. Claim: collagen helps pulley/tendon healing while actively climbing

Mechanistically sensible: pulleys and tendons are collagen-rich loaded tissues.

But no RCT demonstrates faster A2 pulley healing or reduced reinjury in climbers.

## Tier

**[Bioplausible]**
Not evidence-backed.

# Priority 2 — Additional supplements

# Creatine monohydrate

## Mechanism

Increases phosphocreatine resynthesis → repeated maximal contractions, power, training volume.

Relevant to:

* limit bouldering
* campus board
* repeated max hangs

## Evidence

Large sports literature supports strength/power generally. No strong climbing-specific RCT located.

## Caveat

Water retention / body mass gain can impair strength-to-weight ratio.

## Tier

**[Evidence-backed]** for strength/power generally
**[Bioplausible]** for climbers

## Dose

3–5 g/day maintenance.

---

# Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)

## Mechanism

Anti-inflammatory lipid mediators; may modulate soreness, joint pain, tendon cell signaling.

## Evidence

Mixed RCTs for tendinopathy/joint pain; no strong climbing data.

## Tier

**[Bioplausible]**

## Dose

1–3 g/day combined EPA+DHA commonly used.

---

# Magnesium

## Mechanism

ATP reactions, neuromuscular function, sleep.

## Evidence

Helps when deficient; little ergogenic benefit if replete.

## Tier

**[Evidence-backed]** for correcting deficiency
**[Bioplausible]** for sleep-mediated recovery

## Dose

Elemental magnesium 100–350 mg/day depending form/tolerance.

---

# Beta-alanine

## Mechanism

Raises muscle carnosine → buffers H+ accumulation.

Highly relevant to pumpy forearm efforts 1–4 min.

## Evidence

Climbing-specific RCT showed improvements in campus-like repeated movements and easy traverse endurance.

## Tier

**[Evidence-backed]** for some climbing endurance tasks.

## Dose

3.2–6.4 g/day for 4–8 weeks divided doses.

([PubMed][3])

---

# Caffeine

## Mechanism

Adenosine antagonism → alertness, reduced RPE, motor drive.

## Evidence

Broad sports evidence strong. Recent climbing study at **3 mg/kg** found no significant grip/pull-up benefit.

Likely may help focus, reaction, confidence more than grip force at low dose.

## Tier

**[Evidence-backed]** general ergogenic aid
**[Mixed / limited]** climbing-specific

## Dose

3–6 mg/kg 45–60 min pre-event (individual tolerance critical).

([MDPI][4])

# 3. Practical assessment of your collagen protocol

## Current protocol

**15 g hydrolyzed collagen peptides pre-climbing, no vitamin C**

## What benefit is supported?

### Tendon/pulley remodeling

**[Bioplausible]**
May support matrix turnover when paired with loading. No direct climbing proof.

### Injury prevention

**[Bioplausible]** but unproven.

### Faster pulley healing during active climbing

**[Bioplausible]** not demonstrated.

### Skin recovery

**[Folklore]**

### Better climbing performance same day

No evidence.

---

## What is missing?

1. **Vitamin C adequacy**
   Either co-ingestion or confirmed sufficient daily intake.

2. **Precise timing**
   Best evidence = ~60 min pre-loading.

3. **Structured loading stimulus**
   Collagen without progressive rehab/loading likely less useful.

4. **Longitudinal monitoring**
   Pain, finger tolerance, training capacity.

---

## What would the most evidence-aligned protocol look like?

**[Best current evidence-informed version]**

* 15 g hydrolyzed collagen or gelatin
* * ~50 mg vitamin C (or vitamin C-containing fruit)
* 45–60 min before session
* paired with targeted loading:

  * fingerboard rehab
  * progressive hang protocol
  * tendon-strength session

3–5×/week during rehab block.

Still **not strongly evidence-backed for outcomes**, only the surrogate pathway.
