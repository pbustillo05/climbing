## 2) Narrative synthesis

### Priority 1 — Hydrolyzed collagen peptides (detailed)

#### 1) Shaw et al. (2017): what it did and did not show
- **Design**: randomized, double-blind, crossover in **8 healthy young men**; placebo vs **5 g** or **15 g** **vitamin C–enriched gelatin**; supplement taken **1 h pre** 6 min rope skipping; repeated **3×/day for 3 days**. [Evidence-backed] ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Primary biochemical outcome**: serum **P1NP (PINP)**, a **type I collagen synthesis marker**. [Evidence-backed] ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Reported direction/magnitude**: **15 g** condition reported to produce **~2× higher P1NP** vs control (surrogate marker). [Evidence-backed] ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **“Functional tendon stiffness” nuance**: the study’s “mechanics” testing was performed on **engineered ligaments treated with participant serum** (in vitro bioassay), not direct measurement of **in vivo** tendon stiffness or pulley mechanical properties. [Evidence-backed] ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Major limitations**: **n=8**, male-only, healthy tissue (not injured), short duration, climbing-irrelevant loading model (rope skipping), and reliance on a **surrogate** (P1NP) plus an **in vitro** mechanics proxy. [Evidence-backed] ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  

**Tier classification of “collagen + vit C + timed loading increases a collagen-synthesis biomarker acutely”**: **[Evidence-backed]** (but only for biomarkers/in vitro proxies; not for climbing injury reduction or pulley healing).

#### 2) Vitamin C co-factor and the “no vitamin C” protocol gap
- **Mechanism**: ascorbate is required for prolyl/lysyl hydroxylase activity, enabling hydroxylation of proline/lysine residues and stabilizing the collagen triple helix; deficiency impairs collagen maturation. **[Bioplausible]** (mechanism is textbook, but the “collagen supplement works only if you co-ingest C” claim is not directly proven in climbing tendons with controlled trials).
- **What Shaw actually used**: **48 mg vitamin C** in the supplement vehicle (for placebo and gelatin conditions). **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Protocol gap inference**: if the user co-ingests **0 mg vitamin C with the shake**, the key question is **total vitamin C status** (dietary intake + baseline tissue saturation), not just “in-shake C.” Shaw standardized inclusion of 48 mg, but did **not** compare “15 g gelatin with 0 mg vitamin C” vs “15 g gelatin with vitamin C.” Therefore, “no C co-ingestion eliminates benefit” remains **unproven**. **[Bioplausible]** (gap: lack of direct comparator arm).
- **What “adequate vitamin C” means**:
  - **RDA-level adequacy** (diet sufficiency) is a different question than **acute co-ingestion** used to standardize mechanistic studies. The only hard number in your seed protocol here is Shaw’s **48 mg**. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
  - Any recommendation that “RDA alone is enough to match Shaw’s effect” is **[Bioplausible]** unless supported by controlled trials stratified by vitamin C status/intake (not identified in the accessible sources above).

**Tier classification of “co-ingesting ~50 mg vitamin C is necessary for benefit”**: **[Bioplausible]** (mechanistically plausible; not directly tested as a required condition in RCTs).

#### 3) Timing window: does “pre-climbing” match the evidence?
- In Shaw, collagen-derived amino acids (glycine/proline/hydroxyproline) **peaked ~1 h after ingestion**, and the exercise bout was scheduled at that time. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Translation to climbing**:
  - If “before climbing” means **~45–75 min pre-loading**, it matches the Shaw timing logic (maximize substrate availability during loading + post-loading synthesis window). **[Bioplausible]** (mechanistic translation; not tested in climbers).
  - If “before” is **immediately pre** (0–15 min), it likely misses the modeled peak AA window. **[Bioplausible]** (based on Shaw’s time-to-peak AA observation). ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  

**Tier classification of “~1 h pre is better than immediately pre”**: **[Bioplausible]** (supported by AA pharmacokinetics in Shaw but not directly linked to clinical endpoints).

#### 4) Dose: is 15 g evidence-supported?
- Shaw explicitly tested **5 g vs 15 g** gelatin (with 48 mg vitamin C), showing dose-dependent increases in circulating collagen-related AAs and the strongest signal at **15 g**. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- Lis & Baar (2019) used **15 g** vitamin C–enriched gelatin and **15 g hydrolyzed collagen**, reporting similar AA increases and a **~20%** “tendency” for P1NP to rise (non-significant). **[Evidence-backed]** ([researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/331696084_Effects_of_Different_Vitamin-C_Enriched_Collagen_Derivatives_on_Collagen_Synthesis?utm_source=openai))  

**Tier classification of “15 g is a reasonable target dose for acute biomarker studies”**: **[Evidence-backed]** (for biomarkers only; not proven for injury prevention/healing in climbers).

#### 5) Claim: collagen helps *climbing skin healing* (flappers, splits, chronic thinning)
- I did not identify (in the searches above) any RCTs specifically testing **collagen peptides** for **climbing-specific skin injury recovery** (flappers/tears/chronic fingertip skin tolerance). **[Evidence-backed: absence of direct evidence in retrieved sources]**  
- Therefore, “collagen heals climber skin faster” is best classified as **[Folklore: climber community/coaching practice]** unless you provide a specific peer-reviewed trial to evaluate.

#### 6) Claim: collagen helps *pulley/tendon healing during active climbing*
- Mechanism: collagen peptides provide collagen-enriched amino acids (glycine/proline/hydroxyproline) that could increase substrate availability for **type I collagen** synthesis in loaded connective tissue. **[Bioplausible]** (mechanism; gap: direct pulley healing RCTs in climbers).
- Shaw supports an **acute increase in a synthesis marker** (P1NP) and an **in vitro engineered ligament** improvement when using post-supplement serum. **[Evidence-backed]** for surrogate/in vitro outcomes only. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- Clinical inference (reduced pulley injury incidence, faster recovery while still climbing) remains **unproven**. **[Bioplausible]**

**Tier classification**:
- “May increase collagen synthesis signaling/biomarkers when paired with timed loading”: **[Evidence-backed]**. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- “Improves pulley/tendon healing outcomes in climbers who keep climbing”: **[Bioplausible]** (gap: no climbing injury RCTs).

#### Systematic review context (tendon outcomes)
- There are systematic reviews on collagen peptide supplementation and connective tissue-related outcomes, but conclusions depend heavily on heterogeneity (population, outcomes, co-interventions, industry involvement). Example: Khatri et al. 2021 systematic review on collagen peptides + exercise (DOI: 10.1007/s00726-021-03072-x). **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8521576/?utm_source=openai))  
- A newer tendon-focused systematic review exists (Buchalski et al., 2026, DOI: 10.3390/jfmk11010130). **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC13028264/?utm_source=openai))  
- Neither of these, by themselves, establishes a climbing-specific pulley/skin endpoint. **[Evidence-backed]** (scope limitation).

### Priority 2 — Additional supplements (brief, climbing-relevant)

#### Creatine monohydrate
- Mechanism: increases phosphocreatine availability → improved repeated high-power contractions; potentially relevant to bouldering dynos / repeated hard moves. **[Bioplausible]**
- Climbing-specific RCT evidence: not identified in the searches performed above. **[Evidence-backed: absence in retrieved sources]**
- Tier: **[Bioplausible]** for climbing performance; **[Evidence-backed]** for strength/power sports generally (not climbing-specific; not cited here).
- Dose/timing (if used): **3–5 g/day** daily; timing likely secondary to saturation. **[Bioplausible]** (general sports nutrition practice; not climbing-specific).

#### Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA)
- Mechanism: may modulate inflammation/resolution pathways; could affect DOMS perception, potentially tendon pain signaling, and recovery. **[Bioplausible]**
- Direct RCT evidence for tendinopathy/connective tissue remodeling: not established from the sources retrieved here. **[Evidence-backed: not located in retrieved sources]**
- Tier: **[Bioplausible]** for tendon/joint health in climbers given current retrieval.
- Dose: commonly **1–3 g/day EPA+DHA**; timing flexible. **[Bioplausible]**

#### Magnesium
- Mechanism: neuromuscular function; potential sleep/insomnia symptom improvement in poor sleepers. **[Bioplausible]**
- Evidence example (sleep, not climbing): magnesium bisglycinate **250 mg elemental/day** improved ISI at 4 weeks with small effect (**d≈0.2**, CI reported for ISI change). **[Evidence-backed]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40918053?utm_source=openai))  
- Tier for climbing performance: **[Bioplausible]** (sleep improvement may indirectly aid recovery; no direct climbing performance RCT here).
- Dose: if targeting sleep and intake is low, **~250 mg elemental/day** per trial. **[Evidence-backed]** for insomnia symptoms in poor sleepers. ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40918053?utm_source=openai))  

#### Beta-alanine
- Mechanism: increases muscle carnosine → improves H+ buffering during high-intensity efforts ~0.5–10 min; plausible for pumpy forearm work and repeated campus bouts. **[Bioplausible]**
- Climbing-specific trial: 4 weeks **4.0 g/day** improved certain climbing-specific tasks (campus-board volume; “easy traverse” time to failure/moves) with large reported ds. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34069981/?utm_source=openai))  
- Tier: **[Evidence-backed]** (at least one sport-specific double-blind trial).
- Dose: **4.0 g/day** split doses for 4 weeks (per study). **[Evidence-backed]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8157844/?utm_source=openai))  
- Caveat: outcomes were specific tasks; “hard traverse” showed no clear benefit; sample small; possible training-season confounding. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34069981/?utm_source=openai))  

#### Caffeine
- Mechanism: adenosine antagonism → increased alertness, reduced perceived effort; may improve power/endurance depending on task. **[Bioplausible]**
- Climbing-specific RCT (2026): **3 mg/kg** caffeine did **not** meaningfully improve pull-up/grip/hang performance vs placebo in 13 male climbers; subjective energy increased. **[Evidence-backed]** ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41599897/?utm_source=openai))  
- Tier: **[Evidence-backed]** for “3 mg/kg does not meaningfully improve these proxy climbing tests in this sample.”
- Dose/timing: **3 mg/kg** acute used; higher doses remain an open question in climbers (and have side-effect tradeoffs). **[Evidence-backed]** (for 3 mg/kg result only). ([pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41599897/?utm_source=openai))

## 3) Practical assessment of the user’s collagen protocol (15 g hydrolyzed collagen pre-climb, **no vitamin C co-ingestion**)

### What benefit is supported (and at what tier)?
1. **Raising circulating collagen-related amino acids** after ingestion of collagen/gelatin derivatives: **[Evidence-backed]** in timed gelatin protocols (1 h peak). ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
2. **Increasing a collagen synthesis biomarker (P1NP) acutely when paired with short loading bouts**: **[Evidence-backed]** (surrogate outcome; not clinical). ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
3. **Reducing pulley/tendon injury risk or accelerating pulley healing while continuing to climb**: **[Bioplausible]** (no climbing injury RCT identified here).  
4. **Improving climbing skin healing (flappers/tears)**: **[Folklore: climber community / coach practice]** (no direct trial identified here).

### What is missing / mismatched vs evidence-modeled protocol?
- **Vitamin C co-ingestion**: Shaw standardized **48 mg** vitamin C in the supplement drink. Your protocol has **0 mg co-ingested**, so equivalence is not established. **[Evidence-backed]** for Shaw’s inclusion; **[Bioplausible]** that omitting C reduces efficacy depending on baseline status. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- **Timing precision**: evidence-model timing is **~1 h pre-loading**; “pre-climb” could be earlier/later. If you drink it immediately before, it likely misses the modeled AA peak. **[Bioplausible]** ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  

### What would an evidence-backed protocol look like (narrowly, per Shaw-style evidence)?
- **15 g** gelatin/collagen derivative **+ ~48 mg vitamin C**, taken **~60 min before** a short bout of connective-tissue loading, repeated with sufficient spacing (Shaw used **3×/day** with ≥6 h spacing). **[Evidence-backed]** for matching the experimental conditions and producing biomarker changes. ([pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5183725/?utm_source=openai))  
- Extending that to “before each climbing session to prevent pulley injuries” remains **[Bioplausible]** (gap: no clinical endpoints in climbers).
