# Document Summary — Hangboarding for Rock Climbing: A Literature Review

> One paragraph per section/subsection. Use this file to quickly locate content and understand what has been written. All section files live in `sections/`. The compiled document is built from `main.tex`.

---

## Abstract (`01_abstract.tex`)

Synthesises the entire review in one paragraph: 10 intervention trials + 1 retrospective cohort (Gilmore, n=527) + 1 meta-analysis (Stien 2023). Reports the three principal conclusions — hangboarding improves MFS and endurance (meta-analytic SDM 0.41), intensity determines adaptation type (≥80% MFS for strength, 60–80% for stamina), and Abrahangs did not significantly outperform climbing-only controls as a standalone. Flags the universal limitations: male-advanced cohorts, short follow-up, surrogate outcomes, no grade-improvement RCT.

## 3. Hangboarding (`04_biomechanics.tex`, `05_intervention_studies.tex`, `06_practitioner_consensus.tex`, `07_discussion.tex`)

Parent section grouping the core scientific review. Contains no introductory text of its own — begins immediately with the biomechanics subsection.

### 3.1 Biomechanical and Physiological Foundations (`04_biomechanics.tex`)

Covers finger pulley biomechanics: A2 pulley forces estimated at ~2.7× fingertip load in crimp (Vigouroux 2006); full-crimp A2 loading ~36× greater than open-hand; cadaveric data confirm 380–700 N A2 loads (Schweizer 2001). Explains why middle/ring fingers bear disproportionate load and why full-crimp is high-risk. Establishes MFS as the single strongest measurable grade predictor; notes general upper-body training does not substitute.

### 3.2 Intervention Studies (`05_intervention_studies.tex`)

The main evidence section. Contains a large study-summary table (11 studies, 2012–2025) and a risk-of-bias table. Subdivided by research question:

- **3.2.1 Study Summary** — full 11-study table (López-Rivera 2012 through Ruiz-López 2025) with design, protocol, main finding, DOI.
- **3.2.2 Max Hangs vs. Intermittent Hangs** — López-Rivera 2019: IntHangs best for endurance (+45%, ES 1.0), MaxHangs best for MFS (+28%, ES 0.6), Combination underperforms both. Devise 2022: 80% MFS is the pragmatic all-purpose intensity.
- **3.2.3 Added Weight vs. Reduced Edge Depth** — Mundry 2021: added weight on 20 mm outperformed edge reduction (p=0.032, ES 0.36); minimal-edge training not evidence-supported.
- **3.2.4 Low-Intensity Daily Hangs ("Abrahangs")** — Gilmore 2024 (retrospective cohort, n=527): Abrahangs alone +2.5% (p=0.12, NS vs. climbing-only); MaxHangs +3.2% (p<0.001); both combined +5.8%. Design is observational; treat as hypothesis-generating only.
- **3.2.5 Rate of Force Development** — Levernier 2019: +8% RFD after 4 weeks. Hermans 2022: +28% within-group (between-group p=0.056). Stien 2023 meta: SDM=0.91 (CI 0.21–1.61).
- **3.2.6 Training Volume and NMES (Dindorf 2024)** — NMES-assisted fingerboard 2×/wk non-inferior to conventional 3×/wk despite ~33% lower volume; preliminary (n=18).
- **3.2.7 Universal Methodological Limitations** — n=11–54 across trials; male-advanced cohorts; ≤10-week duration; surrogate outcomes; no injury data in any RCT.
- **Risk of Bias (unnumbered)** — simplified per-study table: all trials "High" or "Moderate" risk; PEDro 5–7/10.
- **3.2.8 Meta-Analytic Evidence** — Stien 2023 (5 RCTs, n=110): finger strength SDM=0.41 (CI 0.03–0.80), RFD SDM=0.91, forearm endurance SDM=1.23, overall SDM=0.57. No effect on on-wall tests.
- **3.2.9 Injury and Experience-Dependent Risk (Sjöman 2023)** — survey n=434: RFT protective in ≥6-year climbers (χ²=4.57, p=0.03); RFT positively associated with injury in <6-year climbers reaching ≥7a (χ²=4.61, p=0.03).
- **3.2.10 Female-Specific Evidence (Langer 2024)** — only all-female RCT; anecdotal fingerboard advantage (BF₁₀=1.16); on-wall training preferred for enjoyment.

### 3.3 Practitioner Consensus and Contested Topics (`06_practitioner_consensus.tex`)

Summarises seven points of majority practitioner consensus (no beginner max-loading, 15-min warm-up, session freshness, 2–3×/wk with ≥48 h, half-crimp default, overload before reducing edge, deload every 4–8 weeks) — all classified Folklore or Bioplausible. The Contested Topics subsubsection covers five disputed areas: 20 mm as standard edge, minimal-edge vs. added-weight, daily low-intensity hangs, no-hang devices, and full-crimp training.

### 3.4 Discussion (`07_discussion.tex`)

Synthesises the evidence into five numbered conclusions (confidence-rated), lists five open research questions, and presents an evidence-weighted protocol (edge, strength phase, stamina phase, daily loading, deload, injury monitoring). This is the key "what should you actually do" section, clearly labelled by evidence tier throughout.

- **3.4.1 Evidence Synthesis** — five conclusions: hangboarding works (SDM 0.41–1.23); intensity specificity confirmed; combination within one mesocycle underperforms; added weight > edge reduction; Abrahangs non-significant standalone.
- **3.4.2 Open Questions** — grade transfer unproven; beginner/female data absent; no long-term tendon imaging; periodisation untested; Abrahangs RCT needed.
- **3.4.3 Evidence-Weighted Protocol** — concrete protocol: 18–20 mm, max hangs at 85–95% MFS, repeaters at 60–80%, Abrahangs experimental, deload every 4–8 weeks, injury-monitoring thresholds.

## 4. Weekly Training Integration (`09_weekly_integration.tex`)

Large applied section translating the intervention data into practical weekly structure. Preamble flags a citation ambiguity in Stien 2023 volume/page numbers (resolved to 40(1):179–191). Contains a study summary table for studies directly informing programming (Stien 2023, López-Rivera 2019, Devise 2022, Stien 2021, Wilson 2012 concurrent-training meta).

- **4.1 Summary Table** — five studies with full protocol and effect-size detail.
- **4.2 Fatigue Interference Between Modalities** — Evidence-backed: supplemental finger training on top of climbing significantly outperforms climbing alone (Stien 2023). Bioplausible: limit bouldering and max hangs share the same effector tissue; mechanisms of interference are peripheral fatigue, PCr depletion, and central inhibition — not the classical AMPK/mTOR pathway. Applies Wilson 2012 concurrent-training principles by analogy. Moonboard sub-section: treat as equivalent to limit bouldering for scheduling; no Moonboard-specific fatigue data exist. Top-rope sub-section: qualitatively different fatigue profile (aerobic, submaximal); should not immediately precede max-hang sessions.
- **4.3 Session Sequencing Rules** — Bioplausible: max hangs must precede limit bouldering; pre-fatigue shifts an intended F100 session toward F80/F60. Same-day combination feasible if hangboard goes first, volume reduced ~50%, with ≥15-min passive gap. Eva López / Anderson & Anderson practitioner sources cited.
- **4.4 Example Weekly Structures** — three 4-day templates: (A) Strength-Priority Block (hangboard-led, Mon/Fri max hangs, Wed limit bouldering, Sat endurance); (B) Bouldering-Led Block (Mon limit boulder, Tue reduced hangboard, Thu Moonboard, Sat ARC); (C) Evidence-Constrained Balanced Block (closest to actual 2×/wk RCT design). Each day labelled with evidence tier.
- **4.5 Deload Triggers and Protocol** — Bioplausible deload indicators: MVC drop >10% over 2 sessions, RPE increase ≥1.5 pts, finger pain VAS ≥3/10 at rest (mandatory stop), HRV downtrend ≥5 days. Protocol: reduce volume 40–60%, maintain ≥1 high-intensity exposure. Folklore: Lattice/Anderson schedules every 3–6 weeks.
- **4.6 What the Literature Cannot Yet Answer** — six gaps confirmed by ≥3 independent LLM responses: optimal inter-session interval, sequencing effect on long-term adaptation, Moonboard fatigue profile, AMPK/mTOR in climbers, maintenance frequency during redpointing, validated readiness metrics.

## 5. Supplementation (`10_supplements.tex`)

Covers five supplements with climbing relevance. Leads with a study summary table (10 studies, full protocol/effect-size/industry-funding flags). Overview notes no supplement has been tested in a climbing-specific RCT with a functional primary outcome.

- **5.1 Study Summary Table** — Shaw 2017 (gelatin, P1NP), Lis & Baar 2019, Praet 2019 (collagen + Achilles tendinopathy), Khatri 2021 (systematic review), Buchalski 2026 (structural tendon outcomes), Nulty 2024, Sas-Nowosielski 2021 (β-alanine in climbers), Sandford 2018 (omega-3 + shoulder), Grgic 2022 (caffeine + handgrip), Ruiz-López 2026 (caffeine in climbers).
- **5.2 Hydrolyzed Collagen Peptides** — detailed: mechanism (collagen triple-helix, vitamin C cofactor), critical appraisal of Shaw 2017 (n=8, surrogate P1NP endpoint, industry conflict), Lis & Baar non-significant replication, Praet 2019 Achilles tendinopathy data (VISA-A +12.6 vs +5.3), dose (15 g best-supported), 1-hour timing window, vitamin C gap (user protocol assessed: dose correct, vitamin C absent = correctable critical gap), claim for skin healing (Folklore), claim for pulley remodelling (Bioplausible), user protocol correction table.
- **5.3 Creatine Monohydrate** — Evidence-backed for general strength/power; Bioplausible for climbing; body-mass caveat (0.5–2 kg water weight) may impair strength-to-weight.
- **5.4 Omega-3 Fatty Acids (EPA/DHA)** — Bioplausible anti-inflammatory mechanism; best RCT (Sandford 2018, rotator cuff) shows no primary-outcome benefit; no climbing-specific data. Claims of tendon strengthening are Folklore.
- **5.5 Magnesium** — Bioplausible for sleep-mediated recovery in athletes with suboptimal status; Evidence-backed null for cramp prevention (Cochrane/JAMA). Dose: 200–400 mg glycinate/malate/citrate, evening.
- **5.6 Beta-Alanine** — Evidence-backed for 60–240 s high-intensity efforts (Hedges g=0.374, Trexler 2015 meta); climbing-specific RCT (Sas-Nowosielski 2021, n=15): large effects on campus board and easy traverse (d=1.44–1.73), no hard-traverse benefit. Dose: 3.2–6.4 g/day divided.
- **5.7 Caffeine** — Evidence-backed for isometric handgrip (d=0.17, Grgic 2022) and muscular endurance (d=0.41); only climbing RCT (Ruiz-López 2026, n=13) found all outcomes non-significant at 3 mg/kg. Anxiety caveat specific to climbing.
- **5.8 What the Literature Cannot Currently Resolve** — eight gaps: pulley CSA under collagen supplementation, minimum vitamin C co-dose, dose >15 g, collagen for skin, β-alanine and perceived pump on routes, caffeine >3 mg/kg in climbing, omega-3 and finger tendons, supplement interaction effects.

## 6. Finger Injury Management (`11_injury_management.tex`)

Comprehensive applied section on finger injuries. Leads with a warning box: no climbing rehabilitation RCTs exist; all protocols are Bioplausible or Folklore. Contains a study summary table (10 studies: Sjöman 2023, Klauser 2002, Berrigan 2021, Schöffl 2003, Schweizer 2000, Alfredson 1998, Cook & Purdam 2009, Rio 2015, Shaw 2017, Iruretagoiena 2020).

- **6.1 A2 Pulley Injuries** — anatomy (A2 spans proximal phalanx, ring/middle fingers ~98% of pulley injuries), Schöffl grading (I–IV by bowstringing severity), diagnosis (dynamic ultrasound recommended: Klauser 2002 sensitivity 98%/specificity 100%; MRI for equivocal cases; TBD cutoff range 1.9–5.1 mm across studies). Conservative management: immobilisation timelines by grade (0 d/10 d/14 d), return-to-climbing timelines, H-taping (Schweizer 2000: modest biomechanical reduction, no clinical outcome RCT), load-managed rehabilitation via Cook-Purdam continuum and Alfredson eccentric protocol (Achilles analogy), Tyler Nelson progressive loading protocols (Folklore). Surgical management for Grade III–IV: loop reconstruction techniques, case series outcomes.
- **6.2 Flexor Tendon Strains** — anatomy, grading (I–III by US architecture), differentiation from pulley injury (bowstringing vs. tendon-course tenderness), Grade I–II management mirrors pulley rehab; Grade III requires urgent surgical referral.
- **6.3 Skin Management** — flappers: acute management (trim, irrigate, occlusive dressing), return timeline (3–14 d, Folklore); prevention (callus filing, moisturising, volume management — all Folklore). Chronic skin thinning: mechanism (Bioplausible from dermatology), interventions (Antihydral, humidity management, Rhino Skin — Folklore). Collagen supplementation for skin: Evidence-backed for aging/burn populations (skin elasticity/hydration endpoints); Bioplausible for climbing-specific flapper healing; no direct RCT.
- **6.4 General Injury Prevention Principles** — warm-up: Bioplausible (FIFA 11+ analogy); load management: 10% rule is Folklore (specific threshold arbitrary, Gabbett 2016 acute:chronic framework; contested). Experience-dependent risk: Sjöman 2023 evidence re-stated.
- **6.5 What the Literature Cannot Yet Answer** — seven gaps: progressive loading vs. rest for Grade I–II, optimal immobilisation duration, H-taping clinical effectiveness, ultrasound-guided return criteria, pulley load-to-failure during healing, collagen for pulley/skin, structural mediators of experience-dependent risk.

## 7. Recovery (`12_recovery.tex`)

Applied section distinguishing muscle recovery (24–72 h) from connective tissue recovery (days–weeks collagen turnover). Contains a study summary table (11 studies: Mah 2011, Milewski 2014, Halson 2014, Kiviniemi 2007, Dupuy 2018, Roberts 2015, Pearcey 2015, Molouki 2016, Mourot 2015, Shaw 2017, Moore 2009).

- **7.1 Sleep** — Evidence-backed: Mah 2011 (basketball, +110 min/night → sprint +0.7 s, shooting +9%); Milewski 2014 (<8 h → OR 1.70 for injury). Bioplausible: SWS→GH→IGF-1→collagen axis for connective tissue repair. Practical target ≥8 h, 9 h during high-volume blocks. Notes alcohol, late caffeine, and chronic training stress as SWS-reducers.
- **7.2 HRV Monitoring** — Evidence-backed: Kiviniemi 2007 RCT (HRV-guided vs. fixed training: VO₂peak +3.9 vs. +1.4 ml/kg/min, p=0.002); device validity confirmed (Altini & Kinnunen 2021). Critical Bioplausible caveat: HRV reflects autonomic state, not connective tissue repair — a "green" HRV does not clear finger pulleys for load. Folklore: specific RMSSD threshold rules (±0.5 SD from rolling mean). Practical decision table provided.
- **7.3 Contrast Therapy and Cold-Water Immersion** — Evidence-backed for DOMS reduction (Dupuy 2018 network meta, CWT SMD range -0.40 to -0.60). Critical: Roberts 2015 RCT (n=21, 12 wk) shows CWI blunts type-II fibre CSA (+0% vs. +17%) and p70S6K — avoid routine CWI after hangboard/strength sessions. Use CWT only after technique/pump days.
- **7.4 Soft-Tissue Work** — Evidence-backed: massage best modality for DOMS in Dupuy 2018 (SMD -0.72); foam rolling Pearcey 2015 (d=0.59–0.84 DOMS). Forearm-specific: Molouki 2016 (n=44, 5-min massage: grip endurance +21%). Bioplausible for climbing application; contraindication for aggressive pressure directly over acute pulley.
- **7.5 Active Recovery Sessions** — Evidence-backed: lactate clearance 30–40% faster with low-intensity aerobic activity (Cochrane 2004); Mourot 2015 (only climbing-specific recovery RCT, d=0.6 favouring active climbing recovery over walking). Bioplausible threshold: open-hand only, <40% MVC, RPE ≤4/10 to avoid additional connective tissue stress.
- **7.6 Nutrition Timing** — Muscle: Moore 2009 (MPS plateaus at ~0.24 g/kg/meal whey); Morton 2018 (total daily protein 1.6–2.2 g/kg more important than timing). Connective tissue: Shaw 2017 gelatin protocol (15 g + 48 mg vitamin C, 1 h pre-session) — P1NP doubled. RED-S: Mountjoy 2018 (energy availability <30 kcal/kg FFM → elevated injury risk; climbing-specific risk flagged).
- **7.7 Practical Recovery Protocol** — Summary table mapping timing → action → dose → evidence classification for: daily sleep, morning HRV, pre-session collagen, post-session protein/carbs, CWT (pump days only), forearm soft-tissue work, active recovery, total protein intake, energy availability. HRV decision rules (Folklore tier) with three RMSSD thresholds.
- **7.8 What the Literature Cannot Yet Answer** — six gaps: sleep × finger performance, HRV and finger injury prediction, CWT/CWI and tendon adaptation, collagen supplementation with functional endpoint, active recovery threshold for connective tissue, RED-S and finger tendons.

## 8. Conclusion (`08_conclusion.tex`)

Summarises the full review in one paragraph: hangboarding is evidence-supported for intermediate-to-advanced male climbers (SDM 0.41–1.23 on surrogate measures); no grade improvement proven; max hangs → MFS, repeaters → stamina, 80% MFS is the pragmatic compromise; Abrahangs not significant standalone; injury risk experience-dependent. Flags critical gaps: female data, long-term trials, grade improvement, skin/equipment science. Closes with the recommendation to apply practitioner folklore as biologically plausible guidance, not established protocol.
